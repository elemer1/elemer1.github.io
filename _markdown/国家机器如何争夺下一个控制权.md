Joe Studwell’s core insight in *How Asia Works* is, in essence, a theory of the power dynamics by which state capacity forcibly allocates scarce resources across time. In the industrial era, those scarce resources were fiat money and capital; in the cycle of emergent intelligence, they become compute, energy, and highly cohesive data.

We cannot remain at the surface because doing so reduces this profound political-economic struggle to a mere checklist of regulatory tools, without unpacking the system’s feedback loops or the underlying motives of power. What we need to examine more deeply is this: when intelligence replaces capital as the primary source of rents and productivity, the traditional state machinery’s reliance on monetary issuance and banking licenses may suffer functional obsolescence, but the state will not relinquish power. Instead, it will reshape the physical and legal boundaries of production factors, imposing a new era of **Compute Repression** and **Informational Enclosure**.

## From Capital Repression to Compute and Cognitive Repression

To understand how the state apparatus controls AI, we must first clarify how the object of control has changed. The foundation of finance is the intertemporal allocation of resources and risk; the foundation of AI is the directed reduction of informational entropy and the automated consolidation of decision-making power. The state’s path to controlling the new order bifurcates mainly along two dimensions: the physical and the institutional.

## Physical Upstream Capture and Structural Compute Allocation

What the state apparatus is best at is monopolizing entities in the atomic world. Yet the bit-world of AI must depend on an extremely heavy physical substrate.

The first mechanism is the **weaponization of supply chains**. Governments do not need to censor every open-source model; they merely need to cut off the disorderly flow of advanced semiconductors upstream, such as GPU or TPU clusters. Today’s export controls and equipment-access regimes are, in essence, the modern version of foreign-exchange controls. By controlling who can obtain the latest generation of production tools, the state determines who holds the interpretive power over intelligence.

The second mechanism is an absolute access regime for energy: **energy-grid licensing**. As large-model training and inference move toward hyperscale data centers, their demand for electricity and nuclear energy has already become a load-bearing issue at the level of national infrastructure. Through environmental approvals, grid-access permits, and strategic energy allocation, governments are building a physical moat. AI entities that do not conform to the state’s strategic direction will be cut off at the physical level — quite literally, by having their power severed.

## Institutional Data Enclosure and Sovereign Data Barriers

Data is the fuel of intelligence, and states are using legal fiction to transform freely circulating data into a strategic national asset akin to land or minerals.

**Data sovereignty** and digital tariffs are artificially creating informational friction. This is equivalent to trade barriers in traditional economics, forcing high-value domestic data to circulate only within controlled sovereign data pools.

At the same time, cognitive alignment and compliance audits are evolving into an expensive form of administrative rent. The state is turning value alignment into a high-frequency, ideologically charged black-box audit. Only giants with immense capital and political resources can afford these soaring alignment costs. Objectively, this completes the compliance-based screening process through which industry monopoly is legitimized.

## The Return of Licensing

The core logic of modern state governance is simple: without a license, you are illegal. The future AI industry will not be a laissez-faire cyber-utopia. It is now precisely reproducing the regulatory path once applied to systemically important financial institutions, or SIFIs.

This replay is first visible in the transformation of compute thresholds into a form of capital adequacy requirement. Mandatory reporting and safety reviews for foundation models exceeding a particular computational scale — for instance, $10^{26}$ FLOPS — are mechanically identical to capital adequacy reviews for banks. Through this mechanism, the state ensures that no private model can secretly evolve capabilities beyond the grasp of the state apparatus.

The second manifestation is the franchising of application-layer exits. Open-weight models may escape onto edge devices, but whenever AI attempts to connect to high-margin, high-risk interfaces with the physical social order — autonomous driving, robo-advisors, clinical medicine, legal adjudication — the state will activate stringent licensing procedures. By controlling the monetization exits of application scenarios, the state can discipline upstream model developers in reverse.

## The Paradox of Control

Yet if the state apparatus simply copies the strong interventionism described in *How Asia Works*, it will face systemic backlash far more severe than what occurred when controlling the financial sector. This control regime contains three fundamental logical paradoxes.

The first is the conflict between **bureaucratic lag and exponential cascades**. The key premise behind the success of the East Asian model was catch-up growth: the technological path was already known, and the state could concentrate resources on copying the homework. But the evolution of AGI is an unknown frontier filled with nonlinearity and emergence. The decision cycle of the state bureaucracy is a static system measured in years, while AI evolves as a dynamic system measured in weeks. Once the state uses administrative orders to designate a technological route or lock in compute allocation, it will immediately generate severe resource misallocation and rapidly fall behind by entire technological generations in the race.

The second is the failure of **Ashby’s Law of Requisite Variety**. Ashby’s Law states that for any control system to exercise effective control, the variety of its internal control variables must be greater than or equal to the variety of the system being controlled. Traditional financial systems have relatively simple variables: interest rates, liquidity, reserve ratios. But the information flows and code mutations of the AI ecosystem are growing explosively. If the state apparatus tries to achieve absolute control, it must build a bureaucratic audit system larger and more intelligent than the AI ecosystem itself. Economically, this causes administrative costs to rise exponentially. Ultimately, to maintain control, the state would have no choice but to forcibly reduce the cognitive diversity of society as a whole, resulting in the mediocritization of collective intelligence.

The third is the loss of **export discipline** and the emergence of greenhouse models. This is the most subtle part of Studwell’s theory: the precondition for East Asian states protecting infant industries was that those industries were forced to submit to the brutal test of international markets. Winners took all; losers lost access to credit. But in the AI era, if the state, in the name of security and order, walls off domestic AI inside a closed data island, it automatically destroys this selection mechanism. Lacking the world’s most diverse and adversarial data environments, protected domestic models may be politically compliant, even perfectly so, but in terms of dynamic efficiency they will rapidly deteriorate into overgrown infants with no international competitiveness.

## Endgame Projection

Between the pursuit of AI technological supremacy and the maintenance of internal security and order, the balance will not settle permanently on either side. Instead, it will evolve into an excruciating process of **dynamic tragic optimization**.

In the Nash equilibrium of intense external geopolitical competition, no great power can afford the cost of losing a technological generation. To fall decisively behind means losing the sovereign capacity to set rules — the most total form of loss of control. Therefore, the most likely evolutionary form of the state apparatus is an **authoritarian high-tech enclave**.

In this architecture, the state apparatus will form deep political alliances with a small number of highly transformed AI oligopolies. These giants will enjoy state-level allocations of compute and energy. In return, they will hand over foundational audit backdoors to the state and assume responsibility for maintaining the sovereign firewall. In practice, they will become digital central banks. Outside the core security perimeter, the state will allow a moderate degree of peripheral innovation. It will permit the open-source ecosystem to grow wildly in non-sensitive, low-value application domains, treating it as an outsourced laboratory from which the state and the giants can absorb innovative diversity.

The final point of tilt depends on the threshold of external pressure. When external geopolitical threats rise, the balance will swing violently toward tolerating volatility and pursuing technological leadership. But once internal order is shaken by mass unemployment caused by AI, or by the equalization of access to information, the balance will instantly snap back toward forceful regulation. This game has no endpoint. The state apparatus will remain trapped in endless systemic oscillation between the pursuit of omniscient control and the fear of being dimensionally outmatched.

If we continue extrapolating along this logic of a new era of compute and intelligence repression, then in the old East Asian model, ordinary people at the bottom paid for national industrialization by enduring financial repression — namely, low savings rates that invisibly transferred resources to the state. In the coming AI era, ordinary individuals, as unpaid providers of data and as cognitive subjects to be aligned, will be paying their **intelligence tax** in what form?
