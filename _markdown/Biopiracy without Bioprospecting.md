# Biopiracy without Bioprospecting: Digital Sequence Information, Generative Biology, and the Attribution Gap in Access-and-Benefit-Sharing Law

**abstract. **On November 19, 2025, nine months after the Convention on Biological Diversity launched the Cali Fund, the Fund received its first contribution: one thousand U.S. dollars from a small UK startup. That fact does not, standing alone, prove that AI-mediated biopiracy is occurring or that the Fund has failed. It does, however, expose the fragility of a voluntary, sectoral, non-attribution-based payment model at the same moment that biological discovery is becoming increasingly informational. This Article argues that the Nagoya Protocol, the 2024 WIPO Treaty on Intellectual Property, Genetic Resources and Associated Traditional Knowledge ("GRATK Treaty"), and the Cali Fund should be read as a single access-and-benefit-sharing ("ABS") trigger architecture. Nagoya relies on physical access; the GRATK Treaty relies on patent disclosure; the Cali Fund relies on firm size and sectoral contribution. Each trigger captures an administratively visible event. Contemporary discovery workflows can move value extraction upstream of those events by combining open Digital Sequence Information ("DSI"), traditional-use documentation, natural-product chemical structures, biomedical target data, structural-prediction systems, and generative molecule design. The defect is therefore not merely noncompliance. It is non-attribution. The Article makes four contributions. First, it identifies the common trigger logic of the Nagoya-GRATK-Cali triad. Second, it distinguishes DSI from adjacent provenance-bearing informational inputs: traditional knowledge, natural-product chemistry, biomedical target information, and AI-generated analogues. Third, it tests the architecture against counterfactual reconstructions of the Curcuma longa, Banisteriopsis caapi, and Hoodia gordonii episodes, calibrating each case by claim strength rather than treating them as identical DSI examples. Fourth, it proposes an informational-provenance benefit-sharing regime modeled on a Performance Rights Organization: a registry-mediated, attribution-weighted levy structured as a Calabresi-Melamed Rule 2 liability rule. The proposal preserves the Cali Fund's institutional infrastructure but relocates the trigger from visible downstream revenue to auditable use of registered provenance-bearing inputs.

## I. Introduction

On November 19, 2025, the Cali Fund --- the United Nations' principal vehicle for channeling private-sector monetary benefits from the use of DSI on genetic resources toward biodiversity conservation and indigenous peoples and local communities --- received its first contribution. The contributor was a UK startup called TierraViva AI. The amount was one thousand U.S. dollars. The startup did not, on the publicly reported figures, appear to meet the Fund's indicative contribution thresholds. Nine months had passed since the Fund's formal launch in February 2025 at the resumed sixteenth Conference of the Parties to the Convention on Biological Diversity in Rome.[^1] In the language of the Fund's own chief executive, the contribution was offered as an "ice-breaker," intended to encourage the larger pharmaceutical, cosmetics, agricultural-biotechnology, and AI-services firms whose use of digital genetic data the Fund formally recognizes.[^2] As of May 6, 2026, no publicly identifiable firm clearly meeting the indicative thresholds has been publicly reported as having contributed.

The empirical fact is striking, but it should not be asked to do more work than it can bear. A mechanism described by commentators and biodiversity-finance advocates as capable of mobilizing roughly one billion dollars per year for biodiversity conservation and indigenous-community benefit had, after roughly nine months of operation, publicly recorded only a one-thousand-dollar contribution from a small startup.[^3] That record is a diagnostic signal, not a complete causal proof. Implementation lag, voluntary-compliance friction, uncertainty pending COP17's finalization of thresholds and rates, and ordinary political resistance by large firms are all plausible partial explanations. This Article's claim is narrower and more structural: even if those explanations are true, they do not cure the attribution gap. The Cali Fund formally names AI-related DSI services in its sectoral coverage, but it does not require users to identify which DSI, traditional knowledge, source community, natural-product scaffold, or provenance chain generated value in a given discovery workflow. The empty first months therefore matter because they make visible the fragility of a voluntary, sectoral, non-attribution-based payment model; the deeper defect is that ideal compliance with that model would still leave much AI-mediated informational extraction unattributed.

The diagnosis tracks a pattern broader than the present case: regulatory architectures lag the informational technologies they purport to govern. The Digital Millennium Copyright Act responded to the internet-copyright pressures of the 1990s and preceded the Napster-style peer-to-peer file-sharing explosion by only a year.[^4] FinCEN's virtual-currency guidance followed Bitcoin's January 2009 launch by just over four years.[^5] The present global scramble to retrofit AI-governance architectures around foundation models that are already deployed continues the pattern. What is unique here is that the architecture--technology mismatch is being demonstrated in the empirical record of an instrument's first months of operation, in real time, and with a contributor pool of one. The empirical signal is sharper than usual; the structural argument it supports is the same.

The argument can be stated compactly. The Cali Fund is the most recent layer of an ABS architecture whose two preceding strata --- the 2010 Nagoya Protocol on Access to Genetic Resources and the Fair and Equitable Sharing of Benefits Arising from their Utilization to the Convention on Biological Diversity, and the 2024 WIPO Treaty on Intellectual Property, Genetic Resources and Associated Traditional Knowledge --- share with the Cali Fund a structural feature: each defines the moment at which benefit-sharing, disclosure, or payment is expected by reference to an administratively visible event. Under Nagoya, that moment is the act of accessing physical genetic material under prior informed consent and on mutually agreed terms.[^6] Under the GRATK Treaty, it is the act of filing a patent application whose claimed invention is "based on" genetic resources or associated traditional knowledge.[^7] Under the Cali Fund, it is the use of DSI by a firm above specified asset, sales, or profit thresholds in a sector that directly or indirectly benefits from DSI on genetic resources.[^8]

Each trigger is intelligible against the technological background of the era in which it was designed. Each is rendered substantively incomplete by the technological background of the era in which it now operates. Three converging capabilities account for the gap. The first is open repositories of DSI such as GenBank, UniProt, and the European Nucleotide Archive, which now contain substantial whole-genome, transcriptome, organellar-genome, and protein-sequence data for many medicinally significant plant species used in traditional pharmacopoeias, including the case-study species discussed below, subject to the evidentiary caveats stated in Part IV.[^9] The second is AlphaFold-3 and its peer systems --- RoseTTAFold-All-Atom from the Baker Lab, ESMFold from Meta AI, and Chai-1 from Chai Discovery --- which can support hypothesis generation, target modeling, and prioritization in drug-discovery workflows.[^10] On one widely cited protein-ligand benchmark, Chai-1 reports a 77 percent PoseBusters success rate, compared with 76 percent for AlphaFold-3; these figures are benchmark results for predicted complex structures, not clinical validation metrics.[^11] The third is the growing set of generative and predictive drug-design platforms --- Insilico Medicine's Chemistry42 and Biology42, Generate Biomedicines' Chroma for protein therapeutics, and Recursion/MIT's Boltz-2 for structure and binding-affinity prediction --- that can accelerate lead generation, protein design, virtual screening, and optimization.[^12] In combination, these capabilities allow a sufficiently resourced firm to translate traditional-use claims, public sequence data, natural-product structures, biomedical target information, and AI-generated analogues into candidate molecules without necessarily generating an access event, a facially provenance-revealing patent claim, or a contribution event traceable to the relevant community. The clinical capability claim is bounded: in June 2025, Nature Medicine published Phase IIa trial results for Insilico Medicine's Rentosertib, the first peer-reviewed Phase IIa clinical proof-of-concept for an AI-discovered drug; Rentosertib proves that the broader AI-discovery pipeline can reach clinical proof-of-concept, not that a TK- or DSI-derived candidate has done so.[^13] The integration of generative AI with ethnobotanical and traditional-medicine knowledge bases is itself a documented and growing program of contemporary research.[^14]

Terminology matters to that claim. This Article uses "DSI" to mean genetic sequence, protein-sequence, transcriptomic, genomic, organellar-genomic, and related sequence-derived information. It uses "traditional knowledge" to mean documented community knowledge of use, bioactivity, preparation, or cultural practice. It uses "natural-product chemical information" to mean public structures, scaffolds, synthetic routes, and pharmacological profiles of compounds such as curcumin, harmine, indirubin, berberine, and P57. It uses "biomedical target information" to mean protein structures, pathways, and disease mechanisms. And it uses "AI-generated analogue information" to mean the novel scaffolds or optimized leads produced by computational tools. The thesis is not that every example in this Article is a pure DSI case. DSI is the doctrinal entry point; informational provenance is the regulatory object: the set of provenance-bearing inputs from genetic resources and traditional knowledge that contemporary discovery systems can translate into value without attribution.

This Article makes four contributions. First, it reconceives the Nagoya Protocol, the GRATK Treaty, and the Cali Fund as a single regulatory architecture whose common premise --- that the trigger for benefit-sharing, disclosure, or payment is an administratively visible access, patent, or sectoral-use event --- is rendered substantively incomplete by AI-mediated synthesis from open and provenance-bearing informational inputs.[^15] Second, it situates the architectural diagnosis within the theoretical literature on information, property, and the choice between property rules and liability rules. The proposal advanced in Part VI is, in the Calabresi-Melamed taxonomy, a Rule 2 architecture: an entitlement in traditional-knowledge-holding communities, protected by a liability rule that permits derivative use against payment of a registry-mediated levy, with the entitlement understood not as property in genetic information (which the Supreme Court's Myriad doctrine forecloses)[^16] but as a regulatory obligation arising from documented provenance-bearing use. The theoretical framing is necessary because the proposal must navigate two serious critiques: the formal property-rules school's preference for clean entitlements (Smith, Merrill) and the decolonial school's "romance-of-the-public-domain" objection that treating TK as commons legitimizes its extraction by powerful firms (Chander, Sunder).[^17] Third, through counterfactual reconstructions of three canonical cases --- Curcuma longa (India's Council of Scientific and Industrial Research, the University of Mississippi Medical Center's wound-healing patent); Banisteriopsis caapi (Amazonian indigenous peoples, Loren Miller's U.S. Plant Patent No. PP5,751); and Hoodia gordonii (the San people, CSIR, Pfizer, Phytopharm, Unilever) --- the Article demonstrates that the obsolescence is empirically plausible, while distinguishing complete DSI/pathway bypass from mixed chemical-information and partial-DSI bypass. Fourth, drawing on the collective rights management apparatus of ASCAP and BMI in the music industry --- and the transfer-of-technology-to-innovation-policy pivot identified by Mor Bakhoum and Beatriz Conde Gallego in the post-TRIPS competition-law literature[^18] --- the Article proposes an informational-provenance benefit-sharing regime modeled on a Performance Rights Organization, with five components (Registry, Trigger, Distribution Algorithm, Audit Chain, Dispute Resolution), explicit attention to the antitrust risks of collective licensing, and a falsifiable empirical success-condition tied to the Cali Fund's second-year contribution data.

The Article proceeds in eight parts. Part II reconceives the existing architecture and locates the common trigger, including a comparative-international subsection that surveys implementation experience in the European Union, India, Brazil, South Africa, and the United States, and a documentary subsection (II.G) that separates four evidentiary propositions: the AI-mediated drug-discovery pipeline has reached Phase IIa clinical proof-of-concept through Insilico Medicine's Rentosertib; AI tools are being applied to traditional-medicine-derived natural products in the academic literature; DSI-specific bypass is technically plausible in some pathway-reconstruction settings; and commercial firm use of TK-derived DSI remains publicly opaque under present reporting rules. Part III sets out the theoretical framework --- the property-versus-liability-rule architecture, the second-enclosure-movement scholarship, and the decolonial critique. Part IV runs the three counterfactual reconstructions, presented in increasing order of empirical caveat (Curcuma longa first, Banisteriopsis caapi second, Hoodia gordonii third). Part V treats antitrust as a supporting frame: the DSI-to-AI-to-molecule pipeline creates platform-layer concentration concerns that help justify an attribution-based levy but do not displace the Article's ABS trigger argument. Part VI sets out the Performance Rights Organization proposal, including its five-component execution architecture, its funding pathway, and the instructive precedent of the Department of Justice's eight-decade consent-decree supervision of ASCAP and BMI. Part VII confronts eleven counterarguments, including the inalienability critique drawn from Bavikatte's biocultural-rights framework, the regime-complexity critique drawn from Raustiala and Victor, the antitrust-irony critique that PROs are themselves perpetual antitrust defendants, and the romance-of-the-public-domain critique drawn from Chander and Sunder. Part VIII concludes by stating the falsifiable empirical condition under which the Article's reform argument becomes urgent rather than merely available.

A note on positioning. The trigger problem this Article diagnoses has, more than any other scholar, been mapped by Margo A. Bagley, who served for seven years as Friend of the Chair of the WIPO Intergovernmental Committee on Intellectual Property and Genetic Resources, Traditional Knowledge and Folklore, and as expert advisor to Mozambique and the African Union throughout the GRATK negotiations. Her 2015 monograph Digital DNA, written for the Wilson Center's Synthetic Biology Project, raised the warning a decade before the Cali Fund's first months supplied a striking empirical signal; the present Article's architectural critique is built on the foundation she laid.[^19] Her 2025 GRUR International essays --- Bio-Based Innovation and the Global Patent System and The WIPO 2024 IP Treaties: The Triumph of Inclusivity and Strategic Framing --- read the GRATK Treaty's adoption as a substantial achievement against the historical baseline of WIPO inaction, while acknowledging the treaty's "specific properties" nexus and its silence on DSI as work the Treaty's four-year review will need to undertake.[^20] This Article accepts Bagley's diagnosis of the trigger problem and her assessment that the GRATK Treaty's text leaves it unaddressed; it departs by proposing a structural alternative she has not advanced: a benefit-sharing mechanism modeled on collective rights management rather than on patent disclosure alone. A targeted search of Westlaw, SSRN, major intellectual-property and environmental-law databases, Patent Trial and Appeal Board decisions, USPTO guidance, and federal case law conducted on May 5, 2026 did not identify a published article squarely addressing the tripartite interaction on which this Article turns: the absence of a freestanding AI-use disclosure obligation in U.S. patent practice, the materiality-limited duty of disclosure under 37 C.F.R. § 1.56, and the international ABS architecture's failure to engage AI-mediated synthesis from open DSI and related provenance-bearing inputs.[^21] A Westlaw AI Deep Research memorandum was used only as a supplementary cross-check, not as the sole basis for the novelty claim.[^22]

## II. The Architecture We Have

### A. The Bonn-to-Nagoya Genealogy: The Origins of the Physical-Access Trigger

![](./media/image1.png)

*FIGURE 1. Regulatory Architecture vs. Technology Timeline (1992--2026). The regulatory track (top) lags the technological substrate (bottom) it is built to govern. By the period zoomed at right (2023--2026), the gap has fully opened: the GRATK Treaty (May 2024) and the Cali Fund (decision in Nov. 2024; formally launched at resumed COP16 in Feb. 2025) entered the world contemporaneously with AlphaFold-3 (May 2024), the Recursion--Exscientia merger (November 2024), and the Nature Medicine publication of Insilico Medicine's Rentosertib Phase IIa results (June 2025).*

The conceptual lineage of the present trigger architecture begins with the 1992 Convention on Biological Diversity, whose Article 15 affirms the sovereign right of states over their genetic resources and conditions access on prior informed consent and mutually agreed terms.[^23] The CBD's framework was substantively elaborated in the 2002 Bonn Guidelines on Access to Genetic Resources and Fair and Equitable Sharing of the Benefits Arising out of Their Utilization, a non-binding instrument adopted by the sixth Conference of the Parties.[^24] The Bonn Guidelines structured the access-and-benefit-sharing transaction around four elements that have, with refinements, persisted across every subsequent layer of the architecture: prior informed consent ("PIC") of the source country; mutually agreed terms ("MAT") between provider and user; a permit-and-certificate documentation system; and benefit-sharing arrangements both monetary and non-monetary. The Guidelines were explicit that "access" referred to physical access to genetic resources --- collected specimens, herbarium material, biota in situ --- and the entire architecture flowed from that foundational definition.[^25]

The Bonn Guidelines proved insufficient. Provider-country governments --- particularly Brazil, India, the African Group, and the Like-Minded Megadiverse Countries --- argued that voluntary guidelines without enforcement teeth produced compliance only at the margins, with most "utilization" of genetic resources continuing to occur without traceable benefit-sharing.[^26] The negotiations that produced the Nagoya Protocol, opened for signature in 2010 and entering into force in October 2014, were animated by the demand for a binding regime with traceable PIC/MAT documentation.[^27] What the negotiations did not, and could not, contemplate was the transition from physical-specimen-based research to sequence-data-based research that was already accelerating as the negotiations concluded. The Sanger-sequencing-to-next-generation-sequencing transition that began in the mid-2000s was complete by the time Nagoya entered into force; whole-genome sequencing of medicinal plant species was routinizing; and the deposition of sequence data into open repositories was becoming the publication-norm in molecular biology.[^28] The architecture Nagoya codified --- physical access, PIC, MAT --- was, in 2014, already the architecture for the world Nagoya was negotiated to address, not the world it was entering into force in.

The genealogical observation matters for what follows. The trigger architecture this Article calls obsolete is not obsolete because of negligence or insufficient political will at the time of its adoption; it is obsolete because the underlying technological substrate of biotechnology research has shifted out from under it during the period of its operationalization. The instruments and regional frameworks that have built on the CBD/Nagoya access-and-benefit-sharing model since 2014[^29] --- including the Pacific Regional Strategy on Genetic Resources and Traditional Knowledge (2017),[^30] the African Union Practical Guidelines for the Implementation of the African Model Law (2015),[^31] and most recently the WIPO GRATK Treaty (2024) and the Cali Fund (2024--25) --- have inherited the original access-centered premise and elaborated its administrative apparatus, without fully revisiting the trigger itself. The single-system reading developed below is therefore not an aggressive interpretive move but a recognition of what the architecture's own genealogy makes plain.

### B. Nagoya as the Prototype: Physical Access, Prior Informed Consent, Mutually Agreed Terms

The Nagoya Protocol, adopted October 29, 2010, and entered into force October 12, 2014, supplements Article 15 of the 1992 Convention on Biological Diversity by requiring that access to genetic resources for utilization be subject to the prior informed consent of the country providing the resource and to mutually agreed terms between the provider and the user.[^32] Article 5 establishes the corresponding obligation: benefits arising from the utilization of genetic resources, and from subsequent applications and commercialization, shall be shared in a fair and equitable way with the provider country and, where applicable, with the indigenous and local communities holding traditional knowledge associated with those resources.[^33] Article 6 requires parties to establish domestic legislation requiring PIC. Article 7 extends the same architecture to traditional knowledge associated with genetic resources, conditioning access on PIC of the indigenous or local community holding that knowledge. Article 12 specifies the role of community protocols and customary law.[^34]

The structural feature that matters for present purposes is the trigger. The Nagoya obligation arises at the moment of *access* to a *physical genetic resource*, and the entire downstream architecture of benefit-sharing flows from that access event. The obligation does not arise from possession of sequence data alone, from publication of sequence data into a public repository, or from derivative use of sequence data divorced from any prior access transaction. The drafters in 2010 understood the limits of this conception --- Article 10 invited consideration of a "global multilateral benefit-sharing mechanism" for situations in which it is not possible to identify the specific provider of a resource --- but the Article 10 mechanism was deferred for future negotiation, and the operative architecture remained physical-access-triggered.[^35]

The disconnect this creates is straightforward. Genetic information exists in physical form in living tissue and in informational form in sequence databases. The same nucleotide sequence is equally present in both. The Nagoya Protocol regulates only the first. A research organization that wishes to study the biosynthetic genes of *Hoodia gordonii* by sequencing fresh plant material collected in the Northern Cape of South Africa is squarely within Nagoya's scope. The same organization, accessing the same sequence already deposited in GenBank by another researcher who collected the material a decade earlier, is not. Nor is the organization that uses AlphaFold-3 to model the three-dimensional structure of a *Hoodia*-derived enzyme, generates a synthetic analogue using Insilico Medicine's Chemistry42 platform, and produces the resulting compound through heterologous expression in *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*.

### C. The 2024 GRATK Treaty: A Disclosure Overlay, Not a Trigger Replacement

The WIPO Treaty on Intellectual Property, Genetic Resources and Associated Traditional Knowledge, adopted by consensus at the WIPO Diplomatic Conference in Geneva on May 24, 2024, is the first WIPO treaty to address the interface between intellectual property and genetic resources, and the first to include provisions specifically directed at indigenous peoples and local communities.[^36] The treaty's central operative provision is Article 3, which requires that, where the claimed invention in a patent application is "based on" genetic resources or associated traditional knowledge, the applicant disclose the country of origin or source of the resources or knowledge.[^37] Article 5 addresses sanctions and remedies: parties are required to provide appropriate, effective, and proportionate measures to address non-compliance, but no party shall revoke, invalidate, or render unenforceable a patent solely on the basis of failure to comply with the disclosure requirement; post-grant sanctions or remedies may be provided where fraudulent intent is shown.[^38] Article 8 commits the parties to a review of the treaty's operation, including consideration of "extension of the disclosure requirement to other areas of intellectual property and to derivatives, and other issues arising from new and emerging technologies," four years after entry into force.[^39]

As of May 6, 2026 --- approximately twenty-four months after adoption and approximately twelve months after the close of the treaty's signature window --- three jurisdictions (Malawi, Uganda, and Albania) had deposited instruments of ratification or accession. The treaty requires fifteen ratifications or accessions for entry into force. It is therefore not in force, and no party is presently bound to implement Article 3.[^40]

Two features of the GRATK Treaty matter for the architectural critique. The first is the "based on" trigger. The treaty defines "based on" through a two-part test: the genetic resource or associated traditional knowledge must have been necessary for the claimed invention, and the claimed invention must depend on the specific properties of the resource or knowledge.[^41] The trigger is, in other words, a test applied at the moment of patent prosecution. It does not reach uses of genetic resources or traditional knowledge that do not result in a patent application --- including, critically, defensive trade-secret strategies, non-patentable methods, and uses that produce functional analogues whose claim language is drafted to obscure the genetic-resource derivation. Bagley has noted, in her assessment of the negotiation outcome, that the "specific properties" nexus was the compromise that made the treaty negotiable; it is also the compromise that the AI-mediated synthesis pipeline most readily exploits.[^42]

The litigation problem can be made concrete. Claim A --- "A method of promoting wound healing by administering turmeric powder" --- is the old biopiracy form: the traditional use appears on the face of the claim, prior-art documentation can anticipate it, and a GRATK-style disclosure obligation would be straightforward. Claim B --- "A compound of Formula I for modulating NF-κB-mediated inflammatory response, wherein R1, R2, and R3 are substituted aryl groups" --- is the AI-era form. It may originate in the same Curcuma/TK-derived bioactivity insight, but chemical novelty and target-language drafting make the "based on" and "specific properties" nexus contestable. The treaty's doctrinal problem is not that Article 3 is incoherent; it is that the cases most likely to matter commercially are precisely the cases in which provenance has been translated into a claim format that makes Article 3 harder to apply.

The second feature is the treaty's silence on Digital Sequence Information. The text of the treaty makes no operative reference to DSI. The negotiating record is candid about why: developing-country and indigenous-organization positions favored DSI inclusion, developed-country and biotechnology-industry positions opposed it, and the compromise text excluded DSI in exchange for the Article 8 review commitment.[^43] The Article 8 review is a placeholder, not a binding commitment to reach a DSI rule by a particular date; it is, moreover, conditioned on entry into force, which is itself contingent on twelve further ratifications or accessions. The realistic scenario is that the GRATK Treaty's operation, when it begins, will operate in parallel with --- but separate from --- whatever DSI rule the CBD process produces under its own track.

### D. The Cali Fund: Operationalizing the Multilateral Mechanism

The CBD's parallel DSI track culminated, after the in-principle establishment of a multilateral mechanism in COP15 Decision 15/9 (Montreal, December 2022), in COP16 Decision 16/2 (Cali, October--November 2024), which operationalized the Cali Fund. The Fund's contribution architecture defines the trigger by a combined size-and-sector test: users in sectors that directly or indirectly benefit from DSI on genetic resources and meet at least two of three thresholds (total assets of \$20 million or more; sales of \$50 million or more; or profits of \$5 million or more, averaged over the preceding three years) are expected to contribute either 0.1 percent of revenue or 1 percent of profit, as indicative rates. The indicative sector list includes pharmaceuticals, nutraceuticals, cosmetics, animal and plant breeding, biotechnology, sequencing-related laboratory equipment, and information, scientific, and technical services related to DSI, including artificial intelligence; at least half of funding should support the self-identified needs of indigenous peoples and local communities, where appropriate and subject to national circumstances and legislation.[^44] The Fund is hosted by the Multi-Partner Trust Fund Office in partnership with the United Nations Development Programme and the United Nations Environment Programme; the Cali Fund Secretariat is hosted by the CBD Secretariat itself.[^45]

The contribution structure is voluntary and indicative in the formal sense: Decision 16/2 uses "should contribute" language, identifies 1 percent of profits or 0.1 percent of revenue as indicative rates, and leaves the Conference of the Parties to establish thresholds and contribution rates at COP17.[^46] But the structure is plainly designed to function through reputational and policy pressure on firms above the thresholds --- the implicit expectation being that public companies in the named sectors will face investor, regulator, and civil-society pressure to demonstrate participation. The important point for this Article is not that AI services are outside the Fund. They are expressly named. The point is that the Fund's monetary trigger is firm-size-and-sector-based rather than attribution-based.

That expectation has not, in the Fund's first year, materialized in the public record. The Fund launched on February 25, 2025. It received no publicly reported contributions through most of its first nine months. On November 19, 2025, the UK startup TierraViva AI made the first contribution, at one thousand U.S. dollars. The CEO of the contributing firm, in a statement to Carbon Brief, characterized the contribution as an "ice-breaker" intended to "encourage others who may be hesitating."[^47] In the months since, no publicly identifiable firm clearly meeting the indicative asset, sales, or profit thresholds has been publicly reported as having contributed.

### E. Doctrinal Anchors: Bowman, Myriad, Moore

Three United States Supreme Court and California Supreme Court decisions provide doctrinal anchors that illuminate, by analogy and contrast, the architectural critique this Article advances. *Bowman v. Monsanto Co.*, 569 U.S. 278 (2013), held that the patent exhaustion doctrine does not permit a farmer to reproduce patented seeds by planting and harvesting saved crop seeds without the patent holder's permission; the Court reasoned that planting seed is an unauthorized "making" of the patented product even if the original sale exhausted the patentee's rights as to the specific seed sold.[^48] *Bowman*'s holding distinguishes between the *use* of a patented item (exhausted by first sale) and the *reproduction* of new copies of the patented invention from that item (not exhausted). The analogy to DSI is precise: a researcher who lawfully accesses a deposited sequence in GenBank may "use" it for the purpose of her own research, but the question of whether subsequent commercial deployment of synthesized analogues constitutes a "making" outside the scope of any consent the original depositor or source community provided remains entirely unaddressed by either patent law or the ABS architecture.

*Association for Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics, Inc.*, 569 U.S. 576 (2013), held that naturally occurring isolated DNA segments are not patent-eligible under 35 U.S.C. § 101, while complementary DNA (cDNA) is patent-eligible because it does not exist in nature.[^49] The holding's relevance to the present architectural critique is structural rather than doctrinal: *Myriad* establishes that, in the eyes of U.S. patent law, the *informational content* of a natural DNA sequence --- the sequence itself, considered as a string of information --- is not the kind of thing in which exclusive property rights can be created. Sherkow, Cook-Deegan, and Greely's recent ten-year retrospective demonstrates that the ruling's commercial impact has been narrower than first anticipated, as patenting moved toward methods of use, diagnostics, and synthetic constructs that remain patent-eligible.[^50] What *Myriad* does establish, however, is the conceptual point on which this Article's trigger argument rests: information as such is not property as such, and any architecture that purports to channel benefits from the informational use of genetic data must reach beyond the property-rights framework on which Nagoya and the GRATK Treaty rely.

*Moore v. Regents of the University of California*, 51 Cal. 3d 120, 793 P.2d 479, 271 Cal. Rptr. 146 (1990), held that a patient whose excised cells were developed into a patented and commercially valuable cell line had no cause of action in conversion against the researchers and university that had used the cells, while holding that the treating physician owed a fiduciary duty to disclose his financial interest in the materials being harvested.[^51] *Moore* is the doctrinal anchor for what one might call the *information-versus-thing* asymmetry in U.S. property law: the donor of biological material retains no property right in derivative information or commercial products, but the relationship between donor and researcher imposes disclosure obligations. The architectural analog is again precise: the existing ABS architecture analogizes traditional-knowledge-holding communities to *Moore*-style donors with no ongoing property interest, when the structural question is whether the analog should instead be to ASCAP-style attribution-holders with a continuing claim to compensation for derivative use, as Part VI will argue.

### F. Comparative Implementation: India, Brazil, the European Union, South Africa, and the United States

The architectural critique gains traction from observation that the failure mode this Article describes is not idiosyncratic to the multilateral instruments examined above but is replicated, with variation, in the leading national-level implementations of ABS regimes over the past decade.

India. India's Biological Diversity Act of 2002, supplemented by the Biological Diversity (Amendment) Act, 2023, establishes a centralized National Biodiversity Authority empowered to approve access to genetic resources and traditional knowledge.[^52] The 2023 Amendment was negotiated against a backdrop of complaint from the Indian biotechnology industry that the regulatory burden of the original 2002 Act discouraged research; the Amendment narrowed the access-permission requirement for codified traditional knowledge and exempted certain research activities from the prior approval requirement, while strengthening the framework for traditional knowledge held by indigenous and local communities.[^53] India's Traditional Knowledge Digital Library, established in 2001 in the wake of the *Curcuma longa* patent challenge described in Part IV.B, has integrated with the European Patent Office, the United Kingdom Intellectual Property Office, and the United States Patent and Trademark Office, and is the most operationally mature defensive-documentation system anywhere in the world.[^54] The TKDL's success against patents that *facially* claim traditional knowledge is well established. Its structural inadequacy against the AI-mediated synthesis pipeline this Article documents is the empirical predicate for the *Curcuma longa* counterfactual in Part IV.B: the TKDL captures the patent application; it does not capture the firm that uses TKDL itself as a training corpus to design chemically novel curcuminoid analogues that no patent examiner would identify as TK-derived.

Brazil. Brazil's Law No. 13,123 of 2015 (the Brazilian Biodiversity Law) replaced the more restrictive provisional measure regime that had governed access to Brazil's genetic patrimony since 2001, with the operational support of the SisGen registration system administered by the Ministry of the Environment.[^55] Brazil is the most important partial counterexample to a simple physical-access-trigger thesis. Its Biodiversity Law and SisGen architecture are more information-sensitive than classic Nagoya implementation: "genetic heritage" is defined as genetic-origin information, associated traditional knowledge may be recognized through publications, registries, databases, or cultural inventories, and registration obligations attach at downstream checkpoints including IP applications, commercialization, publication, and product notification. The remaining gap is therefore not that DSI or genetic-origin information is categorically outside SisGen. The gap is that monitoring foreign users, pre-existing public-database sequences, AI-mediated derivative use, and attribution to specific communities remains difficult. Brazil strengthens the Article's claim when read correctly: even an information-sensitive ABS regime still struggles with extraterritorial enforcement and workflow-level attribution.

The European Union. Regulation (EU) No 511/2014 implements Nagoya within the EU and imposes due-diligence obligations on users of genetic resources within the Union's territory.[^56] The Regulation has been criticized in the European scholarly literature for being narrow in scope (covering only research and product-development utilization, not subsequent commercialization), under-enforced at the Member State level, and insufficient to address DSI.[^57] European Commission implementation materials and Member State reporting have focused on due-diligence, checkpoint, and enforcement capacity under Regulation 511/2014, but have not resolved the DSI question.[^58] For present purposes, the safer institutional point is that Decision 16/2 as adopted does not itself impose a binding firm-level legal obligation enforceable through EU law; any such obligation would require EU or Member State implementation. The EU example therefore illustrates a familiar gap between international soft-law contribution architecture and domestic enforceability, rather than proving any particular negotiating posture.

South Africa. South Africa's National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Act of 2004 and its 2008 Bioprospecting, Access and Benefit-Sharing Regulations were drafted in the immediate aftermath of the *Hoodia* case described in Part IV.D, and represent an attempt to learn from the Hoodia experience by requiring formal benefit-sharing agreements with traditional knowledge holders prior to bioprospecting permits.[^59] The post-Hoodia regulatory framework has produced a small number of additional benefit-sharing agreements (most notably the rooibos-related agreement of 2019);[^60] it has not, on the available record, produced sustained commercial flows to traditional knowledge-holding communities. The structural reason is again the trigger problem: the regulatory architecture engages firms that seek bioprospecting permits, and such firms are an increasingly small fraction of the firms whose research draws value from Southern African genetic resources.

The United States. The United States is not party to the Convention on Biological Diversity and therefore not party to the Nagoya Protocol; nor has it signed the GRATK Treaty.[^61] The U.S. position in international negotiations has consistently emphasized the importance of protecting U.S. biotechnology industry interests against perceived overreach by source-country ABS regimes, and the U.S. has historically opposed disclosure-of-origin requirements as inconsistent with the simplification of patent prosecution.[^62] The U.S. nonetheless hosts a substantial fraction of the global biotechnology industry, and U.S.-based or U.S.-linked firms --- including Recursion Pharmaceuticals, Generate Biomedicines, Chai Discovery, Insilico Medicine's U.S. operations, and Alphabet-linked Isomorphic Labs --- operate or finance parts of the AI-mediated synthesis pipeline at the platform layer this Article identifies as the principal locus of value capture. The architectural critique therefore lands particularly hard on the U.S. case: U.S.-market firms benefit from activity the existing architecture struggles to reach, and the U.S. is simultaneously the jurisdiction least obligated to implement any of the architecture's trigger mechanisms.

The cross-jurisdictional pattern is, in summary, that most major ABS implementations --- multilateral, regional, and national --- remain built primarily around physical-access, transaction-based, or checkpoint-based triggers. Even newer DSI provisions remain implementation-dependent and therefore vulnerable to activity that occurs upstream inside AI-mediated discovery pipelines. The pattern is not an accident of negotiation in any particular jurisdiction; it is an inheritance of the conceptual architecture established by the Bonn Guidelines and codified by Nagoya, an architecture whose technological substrate has shifted out from under it. The most recent national-level developments --- India's Biological Diversity Rules, 2024, issued on October 22, 2024 and effective upon expiry of sixty days from publication in the Official Gazette, and the Biological Diversity (Access to Biological Resources and Knowledge Associated thereto and Fair and Equitable Sharing of Benefits) Regulations, 2025, which expressly include Digital Sequence Information and set turnover-based benefit-sharing rates for commercial utilization --- are illustrative.[^63] On their face, the 2024--2025 Indian instruments represent the most operationally sophisticated national-level attempt to date to extend the ABS architecture to DSI. In practice, as Indian commentary has begun to observe, the National Biodiversity Authority "has not formally initiated such measures for comprehensive biopiracy tracking,"[^64] and the implementation gap that has characterized the Indian regulatory architecture since the 2002 Biological Diversity Act may persist unless monitoring and enforcement capacity catches up with the new text.

### G. Evidence of Capability, TK Application, and Attribution Risk

The architectural critique advanced in this Article rests on a claim about technological capability --- that contemporary discovery workflows can translate open DSI, traditional-use documentation, natural-product chemistry, and AI-enabled structure/design tools into candidate molecules before any existing ABS trigger becomes visible. The capability claim is often treated in the DSI literature as a future risk rather than as a documented present vulnerability.[^65] This subsection separates four evidentiary propositions that should not be collapsed. First, the AI-driven drug-discovery pipeline has reached clinical proof-of-concept. Second, AI is being applied in the academic literature to traditional-medicine-derived natural products and phytochemicals. Third, DSI-specific bypass is technically plausible in some cases involving biosynthetic pathway reconstruction, sequence-to-structure inference, or heterologous expression. Fourth, commercial firm use of TK-derived DSI or TK-derived natural-product scaffolds remains publicly opaque because the relevant regulatory regimes impose no freestanding reporting obligation for that use.

The first peer-reviewed Phase IIa proof-of-concept for an AI-discovered drug. On June 3, 2025, Nature Medicine published the Phase IIa trial results for Rentosertib (development designation ISM001-055), a first-in-class TNIK kinase inhibitor for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis discovered and designed using Insilico Medicine's Pharma.AI generative platform.[^66] The GENESIS-IPF trial enrolled seventy-one patients across twenty-one sites in China, with the 60-mg-once-daily arm producing a mean +98.4 mL improvement in forced vital capacity against a -20.3 mL decline in the placebo arm.[^67] The publication was framed by Insilico, by Nature Medicine, and by the trade press as the first clinical proof-of-concept for an AI-discovered drug.[^68] The technical pipeline that produced Rentosertib --- generative AI target identification (TNIK), generative-chemistry molecular design, structure prediction, and accelerated optimization toward a clinical-grade candidate within timeframes shorter than the traditional drug-discovery cycle --- is the same pipeline this Article identifies as the principal threat vector to the existing ABS architecture. Rentosertib itself is not a TK-derived compound; the TNIK target was identified through standard pharmacological pathways. But the proof-of-concept for the pipeline is now empirical, peer-reviewed, and clinical, not theoretical.

The active-research integration of generative AI with ethnopharmacology. A 2025 review in Pharmacological Research --- *Reimagining Ethnopharmacology with Generative AI: Towards Inclusive, Ethical, and Data-Driven Traditional Medicine* --- synthesizes the growing body of work integrating generative AI tools with traditional-medicine knowledge bases and addresses the ethical, legal, and cultural considerations the integration raises.[^69] A 2025 synthesis in Intelligence-Based Medicine on AI in natural-product drug discovery quantifies the broader trajectory: AI mining of ethnobotanical knowledge, phytochemical libraries, and genomic information has the potential, on the most aggressive published estimates, to reduce drug-discovery timelines by up to seventy percent.[^70] Tools specifically targeting natural-product discovery from public sequence and chemistry data --- including DeepRiPP for ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides and DeepBGC or e-DeepBGC for biosynthetic gene cluster prediction --- have moved from research prototypes toward operational use.[^71] The integration the architectural critique anticipates is not a forecast; it is a documented program of contemporary research.

Documented application to traditional-medicine-derived compounds. The integration just described has, in the past eighteen months, produced peer-reviewed published instances of AI-assisted natural-product optimization operating on specific traditional-medicine-derived compounds. These instances do not all prove a complete open-DSI-to-product pipeline. They prove the adjacent proposition that AI tools can translate TK-associated natural-product starting points into optimized analogues whose provenance may disappear in chemically novel claims. Three illustrative instances bear examination.

First, a 2025 Scientific Reports paper by Nouman Ali, Roman Akbar, and colleagues documents an AI-based natural-inhibitor design pipeline targeting the RPS20 ribosomal protein for colorectal cancer treatment.[^72] The pipeline begins with indirubin, a bis-indole alkaloid derived from Indigofera tinctoria and Isatis indigotica, with a documented pharmacological history of more than a thousand years in Chinese traditional medicine --- where it is a principal active component of the formulation Qing Dai, used historically and in modern research for hematologic and inflammatory indications.[^73] The pipeline obtains the RPS20 protein structure from AlphaFold; uses the WADDAICA AI design platform to generate optimized analogues; validates the lead through molecular docking, molecular dynamics simulation, and MMGBSA free-energy calculation; and reports a final AI-optimized lead with a best docking score reported as -6.7 by the authors, improved ADMET predictions, and a one-class reduction in predicted toxicity (Class 5 versus the parent's Class 4).[^74]

*Second*, a 2026 Computational Biology and Chemistry paper documents an AI fragment-based optimization pipeline targeting the aryl hydrocarbon receptor for dementia therapy.[^75] The pipeline begins with phytochemicals isolated from saffron (*Crocus sativus*, traditional Persian, Indian Ayurvedic, and Chinese medicinal usage spanning more than three thousand years for cognitive and mood indications) and chamomile (*Matricaria chamomilla*, with documented Egyptian, Greco-Roman, and European traditional usage); applies AI fragment-based design to optimize lead phytochemicals --- safranal and bisabolone oxide A from saffron, bisabolol and apigenin from chamomile --- for AhR inhibitor activity; conducts protein network mapping, density functional theory calculations, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics simulations to determine binding affinity and interaction stability; and produces a portfolio of optimized analogues whose ADMET profiles support further development.[^76]

*Third*, the recent traditional-Chinese-medicine deep-learning literature documents a model integrating molecular graph representation with Extended Connectivity Fingerprinting that successfully predicted four highly active berberine derivatives for anti-*Helicobacter pylori* activity, with parallel ensemble-machine-learning identification of protoberberine alkaloids (including coptisine and berberine itself) as RORγt inhibitors validated through surface plasmon resonance binding.[^77] Berberine is a core pharmacological agent of Chinese traditional medicine, derived from *Coptis chinensis* (Huang Lian, 黄连) and other species with millennia of documented therapeutic use across Chinese, Indian Ayurvedic, and Middle Eastern traditional medical systems.[^78]

The three instances together establish a narrower but important pattern. The pipeline is not hypothetical. The targets are not generic. The compounds have specific traditional-medicine provenance, named source organisms, and documented histories of use spanning, in the case of indirubin and berberine, more than a thousand years. The published reports show, with technical specificity, AlphaFold-class structural prediction, AI-assisted or generative analogue design, and computational validation operating in sequence to produce novel compounds whose structural distinctness from the parent traditional-medicine compound may support patentability if novelty and non-obviousness are otherwise shown, and may make the GRATK Treaty's "specific properties" nexus contestable in practice. What the reports do not always show is equally important: they do not necessarily depend on source-organism genetic sequence data, and they do not necessarily establish a commercial open-DSI pipeline. Their function in the Article is therefore not to prove that every TK-derived natural-product optimization is DSI-mediated. Their function is to show that provenance-bearing informational inputs --- TK documentation, natural-product structures, target information, and sometimes DSI --- can be converted into novel molecules without engaging any layer of the existing ABS architecture. None of the three publications, on its face, identifies an ABS, consultation, or benefit-sharing pathway tied to the traditional-medicine knowledge base from which the lead compounds were selected.

The absence-as-evidence corollary. A skeptical reader may observe that the documentary record above does not include named instances of commercial firms running the pipeline on TK-derived compounds to commercial product. The observation is correct, and the Article does not need to deny it. Two analytical claims must be distinguished. The first is that the broader AI drug-discovery pipeline operates technically: Rentosertib establishes clinical proof-of-concept for AI-discovered drug development, and the three academic examples establish AI-assisted optimization of traditional-medicine-derived compounds. The second is that commercial firms are presently operating the same pipeline on TK-derived targets or DSI-linked TK inputs: on this, the documentary record is largely silent. That silence should not be converted into affirmative proof of misconduct. It should be read as an institutional opacity problem. Commercial firms are generally not required, by Nagoya, by GRATK, by the Cali Fund, or by domestic patent disclosure doctrine, to identify ethnobotanical inspiration, TK-derived scaffolds, or AI use in discovery workflows unless those facts become material under an independent legal rule. The Article's argument therefore does not depend on proving a presently identifiable commercial biopiracy case. It depends on the more modest proposition that the architecture has little purchase on such activity if it occurs.

The absence of a freestanding AI-use disclosure obligation. The United States Patent and Trademark Office's Revised Inventorship Guidance for AI-Assisted Inventions, published November 28, 2025, rescinds the February 2024 guidance and confirms that the same inventorship standard applies whether or not AI systems were used: AI systems are tools, not inventors, and patentability turns on whether one or more natural persons conceived the claimed invention.[^79] Thaler itself expressly reserved the question of AI-assisted invention, noting that the Federal Circuit was "not confronted today with the question of whether inventions made by human beings with the assistance of AI are eligible for patent protection."[^80] The natural-person conception standard the Federal Circuit's pre-Thaler doctrine establishes --- most recently reaffirmed in Regents of the University of California v. Broad Institute, Inc., 136 F.4th 1367 (Fed. Cir. 2025), in Blue Gentian, LLC v. Tristar Products, Inc., 70 F.4th 1351 (Fed. Cir. 2023), and in Kim v. Kettell, 694 F. Supp. 3d 1379 (D. Colo. 2023) --- supplies the operative test in the AI-assisted context.[^81] As construed under 37 C.F.R. § 1.56, there is no freestanding obligation to disclose AI use merely because AI assisted the inventive process. Disclosure becomes relevant only if AI use bears on ordinary patentability, correct inventorship, or materiality under existing doctrine.[^82] The Federal Circuit's en banc decision in Therasense, Inc. v. Becton, Dickinson & Co., 649 F.3d 1276 (Fed. Cir. 2011), tightened the inequitable-conduct doctrine to require both but-for materiality and specific intent to deceive --- a framework recently applied or reaffirmed in Entangled Media, LLC v. Dropbox Inc., 795 F. Supp. 3d 1191 (N.D. Cal. 2025), Buergofol GmbH v. Omega Liner Co., 2024 WL 3541248 (D.S.D. July 25, 2024), and Luv n' Care, Ltd. v. Laurain, 98 F.4th 1081 (Fed. Cir. 2024), and reinforced in the inventorship-materiality context by Siemens Gamesa Renewable Energy A/S v. General Electric Co., 617 F. Supp. 3d 55 (D. Mass. 2022).[^83] The post-AlphaFold-3 commentary in the European IP press summarizes the operational consequence: pharmaceutical companies are not required to disclose to regulatory authorities or in patent filings that AlphaFold-3 was used in the initial screen.[^84] The default architecture for AI-assisted drug discovery is therefore not an express permission to hide AI use; it is the absence of a process-transparency duty unless existing patentability, inventorship, or materiality doctrine independently makes the fact relevant.

The doctrinal vacuum is not the result of judicial oversight; it is the product of judicial non-engagement. A targeted survey of federal cases, Patent Trial and Appeal Board decisions, USPTO examination guidance, and secondary literature through May 2026 found no Federal Circuit opinion applying Therasense to undisclosed AI involvement in conception; no precedential PTAB decision identifying AI-use disclosure as a duty under § 1.56; no MPEP revision requiring applicants to identify AI tools used in conceiving a claimed invention; and no federal case deciding § 101 eligibility specifically on the ground that AI mediated the structural variation of a natural-product starting point.[^85] The application of Myriad and Diamond v. Chakrabarty, 447 U.S. 303, 310 (1980), to AI-designed compounds derived from natural products is therefore, as of the date of this Article, a question of first impression. The Federal Circuit's 2026 REGENXBIO decision is best read more narrowly: it reaffirmed, for genetically engineered host cells, a composition-focused Chakrabarty inquiry into whether the claimed composition as a whole is naturally occurring. By inference, any future § 101 challenge to AI-designed natural-product analogues would likely focus on the resulting compound's characteristics rather than the tool used to generate it; REGENXBIO itself did not decide that AI-compound question.[^86] That doctrinal architecture is precisely what makes the attribution problem hard: chemically novel analogues can be drafted around the natural-product starting point, while the patent filing need not disclose the AI tools or the traditional-knowledge bioactivity that informed the design unless some existing doctrine independently makes those facts material.

The platform-consolidation reality. The platform layer at which the value capture this Article diagnoses occurs has consolidated rapidly through 2024 and 2025. Recursion Pharmaceuticals' November 2024 merger with Exscientia, which closed November 20, 2024 with a later reported accounting purchase price of approximately \$635.9 million, produced a combined entity with more than ten clinical and preclinical programs and substantial potential partnership economics, including milestones and royalties, under existing collaboration agreements.[^87] Generate Biomedicines entered a \$1 billion partnership with Novartis in 2024.[^88] Isomorphic Labs, the Alphabet subsidiary holding the commercial channel for AlphaFold-3, raised \$600 million in early 2025.[^89] Insilico Medicine itself reported, for fiscal year 2025, \$1.3 billion in newly signed collaboration agreements and cumulative collaboration value of \$4.6 billion.[^90] The platform layer's reported deal values, accounting purchase prices, and capital commitments exceed, by orders of magnitude, the Cali Fund's public contribution record.

Five clarifications on the scope of the architectural claim. The doctrinal record summarized above generates a set of follow-on questions that bear on the precise reach of the Article's argument. First, the argument addresses both de novo AI compound design (where generative molecular models propose novel scaffolds without a human-defined starting structure) and AI-assisted optimization of a lead compound (where a human chemist defines the scaffold and AI tools optimize structural variants). The architectural failure operates in both modalities when provenance-bearing inputs materially shape the pipeline and no existing trigger records that use. Second, the human-conception standard under the USPTO's revised 2025 guidance is indeterminate at the margin: a human who selects training data, validates AI output, or directs the screening criteria plausibly meets the standard, while a human who merely operates an end-to-end generative pipeline plausibly does not. The Article's architectural argument does not depend on resolving the indeterminacy; it depends on the observation that no court or PTAB decision has resolved it in this setting, and that the absence of a freestanding AI-use disclosure rule leaves the attribution question outside ordinary filing practice. Third, the Article's argument addresses both composition-of-matter claims (the dominant patent format for AI-designed pharmaceutical compounds) and method-of-treatment or process claims (where the AI's role in identifying a candidate may more readily map onto an abstract-idea or natural-law framing under Alice and Mayo).[^91] Fourth, the "physical-access/transaction-trigger obsolescence" claim does not require that DSI synthesis entirely eliminate the need to access a physical biological resource. The claim is the narrower one that, even where physical access remains relevant in some downstream phase of drug development, the existing ABS architectures cannot detect or capture AI-mediated synthesis or provenance-bearing input use at the point where much value has already been created. Fifth, the Article does not identify a specific issued patent for a commercial AI-designed compound derived from a TK-traceable natural-product starting point. The absence of such a public case study is a limitation of the evidentiary record; it is also consistent with the institutional opacity the Article diagnoses.

The architectural inference. Read together, these documentary clusters establish that (a) the AI-mediated drug-discovery pipeline has reached peer-reviewed Phase IIa clinical testing, not merely theoretical capability (Rentosertib); (b) the integration of generative AI with traditional-medicine knowledge bases is an active and growing program of pharmaceutical and biotechnology research, with peer-reviewed academic instances operating on specific traditional-medicine-derived compounds with documented traditional-use provenance (indirubin, saffron, chamomile, berberine); (c) some DSI-specific bypasses are technically plausible where public sequence data, biosynthetic pathway reconstruction, or heterologous expression supply the relevant input; (d) the present regulatory architecture generally lacks a freestanding duty to disclose AI use or TK-derived informational provenance in discovery workflows; and (e) the commercial scale of the platform layer is in the multi-billion-dollar range, with the consolidation pattern that accompanies platform-economy concentration. The capability the architectural critique relies on is no longer hypothetical. The attribution architecture needed to govern it remains missing.

Read together --- and the existing literature has not, until now, read them together as a single architecture --- Nagoya, GRATK, the Cali Fund, and their national-level implementations share a structural premise: the act that triggers benefit-sharing is a *transaction* (Nagoya: access agreement; GRATK: patent application) or a *threshold-crossing in a defined sector* (Cali Fund). Each instrument was designed for a world in which the value chain ran from physical access to laboratory characterization to patent prosecution to commercial deployment, in roughly that order, and in which the instrument's trigger captured a moment within that chain at which compelling disclosure or compelling payment was administratively feasible.

The pipeline this Article documents --- open DSI repository to AlphaFold-3-class structural prediction to generative molecule design to synthetic functional analogue --- does not reliably pass through any of the three triggers in a form that produces attribution. The physical-access trigger is bypassed where the workflow uses pre-deposited sequence data without new access. The patent-disclosure trigger becomes contestable where trade-secret protection, chemical novelty, and the "based on" / "specific properties" nexus obscure provenance. The sectoral-revenue trigger has not yet produced publicly recorded contributions from clearly threshold-eligible firms, and even if it did, it would not attribute value to the specific DSI, TK, source community, or provenance chain used in a particular workflow.

The architecture is not weak in any of its components considered individually. Each instrument was negotiated against the technological background of its era, and each captures, more or less faithfully, the value flow that background contemplated. The architecture is *substantively obsolete* against the technological background of 2026 --- and the cost of that obsolescence is that traditional-knowledge-holding communities, on whose accumulated bioactive insights the contemporary biotech industry continues to draw, are not being compensated for the use of their knowledge in the form in which that use now most readily occurs.

## III. Theoretical Framework: Information, Property, and the Liability-Rule Alternative

The architectural diagnosis advanced in Part II is, on its face, an empirical claim. Empirical claims about regulatory failure depend, however, on a theoretical apparatus that explains *why* certain regulatory architectures succeed in capturing certain kinds of value flow and others fail. The proposal advanced in Part VI presupposes such an apparatus --- specifically, the apparatus that the law-and-economics literature on property and liability rules has developed since *Calabresi and Melamed*'s 1972 cathedral, the apparatus that the public-domain scholarship has developed since Boyle's "second enclosure" essay, and the apparatus that the decolonial IP-theory school has developed since Chander and Sunder's *Romance of the Public Domain*. This Part sets out the theoretical framework that the empirical diagnosis and the structural proposal jointly require.

### A. Information as Property, Information as Commons, Information as Other

The first theoretical question is whether the architectural object --- DSI traceable to traditional knowledge --- can usefully be conceived as *property* at all. The answer the United States patent-eligibility doctrine supplies is "no" with respect to the informational content of natural DNA sequences: *Myriad* held that isolated naturally-occurring DNA is not patent-eligible because the information it encodes pre-exists the patent applicant's intervention.[^92] The answer the Berne Convention and TRIPS supply is "no" with respect to facts and ideas as such: copyright protects expression, not information.[^93] The answer the trade-secret architecture supplies is "yes, but only conditionally": information is property to the extent that its holder maintains it in confidence, an answer that breaks down at the moment the information enters a public repository.[^94] No major doctrinal regime treats DSI, deposited in GenBank or its analogs, as the kind of thing in which exclusive property rights subsist.

The doctrinal vacuum has historically been read by IP scholars as inviting either of two framings. The *commons* framing, associated with Boyle's account of the "second enclosure movement," treats the public deposit of genetic information into open repositories as an act of contribution to a global intellectual commons --- analogous to the eighteenth-century commons whose enclosure Boyle's first enclosure movement enacted.[^95] On this framing, DSI is a public good, deposited by researchers as a contribution to scientific progress, available to all without restriction, and protected against re-enclosure (whether by patent or by data-exclusivity arrangements) by the doctrinal apparatus the commons scholarship has developed.[^96] The *anticommons* framing, associated with Heller's account of biomedical research, recognizes that the proliferation of patent rights over upstream research inputs (gene fragments, research tools, screening assays) can produce a tragedy of the anticommons in which downstream innovation is foreclosed by transaction costs and holdout problems.[^97] Both framings, in their conventional articulations, treat the open public-repository system as a substantial good, to be defended against enclosure and to be supplemented, where necessary, by patent-pool or compulsory-licensing arrangements that route around the anticommons problem.[^98]

The conventional articulations are, however, vulnerable to a critique that the decolonial IP literature has developed with particular force, and that this Article must engage seriously. The critique is that the commons framing of DSI --- and of traditional knowledge generally --- operates as ideological cover for what is, in operational reality, the systematic appropriation of indigenous-community knowledge by powerful firms. Anupam Chander and Madhavi Sunder formulate the critique most pointedly in *The Romance of the Public Domain*: "\[T\]he romance of the commons is the belief that because a resource is open to all by force of law, it will indeed be equally exploited by all."[^99] On the Chander--Sunder analysis, classifying traditional knowledge as commons or public domain produces a distributional asymmetry: the formal openness of the commons disguises the substantive reality that some users --- well-resourced, technologically sophisticated, geographically privileged --- extract from the commons at scale, while others --- including the indigenous communities whose accumulated practice constitutes much of what the commons contains --- receive nothing from the extraction. The "romance" of the commons, in their phrase, is the romance of formal equality masking substantive inequality.[^100]

The Chander--Sunder critique applies with particular force to DSI. Genetic information is "open to all" in the formal sense in GenBank, UniProt, and the European Nucleotide Archive. The substantive distribution of who actually extracts value from that openness is starkly asymmetric: the firms operating the AI-mediated synthesis pipeline are concentrated in a small number of jurisdictions, are well-resourced, and possess the proprietary platforms (AlphaFold-3 commercial channel via Isomorphic Labs, Chai-1, Insilico's Chemistry42) that translate open sequence data into competitively patentable molecules. The indigenous communities whose accumulated bioactivity insights are embedded in the ethnobotanical context that gives the sequence its commercial value are, by definition, not among the extractors. The "openness" of GenBank to those communities is meaningful only in the trivial sense that they too could, in principle, download a sequence; it is not meaningful in the substantive sense that they could translate a downloaded sequence into a marketable functional analogue. A theoretical framework for the proposal advanced in Part VI must take this critique seriously, not as a marginal qualification but as a primary operating condition.

The conclusion this Article draws from the Chander--Sunder critique is not that DSI should be re-enclosed --- the patent-eligibility doctrine forecloses that route, and the operational success of open repositories in advancing scientific research counsels against it even where doctrine permits --- but that the question of *who benefits from open access* must be answered through a regulatory mechanism distinct from the property-rights apparatus that doctrine has foreclosed. The mechanism this Article proposes is, in the property-theory literature's vocabulary, a *liability rule* operating against the formal openness of the DSI commons, with the entitlement structured to channel value flow to the indigenous and local communities whose accumulated knowledge underwrites the substantive value of what the formal openness provides.

### B. The Calabresi--Melamed Framework and the Choice of Rule

Guido Calabresi and A. Douglas Melamed's 1972 essay *Property Rules, Liability Rules, and Inalienability: One View of the Cathedral* supplies the analytical apparatus this Article uses to position the Performance Rights Organization proposal in Part VI.[^101] The Calabresi--Melamed framework distinguishes, for each entitlement, between the question of *who holds it* and the question of *how it is protected*. Two protection regimes are jointly exhaustive of the central possibilities. A *property rule* protects the entitlement by requiring that any transfer occur through voluntary exchange between holder and prospective taker, at a price set by the holder; the legal apparatus enforces non-consensual transfers through specific performance, injunction, or criminal sanction. A *liability rule* protects the entitlement by permitting non-consensual transfer at a price set by an external authority (a court, a regulatory body, a tariff schedule), with the holder's entitlement to compensation enforced after the fact rather than the transfer itself prevented in advance.[^102]

The four-rule taxonomy Calabresi and Melamed develop crosses the property/liability distinction with the question of who holds the entitlement. Rule 1: entitlement in P, protected by property rule (the standard owner-controlled transfer regime). Rule 2: entitlement in P, protected by liability rule (the standard eminent-domain regime). Rule 3: entitlement in T (the prospective taker), protected by property rule (the regime in which polluters or other "takers" hold the entitlement and the affected party must purchase forbearance). Rule 4: entitlement in T, protected by liability rule against P's compelled forbearance (the regime in which P can enjoin T's activity but only by paying T to cease).[^103]

The proposal advanced in Part VI is, in this taxonomy, a *Rule 2* architecture with respect to traditional-knowledge-holding communities and the firms operating the AI-mediated synthesis pipeline. The entitlement is in the communities --- the moral and (under appropriate domestic-law architectures) legal entitlement to compensation for the use of their accumulated bioactivity knowledge. The protection regime is a liability rule: firms may use the knowledge (as documented through DSI traceable to registered traditional-knowledge claims) without prior consent, but are obligated to compensate the entitled community through a registry-mediated levy administered by a Performance Rights Organization. The choice of Rule 2 over Rule 1 is dictated by two considerations the law-and-economics literature has elaborated. *First*, the transaction costs of bilateral negotiation between every prospective extracting firm and every TK-holding community whose knowledge is potentially implicated in the firm's R&D pipeline are prohibitive --- the same observation that has historically driven the choice of liability rules over property rules in eminent domain, public utility regulation, and copyright's compulsory-licensing provisions for cover recordings.[^104] *Second*, the holdout problem inherent in property-rule regimes --- any single TK-holder can refuse consent and block downstream development --- is acute where the relevant bioactivity knowledge is shared across many communities (as is typical for medicinally significant plant species used across regions).[^105]

The choice of liability rule over property rule does, however, generate a corresponding theoretical objection that the proposal must confront. The objection is associated with Henry Smith and Tom Merrill's *information-cost* defense of property rules: clean property boundaries minimize the information that downstream parties must acquire about the entitlement, which reduces transaction costs in the aggregate.[^106] Liability rules, on this analysis, externalize information costs onto the regulatory apparatus that must determine the appropriate compensation rate; they generate ongoing administrative complexity that property rules avoid through the simple expedient of bilateral negotiation. The Smith--Merrill critique is not, however, a knockdown objection to liability rules; it is an argument for property rules where bilateral negotiation is feasible and an argument for liability rules where it is not. The architectural diagnosis in Part II is precisely the demonstration that bilateral negotiation between extracting firms and TK-holding communities, in the post-AlphaFold-3 environment, is not feasible: the firms can avoid the bilateral transaction by operating from sequence data, and the communities lack the institutional capacity to enforce property-rule entitlements against AI-mediated extraction occurring in jurisdictions and through technical pipelines they cannot effectively monitor. The conditions that make property rules optimal in the Smith--Merrill analysis are absent here. The conditions that make liability rules optimal --- high transaction costs of bilateral negotiation, holdout problems, asymmetric information about the entitlement's value --- are present.

### C. The Second-Enclosure-Movement Frame and Its Limits

James Boyle's *Second Enclosure Movement* essay supplies a third theoretical lens that the proposal must engage. Boyle's claim, briefly, is that the late-twentieth-century expansion of intellectual property rights --- software patents, business-method patents, gene patents, the *sui generis* rights for databases under the EU Database Directive, the term extensions of the Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act --- represents an "enclosure" of the intellectual commons analogous to the eighteenth-century enclosure of the agricultural commons in England.[^107] On Boyle's framing, the appropriate response to second-enclosure expansion is the defense and reconstruction of an intellectual commons --- a public domain robust enough to support follow-on innovation, scientific progress, and democratic participation in cultural production.

The Boyle framing is helpful for understanding the doctrinal architecture that surrounds DSI. The patent-eligibility doctrine of *Myriad*, the open-data norms of GenBank and its analogs, the limited copyrightability of databases under U.S. law (*Feist Publications*),[^108] and the fair-use doctrine's accommodation of research and analysis are all defensible as commons-protective measures resistant to the second-enclosure pressure. The framing has, however, two limitations the proposal must confront. *First*, the Boyle framing is internal to the doctrinal architectures of the developed-world intellectual property regimes; it does not, on its own terms, address the distributional question that the Chander--Sunder critique highlights. A robust intellectual commons in DSI is, on Chander and Sunder's analysis, not necessarily distributionally equitable; it may, on the contrary, function as the substrate on which extractive value-capture proceeds. *Second*, the Boyle framing accepts the property-versus-commons binary that the proposal seeks to transcend. The proposal is neither a re-enclosure of DSI into a new property right nor a defense of the existing commons; it is an architectural overlay on the existing commons that channels value flow through a liability-rule mechanism without converting the underlying information into a property entitlement.

The synthesis these three theoretical lenses jointly enable is the framework within which Part VI develops the Performance Rights Organization proposal. The proposal accepts the *Myriad* doctrine that DSI is not patentable as such; it accepts the Boyle position that the open-repository commons in DSI is, on balance, a public good worth defending; it accepts the Chander--Sunder critique that the commons framing alone is insufficient to address the distributional question; and it builds, on the *Calabresi--Melamed* framework, a Rule 2 liability architecture that channels value to TK-holding communities without re-enclosing the commons into a new property regime.

## IV. Three Counterfactual Reconstructions

### A. Methodology

A note on what the three case studies that follow attempt and what they do not. The documentary record established in Section II.G --- Insilico Medicine's Rentosertib Phase IIa clinical proof-of-concept, the active integration of generative AI with ethnobotanical knowledge bases, the absence of a freestanding AI-use disclosure duty, and the multi-billion-dollar platform consolidation --- establishes that the AI-mediated drug-discovery pipeline has reached peer-reviewed Phase IIa clinical testing by 2026. What the case studies cannot present is the corresponding firm-level documentation that any specific present-day pharmaceutical firm has run that pipeline end-to-end on a specific TK-derived bioactivity to a specific commercial product, because the proprietary nature of contemporary biotech R&D means firm-level pipeline records traceable to specific TK source organisms are not publicly available in the form a documented case study would require. The case studies therefore run a counterfactual on three historical biopiracy cases: for each, the Article asks what would have changed if the 2024-vintage stack had been available at the moment of extraction. The counterfactual reconstruction operates as the available analytical substitute for the documented-case-study form, with the documentary anchor in Section II.G establishing that the technical capability the counterfactuals presuppose is no longer prospective. Where the case studies make technical claims about the feasibility of present-day reconstruction, the claims are anchored to the published 2024--25 literature on AlphaFold-3, Chai-1, RoseTTAFold-All-Atom, and the leading generative-chemistry platforms (including the Nature Medicine publication of Rentosertib's Phase IIa results), and not to manufacturer or vendor representations. The counterfactual is not a claim that the historical extractors would have made the choices the counterfactual posits; it is a claim that the options available to extractors today are categorically different from the options available at the historical moment, and that the existing trigger architecture would not have captured those options had they been present.

The counterfactual proceeds for each case in three steps: factual chronology of the historical episode; assessment of present DSI availability for the relevant species; assessment of whether 2024-vintage tools would have permitted reproduction of the bioactivity (or a functional analogue) without the physical-access transaction that the historical episode in fact involved. The counterfactual's conclusions are calibrated to what the published 2024 literature establishes that the technology can do under realistic experimental conditions, not to the most optimistic claims of the platform-firm marketing materials. Where wet-lab validation steps remain unavoidable, the counterfactual notes the constraint and assesses its implications for the trigger architecture: a counterfactual extractor who must, at the validation stage, acquire physical material has a substantially asymmetric bargaining position relative to a source community that learns of the extraction only after the *in silico* design phase is complete, and the architectural significance of the validation-stage transaction is, for that reason, fundamentally different from the architectural significance the existing trigger architecture assumes. Where the assessment requires technical claims about wet-lab feasibility, the Article relies on the published 2024 literature and notes the relevant technical caveats; the conclusion in each case is calibrated to that literature, not to manufacturer claims.

The cases are presented in increasing order of empirical caveat. Curcuma longa is presented first because its biosynthetic pathway is among the best-characterized of any plant secondary metabolite, the whole-genome assembly is publicly available, and de novo curcuminoid biosynthesis in Saccharomyces cerevisiae has been experimentally validated; the counterfactual is therefore strongest in this case. Banisteriopsis caapi is presented second; both the chloroplast and mitochondrial genomes are publicly available, the principal B. caapi beta-carbolines are biosynthesized through tryptophan-derived alkaloid pathways, and total chemical synthesis of the principal harmala alkaloids has been routine for decades. Hoodia gordonii is presented third because the empirical record is the most caveated: no whole-genome assembly is publicly available; the biosynthetic pathway for the active P57 compound's distinctive cymarosyl-glycoside linkage is only partially characterized; and the counterfactual reconstruction therefore depends more heavily on chemical-synthesis pathways than on heterologous expression. The ordering choice is pedagogical: the cases jointly establish the architectural pattern, and presenting the strongest case first builds the analytical framework against which the more caveated case can be read.

### B. Case 1: Curcuma longa and U.S. Patent No. 5,401,504

In March 1995, the United States Patent and Trademark Office issued U.S. Patent No. 5,401,504, "Use of Turmeric in Wound Healing," to Suman K. Das and Hari Har P. Cohly, researchers at the University of Mississippi Medical Center.[^109] The patent's claims covered a method of promoting healing of a wound by administering an effective amount of turmeric powder, orally or topically, in defined dosages. Turmeric (*Curcuma longa*; Sanskrit *haridra*) is a foundational article of the Indian Ayurvedic pharmacopoeia, with documented use for wound healing in the *Charaka Samhita* (circa second century BCE) and the *Sushruta Samhita* (circa 250 BCE), and continuous transmission through the Ayurvedic medical tradition for the more than two millennia between those texts and the Mississippi patent.[^110]

In October 1996, India's Council of Scientific and Industrial Research filed a request for reexamination of the patent at the USPTO, supported by thirty-two prior-art references in Sanskrit, Urdu, and Hindi documenting the traditional use of turmeric for wound healing --- including a 1953 article in the *Journal of the Indian Medical Association*.[^111] In November 1997, the USPTO issued a rejection order canceling the patent's claims on grounds of obviousness and anticipation. The reexamination certificate finalizing the cancellation was issued in 1998.[^112] The case became, in India and internationally, the originating instance for a sustained Indian effort to make traditional knowledge searchable and visible to foreign patent offices, and led directly to the establishment in 2001 of the Traditional Knowledge Digital Library, now integrated into the search protocols of the European Patent Office, the United Kingdom Intellectual Property Office, and the United States Patent and Trademark Office.[^113]

The counterfactual reconstruction begins with the present state of the public scientific record. In 2021, Chakraborty and colleagues at the Indian Institute of Science Education and Research, Bhopal, published a draft whole-genome assembly of *Curcuma longa*: 1.02 gigabase pair assembly, 50,401 coding sequences, deposited under NCBI BioProject accession PRJNA660606.[^114] The curcuminoid biosynthetic pathway is among the best-characterized secondary-metabolite pathways in plant biochemistry, with the curcumin synthases CURS1, CURS2, and CURS3 cloned and structurally characterized;[^115] the upstream diketide-CoA synthase (DCS) and its substrate specificities have been mapped; and the entire pathway from the phenylpropanoid precursors to curcumin and its bisdemethoxy analogs has been computationally and experimentally elaborated.[^116] In 2024, *de novo* curcuminoid biosynthesis was demonstrated in *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*, with the entire pathway expressed heterologously to yield curcumin titers of up to 4.2 mg/L in optimized de novo conditions.[^117]

The combination of public whole-genome data, experimentally validated heterologous-expression pathways, AlphaFold-3-class structural prediction for hypothesis generation and enzyme modeling,[^118] and generative-chemistry platforms supports the following counterfactual claim with high confidence. Chemistry42 illustrates small-molecule generative design capacity; separately, Insilico's Biology42 / Generative Biologics work illustrates rapid AI-assisted peptide and biologics design. The Curcuma counterfactual relies on the former for curcuminoid analogue design and on pathway reconstruction for the DSI-specific route, not on peptide benchmarks.[^119] A present-day research program, operating in 2026 without contact with India, the TKDL, or any newly accessed Curcuma longa genetic material, could:

(1) Identify the wound-healing bioactivity through public ethnobotanical literature, biomedical-database searches of curcumin's pharmacological profile, and the substantial peer-reviewed literature on curcumin's role in cutaneous collagen deposition, anti-inflammatory cytokine modulation, and antimicrobial activity.[^120]

The counterfactual is the strongest of the three the Article presents because it contains both a chemical-information route and a genuine DSI/pathway route. The Indian innovation in defensive prior-art documentation was a successful response to the architecture of 1995. It is incomplete against the architecture of 2026.

### C. Case 2: Banisteriopsis caapi and U.S. Plant Patent No. PP5,751

In June 1986, the United States Patent and Trademark Office issued U.S. Plant Patent No. PP5,751 to Loren S. Miller of the International Plant Medicine Corporation. The patent claimed a new and distinct cultivar of *Banisteriopsis caapi*, designated "Da Vine," that Miller represented to have collected from a domestic garden in the Amazon rainforest.[^122] *B. caapi* is the principal ingredient of ayahuasca, a psychoactive brew used for spiritual, healing, and ceremonial purposes by indigenous peoples throughout the Western Amazon --- including the Quechua, Shipibo-Konibo, Kaxinawa, and many others --- for centuries.[^123] The patent claim, on its face, asserted private property rights over a cultivated variety of a plant that was not Miller's discovery in any meaningful sense, but rather a commodity of the indigenous botanical commons.

In March 1999, the Coordinating Body of Indigenous Organizations of the Amazon Basin ("COICA"), the Center for International Environmental Law ("CIEL"), and the Amazon Alliance filed a request for reexamination of the patent (USPTO Reexamination Control No. 90/005,307), supported by botanical-museum specimens predating the patent and by ethnobotanical documentation of *B. caapi*'s traditional use.[^124] In November 1999, the USPTO examiner rejected the patent's claims as anticipated by the prior art. Miller was permitted to submit additional evidence in support of his claim; the petitioners were not afforded a parallel opportunity to respond to that evidence; and in April 2001, the examiner reversed himself and issued a certificate confirming the patent's validity for the remainder of its term. The patent expired by operation of its own term on June 17, 2003.[^125]

No formal benefit-sharing agreement was negotiated. No indigenous community received compensation. The case became a referent in the biopiracy literature for the institutional failure of the U.S. patent system to protect traditional knowledge against speculative private claims.[^126]

The present-day record on DSI availability for B. caapi is substantial. In May 2018, the complete chloroplast genome of Banisteriopsis caapi was sequenced and deposited in GenBank under accession MH041648 (158,473 base pairs, 126 genes), with specimens drawn from the United States Botanic Garden's accession holdings.[^127] In July 2024, the complete circular mitochondrial genome (503,502 base pairs) was published.[^128] The principal B. caapi alkaloids relevant to ayahuasca pharmacology are beta-carbolines --- harmine, harmaline, and tetrahydroharmine --- which act principally through monoamine-oxidase inhibition and related serotonergic effects; DMT is typically supplied by admixture plants such as Psychotria viridis or Diplopterys cabrerana, rather than by B. caapi itself.[^129] The beta-carboline and broader tryptophan-derived alkaloid pathways are sufficiently characterized in the plant-biosynthesis literature to make homology-based inference and analogue design plausible, while total chemical synthesis of the harmala alkaloids has been routine for decades.

The counterfactual question is therefore not whether contemporary tools would enable a hypothetical present-day extractor to produce the principal B. caapi beta-carbolines without contact with B. caapi --- they could have done so for decades on the strength of ordinary organic chemistry. The counterfactual question is whether contemporary tools would have permitted the strategic move of using public organellar sequence data, related plant-alkaloid literature, and known beta-carboline chemistry to identify candidate biosynthetic enzymes, generate novel beta-carboline analogues with optimized pharmacological profiles, and patent those analogues as compositions of matter without any clear disclosure of the underlying ethnobotanical inspiration. The answer is qualified. The Banisteriopsis counterfactual is best understood as a mixed chemical-information and partial-DSI bypass. Public organellar genomes do not by themselves reconstruct the beta-carboline biosynthetic pathway, and the absence of a complete public nuclear genome materially weakens the DSI claim. But the case still illustrates how ethnobotanical knowledge, known alkaloid chemistry, related-species inference, and partial sequence records can support analogue development without any new access event, with a contestable GRATK disclosure obligation and with Cali Fund contribution depending on firm size, sector, and eventual implementation.

The case is particularly instructive because the historical patent-revocation pathway --- the principal indigenous-community remedy under the existing architecture --- would be substantially less available against the present-day extractor. The COICA/CIEL/Amazon Alliance reexamination challenge in 1999 succeeded (initially) on the ground that Miller's "Da Vine" cultivar was not novel; it was a previously existing variety of an extensively documented Amazonian species. Chemically novel beta-carboline analogues would not be vulnerable to the same cultivar-prior-art challenge; novelty would turn on chemical structure and the prior-art search would be less likely to anticipate them. The patent system's existing architecture for capturing TK-derivative claims operates on the patentability of the claimed invention; the extraction architecture this Article describes operates by ensuring that the claimed invention is patentably distinct from any traditional knowledge it draws upon.

### D. Case 3: Hoodia gordonii and the P57 Compound

The South African Council for Scientific and Industrial Research began researching *Hoodia gordonii*, a succulent indigenous to the Kalahari and Namib regions, in the 1960s, drawing on the appetite-suppressant use of the plant by San hunters whose familiarity with it had been observed by Dutch ethnobiologists since the 1930s.[^130] In the late 1970s, CSIR scientists isolated the active compound, an oxypregnane steroidal glycoside subsequently designated P57. By the mid-1990s, CSIR had filed patent applications covering the isolated compound, and in 1997 it licensed development rights to the United Kingdom firm Phytopharm, which in turn sublicensed Pfizer for clinical development of an anti-obesity drug.[^131]

The San communities whose traditional knowledge had supplied the original bioactivity were not party to any of these arrangements until 2001, when the Working Group of Indigenous Minorities in Southern Africa ("WIMSA") confronted CSIR following NGO criticism. On March 24, 2003, CSIR and the San representative entities signed a benefit-sharing agreement establishing the San Hoodia Benefit-Sharing Trust, under which the San would receive six percent of all milestone payments and eight percent of all royalties derived from commercialization of P57.[^132] Approximately three months later, in July 2003, Pfizer terminated the development program. Unilever later assumed development rights for an over-the-counter product line and discontinued the program in 2008. No commercial product reached the market. Milestone payments flowed to the Trust, but the royalty stream the agreement contemplated never materialized, because there was nothing to royalty.

The case is canonical in the ABS literature for two reasons: it produced one of the first and most-cited formal benefit-sharing agreements with an indigenous community,[^133] and it demonstrated that even where a benefit-sharing agreement exists, commercialization failure can leave the indigenous community with little or nothing in real terms.

The case is canonical in this Article for a third reason. As of 2026, no whole-genome assembly of *Hoodia gordonii* is publicly available in GenBank, but transcriptome data is publicly available through the Medicinal Plant Genomics Resource and other repositories, the chemical structure of P57 has been published, and total chemical synthesis of P57 was demonstrated in 2012.[^134] The biosynthetic pathway for steroidal pregnane glycosides is partially characterized, with candidate UDP-glycosyltransferases identified in the *Hoodia* transcriptome; the cymarosyl linkage that distinguishes P57 from related compounds is the residual technical challenge that would require either heterologous expression validation or chemical synthesis.[^135]

Run the counterfactual. Suppose, in 1996, that the present technological stack had been available to a hypothetical anti-obesity research program operating without contact with CSIR or with the San. The program could have located the published ethnobotanical references to San appetite-suppression practices, drawn on the (then-future) Hoodia transcriptome to identify candidate biosynthetic genes, and used AlphaFold-3 to model the substrate specificity of the candidate glycosyltransferases. With Insilico-Medicine-style generative chemistry tools, the program could have generated and screened novel steroidal pregnane glycoside scaffolds optimized against hypothesized appetite-regulation pathways, including the hypothalamic ATP/satiety mechanism most commonly associated with P57 in the literature.[^136] The synthetic functional analogues so generated would have been chemically distinct from P57 itself, potentially sufficient to avoid any patent that CSIR had filed on the natural compound, and would have triggered no Nagoya obligation if no physical access occurred; any GRATK disclosure obligation would depend on whether the claim was treated as "based on" the genetic resource or associated TK; and any Cali Fund monetary contribution would depend on whether the developer met the Fund's sector and size thresholds.

The counterfactual is partial in the Hoodia case --- the absence of a public whole-genome assembly is a material constraint, and the cymarosyl-linkage challenge would require wet-lab validation that the counterfactual does not fully eliminate. But the partial counterfactual is sufficient to make a narrower architectural point. The benefit-sharing agreement that made the historical Hoodia case canonical was triggered by physical access --- by the fact that CSIR had collected the plant and isolated the compound from the plant. A counterfactual program that synthesized analogues from published chemical information, partial transcriptomic data, and target-mechanism literature, without ever collecting the plant, would have been a chemically mediated informational bypass rather than a clean DSI-mediated bypass. The case stands, in the Article's argument, as the demonstration that the architectural failure is not contingent on a perfect sequence-to-product pipeline. Even where the technology requires wet-lab validation that the counterfactual cannot fully bypass, much of the value-generating design work may occur before any access transaction that the existing architecture can observe. The proposal in Part VI is the structural answer to that attribution gap.

### E. Cross-Case Findings

The three cases together establish a calibrated pattern. Curcuma longa shows the failure mode in its strongest form: a TK-derivative bioactivity insight, a public-domain whole-genome assembly, experimentally validated curcuminoid-pathway reconstruction, and a defensive-documentation regime (the TKDL) that has limited purchase against chemically novel patent claims drafted on AI-designed analogues. Banisteriopsis caapi shows an intermediate, mixed case: public organellar genomes and known beta-carboline chemistry do not prove a complete DSI pipeline, but they weaken the patent-revocation pathway against a present-day extractor whose claims are drafted on chemically novel analogues. Hoodia gordonii shows a chemically mediated informational bypass: even where transcriptomic evidence and chemical synthesis rather than a complete genome do most of the work, value-generating design can occur before an observable access transaction.

The architectural common feature is the trigger. In each case, the historical episode involved a physical-access or facially provenance-revealing transaction that the existing architecture could in principle catch. In each counterfactual, the 2024-vintage technological stack permits an extractor to operate from some combination of sequence data, traditional-use documentation, natural-product chemical information, biomedical target data, and generative design, producing chemically novel analogues without a new physical-access transaction. The diagnosis is not that the existing architecture is unprincipled, or that every example is a pure DSI case. The diagnosis is that the triggers are under-inclusive for informational extraction. The triggers are still there. Much of the activity that once would have engaged them can now occur through a pipeline that does not.

The variation across the three cases also illuminates a useful taxonomy of failure modes that the proposal in Part VI must address. The Curcuma longa failure mode is the most demanding: a near-complete bypass of the existing architecture at every layer (Nagoya, GRATK, Cali Fund, TKDL), with one route enabled by the public-domain status of the whole-genome assembly and another by public chemical information. The Banisteriopsis caapi failure mode is intermediate: a likely bypass of the access architecture, with the patent-revocation backstop weakened by chemical novelty but the DSI claim materially caveated by the lack of a public nuclear genome. The Hoodia gordonii failure mode is the least technically demanding but the most institutionally diagnostic: even where the historical benefit-sharing agreement was negotiated and signed, the architecture's reliance on bilateral negotiation and downstream royalties left the San with little when commercialization failed, and a present-day analogue-design path could move substantial value-creation earlier in the pipeline. The proposal in Part VI is calibrated to address all three failure modes by focusing on provenance-bearing informational inputs rather than DSI alone.

## V. Antitrust as Supporting Frame

### A. The Bakhoum--Conde Gallego Pivot

In their contribution to the 2016 Max Planck volume *TRIPS plus 20*, Mor Bakhoum and Beatriz Conde Gallego identified a structural shift in the policy architecture surrounding the World Trade Organization's Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights.[^137] Their claim was that the TRIPS framework, originally argued in development discourse as a "transfer of technology" regime --- under which strong IP protection in developed countries would induce technology flow toward developing countries --- has, in twenty years of operation, more usefully been read as an *innovation policy* regime, in which the relevant analytical question is not transfer-quantity but the structure of competitive incentives along the global innovation chain. The shift, in their reading, makes competition law (rather than the Articles 7 and 8 development carve-outs of TRIPS itself) the primary lever through which TRIPS' developmental purposes can be served. Where IP rights, particularly in their digital-platform-era deployment, generate concentration that suppresses follow-on innovation in developing-country markets, the appropriate response is competition-policy intervention rather than IP-rule weakening.[^138]

The Bakhoum--Conde Gallego framework provides analytical purchase that the TK-sovereignty framing alone does not. The TK-sovereignty framing of biopiracy treats the harm as one of unconsented cultural appropriation, with corresponding remedies in restitution, attribution, and consent. Those remedies are normatively important. They are also, on the empirical record of the past three decades, weak operationally --- the Cali Fund's empty months are the most recent demonstration. The Bakhoum--Conde Gallego framework permits a parallel diagnosis: the harm is not only one of unconsented appropriation but one of *concentration of value capture* in a small number of platform firms positioned at the AI-mediated synthesis chokepoint. That harm is one to which competition law has analytical and operational tools.

### B. The Pipeline as Concentration Risk

The DSI-to-AI-to-molecule pipeline is, on present evidence, structurally concentrated at the model layer. The leading structural-prediction systems are operated by Alphabet (Isomorphic Labs, holding the commercial channel for AlphaFold-3), Meta (ESMFold), and a small number of well-capitalized startups (Chai Discovery for Chai-1; the Baker Lab spin-outs for RoseTTAFold derivatives).[^139] The leading generative-chemistry platforms are operated by Insilico Medicine, Generate Biomedicines, Recursion Pharmaceuticals, and a handful of others.[^140] The training data --- open sequence repositories and proprietary chemistry databases --- is, in the case of the open repositories, accessible to all but, in the case of the proprietary chemistry databases, increasingly concentrated in firms whose business model depends on differential access.

The concentration risk is twofold. First, the platform layer is positioned to capture value generated downstream by client firms that use AI systems for drug discovery, agricultural-biotechnology design, or industrial-enzyme engineering. The Cali Fund does not formally ignore AI: Decision 16/2 names information, scientific, and technical services related to DSI, including artificial intelligence. Its weakness is different. It uses a voluntary, indicative, firm-size-and-sector contribution model rather than an attribution-based trigger that links a particular workflow to particular DSI, TK, source communities, or provenance chains. Second, the platform layer is positioned to control access to the traditional-knowledge-derived bioactivity inputs of the future --- by aggregating ethnobotanical literature, by building proprietary databases of TK-derived structural motifs, and by negotiating bilateral data-licensing arrangements with TK-holding source countries that cut out the multilateral architecture entirely.[^141]

### C. The Recent Antitrust-of-AI Scholarship

The Bakhoum--Conde Gallego framework was developed in 2016, before the post-AlphaFold-2 (2021) and post-AlphaFold-3 (2024) acceleration of AI-mediated structural biology and before the post-ChatGPT (2022) acceleration of foundation-model competition policy. The framework has been extended, in the 2022--2026 antitrust scholarship, in ways that bear directly on the present case. Lina Khan's foundational Amazon's Antitrust Paradox in the 2017 Yale Law Journal established the conceptual case for treating dominant platforms as antitrust subjects rather than as the default state of the digital economy;[^142] her tenure as Chair of the Federal Trade Commission from 2021 to 2025 produced a sustained policy effort to apply the conceptual framework to AI infrastructure firms, including the FTC's 2023 inquiry into the Microsoft--OpenAI partnership and the parallel inquiry into Anthropic's relationships with cloud-infrastructure providers.[^143] Tim Wu's Curse of Bigness (2018) and Matt Stoller's Goliath (2019) supplied the popular-press articulation of what the academic antitrust literature was building toward; the 2024 district-court ruling in the DOJ's United States v. Google LLC search-monopoly case supplied the leading judicial articulation of distribution-based entry barriers in digital-platform markets, a ruling that subsequent commentators and regulators have treated as relevant to AI-platform competition because of its analysis of default distribution, data scale, and entry barriers.[^144] The European Union's Digital Markets Act, which entered into force on November 1, 2022, and became applicable to gatekeepers from March 7, 2024, supplies the parallel European architecture for designated gatekeepers in existing core platform services, while Commission-review discussions and emerging scholarship have begun to ask whether AI and cloud infrastructure should be folded into gatekeeper analysis.[^145]

The relevance to the present case is straightforward but supporting rather than freestanding. The architecture this Article diagnoses is principally an ABS attribution failure. It also has a competition-policy dimension: value generated by AI-mediated synthesis from open and provenance-bearing informational inputs can be captured upstream, at the platform layer, with downstream extraction risk concentrated in a small number of firms whose market position is reinforced by training-data scale and proprietary-chemistry-database differential access. The Cali Fund's present contribution architecture is not structurally designed to attribute that value to the informational inputs that helped generate it. Competition-policy intervention at the platform layer may therefore be a complement to benefit-sharing reform, but the proposal in Part VI remains calibrated as a trigger-layer and distribution-layer reform rather than as a full antitrust program.

### D. TRIPS Article 7 as Analytical Anchor

TRIPS Article 7 declares that the protection and enforcement of intellectual property rights "should contribute to the promotion of technological innovation and to the transfer and dissemination of technology, to the mutual advantage of producers and users of technological knowledge and in a manner conducive to social and economic welfare, and to a balance of rights and obligations." Article 8(2) authorizes parties to take measures to "prevent the abuse of intellectual property rights by right holders or the resort to practices which unreasonably restrain trade or adversely affect the international transfer of technology."[^146] Bakhoum and Conde Gallego read these provisions as supplying the textual hook for a competition-policy-led reading of TRIPS' development purposes; the present Article extends that reading to the DSI-to-AI-to-molecule pipeline, with the post-2022 antitrust scholarship supplying the doctrinal scaffolding the original Bakhoum--Conde Gallego argument lacked.

The extension is straightforward in analytical structure. The pipeline produces concentration of value-capture upstream (at the platform layer) and atomization of risk downstream (at the TK-source-community level). The Article 7 / Article 8(2) hook permits competition-law intervention against the upstream concentration; it does not, on its face, supply a basis for direct compensation flow to the downstream TK-holding communities. The proposal in Part VI is the bridge: a compensation architecture that operates orthogonally to the existing IP-rights enforcement framework, and that derives its enforcement purchase precisely from the upstream-concentration that competition policy would otherwise be invoked to break up.

### E. Why the Competition Frame Matters

A note on framing. The TK-sovereignty framing, taken alone, treats traditional-knowledge-holding communities as moral claimants whose rights-violation generates a duty of restitution. The framing is correct as far as it goes; it has been the dominant register of the biopiracy literature for three decades; and it has not, on the empirical record, produced a robust, attribution-based flow of benefits. The Bakhoum-Conde Gallego framing, supplemented cautiously by the post-2022 antitrust-of-AI scholarship, supplements the moral register with an economic register. Uncompensated extraction of traditional-knowledge-derived bioactivity is not only a sovereignty violation; it is an economic distortion. Value is being created and captured without the corresponding obligation that ordinary economic theory would attach to its inputs. The distortion is bad for source communities (who may receive little or nothing through the current architecture) and, in the longer run, bad for firms (who face accumulating reputational, regulatory, and political risk that the absence of a functional architecture leaves them no reliable way to discharge). The argument for an architecture that actually captures the value flow is therefore not a charity argument but a market-design argument. The audience for this argument is not only biodiversity-law specialists, but also IP, development, and competition-law policymakers concerned with how legal regimes allocate the economic value generated by informational inputs.

## VI. The Proposal: A Performance Rights Organization for Traditional Knowledge

### A. The ASCAP/BMI Analogy: What Works, What Breaks

The American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers ("ASCAP") and Broadcast Music, Inc. ("BMI") are Performance Rights Organizations that license the public performance of musical compositions on behalf of songwriters and music publishers, collect fees from licensees (radio stations, television networks, restaurants, streaming services), and distribute royalties to rightsholders according to documented performance.[^147] The model has worked, with significant strain and recurring litigation, for over a century in the United States, and analogous collective rights organizations operate in essentially every developed-country music market. Daniel Gervais and João Pedro Quintais's authoritative treatment of collective rights management identifies four conditions under which the PRO architecture functions: usage is observable (radio play counted; streaming logged); membership is voluntary on the rightsholder side and effectively mandatory on the user side (licensees cannot lawfully use covered works without a license); revenue flows through blanket licenses (no per-use negotiation); and distribution is formula-driven, transparent in protocol, and imperfect in fairness (as all distribution must be).[^148]

The applicability of the model to traditional knowledge is partial. It works in two respects. The mandatory-on-the-user-side feature is precisely what the existing ABS architecture lacks: the Cali Fund's sectoral-revenue trigger is voluntary in the formal sense and weakly enforced in practice, and the model's contribution is to make the user-side obligation triggered by *use* rather than by transaction. The blanket-license feature aligns with the architectural intuition that an extractor's compensation obligation should attach to its *sectoral activity* rather than to per-resource per-transaction negotiation, which the Nagoya bilateral architecture has demonstrated does not scale.

It breaks in two respects, both of which the proposal addresses. First, music PROs operate over a domain (musical compositions) in which rightsholders are relatively easily identifiable and aggregable, the relevant unit of compensation (the song) is discrete, and use is comparatively observable (radio play count; streaming events). Traditional knowledge is none of these things: rightsholders may overlap across communities, the relevant unit of compensation is not a discrete artifact but a body of accumulated practice, and use occurs invisibly inside firm research-and-development pipelines. Second, music PRO membership is voluntary on the rightsholder side, with the implicit assumption that non-members can opt out of the system; traditional-knowledge-holding communities cannot opt out of having their knowledge used by extractors who operate from public DSI repositories.

The proposal that follows is therefore not a transplantation of the ASCAP/BMI model but an *inversion* of one of its key assumptions: rather than voluntary on the rightsholder side and mandatory on the user side, the proposed regime is mandatory on the user side (with the Cali Fund and complementary domestic legislation supplying the legal obligation) and *active* on the rightsholder side (with a TK-provenance registry supplying the documentation infrastructure that makes attribution-based distribution feasible).

### B. The Department of Justice's Eight Decades of ASCAP/BMI Supervision: An Instructive Precedent

The proposal's invocation of the music-PRO model carries with it the obligation to engage the most distinctive feature of the music-PRO experience in the United States: more than eighty years of Department of Justice antitrust supervision through consent decrees that have shaped, in continuous adjustment, the operational parameters of both ASCAP and BMI. ASCAP entered into its first antitrust consent decree with the Department of Justice in 1941, with substantial revisions in 1950, 1960, 2001, and the still-pending 2019--25 review.[^149] BMI entered into its consent decree in 1966, with material revisions in 1994.[^150] The decrees have, in their cumulative effect, addressed three sets of concerns that the proposal must address by analogy: monopoly power in the licensing market (the decrees require non-exclusive licensing, permitting direct licensing by individual rightsholders); discriminatory pricing (the decrees impose nondiscrimination requirements and rate-court oversight for disputed license fees);[^151] and the integration of streaming-era distribution channels (the 2014--25 review proceedings have addressed the application of the decrees to digital streaming services).[^152]

The eighty-four-year arc of antitrust supervision is, on one reading, an indictment of the music-PRO model: a regulatory architecture that requires perpetual oversight to prevent the collective-licensing apparatus from devolving into anticompetitive collusion. On a second reading, the arc is an endorsement: the architecture has produced a workable royalty-flow mechanism for a domain (music licensing) where bilateral negotiation between every prospective user and every rightsholder is administratively impossible, and the antitrust supervision is the operational price of the workability. This Article adopts the second reading, with the proposal's design explicitly incorporating analogous regulatory-supervision provisions: the Performance Rights Organization for Traditional Knowledge would be subject to ongoing oversight by an international body (the most natural locus is the WTO's Committee on Trade and Environment, with parallel oversight at the CBD Conference of the Parties), with rate-determination procedures modeled on the U.S. rate-court mechanism and with explicit provisions permitting direct licensing where a rightsholder-community wishes to negotiate bilaterally outside the collective architecture.

The DOJ--ASCAP--BMI history also supplies a precedent for one of the proposal's most contested design choices, addressed in subsection C below: the question of whether the Performance Rights Organization is itself an antitrust concern, and how the proposal should be structured to avoid recreating the concentration problem the Article diagnoses at the platform layer.

### C. The Five-Component Design

![](./media/image3.png)

*FIGURE 3. The Five-Component Performance Rights Organization Architecture. The proposal modifies the trigger and the distribution algorithm of the existing Cali Fund and embeds the registry function within existing regional architectures (TKDL, Pacific Community, Andean Community, African Union); it does not propose a new international institution. The five components---Registry (TKPR), Trigger (use-based), Distribution Algorithm (attribution-weighted), Audit Chain (four layers), and Dispute Resolution (tiered WIPO-style)---operate over the existing Cali Fund infrastructure (preserved). See Article §§ VI.C, VI.F.*

#### 1. The Registry

A Traditional Knowledge Provenance Registry ("TKPR"), operated under the auspices of the CBD Secretariat in partnership with WIPO, with regional implementing partners in major TK-holding states (India's TKDL is the obvious model and natural integration point; the Pacific Community, the Andean Community, and the African Union supply natural counterparts).[^153] The TKPR would catalog (a) registered traditional-knowledge-holding communities with established governance entities qualified to receive distributions; (b) documented bioactivity-knowledge claims linked to specific genetic-resource species, compounds, or therapeutic uses, with prior-art dating sufficient to establish provenance; (c) known natural-product compounds, scaffolds, and biosynthetic pathway records associated with those claims; (d) corresponding deposited DSI in public sequence repositories, where present and where attributable to a community-known specimen; and (e) the metadata trail by which a downstream researcher's use of relevant DSI or other provenance-bearing informational inputs can be traced to a registered community-knowledge claim. Registration would be community-controlled --- communities decide what to register, in what level of granularity, with what restrictions --- and consistent with the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples.[^154]

The registry must also be designed around indigenous data sovereignty rather than against it. Non-registration should never be treated as waiver. The TKPR must permit negative, confidential, or placeholder entries for sacred, secret, orally held, or deliberately non-disclosed knowledge, with community-controlled access conditions and no requirement that protected knowledge be made public to generate a benefit claim. A community's decision not to disclose knowledge publicly should affect evidentiary design, not entitlement.

The TKPR is the proposal's most demanding component, both technically and politically. Its feasibility depends critically on the existence of TKDL-style precedents in major TK-holding states and on the willingness of biotechnology firms to integrate TKPR queries into their standard prior-art-search and ethnobotanical-literature-review workflows. The proposal does not assume the TKPR springs into existence in operational form; it proposes a five-year buildout, with India's TKDL as the seed integration, and with a staged onboarding of additional communities and species through 2031.

#### 2. The Trigger

A two-tier use-based trigger, replacing the transaction-based and sectoral-revenue-based triggers of the existing architecture. Tier 1 is a DSI-provenance trigger: the obligation fires when a product, patent claim, regulatory submission, model output, or discovery workflow materially uses registered DSI linked through the TKPR to a species, community, or TK entry. Tier 2 is a TK-informational-provenance trigger: the obligation fires when the workflow materially uses registered TK documentation, named traditional-use bioactivity, natural-product compound information, or biosynthetic pathway records to identify, prioritize, optimize, or commercialize a compound or scaffold, even if no DSI is used. "Materially uses" is defined functionally: the input is queried, downloaded, retrieved, used as input to an AI structural-prediction or generative-chemistry workflow, used to select a target or scaffold, or otherwise relied upon in a pipeline that produces a candidate compound.

The trigger is auditable but not, in the strong sense, observable. A firm that uses TKPR-tagged DSI or TKPR-documented provenance inputs in a closed proprietary workflow leaves no public trace; the trigger therefore relies on attestation by the firm at the point of regulatory engagement (FDA Investigational New Drug filing; EMA marketing authorization; agricultural-biotech registration; and analogous downstream regulatory checkpoints) and on the audit chain described below. The architectural choice trades some loss of trigger-observability for substantial gain in trigger-coverage: the use-based trigger reaches activity that the transaction-based and sectoral-revenue triggers do not, while aligning the proposal with the broader harm theory developed in Parts II through IV.

#### 3. The Distribution Algorithm

Distribution from the Fund (the existing Cali Fund, augmented and operating on the new trigger) to registered TK-holding communities, weighted by attribution: where a candidate compound traces, through the firm's audited TKPR-query log, metadata exposure, or regulatory attestation, to bioactivity claims or provenance-bearing informational inputs held by a specific community, that community receives a proportional share of the Fund disbursement attributable to the firm. Where attribution is multiple --- the same bioactivity, compound, scaffold, or pathway is documented by multiple communities --- the disbursement is divided pro rata, with the TKPR's documented prior-art dates and community registrations supplying the proportions.

The algorithm is deliberately transparent in protocol and acknowledged to be imperfect in fairness. Music PROs distribute royalties by formulas that systematically favor heavily played works over thinly played ones; the TKPR analog would systematically favor heavily documented and heavily queried bioactivities over those that are less. The architectural tradeoff is the same in both domains: a transparent imperfect distribution beats an opaque, theoretically perfect one that does not in fact disburse.

The distribution algorithm must also correct for documentary bias. A system that relies only on publication dates, patent-prior-art records, and colonial archives risks rewarding the earliest or most legible colonial documentation rather than the community whose knowledge is most continuous, authoritative, or locally recognized. Oral-history evidence, community attestation, regional expert panels, and skepticism toward colonial archives should therefore supplement publication dates and prior-art records in the attribution formula.

#### 4. The Audit Chain

Four audit layers. First, firm-level attestation at regulatory checkpoints, with criminal and civil penalties for false attestation modeled on FDA application false-statement liability and on EU regulation of competition-law disclosures. Second, sectoral sampling: an annual random-sample audit of a defined fraction of pipeline programs in each contribution-eligible sector, conducted by an independent technical auditor under terms negotiated by the Fund Secretariat. Third, TKPR query-log integrity: cryptographic logging of TKPR queries, with firms whose queries match downstream candidate compounds presumed (rebuttably) to have incorporated the queried DSI or provenance-bearing input in the relevant pipeline. Fourth, and this addresses the most pressing critique that the use-based trigger fails when firms simply avoid querying the registry: a deposition-side metadata-tagging obligation imposed on the major public DSI repositories (GenBank, ENA, DDBJ) and, where feasible, on participating natural-product and ethnobotanical databases requires that records whose source organisms or documented uses appear in registered TK claims carry attribution metadata travelling with every download. Where a firm cannot demonstrate, through query-log evidence, alternative-source records, or other audited materials, that its candidate compound was developed without exposure to the metadata-tagged input, an evidentiary presumption of incorporation operates, with the burden on the firm to rebut. The presumption is not a strict-liability rule --- a firm can demonstrate that its pipeline used un-tagged sequence data, alternative public sources, or proprietary-database derivatives --- but the presumption shifts the audit asymmetry that makes the voluntary-use evasion strategy attractive: a firm that opts out of the registry while operating in the contribution-eligible sectors faces an enforcement environment where the firm, not the regulator, bears the burden of establishing non-derivation.

The audit-chain architecture is novel, untested, and depends on three empirical assumptions the proposal cannot presently verify: that firms in the contribution-eligible sectors will accept regulatory-checkpoint attestation obligations of the kind the proposal contemplates; that domestic regulators will enforce false-attestation liability with sufficient consistency to produce credible deterrence; and that sectoral random-sample auditing of the proposed scope is administratively feasible at the cost-and-staffing levels the Cali Fund Secretariat could realistically support. The proposal assumes affirmative answers, supported by the parallel-domain evidence of conflict-minerals disclosure under Dodd-Frank Section 1502, supply-chain transparency under California's Transparency in Supply Chains Act, and the European Union's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive --- each of which produced credible firm-level compliance behavior in the absence of perfect enforcement, on the strength of regulatory checkpoint attestation backed by false-statement liability and reputational exposure to investor and civil-society audit.[^155] None of these is the equivalent of copyright enforcement under ASCAP/BMI; each is a near-cousin operating in domains where the underlying activity is similarly unobservable to direct external monitoring. The audit chain is, in this sense, novel in its domain and precedented in its mechanism. A firm sufficiently determined to evade the regime can, with effort, do so. The architectural answer is the same as in the music industry: imperfect enforcement against an architecture that captures most of the value beats theoretically perfect enforcement against an architecture (the present one) that captures essentially none of it.

#### 5. Dispute Resolution and the Distribution-Algorithm Volume Problem

The four components above generate predictable disputes that the proposal's architecture must address through a dedicated dispute-resolution mechanism. Three classes of dispute predominate. *First*, attribution disputes: where multiple registered communities document overlapping bioactivity claims, the distribution algorithm's pro-rata calculation will produce contested allocations. *Second*, audit disputes: where a sectoral audit identifies undisclosed TKPR-traceable use, the firm's response will routinely contest the technical claim that its pipeline incorporated the queried DSI in a materially relevant way. *Third*, registration disputes: where a community's registration of a bioactivity claim is contested by another community, by a national government, or by a research institution that can document independent prior characterization, the registration's validity must be adjudicated.

The proposed mechanism draws on the structure of the WIPO Arbitration and Mediation Center, with three modifications calibrated to the TK context: (a) panels include at least one arbitrator nominated by the registered indigenous-community claimant and at least one arbitrator nominated through the relevant national or regional indigenous representative body; (b) arbitration proceedings are conducted in the language of the registered claimant community at the claimant's option, with translation provided through the Fund Secretariat; (c) appellate review is available to a standing appellate body within the WIPO architecture, with parallel referral available to the CBD Compliance Committee for systemic questions exceeding the bilateral dispute.[^156] Costs of arbitration are funded by the contributing-firm side, modeled on the Investor-State Dispute Settlement architecture's defendant-state cost provisions, with hardship exceptions for indigenous-community claimants who cannot bear cost-shifting.

The dispute-resolution mechanism is the component most likely to be contested by potential contributing firms; it is also the component without which the Article's proposal cannot realistically operate. A regulatory architecture that generates contested allocations and contested attestations without a credible adjudication mechanism produces either non-compliance (firms decline to participate because dispute exposure is unbounded) or capture (the Fund Secretariat's discretionary decisions become the operational architecture, with predictable distributional consequences). The proposed architecture distributes adjudication authority across multiple institutional loci (WIPO arbitration, CBD compliance, national-court enforcement of the underlying attestation obligations) in a deliberate effort to prevent any single institutional capture from determining outcomes.

The distribution-algorithm volume problem requires its own architectural treatment alongside the dispute-resolution mechanism. Music PROs distribute royalties to millions of rightsholders annually using standard formulas (radio play counts, streaming logs); the corresponding scale for a Performance Rights Organization for Traditional Knowledge would be substantially smaller in transaction count (perhaps thousands of registered communities, hundreds of contributing firms, dozens of major distribution events per year in the early years of operation) but substantially more contested per transaction. The proposal addresses this through a tiered architecture. Distributions below a threshold (proposed initially at \$50,000 per attribution event) flow automatically through the algorithm, with rebuttable presumption against any contesting party who failed to pre-register objection. Distributions between \$50,000 and \$500,000 flow through a streamlined administrative determination by the Fund Secretariat, with binding effect absent an arbitration appeal within 90 days. Distributions above \$500,000 trigger the full WIPO Arbitration architecture described above. The tiered structure is calibrated to keep dispute-resolution costs proportional to distribution stakes, modeled on analogous tiered architectures in international commercial arbitration and trademark enforcement.

### D. Execution Path and the Domestic-Legislation Predicate

The proposal is structured for a ten-year buildout, with realistic interim-assessment provisions rather than the more aggressive five-year trajectory the original concept might suggest. Year one: the CBD Secretariat, working with WIPO and India's TKDL, establishes the TKPR's technical specifications and onboards India's existing TKDL corpus. Year two: pilot integrations with the FDA, EMA, and EU agricultural-biotech regulator for use-based attestation at the Investigational New Drug, marketing-authorization, and registration checkpoints. Years three through five: staged onboarding of TK-holding communities in the Pacific Community, the Andean Community, and the African Union, with the existing Cali Fund Secretariat as administrative anchor. Years six through eight: deposition-side metadata-tagging implementation at GenBank, the European Nucleotide Archive, and the DNA Data Bank of Japan, requiring coordination with the host institutions (NIH, EMBL-EBI, and Japan's National Institute of Genetics) and with the major scientific-publisher associations. Year ten: full operation, with the use-based trigger replacing (in the operational sense; the formal Cali Fund decision text would require COP amendment) the sectoral-revenue trigger as the principal mechanism of contribution.

Minimum viable implementation. The proposal need not launch as a full global architecture. A minimum viable version would proceed in four phases: first, a voluntary TKPR pilot with India's TKDL and a small set of participating communities, including metadata tagging for registered provenance-bearing inputs; second, standardized attestations in patent, FDA/EMA, public-grant, and journal-disclosure contexts; third, audit-chain integration with major DSI repositories and model developers for tagged inputs; and fourth, an attribution-weighted levy channelled through the existing Cali Fund and MPTFO infrastructure. This staged version tests the registry, attestation, audit, and distribution functions separately before asking the full regime to operate at scale.

The execution timeline depends on a domestic-legislation predicate that the proposal must acknowledge candidly. In the United States, the regulatory-checkpoint attestation obligation at FDA filings would require either (a) FDA rulemaking under existing statutory authority (likely under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act § 505 for new drug applications and corresponding provisions for biologics), or (b) Congressional amendment to add the disclosure obligation explicitly. The FDA rulemaking pathway is administratively available but politically contested; the Congressional pathway is politically more demanding but produces clearer authority. In the European Union, the corresponding architecture would require either Commission delegated regulation under the Clinical Trials Regulation (EU) 536/2014 or the Pharmaceutical Strategy for Europe instruments, or new primary legislation. In agricultural-biotech jurisdictions, the regulatory architecture varies by jurisdiction (EFSA at the EU level, USDA APHIS in the United States) and would require corresponding implementing measures.

The Article does not pretend that the legislative pathway is uncontested or that the political coalition for these implementing measures is presently assembled. The proposal advances the architectural design that would be operative if the political coalition were assembled; it acknowledges that assembling the coalition is the precondition rather than the consequence of the architectural commitment. Where interim targets are not met, the architecture pivots to a more modest pilot-only phase pending further evidence of feasibility: a pivot at year three based on TKPR coverage falling below 25 percent of major TK-holding states; a pivot at year five based on regulatory-checkpoint adoption falling below 30 percent of contribution-eligible firms in the FDA and EMA jurisdictions. The pivot mechanism preserves the architecture's institutional infrastructure while acknowledging the limits of what political consensus can produce within any given time window.

The political-economy fallback is securities disclosure rather than approval-blocking. If FDA or EMA checkpoint adoption stalls, the same architecture can operate as a second-best SEC disclosure track: contribution-eligible issuers would disclose in Form 10-K, Form 20-F, or a specialized-disclosure channel whether material R&D programs rely on TKPR-attributable DSI and whether the issuer has adopted a Cali Fund contribution policy. That route would not condition drug approval and would therefore capture less value than the FDA/EMA checkpoint, but it would create investor-pressure, market, and reputational consequences around non-disclosure or misleading disclosure. The fallback matters because pharmaceutical lobbying may make approval-stage attestation the hardest domestic predicate; a securities-law pilot would test the disclosure, audit, and reputational-pressure mechanics before the architecture attempts to attach the same obligation to IND or marketing-authorization filings.

### E. Funding the Buildout

Realistic estimates for a TKDL-scale registry buildout in five years run to the high tens of millions of dollars, principally for technical infrastructure, community-engagement support, and audit-chain construction. India's TKDL itself cost approximately \$2 million to establish over its first decade and operates on an annual budget in the low single-digit millions of dollars; a globally federated TKPR architecture with regional implementing partners and audit-chain integration would scale that figure by an order of magnitude.[^157] The natural funders are the Global Environment Facility, the existing Multi-Partner Trust Fund Office hosting the Cali Fund, and a coalition of biotechnology-firm donors whose buy-in (driven by the prospect of a rationalized compliance regime that replaces the present uncertain reputational exposure) is the proposal's principal political precondition. The Article does not pretend that this coalition is presently assembled; it proposes the architectural design that would warrant the coalition's investment if assembled.

### F. Why This Complements, Rather Than Replaces, the Cali Fund

The proposal is not a Cali Fund alternative. It is a re-trigger and re-channeling of the Fund. The Fund's institutional infrastructure --- the Multi-Partner Trust Fund Office host, the UNDP-UNEP partnership, the CBD Secretariat secretariat --- would remain in place, and the fifty-percent-to-IPLCs disbursement floor would remain. What changes is the trigger (from indicative sectoral revenue with size thresholds, to use-based attribution through TKPR query logs, metadata exposure, and regulatory attestations) and the distribution algorithm (from fund-to-IPLC general allocation, to attribution-weighted disbursement to specific registered communities). The COP-decision text amendment that the proposal would require is, in legal-architecture terms, an evolutionary modification of Decision 16/2 rather than an institutional replacement.[^158]

## VII. Counterarguments

### A. "Validation Still Requires Physical Access"

The first counterargument acknowledges the technological capabilities the Article documents but contends that the validation phase of any candidate compound --- toxicity testing, pharmacokinetics, efficacy in animal models, and ultimately human trials --- still requires physical material that, in many cases, ultimately traces to the source organism. The trigger architecture, on this view, retains its purchase at the validation stage: the firm that succeeds *in silico* still needs *in vivo* validation, and the validation step engages a physical-access transaction that Nagoya can in principle catch.

The counterargument is the strongest in the set the Article must address, and is partially correct. Many present-day drug-discovery workflows do still involve physical material at the validation stage; the proposal does not maintain otherwise. The proposal's claim is more carefully bounded: the architecture should trigger on *discovery*, not on *validation*. By the time the validation phase begins, the firm has already extracted the informational value the architecture is meant to channel benefits from --- the design, optimization, and pharmacokinetic-profiling work has been done, the candidate compound exists, and the bargaining position vis-à-vis the source community is structurally asymmetric: the firm holds the molecule; the community holds, at most, the material from which the *next* iteration might be derived. The validation-stage transaction is therefore not the architectural moment that should fire the trigger; it is the moment at which the trigger fires too late, after the value has already been captured. The trend in heterologous expression in standard model organisms (yeast, *E. coli*, Chinese Hamster Ovary cells) is, moreover, accelerating the displacement of source-organism material even from the validation stage; the architecture should be designed for the trajectory the technology is on, not for the residual present-day exceptions.

### B. "ASCAP/BMI Doesn't Translate Because PRO Licensing Is Voluntary"

The second counterargument focuses on the analogy itself. Music PROs are voluntary organizations on the rightsholder side; songwriters and publishers join because membership is more valuable than non-membership in a market that rewards licensed exploitation. Traditional-knowledge-holding communities cannot replicate the voluntarism: their knowledge is being used regardless of any organizational decision they make, and the proposal's architecture therefore involves a category mismatch.

The counterargument has the analogy half right. The proposal does not transplant ASCAP/BMI's voluntarism on the rightsholder side; it inverts the analogy on that dimension, with mandatory user-side obligation and active rightsholder-side registration. Section VI.A acknowledged this expressly. The deeper question the counterargument raises is whether the proposal's mandatory user-side obligation is enforceable in the absence of a domestic-legislation infrastructure analogous to U.S. copyright law's compulsory-license provisions for musical works. The answer is that domestic-legislation infrastructure does not yet exist in operational form for DSI-use, but that the Cali Fund's COP16 architecture supplies the multilateral foundation, and that the proposal's five-year buildout includes the buildout of the corresponding domestic implementation.

### C. "AI Produces Analogues, Not the Original Compound --- So There Is No 'Use' of TK"

The third counterargument is doctrinal and goes to the heart of the trigger argument. If the firm produces a chemically novel compound, even one inspired by traditional-knowledge-derived bioactivity, the resulting molecule is not the traditional compound. There is, on this view, no "use" of traditional knowledge in any sense the architecture is meant to reach.

The counterargument confuses *what is patented* with *what was used*. The patent claim, drafted on the novel analogue, asserts ownership of the analogue. The pipeline that produced the analogue used, as its starting input, the bioactivity insight that the traditional-knowledge-holding community made available to the world over centuries of accumulated practice --- encoded in the public ethnobotanical and biomedical literature, in the public DSI repositories that contain the source organism's sequence, and in the proprietary chemistry databases that have themselves drawn, however indirectly, on the same body of accumulated knowledge. The Nagoya Protocol's underlying premise --- that benefit-sharing arises from the *utilization* of genetic resources, where utilization is defined in Article 2 as "research and development on the genetic and/or biochemical composition of genetic resources" --- reaches the upstream activity even where the downstream patent claim does not.[^159] The GRATK Treaty's "based on" language is meant to capture the same intuition. The proposal extends the same intuition into the AI-mediated synthesis pipeline by defining "use" functionally --- query, download, materially relied upon --- and tying the resulting obligation to the audit chain rather than to the patent claim's facial language.

### D. "Tragedy-of-the-Commons in Fund Collection Without Property Rights"

The fourth counterargument is structural. Music PROs work because copyright law gives songwriters enforceable property rights against unlicensed performers; the threat of infringement litigation is what makes the licensing market function. The proposal abandons the property-rights premise (consistent with the *Myriad* doctrine that information as such is not property) and substitutes a fund-contribution architecture. Without enforceable property rights, the counterargument runs, firms have no incentive to comply, no way to police each other, and no market mechanism to drive prices to the right level.

The counterargument is correct in its premise and wrong in its conclusion. The proposal does abandon the property-rights premise, by design; the *Myriad* doctrine (and its conceptual ancestors) make the property-rights route doctrinally unavailable for sequence information considered as information. The conclusion that nothing else is enforceable does not follow. The audit-chain architecture described in VI.C.4 is the operational substitute for enforcement-by-litigation: regulatory-checkpoint attestation backed by false-statement liability, sectoral random-sample audit, and cryptographic query-log integrity. None of these is the equivalent of copyright enforcement. They are sufficient to capture most of the value flow, where the present architecture captures essentially none of it.

### E. The Voluntary-Use Problem: Why Would a Firm Query the Registry?

The most fundamental architectural objection the proposal must confront is one that the audit-chain architecture in VI.C.4 does not, on its face, answer. A rational firm, aware that its use of TKPR-tagged DSI will trigger contribution obligations and audit exposure, may simply *not query the registry*. It can train its AI structural-prediction and generative-chemistry models on aggregated public data without distinct TKPR queries; it can route DSI access through unaffiliated third parties or subsidiaries domiciled in non-implementing jurisdictions; it can, in the limit, construct its own internal database of public sequence data and dispense with TKPR query-logs entirely. At that point, the use-based trigger does not fire because the firm has avoided the conduct that defines it. The architecture's reach collapses to the firms that voluntarily participate.

The objection is real. The proposal's response is twofold. First, the use-based trigger is paired with a downstream regulatory checkpoint attestation obligation: at the FDA Investigational New Drug filing, EMA marketing authorization, USDA agricultural-biotech registration, or analogous downstream gateway, the firm must affirmatively disclose whether its R&D pipeline materially used registered DSI, TK documentation, named traditional-use bioactivity, natural-product compound information, or biosynthetic pathway records traceable through the TKPR --- a question framed not in terms of whether the firm queried TKPR but in terms of whether the resulting compound is informed by provenance-bearing inputs that TKPR documents. The disclosure obligation is structured as an affirmative compliance burden of the kind familiar from FDA application false-statement liability and from the supply-chain-transparency disclosure regimes (conflict minerals, forced labor) of the past decade. The firm that has avoided querying the registry but has used the underlying provenance-bearing input is therefore exposed at the regulatory-checkpoint layer.

The honest acknowledgment here is that this layer of the proposal is the most novel and the least precedented. The conflict-minerals and supply-chain-transparency regimes that supply the disclosure-obligation analogy are themselves of recent vintage and contested effectiveness; the patent-examination incentive layer is a tax-and-incentive innovation that would require domestic legislation in major IP jurisdictions to operationalize. The proposal commits to neither the easy claim that firms will naturally comply nor the harder claim that mandatory regimes are politically feasible in the short term.

A more aggressive architectural option deserves explicit articulation. The voluntary-use problem can be substantially attenuated by introducing two mandatory elements at the upstream sequence-deposition layer rather than at the downstream disclosure layer. First, a *deposition-side metadata-tagging* requirement: the major public DSI repositories (GenBank, the European Nucleotide Archive, the DNA Data Bank of Japan) could be required, through coordination with their host institutions and funding agencies, to attach TK-provenance metadata to deposited sequences whose source organisms appear in registered traditional-knowledge claims. The metadata would travel with the sequence regardless of how subsequently downloaded or queried; a firm that obtains the sequence by any route inherits the metadata burden. Second, a *publication-side disclosure* requirement: scientific journals (with the major publishers' cooperation, modeled on the existing data-availability statement requirements introduced over the past decade) could require that authors of papers reporting on synthetic functional analogues whose underlying targets trace to TK-derived bioactivity disclose the TK-attribution chain. Both elements convert the voluntary-use architecture into a mandatory-tagging architecture without requiring firm-level compliance from the downstream user; the burden shifts to the upstream infrastructure where coordination is more tractable.

Even with these upstream mechanisms, the voluntary-use problem is not eliminated; a firm sufficiently determined to evade the regime can construct a private DSI database from un-tagged sources and avoid the journal-disclosure requirement by foregoing publication. The proposal commits to the architectural design that makes voluntary compliance *more* likely than the present trigger architecture (which the empirical record demonstrates produces near-zero compliance), to the upstream-tagging architecture that extends the regime's reach beyond firm-level voluntary participation, and to the structural framework within which subsequent mandatory-regime evolution can occur.

### F. Bavikatte's Inalienability Critique: TK as Biocultural Right

Kabir Bavikatte's framework of biocultural rights, developed across his 2014 monograph *Stewarding the Earth* and a body of associated scholarship, supplies a critique that the proposal must take seriously and that goes beyond the standard property-versus-liability-rule literature.[^160] The biocultural-rights framework treats indigenous and local communities' relationships to their ancestral knowledge and territories as *inalienable* --- not in the formal-legal sense in which inalienable entitlements appear in the *Calabresi--Melamed* taxonomy, but in the constitutive sense that the knowledge cannot be separated from the community's ongoing identity, governance, and relationship to land without doing violence to the community itself. On Bavikatte's framework, the proposal's reduction of TK to a registry entry, an attribution-weighted distribution claim, and a royalty-flow mechanism is itself a category mistake: it treats the inalienable as fungible, the relational as transactional, and the constitutive as commercially valuable.

The critique is the most fundamental of the ten the Article addresses, and it is substantially correct in what it identifies as the proposal's normative posture. The proposal does treat TK in the modality of attribution and compensation; it does not treat TK in the modality of relational-constitutive identity. The Article's response is not to deny the critique but to delimit the proposal's claim. The proposal does not contend that attribution-and-compensation is the *complete* account of what is owed to TK-holding communities for the use of their knowledge; it contends that, in the architectural domain the proposal addresses (the AI-mediated synthesis pipeline that the existing trigger architecture cannot reach), attribution-and-compensation is the available institutional mechanism that addresses the value-flow question. The relational-constitutive dimensions of biocultural rights --- community governance over what is registered, what is shared, and on what terms; community veto over uses incompatible with cultural integrity; community participation in the dispute-resolution architecture --- are preserved within the proposal's design. The TKPR's registration is community-controlled; the dispute-resolution mechanism includes community-nominated arbitrators conducting proceedings in community languages; the priority-flow of distributions to indigenous and local communities is preserved at the Cali Fund's existing fifty-percent floor. What the proposal does not do is convert these dimensions into the *primary* architectural mechanism --- because the architectural diagnosis in Part II demonstrates that the primary mechanism, given the technological substrate, must operate at the use-detection and value-flow level, not at the consent-and-relationship level. The Bavikatte critique is correct that the proposal is incomplete; it does not establish that the proposal is wrong about the architectural domain it addresses.

### G. The Information-Cost Critique: Henry Smith and the Numerus Clausus

Henry Smith and Tom Merrill's body of property-theory scholarship advances an information-cost defense of the *numerus clausus* principle: the legal system permits only a limited menu of property forms because each new form imposes information costs on every market participant who must subsequently identify and evaluate the entitlements they encounter.[^161] The proposal, on this critique, creates a new form of legal entitlement --- a registry-recorded provenance interest in TK-derived bioactivity, enforced through audit-chain attestation --- that imposes information costs on every firm operating in the affected sectors and on the regulatory infrastructure that must operationalize the audit chain. The aggregate information cost may, the critique runs, exceed the aggregate value the entitlement creates.

The information-cost critique is theoretically rigorous and operationally relevant. The proposal's response operates on three levels. First, the *numerus clausus* principle is a default in favor of existing property forms, not a prohibition on new ones; the principle's analytical work is to require that proponents of new forms justify the additional information cost by reference to the value the new form creates. The architectural diagnosis in Part II is precisely such a justification: the existing forms (Nagoya bilateral negotiation, GRATK patent disclosure, Cali Fund sectoral levy) demonstrably do not capture the value flow at issue; the proposed form is calibrated to do so. Second, the information-cost burden the proposal imposes is bounded: firms that do not operate in the contribution-eligible sectors face zero compliance obligation; firms that do are required to maintain compliance infrastructure they already maintain for parallel disclosure regimes (conflict minerals, supply-chain transparency, ESG reporting); the marginal information cost is therefore meaningfully smaller than a fresh-form analysis would suggest. Third, the audit-chain architecture is designed to externalize information-cost into a Fund Secretariat infrastructure that is funded by the contribution stream itself, not by the contributing firms in the form of compliance burden. The information cost is real but reasonably contained within the architectural design.

### H. The Antitrust Irony: PROs Are Themselves Perpetual Antitrust Defendants

Subsection VI.B addressed the eight decades of Department of Justice consent-decree supervision of ASCAP and BMI as an instructive precedent for the proposal's design. A counterargument runs in the opposite direction: the ASCAP/BMI experience demonstrates that the music-PRO architecture is itself an antitrust concern requiring perpetual regulatory oversight, and the proposal's invocation of the model recreates the concentration problem the Article diagnoses at the platform layer. To propose a Performance Rights Organization for Traditional Knowledge --- a single entity (or federated set of entities) holding licensing authority across an entire domain of bioactivity inputs --- is to create at the registry layer a concentration whose mirror image is precisely what Part V identifies as the principal pathology at the AI-platform layer.

The irony is real and the proposal must address it. The architectural response is twofold. First, the proposed Performance Rights Organization is institutionally federated rather than centralized: regional implementing partners (TKDL in India, Pacific Community in Oceania, Andean Community in South America, African Union in Africa) operate the registry within their respective regions, with the global TKPR functioning as an interoperability protocol rather than as a centralized licensing authority. The federation reduces the concentration risk by distributing licensing authority across multiple institutional loci; it preserves the operational coherence the use-based trigger requires by establishing common technical specifications and dispute-resolution architecture. Second, the proposal explicitly contemplates the parallel application of competition-policy oversight to the Performance Rights Organization itself, drawing on the DOJ--ASCAP--BMI consent-decree precedent to design ex ante the regulatory-supervision architecture the music PROs developed only ex post. Specifically: non-exclusive licensing (firms may negotiate directly with TK-holding communities outside the collective architecture if both sides prefer); rate-court oversight for disputed contribution rates; mandatory transparency in distribution algorithms; and supermajority voting requirements within the registry's governance for decisions affecting registered communities. The cumulative effect is an architecture that internalizes the antitrust-supervision lessons the music-PRO experience makes available, rather than reproducing the music-PRO experience and discovering the antitrust concerns only after eight decades of operation.

I. Regime Complexity: Raustiala and Victor

Kal Raustiala and David Victor's analysis of "regime complexity" supplies a further critique that the proposal must engage.[^162] On their account, the proliferation of overlapping international institutions in any given policy domain produces fragmentation costs (rule conflicts, forum-shopping by powerful actors, weakening of any individual institution's authority) that frequently exceed the benefits of any individual institutional addition. The international IP-and-biodiversity domain is, on the Raustiala--Victor analysis, already deeply complex: WIPO, WTO, CBD, FAO, WHO, ITPGRFA, UNESCO, and the regional and bilateral architectures all assert overlapping authority; the Cali Fund and the GRATK Treaty have added two further layers in the past three years; the proposal advanced in this Article would add a third. The marginal cost of additional institutional complexity may, on Raustiala and Victor's framework, exceed the marginal benefit.

The critique deserves a serious response. The proposal's response is that the architecture it proposes is not an *additional* institution but a *re-channeling* of an existing one. The Cali Fund, with its institutional infrastructure (Multi-Partner Trust Fund Office host, UNDP--UNEP partnership, CBD Secretariat secretariat), is preserved; the WIPO IGC's negotiating apparatus continues to operate the GRATK Treaty implementation; the regional implementing partners (TKDL, Pacific Community, Andean Community, African Union) operate within their existing institutional architectures. The proposal modifies the trigger and the distribution algorithm of the Cali Fund and embeds the registry function within existing regional architectures; it does not propose a new international institution. The marginal regime-complexity cost is meaningfully smaller than the Raustiala--Victor critique would suggest if the proposal were reaching for a fresh institutional form.

J. The Romance of the Public Domain: Chander and Sunder

The most theoretically pointed counterargument the Article addresses is the one Chander and Sunder articulate in *The Romance of the Public Domain*: any scheme that operates on the "open to all" status of TK-derived information operates on a fiction that conceals the substantive distributional asymmetry between well-resourced extractors and resource-poor communities.[^163] The proposal, the critique runs, operates within the same fiction: by accepting the open-repository status of DSI as the working substrate and proposing only a downstream attribution-and-compensation overlay, the proposal leaves intact the upstream architecture that produces the asymmetric distribution in the first place.

The critique is correct in identifying the proposal's architectural choice and incorrect in characterizing it as an unexamined choice. The proposal accepts the open-repository status of DSI for two reasons. First, the doctrinal route of re-enclosure is foreclosed: *Myriad* and its analogs prevent the conversion of natural sequence information into property rights, and no available legal architecture would re-enclose deposited DSI even where the political will to do so existed. Second, the operational benefits of the open-repository system --- accelerated scientific research, replicability of biological findings, cross-disciplinary integration of biological data --- are real, and the global health and food-security gains the open-repository system has enabled over the past two decades are not gains the Article would willingly forgo. The proposal therefore does not reject the Chander--Sunder critique; it accepts the substantive distributional asymmetry the critique identifies and proposes a regulatory mechanism that operates on the asymmetry through a Rule 2 liability architecture, channeling the value flow that the open-repository system enables to the communities that the open-repository system would otherwise leave uncompensated. The proposal is, in this sense, a partial-compensation mechanism rather than a structural-correction mechanism. The critique is correct that a more complete structural correction would address the upstream architecture as well as the downstream attribution; the Article's response is that the partial correction the proposal advances is the available correction, that the more complete structural correction is not on offer in any politically feasible institutional form, and that the partial correction is meaningfully better than the empirical zero the existing architecture produces.

K. "The Cali Fund Already Does This"

The final counterargument is the one most likely to come from policymakers presently invested in the Cali Fund's success. The Fund, this argument runs, *is* the multilateral mechanism the international community has agreed to; its first year is a buildout phase, not a verdict; and proposing a structural alternative is premature and politically counterproductive.

The counterargument is half right and half wrong in a way that the proposal addresses directly. It is half right that the Cali Fund is the operative architecture, that political capital should not be wasted on premature alternatives, and that the implementing institutions deserve a longer runway than a single year before being declared failures. It is half wrong in characterizing the proposal as an alternative. Section VI.F was explicit on this point: the proposal preserves the Cali Fund's institutional infrastructure (MPTFO host, UNDP--UNEP partnership, CBD Secretariat secretariat, fifty-percent-to-IPLCs floor) and modifies the trigger and the distribution algorithm. The COP-decision-text amendment required is evolutionary, not revolutionary, and the buildout pathway runs through (rather than around) the existing institutions. The longer the Cali Fund operates with its present trigger architecture, the more the empirical record will document what its first year has already begun to document: that the architecture is not capturing the activity it was designed to capture. The proposal is the reform that the Fund's empirical record, on its own terms, suggests will be needed.

## VIII. Conclusion

This Article has documented a pattern: regulatory architectures lag the informational technologies they purport to govern. The pattern is not unique to the Nagoya Protocol or the Cali Fund or the GRATK Treaty. It is the pattern that produced the Digital Millennium Copyright Act in response to the internet-copyright pressures of the 1990s, just before the Napster-style peer-to-peer explosion; FinCEN's virtual-currency guidance just over four years after Bitcoin's January 2009 launch; and the present global scramble to retrofit AI-governance architectures around foundation models that are already deployed. What is distinctive in the present case is that the mismatch is visible in the public record of an architecture's first months of operation. The Cali Fund's one-thousand-dollar first contribution is not proof, by itself, of architectural obsolescence. It is a clean signal that invites the structural inquiry this Article has undertaken.

The June 2025 Nature Medicine publication of Insilico Medicine's Rentosertib Phase IIa trial --- the first clinical proof-of-concept for an AI-discovered drug --- closes one empirical-capability question that the architectural critique had previously treated as forecast: the broader AI drug-discovery pipeline can reach clinical proof-of-concept. The integration with traditional-medicine knowledge bases is an active and documented research program. Some pathway-reconstruction settings make DSI-specific bypass technically plausible. The regulatory architecture generally imposes no freestanding duty to disclose AI use, TK-derived scaffolds, or provenance-bearing inputs unless existing patentability, inventorship, regulatory, or materiality doctrine independently makes those facts relevant. The platform consolidation has produced deal values and capital commitments that exceed the Cali Fund's public contribution record by orders of magnitude. The architectural critique is therefore no longer a speculative warning about distant capability. It is an attribution problem attached to a maturing operating environment. The forest-and-trees diagnosis that Imasogie and Lai applied to the COVID-vaccine intellectual-property-waiver debate translates, with modification, to the present architecture.[^164] The proposed compulsory licensing of vaccine patents addressed a tree (patent rights) while the binding constraint operated through the forest (manufacturing know-how and infrastructure). The Cali Fund's sectoral-revenue trigger addresses a tree (visible downstream commercial revenue in defined sectors) while the binding constraint operates through the forest (the upstream aggregation of DSI, traditional-use documentation, natural-product chemistry, structural prediction, and generative design that produces value before attribution has occurred). A reform that addresses only the visible tree will not capture the value flow it is meant to capture. A reform that addresses the architectural forest --- by relocating the trigger to the provenance-bearing activity that produces the value --- has a chance.

The comparative-jurisprudential implications of the diagnosis extend beyond the ABS architecture. The pattern this Article identifies --- a transaction-or-physical-access trigger architecture rendered incomplete by an AI-mediated synthesis pipeline that operates from public informational substrates --- is, in structural terms, the pattern of regulatory failure now visible across multiple domains in which AI capability has accelerated past the institutional capacity to retrofit governance architectures designed for pre-AI activity. The pharmaceutical-regulatory architecture at the FDA and the EMA is grappling with analogous questions in the context of AI-driven drug discovery; the financial-regulatory architecture at the SEC and FINRA is grappling with analogous questions in the context of AI-driven trading and underwriting; the labor-and-employment architecture at the EEOC and European data-protection authorities is grappling with analogous questions in the context of AI-driven hiring and workplace monitoring. The structural lesson the present Article advances is that regulatory architectures designed around physical, transactional, or sectoral triggers must, in the post-AI period, be retrofitted toward use-based and audit-chain triggers where the regulated value is produced before any visible event occurs. The Performance Rights Organization for Traditional Knowledge is one application of that lesson.

Whether the proposal advanced in Part VI is the right structural correction is, ultimately, a question the empirical record of its operation would have to answer. The argument of this Article is the prior one: that the present architecture requires structural correction, that the corrections so far proposed (treaty amendment to add DSI; Cali-Fund-implementation acceleration; expanded ratification of the GRATK Treaty) are insufficient to the attribution problem, and that the design space for the correction includes the Performance-Rights-Organization analog the Article has sketched. A falsifiable empirical condition for that argument is available. If Cali Fund contributions exceed one hundred million U.S. dollars cumulatively in the Fund's second year of operation (March 2026 through February 2027), the operational performance may justify patience with the existing trigger architecture --- the empirical signal will then be that contributions are slow but real, that the threshold mechanism is engaging, and that structural reform should be deferred until clearer failure modes are observed. If contributions remain below ten million dollars cumulatively over the same period, the case for the structural reform this Article proposes becomes urgent rather than merely available. The Cali Fund's first contribution is one thousand U.S. dollars from a startup that did not appear to meet the indicative contribution thresholds. The architecture that produced that public record is the architecture that needs to be examined. The examination is overdue.
