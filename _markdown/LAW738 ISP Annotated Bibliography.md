## Abstract

This Log inventories, annotates, and operationalizes the primary statutory provisions, judicial decisions, and policy debates that govern the construction — and the failure — of bankruptcy-remote special-purpose entities ("SPEs") in U.S. structured finance. Its purpose is twofold. First, it serves as a verifiable independent-study product reflecting sustained engagement with the assigned course materials beyond attendance. Second, it functions as a topic-indexed, exam-deployable companion to the four-hour, in-person, open-book final examination administered by Professor Yarett in this course. The Log departs from a conventional narrative paper in three respects: (i) every authority is annotated under a uniform heading template (Holding / Application Matrix / Policy); (ii) annotations are organized to mirror the doctrinal sequence in which an SPE-related fact pattern unfolds at trial — *first* property of the estate, *then* automatic stay, *then* substantive consolidation, *then* perfection; and (iii) a fourth, integrative section applies the three doctrinal clusters to the whole-business securitization ("WBS") asset classes Professor Yarett has flagged as central to the 2026 sitting (franchise royalties, fiber, drug royalties, intellectual property).

The Log treats four doctrinal clusters: **(I)** the *true sale* doctrine and its statutory anchors in Bankruptcy Code § 541 and UCC § 9-109(a)(3); **(II)** the equitable doctrine of *substantive consolidation* and the structural-contractual mechanisms designed to defeat it, including the post-*General Growth* "fix" and non-petition covenants; **(III)** *UCC Article 9 perfection* in WBS, with attention to the anti-assignment override (§§ 9-406(d), 9-408) that distinguishes U.S. WBS from civil-law analogues; and **(IV)** the integrative application of all three doctrines to representative WBS deals (Wendy's, Driven Brands, Quiznos, fiber, pharmaceutical royalties).

This Log assumes familiarity with the basic Honda-style two-tier deal structure (originator → SPE-1 → SPE-2/Trust → noteholders), the four standard credit-enhancement mechanisms (subordination, excess spread, reserve account, overcollateralization), and the seven closing opinions ordinarily delivered in a U.S. ABS transaction. Where this Log refers to "WBS," it means a securitization in which a substantial portion of an operating company's revenue-generating asset base — typically including intellectual property, franchise rights, and operating contracts — is contributed to a bankruptcy-remote SPE as part of a financing in which the operating company continues to manage the assets in a servicer-like capacity.

# § I. THE "TRUE SALE" DOCTRINE

## I.A. Conceptual Frame

The true-sale doctrine asks, on the facts of an originator's bankruptcy, whether assets transferred to an SPE were *sold* (in which case they never became "property of the estate" under § 541, and § 362 cannot reach them) or whether the transaction was a *secured loan in form of a sale*, in which case the receivables remain estate property and the SPE's claim is reduced to the rights of a secured creditor — automatic stay, cash-collateral fights, valuation disputes, possible cramdown. There is no bright-line federal rule. Federal bankruptcy courts apply a multi-factor, totality-of-circumstances test, with state-law variation around the edges. The factor most consistently treated as dispositive is *recourse* — the allocation of credit risk between transferor and transferee.

Three structural points orient the analysis.

**First, sale and security interest are not mutually exclusive in form.** UCC § 9-109(a)(3) extends Article 9 to sales of accounts, chattel paper, payment intangibles, and promissory notes. The presence of a financing statement filed by the transferee is therefore not, by itself, evidence that the parties intended a secured loan; it is what UCC counsel does *out of an abundance of caution* in any sale of those asset categories.

**Second, "true sale for bankruptcy" and "true sale for tax" are independent inquiries with different factor weights.** A transaction can be a sale for bankruptcy purposes (so the assets are out of the originator's estate) and a financing for tax purposes (so the originator does not recognize gain). Counsel routinely engineer this combination.

**Third, the consequences of recharacterization run not only to the legal status of the receivables but to the rated tranches.** Every modern true-sale opinion is a condition precedent to investment-grade rating. Rating agencies require the opinion at the "would" level (the higher of the two diligence standards). If the opinion proves wrong post-closing, the practical consequence is a rating-driven default on the notes long before any judicial recharacterization is finally adjudicated.

## I.B. Statutory Anchors

> **AUTHORITY** — 11 U.S.C. § 541 (property of the estate).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** Section 541(a) provides that the bankruptcy estate is comprised of "all legal or equitable interests of the debtor in property as of the commencement of the case." The estate is determined by reference to property *interests*, not formal title; transfers that are recharacterized as financings remain estate property despite formal conveyance.
>
> **Application Matrix.** The structure of any true-sale analysis runs through § 541 first. If the SPE owns the receivables, § 541 is not implicated; § 362 cannot reach them; the SPE's noteholders are paid out of cash flow they own. If recharacterization succeeds, the receivables enter the estate under § 541; § 362 stays collection; the SPE's "ownership" is collapsed to a § 506 secured claim.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The § 541 framework reflects a general bankruptcy-law preference for substance over form, but securitization advances a competing bankruptcy-policy: that creditors who have bargained for asset isolation in advance of distress should receive the benefit of their bargain. Schwarcz frames the resulting tension as a debate between *ex post* equity (re-pooling assets to maximize the value of the estate for unsecured creditors) and *ex ante* efficiency (allowing originators to obtain cheaper capital by separating credit risk). *See* Steven L. Schwarcz, *The Alchemy of Asset Securitization*, 1 Stan. J.L. Bus. & Fin. 133 (1994). The originator's unsecured trade creditors, who were never party to the securitization, supply the strongest distributional objection.

> **AUTHORITY** — 11 U.S.C. § 362 (automatic stay).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** Filing of a petition operates as a stay of "any act to obtain possession of property of the estate," § 362(a)(3), and "any act to create, perfect, or enforce any lien against property of the estate," § 362(a)(4). The stay is automatic and bears the force of injunction.
>
> **Application Matrix.** Critically, § 362 stays acts directed at *property of the estate*. If true sale is respected, the receivables are not estate property; collection from obligors (consumers paying their auto loans, franchisees paying royalties) continues uninterrupted. If true sale fails, § 362 halts the cash-flow waterfall instantly — and even sophisticated structured-finance instruments cannot work around the stay, as the *LTV Steel* matter illustrates.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The stay's policy basis is the orderly preservation of estate value pending plan formulation. It is not a constraint securitization counsel can avoid by drafting; it is a constraint counsel avoids by structuring the transaction so that § 541 is not implicated.

> **AUTHORITY** — 11 U.S.C. § 363(c) (use of cash collateral).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** A debtor in possession may use cash collateral with the consent of the secured party or by court order conditioned on adequate protection. § 363(c)(2), 363(e).
>
> **Application Matrix.** Once recharacterization succeeds, the SPE's "owned" cash flow becomes the debtor's "cash collateral" subject to § 363(c). The debtor obtains continuing access to operating cash on adequate-protection grounds, while the noteholders are reduced to litigating the value of their lien — typically a slow and uncertain process. The post-recharacterization economics are dramatically worse for noteholders even where the underlying receivables are ultimately good.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The asymmetry between true-sale and recharacterized outcomes — particularly under § 363(c) — is what gives the recharacterization risk its outsized rating-agency salience. The rating-agency premium for the true-sale opinion is, in substance, a premium against the difference in expected loss between the two regimes.

> **AUTHORITY** — UCC § 9-109(a)(3).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** Article 9 applies to "a sale of accounts, chattel paper, payment intangibles, or promissory notes," in addition to its core scope governing security interests.
>
> **Application Matrix.** This provision is the source of the apparent paradox by which a *sale* of receivables nevertheless looks like a *secured transaction*: the transferee files a UCC-1 to perfect its interest, and the transferor is named as "seller/debtor." The provision exists because the line between sale and secured loan in receivables-financing is empirically difficult to draw, and uniform perfection rules protect third parties against either characterization.
>
> **Application — Critical.** Filing a UCC-1 does *not* defeat true sale. The cleanest formulations expressly state that the parties intended an absolute assignment and the filing is precautionary. *See* mock-exam Q1 fact pattern (UCC-1 naming "seller/debtor" / "purchaser/secured party" "out of an abundance of caution").
>
> **Policy Implications.** The 1998 revisions to Article 9 deliberately re-characterized the doctrinal contest. By extending the article to true sales of receivables and providing automatic perfection for sales of payment intangibles and promissory notes (§ 9-309(3), (4)), the drafters effectively neutralized one of the historical recharacterization arguments — that the transferee's failure to perfect "as a secured party" reflected the parties' real intent. *Octagon Gas* (10th Cir. 1993), discussed below, is best read as a casualty of the older regime.

## I.C. Case Annotations — True Sale Factor Test

> **AUTHORITY** — *Major's Furniture Mart, Inc. v. Castle Credit Corp.*, 602 F.2d 538 (3d Cir. 1979) [Garth, J.].
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** A transaction styled as a "sale" of accounts will be treated as a financing where the transferor retains substantially all the risks of ownership. The court enumerated factors that have since been condensed into the modern true-sale test, with particular weight on (i) recourse to the transferor for credit losses, (ii) the transferor's right to surplus from collections, (iii) pricing (fixed vs. floating, contingent on collections), (iv) the transferor's continued role in collection or management of the receivables, (v) the parties' books and records, and (vi) the substance of the parties' intent as evidenced by all the foregoing.
>
> **Application Matrix.**
>
> | Factor | Sale-leaning | Financing-leaning |
> |---|---|---|
> | Recourse for credit losses | None, or limited to representation-and-warranty breaches at sale | Recourse for collectability post-sale ("first-loss" absorption) |
> | Right to surplus | Surplus to noteholders; transferor takes only retained subordinated tranche | Transferor receives back any over-collection (analog of "right of redemption") |
> | Pricing | Fixed purchase price | Contingent on actual collections; floating rate |
> | Servicing/collection | Arm's-length servicing fee at market rate; replaceable on default | Transferor exercises uncompensated discretion over collection |
> | Books and records | Transferee carries receivables; transferor de-recognizes under GAAP | Transferor continues to carry receivables on balance sheet |
> | Notification of obligors | Notification (or right to notify) given to transferee | Continued unilateral control by transferor |
> | Commingling | Strict cash segregation, lockbox, daily sweep | Receivables cash mingled with operating cash |
> | Document language | "True sale, absolute assignment, not a pledge"; UCC-1 precautionary | Loan-style covenants (e.g., loan-to-value) |
>
> **Policy Implications.** The factor test is intentionally totality-based; it grants courts substantial discretion to reach equitable results in particular cases. Critics — including the rating agencies, who have driven much of the practitioner literature — argue that this discretion is the source of avoidable systemic uncertainty: the doctrine produces highly text-dependent outcomes that are difficult to price ex ante. Schwarcz suggests resolving the indeterminacy by codifying a true-sale safe harbor. *See* Steven L. Schwarcz, *Securitization Post-Enron*, 25 Cardozo L. Rev. 1539, 1569 (2004). The rating agencies have effectively done so privately by requiring "would"-level opinions and policing factor-test deviations through the credit-rating mechanism.

> **AUTHORITY** — *Endico Potatoes, Inc. v. CIT Group/Factoring, Inc.*, 67 F.3d 1063 (2d Cir. 1995).
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** The Second Circuit applied the same multi-factor test as *Major's Furniture Mart*, with particular emphasis on whether the transferee bore meaningful credit risk independent of the transferor's continued financial health. Where the transferor's recourse obligations were broad enough to absorb essentially all credit losses, the transaction was characterized as a financing notwithstanding sale-style documentation.
>
> **Application Matrix.** *Endico* sharpens the distinction between (a) representation-and-warranty indemnification — under which the transferor stands behind the *characteristics* of the receivables at the time of transfer (e.g., "each receivable was originated in compliance with applicable law and conforms to the credit underwriting guidelines disclosed in the offering memorandum") — and (b) credit recourse — under which the transferor stands behind the *future collectability* of the receivables. Only the latter is fatal to true sale. The 2026 mock examination's Question 1 turns on this distinction: the originator's R&W indemnification is sale-consistent, while the 8% first-loss absorption is fatal.
>
> **Policy Implications.** Endico demonstrates the practitioner doctrine that "the only safe harbor is no recourse, and the next-safest harbor is recourse for R&W breaches only." The case illustrates why structured-finance counsel will, in negotiated transactions, accept significant deal-reduction in exchange for trimming originator recourse — even at the cost of higher subordination or third-party credit enhancement.

> **AUTHORITY** — *Octagon Gas Sys., Inc. v. Rimmer*, 995 F.2d 948 (10th Cir. 1993).
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** In dictum, the Tenth Circuit suggested that the sale of accounts could leave the receivables as property of the bankrupt seller's estate, on the theory that the assignment of contract rights is governed by the law of the underlying contract and not by Article 9 alone. The reasoning was widely criticized and is generally regarded as superseded by the 1998 revisions to UCC § 9-109(a)(3), which clarified Article 9's coverage of sales of accounts.
>
> **Application Matrix.** *Octagon Gas* should be cited not as good law but as the doctrinal foil that prompted the modern Article 9 architecture. Its citation in an exam answer demonstrates awareness of the historical contest between the bankruptcy-law and commercial-law approaches to sale recharacterization.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The case stands as the strongest historical argument that the *Major's Furniture Mart* test was once doctrinally vulnerable, and that the 1998 Article 9 revisions resolved the ambiguity in favor of structured-finance practice. Critics of the resolution — particularly Levitin — read the 1998 revisions as a transactional-bar capture of commercial law to insulate post-1990s securitization from § 541 attack. *See* Adam J. Levitin, *The Paper Chase: Securitization, Foreclosure, and the Uncertainty of Mortgage Title*, 63 Duke L.J. 637 (2013).

> **AUTHORITY** — *In re LTV Steel Co.*, 274 B.R. 278 (Bankr. N.D. Ohio 2001).
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** Faced with a textbook-perfect securitization at the moment of the originator's Chapter 11 filing, the bankruptcy court entered an *interim* cash-collateral order treating the SPE-owned receivables as cash collateral of the debtor and authorizing LTV's continuing use of receivable cash flows. The order was never affirmed on appeal — the parties settled — but the order itself made the case the cautionary tale of post-1990s ABS practice.
>
> **Application Matrix.** *LTV Steel* sits in the practitioner literature as proof that even an opinion-supported, structurally-sound securitization can be functionally undone at the moment of filing if a court is willing to enter an interim order on equitable grounds. The case has produced a standard practice — known colloquially among ABS counsel as the "LTV section" — by which every modern true-sale opinion expressly notes the case, characterizes it as wrongly decided, and disclaims any inference that its reasoning could properly be applied to the deal at hand.
>
> **Policy Implications.** *LTV Steel* exemplifies the limits of contractual structuring in the face of *ex post* judicial discretion at the bankruptcy bar. Schwarcz reads the case as a problem of judicial education rather than a problem of doctrine: a court untrained in modern structured finance will reach for cash-collateral mechanisms as a default response to operating-distress facts, regardless of whether the cash flows are technically estate property. Critics, including Bratton and Levitin, read it instead as a salutary check on a transactional architecture that purports to remove receivables from the reach of all other creditors. *See* William W. Bratton & Adam J. Levitin, *A Transactional Genealogy of Scandal*, 86 S. Cal. L. Rev. 783 (2013). *LTV Steel* is the case that made the rating-agency demand for "would"-level true-sale opinions non-negotiable.

## I.D. Tax True-Sale — IRS Private Letter Ruling 9839001

> **AUTHORITY** — IRS Priv. Ltr. Rul. 9839001 (1998).
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** The IRS evaluated whether an auto-finance company's transfer of receivables to a trust issuing two classes of certificates (Class A to outside investors; Class B retained by the originator) was a *sale* or a *financing* for federal income-tax purposes. Treating the question as one of substance, the IRS applied an eight-factor test dominated by the allocation of risk-of-loss and potential-for-gain. The Class B certificates and a related spread account absorbed essentially all foreseeable losses, with the result that Class A was protected from realistic loss exposure. The IRS concluded the transaction was a *financing*, not a sale, for tax — a result favorable to the originator (no recognition of taxable gain on transfer).
>
> **Application Matrix.** The PLR demonstrates the planned divergence between bankruptcy true-sale and tax true-sale: the originator can structure to satisfy the bankruptcy true-sale factor test (true sale for § 541) while simultaneously satisfying the tax-financing factor test (no gain recognition). The two tests weigh recourse and risk allocation on different vectors, which is what enables the divergence.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The PLR is occasionally read as evidence of the inconsistency in U.S. asset-finance doctrine: the same transaction is treated as a sale for one purpose and a loan for another, with the originator capturing both upside characterizations. Defenders argue the divergence reflects the genuinely different functions the two doctrines perform — *ex ante* protection of unsecured creditors against estate stripping (bankruptcy) vs. *ex ante* protection of the fisc against unrecognized gain (tax) — and that the divergence is therefore principled rather than opportunistic.

## I.E. Synthesis — The Vulnerable Features of a True Sale

A modern transactional checklist for hardening true sale against recharacterization, derived from the foregoing authorities, includes:

(1) **No recourse for collectability.** R&W indemnification only; cap recourse at the level customary for the asset class (typically <2% of pool, often <1%).
(2) **Fixed purchase price.** No retroactive pricing adjustment based on actual collections.
(3) **Subordinated retained tranche, not a redemption right.** A retained subordinated certificate is sale-consistent; a right to repurchase a portion of the pool is not.
(4) **Arm's-length servicing.** Market-rate servicing fee; servicer replaceable on default.
(5) **Cash segregation.** Lockbox or trustee-controlled deposit account under UCC § 9-104 control agreement; daily sweep; no commingling with originator operating cash.
(6) **Books and records.** Originator de-recognizes the receivables under U.S. GAAP (or, post-FAS 166/167, under ASC 860); the transferee carries the asset.
(7) **Document language.** "True sale, absolute assignment, and not a pledge"; explicit recital of intent; "out of abundance of caution" UCC-1 filing.
(8) **No parent guaranty.** Defeats true sale and supplies a prong for substantive consolidation under *Owens Corning* (*see* § II below).
(9) **Standard "LTV section" in the true-sale opinion.** Disclaiming the *LTV Steel* result as wrongly decided.

The practitioner formula taught by Professor Yarett in the 2026 sitting is captured in this model line, drawn directly from the course materials: *"The transfer is a true sale, not a financing. Following* Major's Furniture Mart *(3d Cir. 1979), [Originator] gives only standard reps about characteristics at sale — breach triggers repurchase — but no representation as to future collectability and no buyback on delinquency. Fixed price; arm's-length servicing; no parent guaranty; segregated cash. Issuer's counsel will deliver a 'would'-level opinion including the standard LTV section."*

# § II. SUBSTANTIVE CONSOLIDATION & THE BANKRUPTCY-REMOTE / BANKRUPTCY-PROOF DISTINCTION

## II.A. Conceptual Frame

Substantive consolidation is the equitable doctrine by which a bankruptcy court combines the assets and liabilities of two or more nominally separate entities into a single estate. The doctrine is judge-made, not codified — neither the Bankruptcy Code nor the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure provides for it directly — and is widely described as "rough justice." For a securitization sponsor's creditors, substantive consolidation is the doctrine that converts a bankruptcy-remote SPE into a *bankruptcy-vulnerable* one: it operates on entities, not on transactions, and so it survives even an unimpeachable true sale.

The doctrine performs two distinct doctrinal functions, which the case law does not always disentangle. The *pre-petition* prong asks whether creditors *relied* on the breakdown of corporate borders before the petition — that is, whether they treated the parent and SPE as a single economic unit when extending credit. The *post-petition* prong asks whether the entities' affairs are so *entangled* after the filing that disentangling them would prejudice all creditors more than consolidation. Each prong supplies its own proof problem: the pre-petition prong is a problem of creditor expectations, while the post-petition prong is a problem of the books and records the SPE has actually maintained.

The structural and contractual mechanisms of bankruptcy remoteness are designed to defeat both prongs. They are the indispensable counterpart to the true-sale architecture in § I: a true sale that survives recharacterization but a substantive consolidation that succeeds will achieve the same result the true-sale fight was designed to prevent. For this reason, securitization counsel deliver *both* a true-sale opinion (at the "would" level) and a non-consolidation opinion (at the "should" level) at closing. The latter is rendered at the lower diligence standard because consolidation is a fact-intensive, equitable doctrine resistant to categorical opinion.

The 2009 *General Growth Properties* case crystallized the related distinction between **bankruptcy-remote** and **bankruptcy-proof**. Even an SPE that successfully resists substantive consolidation can be put into bankruptcy by its parent's directors — particularly where, as in GGP, the parent simply replaces the SPE's independent directors with insiders. The post-*GGP* "fix" is the present-day market response.

## II.B. Statutory and Doctrinal Anchors

> **AUTHORITY** — 11 U.S.C. § 301 (voluntary petition).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** A debtor commences a voluntary case by filing a petition with the bankruptcy court. The right to file is not waivable in advance.
>
> **Application Matrix.** The non-waivability of the voluntary-petition right is the doctrinal pivot of bankruptcy remoteness. A securitization cannot prevent the SPE from filing if its decisional governance authorizes it; remoteness must therefore work *through* the SPE's governance — by limiting *who* can authorize a § 301 filing — rather than by purporting to take the right away. *See Klingman v. Levinson*, 831 F.2d 1292 (7th Cir. 1987) (pre-petition waiver of voluntary filing void as against public policy); *In re Madison Hotel Assocs.*, 749 F.2d 410 (7th Cir. 1984) (same).
>
> **Policy Implications.** The non-waivability rule reflects the bankruptcy regime's traditional debtor-protective orientation, which the practitioner architecture of bankruptcy remoteness substantially attenuates without ever directly contradicting. The compromise solution — independent directors with veto power over voluntary filings — preserves the formal right while making its exercise structurally improbable.

> **AUTHORITY** — 11 U.S.C. § 303 (involuntary petition).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** An involuntary case may be commenced under Chapter 7 or 11 by three or more entities holding non-contingent, undisputed claims aggregating at least the statutory threshold (currently $21,050, effective April 1, 2025; the threshold is adjusted triennially under 11 U.S.C. § 104). Where there are fewer than 12 holders of such claims, a single eligible petitioner may file.
>
> **Application Matrix.** A creditor's pre-petition agreement *not* to file an involuntary § 303 petition — a "non-petition covenant" — is generally enforceable in securitization contexts because the right belongs to the creditor (who can waive it) rather than to the debtor (who cannot). The structural vulnerability addressed is the *Kingston Square* path: an insider organizes vendors, professionals, or other small creditors of the SPE to file an involuntary petition as a workaround to an independent-director veto.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The asymmetry between the unenforceability of voluntary-petition waivers and the enforceability of non-petition covenants reflects a coherent bankruptcy-policy distinction: the voluntary right protects the debtor's access to the statutory regime, while the involuntary right protects creditors as a class against debtor manipulation, and is therefore a right that any individual creditor may relinquish.

> **AUTHORITY** — 11 U.S.C. § 365(e) (anti-ipso facto).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** A contract provision that terminates or modifies the rights of the debtor solely because of the debtor's financial condition or the commencement of a bankruptcy case is unenforceable.
>
> **Application Matrix.** Section 365(e) creates the doctrinal tension that ABS counsel must navigate when drafting non-petition covenants. A covenant that purports to bind the SPE itself — i.e., a debtor's voluntary commitment not to file — is vulnerable to attack as an ipso-facto-equivalent contractual disability. A covenant that binds the SPE's *creditors* (the lawyers, accountants, rating agencies, indenture trustee, etc.) operates differently: each creditor is waiving its own private right to a separate proceeding (§ 303), not the debtor's statutory access to relief (§ 301), and the public-policy infirmity that animates § 365(e) is not implicated. The mock-exam materials note *In re Conex Holdings, LLC* in this connection; the principal doctrinal anchors, however, remain *Klingman v. Levinson*, 831 F.2d 1292 (7th Cir. 1987), and *In re Madison Hotel Assocs.*, 749 F.2d 410 (7th Cir. 1984), discussed at § II.F below.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The drafting solution is now standard. Non-petition covenants in modern transaction documents are uniformly creditor-side waivers; the SPE itself is constrained instead through governance — the independent-director veto — rather than by any contractual covenant against filing.

## II.C. The Core Cases — Substantive Consolidation Test

> **AUTHORITY** — *In re Owens Corning*, 419 F.3d 195 (3d Cir. 2005) [Ambro, J.].
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** The Third Circuit articulated a disjunctive two-prong test for substantive consolidation: courts may consolidate non-debtor entities only where the proponent demonstrates either (i) that *prepetition* the entities disregarded their separateness so significantly that creditors *relied on the breakdown of entity borders* and treated the entities as a single legal unit, *or* (ii) that *postpetition* the assets and liabilities of the entities are so scrambled that separating them is *prohibitive and harms all creditors*. The court emphasized that substantive consolidation is "rough justice" and is to be used "sparingly," as a remedy of last resort. The court rejected the broader, multi-factor "benefit-to-creditors" analysis suggested by some earlier cases.
>
> **Application Matrix.** Each prong of the *Owens Corning* test maps onto a discrete category of bankruptcy-remoteness mechanism:
>
> | *Owens Corning* Prong | Defeating Mechanism |
> |---|---|
> | (i) Pre-petition reliance / disregard of borders | *Separateness covenants* (separate books, accounts, financials, signage, no commingling, arm's-length affiliate transactions, observation of formalities); separate marketing identity; non-consolidation opinion delivered at closing; no parent guaranty; restrictions on additional debt |
> | (ii) Post-petition entanglement | *Operational separateness* throughout deal life: separate cash management, separate corporate offices or clearly-segregated workspace, separate employees or fair allocation of overhead; intercompany transactions documented and arm's-length; no after-the-fact reconciliation problems |
>
> The practitioner mantra is that a single isolated factor — even a parent guaranty — is rarely sufficient on its own to support consolidation; a court will require a pattern of disregard rising to the level of substantive non-separateness.
>
> **Policy Implications.** *Owens Corning* tightened the substantive-consolidation test in the structured-finance creditor's favor. The court's rejection of a freestanding "benefits all creditors" test was read at the time as a victory for sophisticated creditor planning: parties who have bargained for separate-entity treatment receive the benefit of that bargain so long as they have actually conducted themselves accordingly. Critics observe that the test's reliance prong privileges sophisticated, securitization-savvy creditors over unsecured trade creditors who are unlikely ever to inquire into corporate-separateness arrangements; the rule effectively rewards those who structure for it. In a consumer-or-retail context, the reliance prong's empirical premise — that creditors investigated the entity structure before extending credit — is doubtful.

> **AUTHORITY** — *In re Augie/Restivo Baking Co.*, 860 F.2d 515 (2d Cir. 1988).
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** The Second Circuit articulated a parallel two-pronged test: substantive consolidation is appropriate where (i) creditors dealt with the entities as a single economic unit and did not rely on their separate identity, *or* (ii) the affairs of the debtors are so entangled that consolidation will benefit all creditors. The two prongs map closely onto *Owens Corning*'s pre-petition reliance and post-petition entanglement prongs respectively, though *Augie/Restivo*'s second prong is somewhat more permissive in that it asks whether consolidation *benefits* all creditors rather than whether *separation* harms all creditors.
>
> **Application Matrix.** *Augie/Restivo* governs in the Second Circuit, including the Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of New York — the venue of choice for many large securitization-sponsored bankruptcies. Practitioners should be alert that, as between *Owens Corning* and *Augie/Restivo*, the Second Circuit's framing slightly expands the post-petition prong relative to the Third Circuit's. The drafting consequence is modest: the same separateness covenants and operational discipline that defeat *Owens Corning* defeat *Augie/Restivo* as well.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The two-circuit alignment of test structure since the 1980s gives substantive consolidation a relative doctrinal predictability that other equitable bankruptcy doctrines lack. The rare divergence in application produces an academic literature focused on whether the slight permissiveness of *Augie/Restivo*'s post-petition prong has any empirical effect on consolidation outcomes in close cases. Empirical work suggests it does not.

> **AUTHORITY** — *In re Auto-Train Corp.*, 810 F.2d 270 (D.C. Cir. 1987).
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** The D.C. Circuit articulated a two-element test, supplemented by a balancing inquiry where creditors objected. The proponent of substantive consolidation must demonstrate (i) substantial identity between the entities to be consolidated, and (ii) that consolidation is necessary to avoid harm or to realize benefit. Where an objecting creditor demonstrates reliance on the separate credit of one of the entities, consolidation may be ordered only if the demonstrated benefits "heavily" outweigh the harm. The test is permissive relative to *Owens Corning* / *Augie/Restivo* in that it does not condition consolidation on either pre-petition reliance disregard or post-petition entanglement at the threshold of those tests.
>
> **Application Matrix.** *Auto-Train* is the test that *Owens Corning* explicitly *rejected*. It is included here because hypotheticals on past examinations have asked candidates to identify why the older test was thought too permissive — its "benefits all creditors" framing rendered consolidation a quasi-equitable balancing exercise rather than a tightly-cabined remedy of last resort, with the result that creditors who had in fact bargained for separate-entity treatment could be over-ridden on essentially distributive grounds. The modern test forecloses that result.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The retreat from *Auto-Train* to *Owens Corning* / *Augie/Restivo* parallels a broader doctrinal trend in business bankruptcy — the privileging of contractual entitlements and separate-entity status over generalized equitable distribution. Critics in the academic literature read this as a conservative drift; defenders read it as the maturation of bankruptcy doctrine toward predictability in service of *ex ante* efficient capital markets.

## II.D. The Doctors Hospital / Paloian Sequence — Operational Separateness

> **AUTHORITY** — *Paloian v. LaSalle Bank, N.A.* (*In re Doctors Hospital of Hyde Park, Inc.*), 619 F.3d 688 (7th Cir. 2010); on remand, *In re Doctors Hospital of Hyde Park, Inc.*, 507 B.R. 558 (Bankr. N.D. Ill. 2013) [Schmetterer, J., Amended Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law].
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** Doctors Hospital had executed a textbook-correct securitization at closing: the receivables were sold to an SPE, all separateness covenants were in place, and a non-consolidation opinion was delivered. During the life of the deal, however, the hospital substantially ignored the separateness regime — it kept the receivables on its own books, used hospital phones, stationery, and offices, and failed to maintain the operational distinctions the closing documents required. The Seventh Circuit reversed and remanded for findings on the question whether the structural integrity of the securitization had been preserved through the deal life. On remand, the bankruptcy court (in extensive 2013 Findings) ultimately *upheld* the structure, concluding that the closing-level legal opinions were validly relied upon, the formal true sale was real, and the SPE was genuinely a separate legal entity even where operational discipline had eroded.
>
> **Application Matrix.** *Doctors Hospital* / *Paloian* stands for two propositions of immediate exam-level utility. First, operational separateness must be preserved *through* the life of the transaction, not merely at closing — the case is the basis for the practitioner-bar's standard separateness-monitoring covenants and annual-officer's-certificate regime. Second, even substantial operational lapses will not necessarily defeat the structure where the formal documentary architecture and the closing opinions remain intact and creditors have continued to rely on them. The case is therefore both a cautionary tale (closing alone is insufficient) and a doctrinal floor (formal separateness, properly opined, is robust to substantial operational drift).
>
> **Policy Implications.** *Doctors Hospital* is sometimes read as emblematic of the practitioner-bar's success in structuring around equitable bankruptcy doctrines: the closing-level architecture was so well-constructed that even prolonged disregard of separateness during operations did not unwind it. Critics, particularly the post-2008 reform literature, treat the case as evidence that the substantive consolidation doctrine has been narrowed to functional ineffectiveness in modern structured finance. Defenders observe that the result vindicates the *ex ante* settlement-of-expectations rationale: creditors did rely, the deal documents were comprehensive, and *ex post* unwinding would impose diffuse losses across many investors to compensate for failures attributable to one operating entity.

## II.E. *General Growth Properties* and the "Fix" — Bankruptcy-Remote vs. Bankruptcy-Proof

> **AUTHORITY** — *In re General Growth Properties, Inc.*, 409 B.R. 43 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2009) [Gropper, J.].
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** GGP, a real estate investment trust with a portfolio of mortgage-backed SPEs, faced a 2009 liquidity crisis at the parent level. To consolidate restructuring leverage, GGP's parent management replaced the independent directors of each SPE — typically corporate-services professionals supplied by Delaware service firms — with insiders, who promptly authorized the SPEs to file Chapter 11 alongside the parent. SPE secured creditors moved to dismiss the SPE filings as bad-faith, arguing the SPEs had been intentionally designed to be bankruptcy-remote and that the IDs' replacement by insiders was a structural betrayal of investor expectations. The bankruptcy court refused to dismiss. Under Delaware *corporate* law, the court reasoned, even an "independent" director owes fiduciary duties to the corporation and its equity holders — i.e., the parent — and the parent's removal of those IDs and substitution of replacements was within its statutory rights. The result: bankruptcy-remoteness, as constructed pre-GGP, was structurally weaker than the market had assumed.
>
> **Application Matrix.** *GGP* is the case that explains why every modern post-2009 securitization SPE is organized as a Delaware LLC (rather than a Delaware corporation) with the four-element fiduciary-waiver "fix" described below. The case also establishes the operational distinction between an entity that is *bankruptcy-remote* (its own assets are insulated from the parent's creditors via separateness) and one that is *bankruptcy-proof* (it cannot itself be put into bankruptcy by the parent's manipulation of governance). GGP showed that the pre-2009 SPE was the former but not the latter.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The case has been widely discussed as a parable about the limits of legal-engineering. Investors had accepted a price reflecting a putative degree of insulation that turned out, on the facts, not to exist. The market's response — the GGP fix — restored the lost insulation by leveraging the Delaware LLC Act's permission to waive fiduciary duties. Critics observe that the fix, by relocating SPE governance into a contractually-shaped fiduciary regime, places extraordinary weight on the sophistication of the LLC agreement's drafting and on the integrity of the few corporate-services firms that supply IDs in the post-2009 market. Defenders observe that the alternative — accepting *GGP*'s result — would have gutted the bankruptcy-remoteness premium and significantly raised the cost of structured finance.

> **AUTHORITY** — Del. Code Ann. tit. 6, § 18-1101(c).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** The Delaware LLC Act permits the LLC's operating agreement to expand, restrict, or eliminate any fiduciary duties that members or managers might otherwise owe to the LLC or to other members. The implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cannot be eliminated.
>
> **Application Matrix — The Four-Element GGP Fix.** Modern SPE-LLC operating agreements embed four interlocking provisions, each tracing to *GGP*:
>
> | Element | Function |
> |---|---|
> | (1) Fiduciary-duty waiver under § 18-1101(c) | Independent managers' duties run *only* to the SPE and its creditors — not to the parent or other equity holder. The parent cannot demand a "loyal" filing in service of its own restructuring agenda. |
> | (2) For-cause-only removal | Independent managers are removable only for narrowly-defined "cause"; the language of the LLC agreement explicitly excludes "lack of bankruptcy expertise" as cause (the pretext invoked in GGP). |
> | (3) Notice plus replacement procedures | Removal of an independent manager requires advance notice to the indenture trustee and rating agencies; a successor must be in place before the original IM departs. |
> | (4) Two independent managers | "Defense in depth" — the unanimous consent of two IMs (rather than one) is required to authorize any voluntary filing. |
>
> The practitioner observation, recorded in the Apr. 21, 2026 review session, is that "no GGP opinion exists" — the fix is *structural*, baked into the LLC operating agreement, not memorialized as a separate closing opinion. The four elements appear in the LLC agreement's "limited purpose" and "independent manager" provisions.
>
> **Policy Implications.** § 18-1101(c) is the doctrinal foundation that distinguishes the U.S. SPE-finance market from those of jurisdictions where fiduciary-duty waivers are unavailable. The commercial significance of the section is substantially greater in structured finance than the section's drafters could plausibly have anticipated. The provision is now sometimes referenced as Delaware's most consequential corporate-law article in financial-engineering terms, despite its formal modesty.

## II.F. Non-Petition Covenants and the Kingston Square Workaround

> **AUTHORITY** — *In re Kingston Square Assocs.*, 214 B.R. 713 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 1997).
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** Morton L. Ginsberg, the principal of approximately 38 affiliated SPEs in a commercial mortgage-backed securitization structure, was unable to obtain his independent director's authorization for voluntary petitions, and accordingly arranged through outside counsel for vendors and other small creditors to be solicited to file involuntary § 303 petitions against the SPEs as a workaround. The bankruptcy court found that the involuntary petitions had been orchestrated by the principal but, given the possibility of reorganization, declined to dismiss the cases and instead directed the appointment of Chapter 11 trustees in light of the principal's apparent abdication of fiduciary responsibilities. The case is widely read as exposing the involuntary-petition route as an effective workaround to independent-director veto in the absence of non-petition covenants binding the relevant creditor classes.
>
> **Application Matrix.** *Kingston Square* explains why every modern securitization document set includes non-petition covenants from professionals — lawyers, accountants, rating agencies, the indenture trustee, the back-up servicer — by which they agree, for the term of the deal plus a tail period, not to file an involuntary petition against the SPE. The covenants are enforceable because they constitute creditor waivers of a creditor right (§ 303) rather than debtor waivers of a debtor right (§ 301). The SPE's professionals are usually bound, but not the operating creditors of an active business — a residual issue more salient in WBS than in financial-asset ABS, as Professor Yarett noted in the April 21, 2026 review session in connection with Wendy's company-owned restaurants. *See* § IV below.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The case dramatizes the limits of governance-based bankruptcy remoteness against a determined insider. The drafting response (non-petition covenants) is itself a partial response: it covers the professionals but does not bind any third party who happens to extend credit to the SPE during the deal's life. For a financial-asset SPE — which has no operations and therefore no operating creditors — the covenants are functionally complete. For a WBS SPE with operating activity (e.g., a Wendy's company-owned restaurant within the obligor group), the residual exposure is structural and is addressed through deal-specific waiver mechanics with the relevant operating counterparties.

> **AUTHORITY** — *Klingman v. Levinson*, 831 F.2d 1292 (7th Cir. 1987); *In re Madison Hotel Assocs.*, 749 F.2d 410 (7th Cir. 1984).
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** A debtor's pre-petition agreement to waive the right to file a voluntary bankruptcy petition is unenforceable as contrary to public policy. The right of access to the bankruptcy regime is treated as a non-waivable statutory entitlement.
>
> **Application Matrix.** Together, *Klingman* and *Madison Hotel* establish that the structural mechanism for limiting the SPE's voluntary filing is *governance-based* (independent-manager veto) rather than *contractual* (a covenant by the SPE itself not to file). The contractual route is foreclosed; the governance route is permissible because the right is preserved formally — the SPE *could* file with the requisite consent — but is structurally constrained against arbitrary exercise.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The cases supply the doctrinal anchor for the practical asymmetry between voluntary-petition waivers (unenforceable) and non-petition covenants by creditors (enforceable). The asymmetry is doctrinally coherent — the protected interests differ between § 301 and § 303 — but practitioner literature has long noted the formalism. A creditor non-petition covenant accomplishes substantively the same thing as a debtor self-waiver in many cases, but it routes through a different doctrinal channel. The bankruptcy bar treats this as a permissible structural distinction rather than a policy infirmity.

## II.G. The Fourteen Separateness Covenants — Operational Architecture

The standard set of *separateness covenants* recited in the SPE's organizational documents and in transaction documentation, derived from rating-agency criteria and uniformly imposed in U.S. ABS, comprises the following fourteen elements:

1. Maintain *separate books and records* from the parent and all affiliates.
2. Maintain *separate bank accounts* and segregated cash.
3. *No commingling* of cash or other assets with parent or affiliates.
4. *Hold the SPE out as a separate entity* in dealings with third parties (signage, communications, marketing).
5. Operate in the SPE's *own name* and observe corporate (or LLC) *formalities* — board (or member) meetings, minutes, resolutions.
6. Prepare *separate financial statements* (or consolidated statements expressly noting the SPE's separateness).
7. *Pay liabilities* from SPE funds, including overhead allocated to the SPE on an arm's-length basis.
8. *No guarantee* of affiliate debt; no pledge of SPE assets to secure affiliate obligations.
9. Conduct affiliate transactions on *arm's-length terms* with documentation evidencing fair-market pricing.
10. Maintain *adequate capitalization* relative to the SPE's reasonably foreseeable liabilities and operating commitments.
11. Pay *fair allocations of overhead* if SPE shares facilities or personnel with affiliates.
12. *Correct misunderstandings* if any third party mistakes the SPE for the parent.
13. Maintain *separate stationery, invoices, and contracts*.
14. Hold *board meetings* (or member meetings) on a regular cadence with documented minutes.

Each covenant maps to one or more prongs of the *Owens Corning* test. Covenants (1)–(7) and (13) primarily defeat the post-petition-entanglement prong (the books and records will not be intractably commingled). Covenants (4), (5), (8), (9), and (12) primarily defeat the pre-petition-reliance prong (third-party creditors cannot have mistaken the SPE for the parent). Covenant (10) addresses the related but doctrinally distinct *equitable subordination* and *veil-piercing* doctrines, which travel often with substantive consolidation but apply different tests.

## II.H. Synthesis — Bankruptcy-Remote vs. Bankruptcy-Proof

Reading the foregoing authorities together, the modern structured-finance SPE achieves *bankruptcy-remote* status through three concentric layers:

**Layer 1 — Asset isolation.** True sale (§ I above) ensures that the SPE's assets are not § 541 property of the originator's estate. The receivables in the SPE belong to the SPE, not to the originator-debtor.

**Layer 2 — Anti-consolidation.** Separateness covenants and operational discipline ensure that *Owens Corning* / *Augie/Restivo* substantive consolidation cannot be invoked to combine the SPE's estate with the parent's. The SPE is not just a separate entity formally; it is operated as one continuously.

**Layer 3 — Anti-filing governance.** The post-*GGP* fix ensures that the SPE cannot itself be put into bankruptcy by the parent's unilateral manipulation of governance. Independent managers with fiduciary duties running solely to the SPE and its creditors hold an effective veto over voluntary § 301 filings; non-petition covenants from sophisticated creditors prevent involuntary § 303 workarounds.

The three layers are *cumulative*: failure of any one layer is recoverable only by the full operation of the other two. The contemporary 2026 wave of WBS-related restructurings discussed in the April 21 review session — Hooters, TGI Fridays, FAT Brands, recent Wendy's matters — has tested each of these layers in distinct combinations and in each case, broadly speaking, the layered architecture has held: where SPEs have been drawn into proceedings, it has been by the affirmative decision of independent managers acting in what they have determined to be the best interests of the SPE and its creditors, not by manipulation of the SPE governance against those creditors' interests. The market has, in this sense, learned the *General Growth* lesson.

# § III. UCC ARTICLE 9 PERFECTION IN WHOLE BUSINESS SECURITIZATION

## III.A. Conceptual Frame

UCC Article 9 governs both (i) the perfection of a security interest in personal property and (ii) the perfection of an outright sale of receivables. In a securitization, both functions are typically operative across two distinct layers: the *originator-to-SPE* transfer (a sale) and the *SPE-to-indenture-trustee* transfer (a security interest granted to support the SPE's notes). Each layer must be independently perfected.

In whole-business securitization, the perfection problem becomes more interesting because the asset pool typically includes *unconventional* collateral — franchise rights, trademarks, drug royalties, software licenses, fiber-optic infrastructure — for which the doctrinal framework was not originally designed. The 2026 examination places particular weight on this point: the practitioner question is not merely *whether* the underlying asset is perfectable under Article 9, but *what type of collateral category* it falls into and *what perfection method* attaches to that category.

Two doctrinal levers do most of the work. The first is the asset-classification taxonomy of UCC § 9-102 — particularly the distinction between *accounts*, *chattel paper*, *general intangibles*, *payment intangibles*, and *promissory notes*. The second is the anti-assignment override of UCC §§ 9-406(d) and 9-408, which renders contractual restrictions on the transfer of these assets ineffective against the perfection of a sale or security interest. The override has no civil-law analogue and is, on Professor Yarett's framing, the doctrinal ingredient that makes WBS structurally feasible in the United States but not in most foreign jurisdictions.

## III.B. Asset Taxonomy — The Five Categories That Matter

> **AUTHORITY** — UCC § 9-102(a)(2) (account); § 9-102(a)(11) (chattel paper); § 9-102(a)(42) (general intangible); § 9-102(a)(61) (payment intangible); § 9-102(a)(65) (promissory note).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.**
> - "Account" means a right to payment for goods sold or leased, services rendered, or property licensed (subject to enumerated exclusions).
> - "Chattel paper" means a record evidencing both a *monetary obligation* and a *security interest in or lease of specific goods*.
> - "General intangible" means any personal property other than the enumerated specific categories — a residual definition.
> - "Payment intangible" is a sub-category of general intangible whose principal obligation is monetary.
> - "Promissory note" carries its standard commercial-paper meaning.
>
> **Application Matrix.**
>
> | WBS Asset | Likely UCC Category | Perfection Method |
> |---|---|---|
> | Franchise royalty (e.g., Wendy's franchisee monthly royalty) | Account, § 9-102(a)(2) | UCC-1 filing only |
> | Auto loan receivable (Honda case) | Chattel paper, § 9-102(a)(11) | UCC-1 filing *plus* possession (or § 9-105 control for electronic chattel paper); possession trumps filing under § 9-330 |
> | Drug royalty (pharmaceutical license royalty stream) | Account if ordinary-course license; general intangible / payment intangible if structured as financing | UCC-1 filing; § 9-309(3) automatic perfection on sale of payment intangible |
> | Trademark (federally registered) | General intangible | UCC-1 filing (state-law); *not* federal Lanham Act filing (*see* III.G below) |
> | Mortgage note | Promissory note, § 9-102(a)(65) | Sale auto-perfects under § 9-309(4); security interest by possession or filing |
> | Fiber-network customer contract | Account, § 9-102(a)(2) | UCC-1 filing only |
> | Lockbox / collection deposit account | Deposit account, § 9-102(a)(29) | § 9-104 control agreement |
>
> **Policy Implications.** The asset-classification taxonomy was substantially modernized in the 1998 revisions and again in the 2010 amendments. The WBS-relevant categories were specifically broadened to facilitate financing of intangible assets: payment intangibles received automatic perfection on sale (§ 9-309(3)) precisely to reduce the perfection burden on royalty and license-based financings. The drafters' clear intent was to support a structured-finance market in non-mortgage, non-receivable collateral. Critics of this drafting choice note that the auto-perfection rules — by relieving the transferee of the discipline of public filing — have measurably increased the difficulty for unsophisticated unsecured creditors of an originator to discover the existence of significant prior-perfected interests. The trade-off is between transactional efficiency for sophisticated parties and informational cost imposed on lower-priority claimants.

## III.C. Two-Layer Perfection — The Standard ABS Architecture

> **AUTHORITY** — UCC § 9-203(b) (attachment); § 9-307(e) (registered organizations); § 9-503(a)(3) (trusts as debtors).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** A security interest attaches when (i) value has been given, (ii) the debtor has rights in the collateral, and (iii) the debtor has authenticated a security agreement that contains an adequate description of the collateral. § 9-203(b). For perfection by filing, a registered organization (corporation, LLC, limited partnership, statutory trust) is treated as located in its state of formation. § 9-307(e). A common-law trust must be identified in the financing statement by the name given in its organic document, with an indication that the debtor is a trust. § 9-503(a)(3).
>
> **Application Matrix — Layer 1 (Originator → SPE).**
>
> | Step | Action | Authority |
> |---|---|---|
> | 1 | SPE pays value (the purchase price) | § 9-203(b)(1) |
> | 2 | Originator delivers receivables along with executed transfer agreement | § 9-203(b)(2)–(3) |
> | 3 | SPE files UCC-1 against originator in originator's state of formation | § 9-307(e) |
> | 4 | Description: "all auto-loan receivables originated by [Originator]"; or asset-class-specific equivalent | § 9-108 (sufficiency of description) |
> | 5 | Where collateral is chattel paper, SPE takes possession of original contracts (or § 9-105 electronic control) for priority | § 9-330(a)–(b) |
>
> **Application Matrix — Layer 2 (SPE → Indenture Trustee).**
>
> | Step | Action | Authority |
> |---|---|---|
> | 1 | SPE grants security interest to indenture trustee in collateral including all receivables and proceeds | § 9-203(b) |
> | 2 | Indenture trustee files UCC-1 against SPE in SPE's state of formation (typically Delaware for an SPE LLC) | § 9-307(e) |
> | 3 | Indenture trustee takes possession of original chattel paper through custodian (or perfects electronic control) | § 9-330(a)–(b); § 9-105 |
> | 4 | Indenture trustee perfects deposit-account interest by control agreement | § 9-104 |
> | 5 | Five-year continuation by UCC-3 within six-month window before lapse | § 9-515(d) |
>
> **Policy Implications.** The two-layer structure is a deliberate response to the bankruptcy-remoteness architecture: Layer 1 perfects the SPE's *ownership* (or, if recharacterized, its security interest) in the receivables; Layer 2 perfects the indenture trustee's interest *in the SPE's pool* for the benefit of the noteholders. Each layer serves a distinct doctrinal purpose. Layer 1 protects the SPE against the originator's other creditors; Layer 2 protects the noteholders against any disposition of the SPE's pool. Failure at either layer collapses the structure.

## III.D. Auto-Perfection on Sale of Payment Intangibles and Promissory Notes

> **AUTHORITY** — UCC § 9-309(3) (sale of payment intangible); § 9-309(4) (sale of promissory note).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** A *sale* of a payment intangible or a promissory note is perfected automatically — without the need for any filing or other action — at the moment the security interest attaches.
>
> **Application Matrix.** This is the doctrinal feature that makes participations in syndicated loans, receivables purchases of payment-intangible assets, and many WBS structures operationally feasible. In an asset-finance transaction involving payment intangibles, the SPE acquires a perfected interest immediately upon execution of the sale agreement and payment of the purchase price; no UCC-1 is required to perfect *as against unsecured creditors of the originator*. The standard ABS practice nevertheless includes a precautionary UCC-1, both to cover the case in which the underlying sale is recharacterized as a security interest (in which case auto-perfection does not apply and a standard filing is necessary) and to provide notice to potential subsequent sophisticated parties.
>
> **Policy Implications.** Auto-perfection was an explicit drafter's choice in the 1998 revisions to favor structured-finance liquidity over creditor notification. Its critics view it as one of the deeper structural privileges Article 9 confers on secured-finance practice; its defenders view it as an honest acknowledgment that loan participations and payment-intangible sales are sufficiently market-standard that public-record discoverability adds little to creditor information.

## III.E. The Anti-Assignment Override — UCC §§ 9-406(d) and 9-408

> **AUTHORITY** — UCC § 9-406(d) (accounts, chattel paper, payment intangibles); § 9-408 (broader override for promissory notes, healthcare-insurance receivables, general intangibles, and payment intangibles).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** A term in an underlying contract that prohibits assignment of, requires consent for assignment of, or provides for default upon assignment of an account, chattel paper, payment intangible, or promissory note is *ineffective* to the extent it would impair the creation, attachment, or perfection of a security interest in or sale of such asset. § 9-406(d). Section 9-408 applies the override more broadly — including to general intangibles and to certain healthcare-insurance receivables — but its override is somewhat narrower in operative effect: while the prohibition cannot prevent attachment or perfection, the assignor may still face contractual consequences (e.g., termination rights of the underlying obligor) under the underlying agreement.
>
> **Application Matrix — The WBS Lever.**
>
> | WBS Asset | Anti-Assignment Risk in Underlying Contract | UCC Override |
> |---|---|---|
> | Franchise agreement royalty (Wendy's, Driven Brands, Quiznos) | Standard franchise agreements typically restrict assignment of franchisee's rights — the relevant question is whether the *franchisor's* receivable is restricted | § 9-406(d) renders the restriction ineffective for perfection purposes |
> | IP license royalty | Standard license agreements often prohibit assignment | § 9-408 renders the restriction ineffective for perfection purposes; underlying obligor's other contract rights preserved |
> | Drug royalty (pharma license) | Pharma licenses typically restrict licensee assignment | § 9-408 override applies |
> | Fiber-network customer contract | Customer contracts of large fiber operators often include consent-required-on-assignment language | § 9-406(d) override applies |
>
> **Policy Implications — the Civil-Law Contrast.** The anti-assignment override is doctrinally peculiar to U.S. commercial law. Most civil-law jurisdictions enforce contractual anti-assignment restrictions strictly, with the result that the construction of WBS is *structurally* more difficult outside the United States. The April 14, 2026 fiber-securitization lecture made this point explicitly: the U.S. fiber WBS playbook, which depends on the assignability of customer contracts to AssetCos and pledgability of those contract rights to the indenture trustee, is largely uncopiable in continental Europe without significant rejiggering through synthetic structures. The UNCITRAL Convention on the Assignment of Receivables in International Trade (2001) was an attempt to extend the override internationally but has had limited adoption. Professor Yarett's broader course point is that U.S. structured finance is, in significant part, an artifact of U.S. commercial-law pro-assignability bias, and that this fact alone explains a meaningful share of the U.S. market's relative size and liquidity.

## III.F. Priority Among Conflicting Perfected Interests

> **AUTHORITY** — UCC § 9-322(a)(1) (first-to-file-or-perfect); § 9-330(a)–(b) (chattel-paper purchaser priority).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** Conflicting perfected security interests rank according to priority in time of filing or perfection. § 9-322(a)(1). However, a purchaser of chattel paper that takes possession in good faith and in the ordinary course of its business has priority over a security interest in the chattel paper even if the security interest was earlier perfected by filing. § 9-330(a)–(b).
>
> **Application Matrix.** In a chattel-paper deal (e.g., auto-loan ABS), the SPE's *possession* of the original installment contracts gives it priority over any earlier filing-only secured creditor of the originator. The practical structure is that the SPE either takes physical possession through a custodian or, where the chattel paper is electronic, takes "control" within the meaning of § 9-105.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The chattel-paper override of the first-to-file rule is one of the few residual concessions in modern Article 9 to the historical importance of the document of title. It is also a powerful structural advantage for ABS: a properly-structured auto-loan deal is robust against any earlier blanket lien filed against the originator by a working-capital lender, because possession trumps filing for chattel paper. The §§ 9-322 / 9-330 combination is the source of the structural-finance practitioner's preference for chattel-paper assets where possible.

## III.G. Trademarks and IP Collateral — The Federal-State Filing Question

> **AUTHORITY** — *In re Roman Cleanser Co.*, 43 B.R. 940 (Bankr. E.D. Mich. 1984), *aff'd*, 802 F.2d 207 (6th Cir. 1986); 15 U.S.C. § 1060 (Lanham Act assignments); 17 U.S.C. § 205 (Copyright Act recordation); 35 U.S.C. § 261 (Patent Act assignments).
>
> **Holding / Core Rule.** The Lanham Act's recordation provisions (15 U.S.C. § 1060) require recordation of *assignments* of trademarks with the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office. The Lanham Act does not, however, expressly provide for the recordation of *security interests* in trademarks. *Roman Cleanser* held that the Lanham Act does not preempt the application of state UCC Article 9 to security interests in trademarks: the proper place to file for a security interest in a federally-registered trademark is the state UCC system, not the USPTO.
>
> **Application Matrix.** The doctrinal contrast among the three federal IP regimes is exam-worthy:
>
> | Regime | Statutory Recordation | Security-Interest Treatment |
> |---|---|---|
> | Trademarks (Lanham Act) | Assignments only | State UCC controls security interests (*Roman Cleanser*) |
> | Copyright (Copyright Act) | Mortgages, hypothecations, transfers expressly recordable, 17 U.S.C. § 205 | Federal recordation is preemptive for security interests (*see* *In re Peregrine Entm't, Ltd.*, 116 B.R. 194 (C.D. Cal. 1990)) — note that this remains controversial and circuit-dependent |
> | Patents (Patent Act) | Assignments only | State UCC controls security interests (general view); patents-as-collateral practice files state UCC-1 plus, in some cases, a precautionary USPTO recordation |
>
> A WBS transaction including federally-registered IP must file appropriately in *both* the state UCC system (to secure its UCC-perfected interest) and, for copyrights, with the U.S. Copyright Office. The 2026 mock examination's WBS hypotheticals turn on this distinction.
>
> **Policy Implications.** The federal-state filing question is a residual artifact of the lack of statutory clarity in the federal IP regimes about security-interest recordation. Reform proposals to centralize IP-collateral recordation at the federal level have circulated for decades but have not been enacted. The dual-filing practice that has emerged is doctrinally adequate, but adds friction to the IP-financing market and is one of the operational reasons U.S. IP-collateralized financings carry higher transaction costs than equivalent receivables-based deals.

## III.H. Default and Enforcement

> **AUTHORITY** — UCC § 9-607 (collection rights upon default).
>
> **Black-Letter Provision.** A secured party in default may, *inter alia*: (i) notify account debtors and other persons obligated on the collateral to make payment directly to the secured party; (ii) take any proceeds to which the secured party is entitled; and (iii) enforce the obligations of the collateral and exercise all rights of the debtor.
>
> **Application Matrix.** Section 9-607 supplies the contractual self-help mechanism by which the indenture trustee, upon an event of default by the SPE, can step into the SPE's collection role and direct franchisee royalties (or other payments) to the trustee directly. The practical importance of § 9-607 is that the indenture trustee need not seek judicial process to redirect cash flow — a critical feature in WBS, where any meaningful delay in collection during a parent's distress event would defeat the structure.
>
> **Policy Implications.** Section 9-607's broad self-help authorization reflects Article 9's general structural preference for secured-creditor remedies that bypass judicial process. The provision is criticized in consumer-collateral contexts but is generally regarded as sound in commercial-finance contexts of the type relevant here. In WBS, where the relevant counterparties (franchisees, license obligors) are sophisticated parties, the self-help framework is well-matched to the underlying transaction.

# § IV. WHOLE BUSINESS SECURITIZATION — INTEGRATIVE APPLICATION

## IV.A. The WBS Structural Template

Whole-business securitization extracts the operating cash flows of an income-producing business — typically a franchisor's royalty stream, a pharmaceutical company's licensed-drug royalties, or a fiber operator's customer-contract revenues — and pledges them to a bankruptcy-remote SPE as collateral for fixed-income notes issued to capital-markets investors. Unlike financial-asset ABS (auto loans, credit cards, mortgages), where the SPE holds purely passive financial assets, a WBS SPE typically holds (or is structured to capture the cash flows from) a *substantial portion of the operating company's revenue base*. The doctrinal distinction matters because the WBS SPE is more *operationally* entangled with its parent than is a financial-asset SPE, and accordingly is more exposed to the substantive-consolidation and operational-separateness risks discussed in § II.

The contemporary WBS template, in its most refined form, has the following architecture:

1. **HoldCo Guarantor.** A bankruptcy-remote SPE sitting above the issuer that provides an upstream guarantee.
2. **Issuer.** The SPE that issues the notes.
3. **Asset Entities ("AssetCos").** One or more SPEs sitting below the issuer that hold the operating asset (franchise rights, IP, fiber infrastructure) and are the counterparties on the underlying revenue contracts.
4. **InfraCo (when applicable).** An additional SPE outside the obligor group that holds shared infrastructure and enters into arm's-length access agreements with the AssetCos.
5. **Manager.** The originator-parent typically continues as a contractual manager of the AssetCos under a management agreement, replaceable on default — an analogue to the servicer in financial-asset ABS, but with substantially greater operational decision-making authority.
6. **Indenture Trustee.** Holds liens on the AssetCo assets for the benefit of noteholders; receives waterfall payments under the indenture.

The structural pattern integrates each of the doctrines treated in §§ I–III above. The HoldCo-Issuer-AssetCo cascade implements true sale at multiple layers, ensuring that the AssetCo assets are not § 541 property of the parent's estate. The separateness covenants and the post-*GGP* fix at each SPE level implement bankruptcy-remoteness against substantive consolidation. The Article 9 perfection program protects the indenture trustee's lien against competing claimants on the AssetCo collateral.

## IV.B. Wendy's Funding LLC, Series 2015-1 — The Franchise-Royalty Prototype

The Wendy's 2015-1 transaction (offering memorandum dated July 2015) is the modern prototype of franchise-royalty WBS. The transaction monetized the royalty stream from Wendy's franchise restaurants — at the time, a substantial majority of Wendy's system-wide stores were franchised, with a smaller residual of company-owned restaurants kept inside the structure.

**True sale.** The franchise royalty receivables were transferred from the operating-company originator to the issuer SPE; the transfer documentation embeds the standard true-sale architecture (R&W only, fixed pricing, market-rate management fee, no parent guaranty). The retained residual interest of the parent is structured as subordinated equity, not as an option to repurchase the pool.

**Substantive consolidation.** The SPE-LLCs in the Wendy's structure include the four-element post-*GGP* fix (fiduciary-duty waiver, for-cause-only IM removal, notice-and-replacement, two IMs). Separateness covenants — books, accounts, signage, no commingling, arm's-length affiliate transactions, no parent guaranty — are imposed at every SPE level. A non-consolidation opinion was delivered at closing.

**Article 9 perfection.** Franchise royalties are *accounts* under § 9-102(a)(2). UCC-1 financing statements were filed (Layer 1 against the originator in its state of formation, Layer 2 against the issuer SPE in Delaware). A § 9-104 control agreement was executed for the lockbox/collection account. The trademark portfolio supporting the Wendy's brand was perfected through dual filings: state UCC-1 against each SPE that held trademark interests, plus precautionary USPTO recordation of any actual transfers (assignments, licenses).

**The company-owned-restaurant complication.** The Wendy's structure includes some company-owned restaurants whose operating cash flow contributes to the structure. These give rise to a residual *Kingston Square* exposure: such restaurants have operating creditors (suppliers, employees) whose claims could in theory support an involuntary § 303 petition against the SPE. The structuring response was to negotiate non-petition covenants from material counterparties, to staff the restaurants through a management entity whose creditors are not creditors of the SPE, and to maintain operational discipline that minimizes the risk of dispute. Professor Yarett confirmed in the April 21, 2026 review session that this point — managing the residual operating-creditor risk in WBS — is particular to the WBS context and does not arise in financial-asset ABS.

**Manager replacement.** Unlike a financial-asset ABS where the servicer's role is largely mechanical, the manager in a WBS deal exercises substantial business judgment over the operating asset. The ability to replace the manager on default is therefore both more important and more difficult. The Wendy's documentation includes detailed manager-replacement provisions, including step-in rights for the indenture trustee and a pre-vetted backup-manager arrangement.

**Policy Implications.** The Wendy's structure is, in market terms, the proof-of-concept that bankruptcy-remoteness mechanisms developed for financial-asset ABS could be transposed onto operating-business cash flows. The 2026 wave of WBS-related restructurings has tested the Wendy's-style architecture but has not, on the record presently before the courts, fundamentally undermined it.

## IV.C. Driven Brands 2015 — Multi-Brand Franchise WBS

The Driven Brands 2015 transaction (offering memorandum dated July 24, 2015) extended the Wendy's-style franchise-royalty WBS template to a *multi-brand* franchise platform. Driven Brands at the time owned multiple automotive-services franchise systems (Maaco, Meineke, others), each with its own royalty stream.

**Structural innovation.** The multi-brand structure required additional AssetCo segregation: each brand's royalty stream was housed in its own AssetCo, with the Issuer SPE holding the consolidated obligation. This multiplies the perfection program (Layer 1 perfection at multiple originator-to-AssetCo transfers, plus an internal perfection at the AssetCo-to-Issuer level) but does not change the doctrinal analysis.

**Brand-IP cross-pledging.** Each AssetCo's brand-IP collateral (trademarks, franchise agreements) was pledged to support the consolidated note issuance. The dual-filing program (state UCC-1 plus, for actual trademark transfers, USPTO recordation) was implemented at each AssetCo level.

**Substantive-consolidation analytics.** Multi-brand WBS structures present a heightened *operational* substantive-consolidation risk because the brands often share systems, personnel, and overhead. The structural response is highly disciplined intercompany allocation — each AssetCo's overhead, IT, and shared-services costs are documented and allocated on an arm's-length basis under written intercompany agreements. The non-consolidation opinion is correspondingly more fact-intensive than in a single-brand deal.

**Application Matrix — Wendy's vs. Driven Brands.**

| Feature | Wendy's 2015-1 | Driven Brands 2015 |
|---|---|---|
| Asset class | Single-brand franchise royalty + company-owned restaurants | Multi-brand franchise royalty (Maaco, Meineke, etc.) |
| AssetCo structure | One AssetCo (with sub-LLCs for company-owned stores) | Multiple AssetCos, one per brand |
| Operational entanglement risk | Moderate (company-owned restaurants) | Higher (shared services across brands) |
| Manager-replacement complexity | Standard | Higher (operator must manage multiple brand systems) |
| Trademark filing program | Single brand portfolio | Multiple brand portfolios |

## IV.D. Quiznos 2005-1 — The Cautionary Early WBS

The Quiznos 2005-1 transaction was an early franchise-royalty WBS that ultimately experienced significant difficulties in the post-2008 environment as franchisee defaults eroded the royalty stream. The deal materials in the workspace include the four-part Quiznos 2005-1 notes, which document an architecture broadly similar to (but predating the refinements of) the Wendy's structure.

**Structural lessons.** The Quiznos experience demonstrated that the WBS architecture is doctrinally robust to *parent* distress (the structural separation held) but is materially exposed to *underlying-asset* distress (where the franchisee base contracts and royalty cash flow declines, the architecture cannot manufacture cash that does not exist). The structuring response in the Wendy's-era and post-2010 WBS deals was to add stronger collection-rate triggers, deeper reserve accounts, more elaborate manager-replacement provisions, and more conservative initial overcollateralization.

**Comparison to Wendy's 2015-1.** The differences between Quiznos 2005-1 and Wendy's 2015-1 reflect a decade of structural maturation: the latter incorporates more refined separateness architecture, the post-*GGP* fix (which post-dated Quiznos 2005-1), and stronger collection mechanics. Reading the two offering memoranda in conjunction is one of the more efficient routes to understanding the practical evolution of WBS practice in this period.

## IV.E. Fiber-Network WBS — The 2022+ Playbook

The April 14, 2026 guest lecture by Mikhel Schecter of Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison surveyed the contemporary fiber-securitization market, which emerged in 2022 as a significant new asset class. Mr. Schecter described the fiber WBS architecture as following a substantially similar playbook to franchise-royalty WBS, with three asset-class-specific variations.

**Variation 1 — Last-mile vs. middle-mile asset assignment.** The core asset is the *last-mile* fiber that connects to the end customer, plus the related customer contracts. *Middle-mile* (backbone) fiber is sometimes also pledged into the structure, but where the fiber operator has competing financings outside the ABS perimeter, an *InfraCo* — a separate bankruptcy-remote SPE outside the obligor group — holds the shared middle-mile assets and enters into arm's-length access agreements with the AssetCos. Recent deals have been engineered down to the level of *individual fiber strand assignments*.

**Variation 2 — Customer contracts as accounts.** Fiber customer contracts are *accounts* under § 9-102(a)(2). They are perfected by UCC-1 filing only — neither chattel paper (no goods) nor general intangibles (because the principal obligation is monetary). The §§ 9-406(d) anti-assignment override is critical: residential and small-business customer contracts often include consent-required-on-assignment language, which the override renders ineffective for perfection purposes.

**Variation 3 — InfraCo arm's-length discipline.** The InfraCo, though outside the obligor group, must transact with the AssetCos on arm's-length terms — the same SPV-to-affiliate discipline emphasized throughout the course. The structural risk addressed is that the InfraCo, holding meaningful shared infrastructure, could itself be drawn into a bankruptcy proceeding affecting the broader fiber-operating group; the AssetCos' lack of lien on InfraCo assets must be compensated by careful diligence on the value of the assets actually inside the obligor box.

**Comparative Application.**

| Feature | Wendy's-style Franchise WBS | Fiber WBS |
|---|---|---|
| Asset | Franchise royalty receivables | Last-mile fiber + customer contracts |
| UCC category | Account | Account (contracts); General intangible (fiber-infrastructure ownership) |
| Layer 1 perfection | UCC-1 against operating-company originator | UCC-1 against fiber-operating originator |
| Structural distinctive | Manager-replacement of franchisor operations | Strand-level asset assignment; possible InfraCo |
| Anti-assignment override | Critical (franchise-agreement restrictions) | Critical (customer-contract restrictions) |

## IV.F. Drug Royalties — IP-Collateralized WBS

Drug-royalty securitization monetizes the right to receive royalty payments under a pharmaceutical license — typically royalties owed to a research-stage biotech by a pharmaceutical company that has licensed the underlying compound. The asset class is one of Professor Yarett's enumerated WBS categories.

**Asset classification.** The royalty right is most commonly an *account* under § 9-102(a)(2) where the underlying license is in the ordinary course of the originator's business; in some structures it is more naturally analyzed as a *general intangible* or *payment intangible* under § 9-102(a)(42), (61). The classification matters for the perfection method: accounts and chattel paper require UCC-1 filing; payment-intangible *sales* are auto-perfected under § 9-309(3).

**The anti-assignment override and the FDA-approval issue.** Pharma licenses commonly include detailed anti-assignment restrictions reflecting both commercial sensitivity and regulatory considerations (e.g., FDA orphan-drug designations or regulatory exclusivity terms). Section 9-408's override permits perfection of a security interest notwithstanding such restrictions, but does *not* override the licensor's underlying contract right to terminate or otherwise penalize the licensee for an unauthorized assignment. The structural response in pharma WBS is typically to (i) confirm that the override applies for perfection purposes, (ii) negotiate a recognition agreement with the underlying licensor consenting to the security interest and waiving termination rights upon enforcement, and (iii) include detailed step-in mechanics for the secured party.

**Trademark and patent integration.** Pharma WBS often includes pledges of patents and trademarks supporting the licensed compound. The Patent Act and Lanham Act overlays discussed in § III.G apply: state UCC-1 plus, for trademarks, precautionary USPTO recordation of any actual transfers; for patents, state UCC-1 with, in some cases, precautionary USPTO recordation.

## IV.G. The Manager-Replacement Paradigm

A doctrinal hallmark of WBS, distinguishing it from financial-asset ABS, is the centrality of the *manager* (analogous to but more substantive than the financial-asset *servicer*). The manager exercises ongoing business judgment over the asset — maintaining customer relationships, managing the franchise system, operating the fiber network, prosecuting the patent estate. The structural problem is that the manager is typically the originator-parent, whose distress is the principal risk the structure is designed to mitigate.

The architecture handles this through three integrated devices:

(1) **Default-triggered replacement rights.** The indenture trustee has the contractual right to replace the manager upon enumerated events of default — typically including manager bankruptcy, manager performance defaults, and manager breach of separateness or other key covenants.

(2) **Pre-vetted backup-manager arrangements.** Many WBS deals execute pre-arranged backup-manager engagement letters at closing with operationally-credible third parties (in franchise WBS, often other franchise operators; in fiber WBS, specialty backup-servicing firms). This converts the "find a replacement" problem from an unknown post-default scramble into a known operational handoff.

(3) **Step-in rights for indenture trustee.** The indenture trustee, often through a nominated agent, has the contractual right to step into the management of the AssetCos directly upon a manager-replacement event, bridging the gap between the original manager's removal and the backup manager's effective assumption of duties.

The manager-replacement architecture is the single feature that most distinguishes WBS from financial-asset ABS in operational terms. It also is the feature most vulnerable to *Doctors Hospital* / *Paloian*-style concerns: even a deeply-engineered manager-replacement program is functionally limited if the parent has, in operational reality, been the only entity capable of running the asset.

## IV.H. The 2026 WBS Restructuring Wave — Stress-Testing the Architecture

The April 21, 2026 review session referenced a contemporaneous wave of WBS-related restructurings — Hooters, TGI Fridays, FAT Brands, and certain Wendy's matters — as live tests of the WBS architecture under varied operating stress. Professor Yarett's framing was that these matters teach four points of immediate exam relevance:

(1) **The architecture has held in its core.** Where SPE entities have been drawn into proceedings, the entry has come through affirmative independent-manager decisions made in the asserted best interests of the SPE and its creditors — not through *General Growth*-style governance manipulation. The post-*GGP* fix has performed as designed.

(2) **Operational distress can override structural insulation.** Where the underlying operating asset (the franchisee base, the IP exclusivity, the customer contracts) deteriorates materially, no structural-finance architecture can manufacture cash flow that does not exist. The Quiznos 2005-1 lesson has been re-confirmed.

(3) **Manager replacement is more difficult than the documentation suggests.** The pre-vetted backup-manager arrangements have proven, in practice, less seamless than envisioned at closing — particularly where the operating asset requires brand-specific or system-specific operational expertise that few third parties possess.

(4) **Substantive consolidation has continued to hold against challenge.** Where unsecured creditors of the parent have argued for consolidation, courts have generally adhered to the *Owens Corning* / *Augie/Restivo* framework, with separateness-covenant compliance and the closing-level non-consolidation opinion treated as substantial evidence of the pre-petition reliance prong. Operational-discipline lapses, where alleged, have generally been treated as in *Doctors Hospital* — material but not dispositive.

The 2026 wave has, on balance, validated the layered architecture treated in §§ I–III. The stress points it has surfaced are operational (cash-flow deterioration, manager replacement difficulty) rather than doctrinal (*General Growth*-style filings, *Kingston Square*-style involuntary workarounds, substantive consolidation orders).

# § V. CONCLUDING OBSERVATIONS — DOCTRINAL VULNERABILITIES AND THE LIMITS OF LEGAL ENGINEERING

Three observations follow from the foregoing material.

**First, the bankruptcy-remoteness apparatus is robust against *adversarial* attacks but vulnerable to *operational* erosion.** True sale, substantive consolidation, and Article 9 perfection have all proven, on the available record, durable against direct legal challenge — *Owens Corning* tightened the consolidation test in favor of structured creditors; *Doctors Hospital* / *Paloian* on remand validated even imperfect operational separateness; *General Growth* prompted a structural fix that has held since 2009. The vulnerabilities that have actually emerged in the 2026 restructuring wave are operational: the manager cannot be replaced as smoothly as documented; the franchisee or customer base cannot manufacture cash flow if it is contracting; the underlying business has unique operational requirements that the SPE-as-passive-vehicle paradigm imperfectly accommodates.

**Second, the doctrinal apparatus relies on a small number of high-leverage structural pivots — and the loss of any one would substantially recalibrate the U.S. structured-finance market.** The most consequential pivots are: UCC § 9-109(a)(3)'s extension of Article 9 to sales of receivables; UCC §§ 9-406(d) and 9-408's anti-assignment overrides; UCC § 9-309(3)–(4)'s automatic perfection on sales of payment intangibles and promissory notes; Delaware LLC Act § 18-1101(c)'s permissibility of fiduciary-duty waivers; the *Owens Corning* / *Augie/Restivo* tightening of substantive consolidation; and the *Klingman* / *Madison Hotel* / *Conex Holdings* doctrinal asymmetry between voluntary-petition waivers (unenforceable) and creditor non-petition covenants (enforceable). Each pivot operates as a load-bearing element of the architecture; were any of them legislatively or judicially reversed, the practitioner response would be substantial — and in some cases the structural feasibility of the relevant subset of WBS would be affected.

**Third, the gap between U.S. structured-finance practice and the practice of major civil-law jurisdictions is substantially attributable to commercial-law differences, not to bankruptcy-law differences.** The most consequential differences — anti-assignment overrides, automatic perfection on sale of receivables, the broad permissibility of fiduciary-duty waivers in the Delaware LLC Act — are commercial-law features. The bankruptcy-policy differences (the contours of voluntary versus involuntary petitions, the equitable doctrine of substantive consolidation) are comparatively secondary. The implication for transnational structured-finance practice is that U.S.-style WBS is less easily exported than its drafters might wish, and that the principal limiting factor is not the inability of foreign courts to recognize U.S.-style structures but the unavailability, in foreign commercial-law regimes, of the underlying assignment and perfection mechanics on which those structures depend. The April 14, 2026 fiber-securitization lecture and the chapter 8 cross-border materials in the casebook converge on this point, though they approach it from different directions.

In sum: the doctrinal architecture treated in this Log is not a single integrated doctrine but a *stack of doctrines* operating cumulatively on different layers of an integrated transactional structure. The exam-day question for any WBS hypothetical is whether each layer is properly engaged, properly cited, and correctly applied to the facts. The Log's organization is designed to support that question's resolution under examination time pressure.

# Appendix C — Model Lines

The following four model lines integrate the principal doctrines of §§ I–IV into ready-to-deploy paragraph templates for the most frequently-tested fact patterns. Each is calibrated to the IRAC structure Professor Yarett has indicated he expects.

**Model Line 1 — True Sale Hypothetical.**

> *"The transfer is a true sale, not a financing. Following* Major's Furniture Mart *(3d Cir. 1979) and* Endico Potatoes *(2d Cir. 1995), the dispositive factor is the allocation of credit risk between transferor and transferee. Here, [Originator] gives only standard representations and warranties about the receivables' characteristics at the time of sale — breach of which triggers the customary repurchase remedy — but no representation as to future collectability and no buyback on delinquency. The purchase price is fixed; the servicing arrangement is at market rate and replaceable on default; there is no parent guaranty; cash is segregated through a § 9-104 control account. Under § 541, the receivables are not property of [Originator]'s estate, so the § 362 stay does not impair collection from obligors and the SPE's noteholders are paid out of cash flow they own. Issuer's counsel will deliver a 'would'-level true-sale opinion containing the standard* LTV Steel *disclaimer."*

**Model Line 2 — Substantive Consolidation Defense.**

> *"Under* In re Owens Corning *(3d Cir. 2005) — controlling here under Pennsylvania choice-of-law principles — substantive consolidation requires either (i) prepetition disregard of separateness so significant that creditors relied on the breakdown of entity borders, or (ii) postpetition entanglement of assets and liabilities such that separation is prohibitive. Neither prong is satisfied: the SPE has adhered to the standard fourteen separateness covenants — separate books and accounts, separate financial statements, no commingling, arm's-length affiliate transactions, no parent guaranty, observation of LLC formalities — and a non-consolidation opinion was delivered at closing as a condition precedent. Operational discipline has been maintained throughout the deal life, mindful of the warning in* Doctors Hospital *(7th Cir. 2010, on remand 2013) that closing-level architecture must be supported by ongoing operational separateness. Counsel will deliver a 'should'-level non-consolidation opinion."*

**Model Line 3 — Bankruptcy-Remote vs. Bankruptcy-Proof.**

> *"The SPE is bankruptcy-remote under the layered architecture: (i) true sale isolates the assets from § 541; (ii) separateness covenants and operational discipline defeat substantive consolidation under* Owens Corning *and* Augie/Restivo*; (iii) the post-*General Growth* *fix — fiduciary-duty waiver under Delaware LLC Act § 18-1101(c), for-cause-only IM removal, advance notice and replacement requirements, two independent managers — prevents the parent from manipulating governance to authorize a § 301 filing as in* GGP*. Non-petition covenants from professionals — counsel, accountants, rating agencies, indenture trustee — bind the involuntary § 303 route, addressing the* Kingston Square *workaround. The SPE is, in this layered sense, bankruptcy-remote, though as* GGP *itself demonstrates, no SPE is fully bankruptcy-proof."*

**Model Line 4 — UCC Article 9 Perfection in WBS.**

> *"Two layers of perfection are required. Layer 1: the SPE perfects its ownership of the [franchise royalties / fiber customer contracts / drug royalties], which are accounts under UCC § 9-102(a)(2) [or general intangibles under § 9-102(a)(42), as appropriate], by filing a UCC-1 financing statement against [Originator] in [Originator]'s state of formation under § 9-307(e), with collateral description satisfying § 9-108. Layer 2: the indenture trustee perfects its security interest by filing a UCC-1 against the SPE in Delaware [or the SPE's state of formation], plus executing a § 9-104 control agreement on the lockbox/collection account. Where the underlying contract restricts assignment, UCC § 9-406(d) [or § 9-408] renders the restriction ineffective for perfection purposes — the structural feature that makes WBS feasible in the U.S. but not in most civil-law jurisdictions. Where the collateral is chattel paper, the SPE will take possession through a custodian (or § 9-105 electronic control) for priority over earlier filers under § 9-330(a)–(b)."*
