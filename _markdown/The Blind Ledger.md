# The Blind Ledger: When 92% of Market Cap Becomes Dark Matter

*Financial statements take a snapshot of the generated, while this era's value has migrated to the generator—the causal mechanism that produces cash flow itself. You cannot photograph a function. This essay makes three claims: the true boundary of intangible assets extends far beyond patents and trademarks; the accounting system's blindness to it is by design, and that design is now turning back on itself; and the valuation apparatus built atop accounting output has therefore lost its anchor and is reorganizing in observable ways.*

---

Open NVIDIA's most recent 10-Q filed with the SEC: as of April 26, 2026, shareholders' equity stood at $195.5 billion. Yet in June 2026, as I write this, the market is pricing the company at around $4.9 trillion. Both numbers are "true": the former is constrained by accounting standards and reviewed by auditors; the latter was voted in with real money by global capital. They differ by a factor of 25. Put another way: the balance sheet explains 4% of NVIDIA's value, and the remaining 96% lies beyond the ledger's field of view.[^1]

This is not a NVIDIA anomaly; it is the norm of an entire era. In February 2026, Ocean Tomo released its Intangible Asset Market Value (IAMV) study spanning 50 years: in 1975, 83% of the market capitalization of S&P 500 constituents was made up of tangible assets (plant, equipment, inventory, and other physical capital); by the end of 2025, the ratio had completely inverted—intangibles accounted for roughly 92%, with tangibles reduced to just 8%.[^2] (This figure is often misattributed in circulation to the "Global 500"; the original study covered S&P 500 index constituents, and this essay cites it on that original basis.) The study's authors call these fifty years an "economic inversion": value migrated from what can be touched to what can be thought. The steepest stretch was from 1985 to 2005, when the share jumped from 32% to 79%—covering 47 percentage points in twenty years.

Let me lay out a methodological problem first, because it is itself part of the argument: how is the 92% calculated? The answer is subtraction. You take market cap, subtract net tangible assets, and book everything left over as "intangible."[^2][^3] In other words, 92% is not measured—it is the residual that accounting cannot explain, a mix of genuine intangible assets, market expectations, and possible bubble, with no one able to separate them cleanly. When we talk about the single largest block of value in the 2026 economy, the only instrument in our hands is an act of subtraction. That fact says more than the number 92% itself.

Astronomers facing a similar predicament invented a word: dark matter. Invisible, unmeasurable, inferred to exist only from its gravitational effects. Market cap is that gravitational effect. This essay proceeds in four steps: where the boundary of this dark matter lies, why the ledger is congenitally blind to it, how the valuation apparatus consequently loses its anchor, and where reconstruction is taking place.

## I. The True Boundary of Intangible Assets

Subtract first, then add. "Intangible assets" in the conventional sense are the small cluster that the legal department can establish title to: patents, trademarks, copyrights, franchise rights, plus the goodwill generated by mergers and acquisitions. This is the worldview from 1998, when IAS 38 was drafted. Worth noting in passing: this standard, which to this day governs intangible-asset accounting worldwide, predates the iPhone by nine years, AWS by eight, and is older than nearly every company it is meant to govern.[^4]

In 2005, economists Corrado, Hulten, and Sichel offered a far broader tripartite scheme[^5]: computerized information (software, databases), innovative property (R&D, design, mineral exploration), and economic competencies (brand, organizational capital, firm-specific human capital). Haskel and Westlake carried this framework into *Capitalism without Capital* and distilled four physical properties intangibles share: scalability (one piece of code serves a billion people, marginal cost approaching zero), sunkenness (near-zero salvage value on failure, no secondhand market), spillovers (competitors can learn it away), and synergies (intangibles combining with one another produce superlinear returns).[^6] Spillover deserves a concrete footnote: the Transformer architecture came from a 2017 public Google paper; by 2023, all eight authors had departed, and the company that turned the research into the biggest business was not Google itself. Once knowledge is written into a paper, the fence of property rights cannot hold it in. These four S's are not rhetoric—they are the physics underlying every valuation problem that follows. Set them aside here; Part III will come back for them.

But even the CHS list is still too narrow against reality. Consider a few specifics.

NVIDIA. Its true moat is not the transistors on the GPU (the process node is in TSMC's hands), but CUDA: a software stack accumulated over nearly two decades, the muscle memory of millions of developers, and the default dependency that nearly every mainstream AI framework places on it. The book value of this ecosystem is zero. It satisfies none of IAS 38's recognition criteria: its cost cannot be independently attributed, and it cannot be separated and sold apart from the enterprise as a whole. Yet it is precisely the single largest explanatory term for the gap between $4.9 trillion and $195.5 billion.

TSMC. Advanced-node yield is not written in patents. It is written in the operating habits of tens of thousands of engineers, in databases distilled from decades of incident post-mortems, and hidden in the tacit rapport with equipment suppliers. Such things have a name: organizational capital. The most striking feature of organizational capital is this: people can be poached by rivals; it cannot.

Amazon. The flywheel Bezos sketched in the early days—low prices drive traffic, traffic attracts third-party sellers, sellers enrich selection, selection spreads cost, cost supports still-lower prices. That sketch is itself an asset: a deliberately engineered, self-reinforcing causal structure. Add the six-page memo culture and the working-backwards method, and these "company operating system"–level things are extremely costly to replicate and appear nowhere on the books.

China's examples are just as dense. How much of Moutai's market cap is liquid and plant? Its real asset is a protocol written into social etiquette—what occasion calls for what liquor—plus time itself: the vintage of the base-liquor inventory is physical, but the consensus that "vintage is worth money" is intangible. ByteDance's core asset is the coupling of its recommendation algorithm with its data loop; every swipe pays tuition to the model—the more users, the more accurate the model; the more accurate the model, the more users. What makes Haidilao valuable is not the hotpot-base recipe but the organizational technology that standardizes service to the extreme while preserving frontline-employee discretion. None of this appears on any balance sheet.

Push out one more layer and the list keeps lengthening: user habits and switching costs (think of the psychological cost of dropping an input method you've used for ten years), regulatory licenses and reservoirs of trust (payment licenses, accumulated clinical data), talent density itself (after 2023, the movement of a few researchers between AI labs can directly rewrite a company's valuation), the mindshare and commitment of open-source communities. Various in form, but I would argue they share one common essence, and that essence is the correct definition of "intangible asset":

**They are all generators, not the generated.**

A factory is the generated—a form into which past cash flows have congealed. Patents, brands, algorithms, organizational processes, data flywheels are the causal mechanisms that generate cash flow itself. A patent is a generator wrapped in law; a brand is a generator resident in other people's minds; organizational capital is a generator written into process; a data flywheel is a self-reinforcing generator. The essence of financial statements is to snapshot the generated; but this era's center of value has migrated from the object of the snapshot to the function the camera cannot capture. You cannot photograph a function—only its output. This is the geometry of the blindness.

## II. Why the Ledger Is Blind

Accounting's blindness is not a bug; it is the cost of some feature. To see this clearly we must return to the design site.

Double-entry bookkeeping was systematized into writing by Pacioli in 1494, serving Venetian merchants: ships, goods, receivables and payables. Nineteenth-century railroads institutionalized depreciation, because tracks and locomotives were too expensive and too long-lived—cost had to be spread across multiple accounting periods. After the 1929 crash, U.S. securities law turned financial reporting into mandatory infrastructure; GAAP took shape in the 1930s and 1940s, facing steel mills, auto plants, rail networks. The core concepts of this system—historical cost, depreciation, inventory, construction-in-progress—were all designed for atoms. That posed no problem at the time: until 1975, atoms still made up 83%.[^2]

The blindness is built from three overlapping mechanisms.

First, the asymmetry of recognition. Under current rules (US GAAP expenses nearly all R&D; IAS 38 allows conditional capitalization in the development phase, but research-phase spending, self-created brands, customer relationships, and self-built data are all barred from the books), a brand you built up over ten years of your own investment carries a book value of zero; yet if you buy the same brand from someone else, it instantly appears on the balance sheet at the acquisition price, with the premium booked as goodwill. The same economic resource can differ by tens of billions in book value depending on whether it was built or bought. This yields an absurd but strictly valid corollary: the intangible-asset line items on a balance sheet record not how many intangibles a company owns, but how much M&A it has done. Goodwill is not a measure of assets; it is the tombstone of someone else's intangibles.

Second, the backfire of the prudence principle. Accounting conservatism was meant to prevent management from overstating assets and inflating profits—better to understate than overstate. In a world of atoms this is a virtue: a factory's value is traceable, and the cost of understatement is limited. But when the bulk of economic value becomes intangible investment, "better to understate" turns from a fuse into a generator of systematic distortion: a company that plows all its profit back into R&D and brand shows up on the statements as low-profit, low-net-worth, hard to tell apart from a mediocre, stand-pat firm. A principle designed to prevent overstatement ends up manufacturing the largest understatement in accounting history. Push any principle past the premise it was designed for, and it produces the very thing it was meant to prevent.

Third, the total victory of reliability over relevance. Historical cost is verifiable, auditable, hard to manipulate; fair value is relevant but subjective. In this perennial dilemma, standards almost always choose reliability. How large is the cost? Lev and Gu quantified it in *The End of Accounting*: regressing net income and book value against market cap, in the 1950s the two could explain 80% to 90% of the variation in market cap; over the following six decades this declined steadily, until in recent years only about half remains.[^7] They measured it from the other end too: even if you could perfectly foresee which companies will meet or beat analysts' earnings estimates, the excess return earnable from that information has approached zero. The pricing content of the earnings number itself is drying up. The market's vote-with-its-feet evidence is the proliferation of non-GAAP metrics: from 2003 to 2013, the share of listed companies disclosing non-GAAP earnings doubled from 20% to 40%.[^7] When 40% of public companies feel the statutory statements fail to explain their business and they need to compile a separate set of numbers, "the accounting system has collapsed" stops being an academic verdict and becomes a fact already in evidence and widely tacitly accepted.

Even the system's most devout user withdrew. On February 23, 2019, Buffett announced in his letter to shareholders that the opening metric he had used for nearly thirty years would be retired. That metric was the annual change in Berkshire's book value per share; the reason for retirement was that it "has lost the relevance it once had."[^8] At that year-end, Berkshire's book value per share was $212,500 and its market price $306,000: the ledger could not even explain the 44% premium on its own company. If book value cannot explain even a company like Berkshire—built on heavy assets and insurance float—whom can it explain?

### Kraft Heinz: How Blindness Becomes Incentive

Kraft Heinz deserves more ink, because what it displays is not merely blindness but how blindness manufactures perverse incentives.

In 2015, 3G Capital orchestrated the merger of Kraft and Heinz, then rolled out zero-based budgeting and slashed spending aggressively—a large chunk of which was brand-building investment. Here is the crux: brand investment is an expense in accounting. Cut it, and profit improves immediately. So in the very years brand equity was being quietly liquidated, the income statement looked its best, and capital markets saw "cost synergies realized." Then, in February 2019, the company took a one-time non-cash impairment of $15.4 billion ($7.1 billion goodwill, $8.3 billion intangibles, chiefly against the Kraft and Oscar Mayer trademarks[^9]), posted a $12.6 billion net loss for the quarter, and saw its stock fall 27% the day after the announcement—about $16 billion of market cap evaporated—while it disclosed, the same day, an SEC subpoena over procurement accounting.[^10] What followed was a $62 million settlement with the SEC and a $450 million securities class-action settlement. The whole timeline translates to one sentence: the accounting system first rewarded the liquidation of intangibles (less expense equals more profit), then years later issued a death certificate after the fact (the impairment). The sensor never sounded the alarm; on the contrary, it kept showing a pleasant temperature throughout the fire.

### The Depreciation War: The Blindness Is Full-Spectrum

If Kraft Heinz exposes blindness on the intangible-asset side, the depreciation dispute that began in November 2025 exposes a deeper layer: even the book numbers for tangible assets are now hijacked by intangible variables.

Michael Burry publicly accused the hyperscale cloud providers of inflating profits by stretching the depreciation lives of GPU servers: chip product cycles have shrunk to two or three years, yet depreciation is spread over five or six. He estimated the industry would underbook roughly $176 billion of depreciation from 2026 to 2028, with Oracle's profit inflated by 26.9% and Meta's by 20.8% by 2028.[^11] The providers' rebuttal is not without merit: old chips can serve on along a "frontier training → inference serving → internal rendering" waterfall, so a six-year life is not necessarily unrealistic. I hold my judgment on who is right. More worth watching is a detail from early 2025: Amazon shortened the depreciation life of some servers from six years to five, taking on $920 million of accelerated depreciation for it; at nearly the same time, Meta extended the life of comparable assets to five and a half years.[^12] The same silicon, opposite accounting treatments, both companies' auditors signing off. This shows that "useful life" is not an engineering fact at all but management's subjective judgment about an intangible variable—the pace of technological iteration. The book value of tangible assets, it turns out, is also a function of intangible judgment.

### National Accounts Are the Same

Pull the lens all the way back, and this blindness exists at the national level too. A joint estimate by WIPO and Luiss Business School found that, across the economies covered, intangible investment reached $7.6 trillion in 2024, already exceeding the $5.13 trillion of tangible investment on the same basis, and growing 3.7 times faster than tangible since 2008; yet over 60% of global intangible investment (brand, design, organizational knowledge) does not enter official statistics at all.[^13] Another figure gives the scale on the stock side: WIPO and Brand Finance estimate the total value of corporate intangible assets worldwide approached $100 trillion in 2025 ($97 trillion, up 23% year on year), roughly twice the combined annual GDP of the U.S. and China.[^14] It is not one company's ledger that is blind; the system of national economic accounting wholesale fails to see the economy's main engine.

### A Steelman for Accounting

In fairness, accounting's defense deserves full presentation, because it is not foolish.

The defense has two layers. First, the primary function of financial reporting was never valuation but stewardship and contracting: to show creditors the capacity to repay, to leave auditors a verifiable trail, and to give the various contracts (loan covenants, earnouts, executive pay) a hard-to-manipulate baseline number. Letting the CFO value the brand and book it himself is like swapping the goalkeeper for a striker. Second, the historical lessons are real: in the 1990s, AOL capitalized customer-acquisition marketing and WorldCom capitalized line costs—both classic cases of stuffing expenses into the balance sheet to dress up profit; Enron's mark-to-market demonstrated the extreme fate of abused subjective valuation. Capitalizing intangibles does indeed leave more room for manipulation.

I accept both points, but they do not defend the status quo, and for two reasons. First, Lev and Gu's evidence shows that even the contracting function is degrading: when the earnings number decouples from economic reality, the number written into loan covenants and pay contracts is likewise mispriced. Second, and more fundamentally: "non-recognition" and "non-disclosure" are two different things. The current system is doubly blind: it neither books nor, by and large, requires structured disclosure. The question was never "should we put a number on the brand," but that the entire information architecture stays silent on the mechanism by which value is generated. I leave this distinction to Part IV.

## III. Valuation Has Lost Its Anchor

"The valuation apparatus has collapsed" sounds like rhetoric. It is not. The collapse happens at three levels in measurable ways, each deeper than the last.

First level: ratios break down. The denominator of price-to-book comes straight from the ledger; when the ledger is blind, P/B degrades into noise. The biggest casualty is factor investing: Fama-French's value factor (HML) ranks the whole market by price-to-book, going long the cheap end and short the expensive end. Lev and Srivastava's 2022 paper in *Critical Finance Review* delivered the autopsy: the value strategy has been essentially unprofitable for nearly thirty years, with only a brief revival after the dot-com bust. One of its two causes of death is accounting: full expensing of internally generated intangibles systematically distorts book value, so the HML ranking sorts not "cheap versus expensive" but "intangible-sparse versus intangible-dense."[^15] Shorting high-P/B stocks in the 2010s amounted, in effect, to shorting R&D and software. P/E fares no better: the E of R&D-intensive companies is doubly depressed—current investment goes fully to expense, and historical investment formed no asset and so has no amortization to add back. The P/E of intangible-dense industries therefore looks systematically expensive. Comparing software companies and steel mills on P/E is like measuring blood pressure with a thermometer: the reading is precise, the object is wrong.

Second level: comparability dies. The entire legitimacy of multiples-based valuation rests on the word "comparable." But the asymmetry of recognition means that two economically identical companies—one built, one acquired—can differ wildly in net assets, profit, and ROE. The denominator of the multiple is contaminated by each company's accounting history. Anyone who has done buy-side due diligence has muscle memory for this: the first thing you do with a set of statements is recapitalize R&D and selling expenses and re-amortize them over your own assumed lives, manually rebuilding an "economic statement." That act is itself the industry's silent vote on accounting's collapse: professional pricers long ago concluded that statutory statements cannot be used directly—no one just wrote it into the textbook.

The third level is the most fundamental: intangibles change the shape of the outcome distribution, and the shape that mainstream valuation tools assume is wrong. Return to the four S's planted in Part I. Scalability plus synergy means winner-take-all—one product can take the global market at near-zero marginal cost; sunkenness means losers go to zero—failed R&D has no factory to auction and no salvage to recover. Stack the two ends, and the cross-sectional distribution of enterprise value shifts from "approximately normal, clustered around a mean" to "power-law, dominated by extreme values."

This is exactly where the ergodicity problem enters. A power law plus an absorbing zero state means: the ensemble average is dominated by a tiny minority of paths, but any specific company lives on only its own single path—the time average and the ensemble average part ways. Running a point-estimate DCF on a single intangible-dense company is, in essence, pricing the expected path, and the expected path is propped up by the tail, far from the vast majority of real paths, never actually experienced by anyone. This lens simultaneously explains two phenomena long blamed on "market irrationality." First, why the portfolio logic of venture capital works while precise valuation of a single early-stage intangible company is futile: a portfolio bets at the ensemble level, and the ensemble average is realizable for the portfolio holder; a single company's founder and concentrated shareholders can realize only the time average. Second, why the share prices of intangible-dense companies are so sensitive to marginal information: every new piece of information re-weights the tail of the entire path distribution, rather than fine-tuning a reading near a stable mean.

DCF is not dead, but it has deformed. When the balance sheet has nothing to say, the entire weight of valuation presses onto assumptions about the generator's survival (growth rate, retention rate, the half-life of the moat); terminal value's share grows distended—in a standard ten-year model, terminal value contributing 60% to 80% of present value is the norm. Valuation shifts from "pricing a pile of assets" to "pricing the survival probability of a causal mechanism." This is not a degradation of technique; the object changed and the toolbox did not.

So only one instrument is still working: the market itself. Price becomes the sole measurement of the dark matter—like astronomers inferring the distribution of dark matter from gravitational lensing, we infer the existence of intangibles from market cap. That is how Ocean Tomo computed its 92%. But this instrument has a congenital flaw: it is reflexive. Once the book anchor fails, price can only calibrate itself with price—analysts look at comparable companies, comparable companies look at each other. A fleet whose anchor chains are tied to one another moves in unison in calm weather and drifts together when the storm comes. The valuation apparatus did not die from some single error; it died from losing its external anchor.

There is also a piece of corroborating evidence that old theory has lost predictive power over the new object. By the textbook, intangible-dense companies have long-duration cash flows and low collateral value, and should be the most sensitive to interest rates; yet from 2020 to 2025, the Federal Reserve executed the most aggressive tightening cycle in forty years, and the S&P 500's intangible share sat unmoved at around 90%. Even Ocean Tomo itself concedes this stability "needs explaining" and has flagged it for a separate paper.[^2] The anomalies are accumulating. As Kuhn said, a paradigm is not refuted by argument; it is crushed by anomalies it cannot explain.

## IV. Reconstruction: Measuring the Generator

The other face of collapse is that reconstruction is already underway—just outside the standards. Look at the relative speed of three forces, and the conclusion surfaces on its own.

The fastest is inside companies. Silicon Valley firms long ago stopped using GAAP to manage themselves: North Star metrics, cohort retention curves, LTV/CAC, net revenue retention, R&D productivity. These metrics share one thing: everything they measure is the state variables and first derivatives of the generator. How fast the flywheel turns, how much the funnel leaks, whether unit economics are positive. Management accounting has quietly completed a paradigm shift internally; only external reporting still uses syntax fixed in the 1930s. Next is capital markets' self-rescue: non-GAAP metrics, KPI disclosures, the entire alternative-data industry chain (web scrapers, card-swipe data, satellite imagery, hiring feeds)—all gray infrastructure that bypasses the statutory statements and installs sensors directly on the generator. Lev and Gu's proposed "Strategic Resources & Consequences Report" (disclosing, by industry, the acquisition, maintenance, and output of key resources) is the formalized version of this direction.[^7]

The slowest is the standard-setters, slow by an order of magnitude. To give a set of time coordinates: IAS 38, which governs intangible-asset accounting worldwide, was finalized in 1998. The FASB put intangibles back on its research agenda in December 2020; more than five years on, the concrete deliverable is a targeted revision on software costs, far from a wholesale reconstruction[^16]; the IASB, at its meeting of May 19, 2026, was still at the stage of "researching users' information needs regarding recognized and unrecognized intangible assets," with no decision made.[^4] The value migration took twenty years to complete; the standards' response is measured in decades. But a detail revealed at the October 2025 joint education meeting of the two standard boards is worth savoring: investor feedback does not support booking more intangibles at valuation—what they want is disclosure that helps them understand the value-creation process.[^17] This confirms the judgment at the end of Part II: the way out is not tinkering with recognition rules (letting management value the brand only opens a new surface for manipulation), but redesigning the information architecture—mandatory, structured, auditable disclosure of the generator's operating parameters.

China runs a globally rare positive experiment on this front, worth a separate note. In August 2023, the Ministry of Finance issued the *Interim Provisions on the Accounting Treatment of Enterprise Data Resources* (Cai Kuai [2023] No. 11), effective January 1, 2024, allowing qualifying data resources to be reported as intangible assets or inventory—so-called "putting data assets on the books."[^18] Directionally this is a direct response to the blindness; but look closely at the rules and you find that on-book recognition follows the existing recognition conditions of Standard No. 6 on intangible assets and measures by the cost method: book whatever was spent.[^19] In other words, what can go on the books is only "data resources whose cost is attributable," while the part of data that is truly valuable (the flywheel effect, the coupling with algorithms, network externalities) is precisely what has no attributable cost and still cannot enter the books. Even the most radical experiment is stuck at the threshold of the atomic age: cost must be measurable. This is not a criticism; it is a precise gauge of how hard the problem is.

So what does "measuring the generator" concretely look like? I would argue the outline is already clear; what's missing is only standardization. A generator-oriented disclosure should contain at least four classes of parameters, each with verifiable anchors.

The first class is rotational speed: cohort retention, repeat frequency, net revenue retention—does the flywheel self-reinforce, and how large is the reinforcement coefficient. The second is efficiency: unit economics, customer-acquisition payback period, R&D productivity—how much new generating capacity each unit of input buys. The third is half-life: the tech stack's iteration exposure, patent expiration structure, key-talent concentration—the rate at which this generator depreciates. The fourth is coupling: ecosystem scale, API call volume, developer count, the interlock depth of data and model—whether generators can amplify one another. These numbers today are scattered across investor-relations slide decks, with definitions drifting at management's mood. Standardizing them and making them auditable is what standard-setters ought to do—rather than continuing to argue over whether the brand should be booked at fair value.

Let me close this section in the language of cybernetics. Financial reporting is the sensor in the feedback loop between the enterprise and the capital market. When the sensor goes blind, both ends of the loop drift systematically: the drift on the management end is called Kraft Heinz, where incentives point toward liquidating intangibles; the drift on the pricing end is called pure-narrative markets, where price has only story left to anchor to. And the largest redistribution during the blind period occurs in the information asymmetry itself: when public reporting fails, private telemetry becomes the source of excess return. Companies staying private ever longer, the widening edge of insiders over outsiders, alternative data selling at sky-high prices—these are three facets of the same thing: arbitrage on accounting's blindness. This is also the root reason I judge reconstruction inevitable: when information asymmetry grows large enough, the market grows new sensors of its own, and the standards merely, sooner or later, ratify a fait accompli.

## Coda: The Exoskeleton and What It Holds

Donella Meadows ranked "paradigm" as the highest leverage point for intervening in a system, and a paradigm's most concrete form is what a civilization chooses to measure. What you measure is what you manage; what the ledger cannot see, the organization systematically under-resources. Society's investment in R&D, brand, and organization-building is still, to this day, paying a discount for accounting's blindness. Double-entry bookkeeping ruled for five hundred years not because it was sacred, but because it happened to match the physical structure of those five hundred years of commerce: value congealed into countable objects. When value migrates to the generating function itself, the object of the next-generation ledger can only migrate with it.

Let me leave one picture I have not fully thought through myself. In 2026, four companies plan to spend roughly $725 billion in capital expenditure in a single year, up another 77% from the year before[^20], with Microsoft's full-year guidance alone reaching $190 billion[^21]. The largest tangible-asset construction boom in history is happening precisely at the peak where intangibles make up 92%. On the surface a paradox; taken apart, not. All this concrete, power, and silicon exists to produce and run one new class of asset: model weights. A digital file of a few hundred gigabytes to a few terabytes, close to the compressed state of pure knowledge. It has no meaningful historical cost (electricity, data licensing, the spillover from decades of public research—which of these counts as its cost?), no observable market price, no measurement attribute an auditor knows how to sign off on—yet it is the entire purpose of all this spending. Accounting can see the $725 billion exoskeleton; it cannot see what the exoskeleton holds.

When Pacioli wrote down the rules of double-entry bookkeeping in 1494, a Venetian merchant's entire fortune was loaded onto ships. Today the most valuable asset is loaded into a file. And we are still using the same set of rules to account for it—more precisely, not to account for it at all.
