# Abstract

Umbrella clauses remain among the most contested provisions in international investment law. This paper proposes that tribunals adopt a rebuttable presumption against elevating contract breaches into treaty violations unless two cumulative conditions are satisfied: first, the umbrella clause text clearly and unambiguously covers contractual obligations "with regard to" specific investments; and second, the impugned state conduct involves an exercise of governmental authority (*jure imperii*) rather than mere commercial performance or non-performance (*jure gestionis*). Through comparative analysis of the Switzerland--Pakistan and Switzerland--Philippines BITs and the landmark decisions in *SGS v. Pakistan* (2003) and *SGS v. Philippines* (2004), with brief reference to *Noble Ventures v. Romania* (2005), this paper argues that the restrictive reading in *SGS v. Pakistan* is doctrinally sounder under Article 31 of the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, and that Article 32 strengthens rather than weakens this conclusion by exposing the limits of treating fine textual differences as conclusive proof of negotiator intent. Recent Most-Favoured-Nation jurisprudence, including *Grupo Energía Bogotá v. Guatemala* (2023) and *Orazul v. Argentina* (2023), reinforces the principle that extraordinary treaty effects require explicit state consent. The proposed Two-Step Test connects interpretive methodology to broader concerns about regulatory autonomy, contractual discipline, and ISDS legitimacy. The framework is primarily addressed to tribunals interpreting legacy BITs, and secondarily to drafters seeking to close the interpretive gap prospectively; contemporary drafting trends toward narrow or omitted umbrella clauses---exemplified by the 2012 U.S. Model BIT and the 2019 Netherlands Model BIT---confirm its alignment with an emerging recalibration in state practice.

# I. Introduction

The question whether umbrella clauses transform ordinary contract disputes into international treaty claims has divided investment arbitration tribunals for two decades. No doctrinal fissure better illustrates this division than the contrast between *SGS Société Générale de Surveillance S.A. v. Islamic Republic of Pakistan*[^1] and *SGS Société Générale de Surveillance S.A. v. Republic of the Philippines*,[^2] two decisions issued within less than six months of each other concerning nearly identical factual settings yet reaching diametrically opposed conclusions on the scope of umbrella clause protection.

This paper advances a structured approach to umbrella clause interpretation built on a rebuttable presumption against elevation. The framework proposed here synthesizes existing jurisprudential strands into a coherent test that tribunals can operationalize. That presumption may be overcome only where both of the following conditions are satisfied:

1.  **Textual clarity**: The umbrella clause wording unmistakably covers contractual obligations "with regard to" specific investments, assessed under the interpretive framework of Article 31 of the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT), and tested, where evidence permits, against Article 32 supplementary means.[^3]

2.  **Sovereign conduct**: The state's alleged breach involves an exercise of governmental authority (*jure imperii*), regulatory, legislative, or administrative interference, rather than ordinary commercial conduct (*jure gestionis*) such as non-payment of invoices or routine performance disputes.

On this basis, the restrictive reading in *SGS v. Pakistan* represents the doctrinally sounder approach. The expansive interpretation in *SGS v. Philippines*, while defensible on its particular treaty language, rightly tempered its jurisdictional finding by staying proceedings to respect the contractual forum: a procedural caution that implicitly acknowledges the need for additional filtering mechanisms.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it integrates the interpretive discipline of VCLT Articles 31 and 32 into umbrella clause doctrine, arguing that textual analysis must be tested against, and where necessary disciplined by, supplementary means of interpretation---while recognizing that the limited travaux record for most BITs counsels a default rule of restraint rather than untethered intuitions about state intent. Second, it synthesizes recent Most-Favoured-Nation (MFN) jurisprudence on attempted importation of umbrella clauses with the umbrella clause doctrine itself, demonstrating that both rest on a common principle: tribunals should not infer broad treaty protections from ambiguous or silent text. Third, it operationalizes the *jure imperii* distinction into a practical decision framework that tribunals can apply, incorporating an anti-abuse safeguard to prevent states from recharacterizing sovereign measures as "mere contract" disputes. Together, these contributions connect interpretive methodology to the broader right-to-regulate discourse and to concerns about ISDS legitimacy, which Section VI develops in full.

A clarification of audience is warranted. The framework proposed here is primarily addressed to tribunals currently interpreting legacy BITs, which remain the dominant substrate of ISDS practice and which frequently contain older, template-derived umbrella clauses whose drafting history is thin or unavailable. For those tribunals, the Two-Step Test offers a structured default that coordinates fragmented jurisprudence without requiring implausible reconstructions of negotiator intent. At the same time, the framework is addressed secondarily to treaty drafters. Interpretive practice and treaty design operate as a feedback loop: tribunals read ambiguous legacy text, states observe jurisprudential outcomes, and subsequent drafting responds. The Netherlands Model BIT's explicit "exercise of governmental authority" requirement, the 2012 U.S. Model BIT's deliberate omission of any umbrella clause, and UNCTAD's documentation of narrowing treaty language illustrate how states can encode interpretive defaults directly into treaty text. Section VIII returns to this feedback loop and its implications for drafting.

The normative stakes are considerable. In sectors such as energy, infrastructure, and extractive industries, states often assume hybrid roles: as regulators setting tariffs and license conditions, and as commercial counterparties in power purchase agreements, concessions, and capacity payment schemes. An expansive umbrella clause doctrine risks collapsing these roles, transforming virtually any tariff adjustment, license modification, or renegotiation into a potential treaty claim. The wave of renewable-energy arbitrations against Spain under the Energy Charter Treaty, exemplified by *Charanne*, *Eiser*, and *Novenergia*, illustrates how regulatory changes in heavily regulated sectors can generate serial arbitration claims.[^4] Although these were not umbrella clause cases, they demonstrate the systemic exposure that an unconstrained umbrella doctrine would amplify.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section II presents the relevant treaty texts and applies the interpretive framework of Articles 31 and 32 of the VCLT, explicitly addressing whether fine textual distinctions can bear the weight often placed on them. Section III analyzes the *SGS* decisions and *Noble Ventures* through a parallel analytical structure. Section IV shows how recent MFN jurisprudence reinforces the explicit-consent principle underlying the proposed test. Section V synthesizes these findings into an operational two-step framework. Section VI articulates the normative justifications for interpretive restraint. Section VII addresses investor-side counterarguments. Section VIII concludes with drafting implications aligned with contemporary treaty practice, returning to the audience and feedback-loop distinction introduced above.

## B. VCLT Article 31: Ordinary Meaning, Context, and Purpose

Article 31(1) of the VCLT provides that a treaty "shall be interpreted in good faith in accordance with the ordinary meaning to be given to the terms of the treaty in their context and in the light of its object and purpose."[^5] Applying this framework reveals why the Switzerland--Pakistan clause lends itself to a restrictive reading.

Ordinary meaning. The term "commitments" in the Switzerland--Pakistan BIT is less expansive than "any obligation" in the Switzerland--Philippines and U.S.--Romania formulations. "Commitments" may connote voluntary undertakings of a particular character, whereas "any obligation" encompasses the full range of legal duties, contractual or otherwise. The phrase "guarantee the observance" imposes a secondary obligation (to ensure compliance) rather than a primary obligation (to observe directly), introducing interpretive ambiguity about whether the clause creates an independent cause of action or merely reinforces other treaty standards.

Context. The location of umbrella clauses at the end of BITs, often following dispute resolution provisions, has prompted debate about their status. The *SGS v. Pakistan* tribunal found this placement suggestive of a non-substantive or confirmatory function.[^6] The *SGS v. Philippines* tribunal dismissed this reasoning and criticized interpretations that deprive the umbrella clause of operative effect.[^7] On balance, placement alone is inconclusive, but it does not support reading extraordinary effects into ambiguous text.

Object and purpose. Investment treaties pursue dual objectives: investor protection and preservation of regulatory space. Neither objective should eclipse the other. An expansive umbrella clause reading that elevates every contract breach into a treaty claim subordinates the regulatory-space objective to investor protection without clear textual warrant. Where the object and purpose are balanced, ambiguous text should not be construed to produce "extraordinary" results, a concern the *SGS v. Pakistan* tribunal explicitly articulated.[^8]

On this analysis, the Switzerland--Pakistan clause, with its narrower "commitments" language and passive "guarantee" construction, does not clearly authorize elevation. The Switzerland--Philippines clause, with its "any obligation ... with regard to specific investments" formulation, has stronger textual credentials for Step 1. But textual analysis of this kind invites an obvious and important challenge: how much weight can fine-grained comparisons of treaty wording actually bear as evidence of negotiator intent? The next subsection takes that challenge seriously, engaging Article 32 of the VCLT to discipline the foregoing analysis.

## C. VCLT Article 32: Supplementary Means and the Limits of Textual Inference

The foregoing Article 31 analysis can and should be pressed by a question of state intent. Does the distinction between "commitments" and "any obligation," or between "guarantee the observance" and "shall observe," reliably capture differences in what the contracting parties actually meant at the time of drafting? Or is the textual analysis asking treaty language to do more interpretive work *ex post* than negotiators ever intended it to bear? This question is not rhetorical. It is raised fairly whenever finely drawn textual inferences carry large doctrinal consequences, and the umbrella clause literature has increasingly acknowledged it.[^9]

Article 32 of the VCLT provides for recourse to supplementary means of interpretation, including the preparatory work of the treaty and the circumstances of its conclusion, either to confirm the meaning resulting from the application of Article 31, or to determine the meaning where Article 31 leaves it ambiguous or obscure or leads to a manifestly absurd or unreasonable result.[^10] In the umbrella clause context, Article 32 should therefore operate as a discipline on Article 31 reasoning, not as an invitation to speculate about intent. When textual distinctions are asked to carry major consequences, supplementary means should be consulted where they exist; where they do not exist, their absence should itself make tribunals more cautious about imputing finely calibrated choices to the parties. Three observations must be made candidly.

First, the empirical record supporting finely drawn intentional distinctions between umbrella clause formulations is thin. Most BITs of the 1990s, including both the Switzerland--Pakistan and the Switzerland--Philippines BITs, were negotiated quickly from treaty templates rather than drafted *ab initio*. Lauge Poulsen's study of BIT programs in developing countries documents how states often signed BITs on the basis of bounded rationality and template diffusion rather than careful cost-benefit calibration.[^11] Against that background, imputing to the parties a deliberate decision to use "commitments" rather than "any obligation," or "guarantee the observance" rather than "shall observe," risks reading too much into language that may reflect template inheritance more than negotiated choice.

Second, travaux préparatoires for most BITs are either unpublished or substantively thin. Unlike multilateral conventions such as the VCLT itself, bilateral investment treaty negotiations rarely generate rich interpretive records. Tribunals deploying Article 32 in this area therefore face an evidentiary gap: the supplementary means often do not reveal a clear answer one way or the other on clauses such as Article 11 of the Switzerland--Pakistan BIT or Article X(2) of the Switzerland--Philippines BIT. The Swiss government's post-decision intervention in *SGS v. Pakistan*, expressing "concern" about the tribunal's reasoning, is itself instructive: it is not travaux in the Article 32 sense but rather a later-stated view by one contracting party. Tribunals rightly treat such interventions with caution precisely because they risk being shaped by the litigation then before them.

Third, and following from the preceding two points, a candid acknowledgment is required: some of the textual distinctions on which umbrella clause jurisprudence has come to turn may be finer than drafters actually intended them to bear. This is a real limitation of any heavily text-centric approach, and it must not be hidden. Recognizing it, however, does not undermine the proposed Two-Step Test; it clarifies the Test's function.

Article 32 plays two complementary roles here. Where supplementary means are available and point clearly in a particular direction, they may confirm or, in cases of manifest ambiguity, determine the Article 31 reading. In the absence of such evidence---the typical situation in BIT practice---the absence of supplementary confirmation should discipline the Article 31 analysis rather than free tribunals to infer broad effects from uncertain text. The presumption against elevation proposed in Step 1 performs precisely this function. Where negotiators' intent cannot be reconstructed with confidence, tribunals face a choice between two forms of *ex post* interpretation: one that reads extraordinary effects into ambiguous text, thereby expanding state obligations beyond clearly negotiated consent; and one that withholds such effects absent clear textual warrant, thereby preserving the baseline of state consent. Placing the default against expansion is not an independent Article 32 rule. It is a consent-based default that follows from applying Articles 31 and 32 together when the ordinary meaning remains uncertain and supplementary means do not clearly resolve the uncertainty.

Two implications follow. First, the Swiss government's later-articulated discomfort in *SGS v. Pakistan*, while not dispositive, is a data point suggesting that the restrictive reading produced results which at least one contracting party found surprising. That cuts both ways: it supports neither a return to expansive elevation nor its inverse. It does indicate that the interpretive terrain was and remains genuinely contested---which is itself an argument for a stable default rule rather than case-by-case intuitionism.

Second, modern drafting practice should be used carefully. The evolution of umbrella clause language, traced in Section VI.D below and catalogued in Appendix B, is not Article 32 evidence of what Switzerland, Pakistan, or the Philippines intended in the mid-1990s. It is not travaux for those treaties, nor is it subsequent practice in their application. Its proper role is different: it demonstrates how states, having observed the jurisprudential split, have begun to resolve the same problem prospectively through drafting. States have increasingly omitted umbrella clauses (2012 U.S. Model BIT; CPTPP; CETA; India Model BIT) or narrowed them to governmental-authority breaches (Netherlands Model BIT 2019). This drafting evolution supports the paper's secondary audience claim: interpretive controversy feeds back into treaty design, but it should not be smuggled into Article 32 as proof of original intent.

In sum, Article 32 neither rescues the expansive reading nor dethrones the restrictive one. It disciplines Article 31 analysis by exposing the limits of what textual distinctions can be made to carry, and it makes the case for restraint stronger where supplementary means fail to confirm that the parties actually intended ordinary contract breaches to become treaty breaches. This is the methodological contribution of the proposed framework: not that text alone solves the problem, but that a text-anchored presumption, disciplined by Article 32, produces more stable and more legitimate outcomes than untethered intuitions about treaty purpose. The case studies in Section III apply this disciplined approach to the two landmark *SGS* decisions.

# III. Case Studies

## A. SGS v. Pakistan (2003)

### 1. Background and Treaty Provisions

SGS, a Swiss company providing inspection and verification services globally, contracted with Pakistan in 1994 to provide pre-shipment inspection services under a "PSI Agreement." The contract was substantial: SGS would inspect goods destined for import into Pakistan to verify customs classifications, quantities, and values, thereby assisting Pakistan's customs administration. Critically, the PSI Agreement included a comprehensive dispute resolution mechanism: a forum-selection clause designating Pakistani courts and arbitration under the Pakistani Arbitration Act as the exclusive mechanisms for resolving disputes arising under the contract.

When payment disputes arose, Pakistan allegedly owed SGS approximately USD 8.4 million in unpaid invoices, with SGS seeking substantially larger sums in damages. The procedural sequence became complex. Pakistan initiated arbitration under the PSI Agreement in 2000. SGS subsequently filed an ICSID arbitration request in October 2001, invoking the Switzerland--Pakistan BIT and claiming both treaty violations (failure to accord fair and equitable treatment, expropriation) and elevation of contract breaches through the umbrella clause.

Article 11 of the Switzerland--Pakistan BIT provides: "Either Contracting Party shall constantly guarantee the observance of the commitments it has entered into with respect to the investments of the investors of the other Contracting Party." This formulation differs materially from the Switzerland--Philippines clause, as noted in Section II.A above.

### 2. Tribunal's Interpretive Approach

The tribunal, chaired by Judge Florentino P. Feliciano with Mr. André Faurès and Mr. J. Christopher Thomas QC as co-arbitrators, adopted a sharp separation between treaty claims and contract claims. The tribunal's analysis proceeded through several stages of reasoning.

First, the tribunal examined whether Article 11 could convert a contract breach into a treaty breach. It declined to so hold, reasoning that such an interpretation would effect an "indefinite expansion" of treaty obligations.[^12] The tribunal emphasized that the clause's language---"guarantee the observance of the commitments"---was not unambiguously directed at contractual undertakings. Moreover, interpreting the clause to elevate all contract breaches would transform ICSID into a forum for "an unlimited number of State contracts" without clear evidence that the contracting states intended this extraordinary result.

Second, the tribunal considered the interpretive principle that treaty provisions should not be assumed to have dramatic effects absent clear textual warrant. The tribunal quoted the International Court of Justice's cautionary statement that international instruments should be interpreted with restraint, particularly when far-reaching consequences would follow from broad readings. To hold that every contract breach becomes a treaty breach would, the tribunal noted, fundamentally alter the calculus of sovereign risk in international commerce.

Third, the tribunal gave substantial weight to the PSI Agreement's forum-selection clause, treating it as reflecting the parties' negotiated expectations about where contract disputes would be resolved. The forum-selection clause represented a bargain: SGS accepted Pakistani dispute resolution as a condition of obtaining the contract. To permit ICSID jurisdiction over contract claims would, in the tribunal's view, "override the specific dispute-settlement arrangements" that SGS and Pakistan had explicitly agreed upon.[^13] The tribunal distinguished between the treaty's offer of ICSID arbitration for treaty claims and the contract's designation of Pakistani arbitration for contract claims.

Fourth, the tribunal addressed the Swiss government's intervention. In an unusual step, Switzerland submitted observations expressing "concern" about the tribunal's reasoning and its potential impact on the BIT's umbrella clause. The tribunal acknowledged this intervention but maintained its position, noting that interpretive authority rested with the tribunal applying standard principles of treaty interpretation, not with the unilateral views of one contracting party. As Section II.C discussed, this post-decision intervention is best read as evidence of genuine contestation rather than as reliable Article 32 material.

### 3. Classification and Implications

Under the standard typology of umbrella clause interpretations---"elevate," "incorporate," or "no independent effect"---the *SGS v. Pakistan* tribunal's reasoning places Article 11 in the "no independent effect" or, at most, "incorporate-with-limits" category. The clause might reinforce other treaty standards or serve as an interpretive guide, but it does not independently convert contract breaches into treaty breaches cognizable before ICSID.

This outcome supports the first step of the proposed test: where umbrella clause text is ambiguous, tribunals should apply a presumption against elevation. The inquiry can appropriately end at Step 1 without proceeding to the sovereign-conduct filter, because the textual threshold is not satisfied. The *SGS v. Pakistan* decision thus represents a paradigm of interpretive restraint grounded in textual analysis, respect for contractual arrangements, and concern about systemic consequences.

## B. SGS v. Philippines (2004)

### 1. Background and Treaty Provisions

SGS contracted with the Philippines in 1991 under a "CISS Agreement" (Comprehensive Import Supervision Services Agreement) for comprehensive import supervision services. The contract's scope was similar to the Pakistan arrangement: SGS would provide pre-shipment inspection services to assist Philippine customs authorities. The agreement specified a payment mechanism based on a percentage of the value of inspected imports. Like the PSI Agreement in Pakistan, the CISS Agreement included a forum-selection clause, though this one designated Philippine courts and local arbitration rather than a specific arbitral mechanism.

When payment disputes arose in the late 1990s, the Philippines allegedly owed SGS substantial sums. According to the tribunal's decision, SGS claimed CHF 202,413,047.36 in unpaid billings under the CISS Agreement.[^14] Unlike the Pakistan situation, there was no prior contractual arbitration when SGS initiated ICSID proceedings in 2002 under the Switzerland--Philippines BIT. This procedural difference, however, did not materially affect the tribunal's jurisdictional analysis regarding the umbrella clause.

Article X(2) of the Switzerland--Philippines BIT provides: "Each Contracting Party shall observe any obligation it has assumed with regard to specific investments in its territory by investors of the other Contracting Party." This formulation is materially stronger than the Pakistan clause in several respects detailed in Section II.A above.

### 2. Tribunal's Interpretive Approach

The tribunal, composed of Dr. Ahmed El-Kosheri (President), Professor James Crawford, and Mr. Antonio Crivellaro, adopted a text-driven expansive reading of Article X(2). The tribunal's analysis directly engaged with and explicitly criticized the earlier *SGS v. Pakistan* decision, creating the famous doctrinal split that has animated umbrella clause jurisprudence ever since.

The tribunal began with textual analysis. It emphasized that "any obligation ... with regard to specific investments" is linguistically capable of encompassing contractual undertakings related to a particular investment.[^15] The phrase "any obligation" is not limited to public law obligations, treaty commitments, or international law duties; on its face, it includes contractual obligations that the state has assumed. The tribunal stated that the provision "means what it says," a phrase that has become synonymous with the expansive approach.

The tribunal explicitly criticized the *SGS v. Pakistan* reasoning. It found that the earlier tribunal's analysis had "failed to give any clear meaning to the 'umbrella clause,'" rendering it a "very much empty vessel."[^16] The *SGS v. Philippines* tribunal invoked the principle of *effet utile*, that treaty provisions should be interpreted to have a meaningful effect rather than to be rendered nugatory. If the umbrella clause does not cover contractual obligations, the tribunal asked, what does it cover? States already have treaty obligations under FET, expropriation, and other standards; an umbrella clause that merely restated existing treaty protections would serve no independent function.

The tribunal also addressed the question of "extraordinary" results. While acknowledging that elevating contract breaches to treaty status is significant, the tribunal found that the text clearly warranted this result and that parties entering into BITs with such language must be taken to have consented to this consequence. The tribunal distinguished between reading effects into ambiguous text (impermissible) and giving effect to clear text (required by good-faith interpretation).

Critically, however, the tribunal did not proceed directly to the merits of SGS's contract claims. Despite accepting jurisdiction over the umbrella clause claim, it stayed proceedings pending resolution of the contract dispute in the contractual forum.[^17] The tribunal reasoned that establishing a breach of the umbrella clause required first establishing a breach of the underlying contract, a determination that the parties had agreed should be made by Philippine courts or local arbitrators. To proceed directly to quantifying treaty damages without first resolving the contract dispute would improperly bypass the contractual mechanism and potentially produce inconsistent results.

The stay thus reflected procedural coordination rather than abdication of treaty jurisdiction. The tribunal retained jurisdiction but deferred its exercise until the contractually designated forum had addressed the foundational question of contractual breach. This approach---asserting jurisdiction while coordinating with contractual fora---has influenced subsequent tribunals addressing similar situations.

### 3. Classification and Implications

Article X(2) functions as an "elevate" clause under the standard typology: its text is sufficiently clear to bring contractual obligations within the treaty's protective umbrella, satisfying Step 1 of the proposed test. The tribunal's textual analysis, emphasizing the breadth of "any obligation" and the explicit nexus to "specific investments," demonstrates how clear language can meet the threshold.

Yet the tribunal's stay indicates that even "elevate" clauses do not automatically displace contractual fora or permit immediate progression to treaty damages. The stay implicitly acknowledges that additional considerations---including efficiency, consistency, and respect for party autonomy---remain relevant even when jurisdiction is established. More broadly, it suggests that textual clarity, while necessary, may not be sufficient to determine how umbrella clause claims should proceed.

This outcome motivates Step 2 of the proposed test. Even where text clearly encompasses contractual obligations, not every contractual non-performance should proceed directly to treaty merits and damages. Distinguishing sovereign interference from ordinary commercial breach provides the necessary substantive filter. The *SGS v. Philippines* tribunal's procedural caution---its recognition that not all contract disputes are equally suited for treaty resolution---supports the principle that elevation should be reserved for cases involving genuinely sovereign conduct.

## C. Noble Ventures v. Romania (2005)

### 1. Overview

*Noble Ventures, Inc. v. Romania* involved a privatization agreement under which Romania sold a steel company to an American investor.[^18] When disputes arose, Noble Ventures filed an ICSID claim under the U.S.--Romania BIT, invoking Article II(2)(c): "Each Party shall observe any obligation it may have entered into with regard to investments."

### 2. Tribunal's Approach

The tribunal treated Article II(2)(c) as capable of elevating contractual commitments to treaty status where the text and context supported this result.[^19] It noted that the wording closely mirrors the Switzerland--Philippines formulation and found that Romania's undertakings in the privatization agreement fell within the clause's scope. The tribunal also considered privity and attribution issues, asking whether the state was properly bound by the relevant commitments.

### 3. Implications for the Two-Step Test

*Noble Ventures* reinforces Step 1: where treaty language explicitly covers obligations "with regard to investments," elevation is textually justified. The case confirms that wording and context together determine classification, and that even among "elevate" clauses, scope depends on specific treaty language and the nature of the underlying commitment. Moreover, a privatization agreement executed through formal governmental privatization authority is a paradigm setting where, if Step 1 is cleared, Step 2 is also likely satisfied---the sovereign acts of transferring state assets and issuing privatization commitments are not conduct any private party could undertake. The next section demonstrates that recent MFN jurisprudence further supports the explicit-consent principle underlying this textual approach.

# IV. MFN and Umbrella Clauses: The Explicit-Consent Principle

Recent jurisprudence on MFN clauses and umbrella clause importation reinforces the interpretive principle underlying Step 1 of the proposed test: extraordinary treaty effects require explicit state consent, and tribunals should not infer broad protections from ambiguous or silent text. This section traces that principle through two 2023 decisions before returning to the operational framework.

## A. Grupo Energía Bogotá v. Guatemala (2023)

In this case arising under the Colombia--El Salvador--Guatemala--Honduras Free Trade Agreement (FTA), the investors sought to import umbrella clauses from ten third-state treaties via the FTA's MFN clause.[^20] The FTA itself contains no umbrella clause, so the claimants argued that MFN treatment entitled them to the benefit of umbrella clauses in Guatemala's other investment agreements. The tribunal rejected this attempt.[^21] It emphasized that umbrella clause protection requires explicit consent in the base treaty and cannot be acquired through MFN mechanisms. This reasoning rests on the same foundation as a restrictive umbrella clause reading: states must clearly and specifically agree to protections that transform the character of their international obligations.

## B. Orazul v. Argentina (2023)

The *Orazul* tribunal, following the earlier *Teinver* decision,[^22] held that under the Argentina--Spain BIT, an umbrella clause sits outside the scope of "matters governed by the agreement" to which the MFN clause applies.[^23] Consequently, umbrella clause protection cannot be imported via MFN. The tribunal granted Argentina considerable margin of appreciation for crisis measures in the electricity sector, an outcome aligned with the sovereign-conduct analysis proposed in Step 2.

## C. Connection to the Two-Step Test

Although these are MFN cases, they reinforce Step 1's presumption against elevation. Just as MFN cannot serve as a back door to umbrella protection, ambiguous umbrella clause text should not achieve what explicit drafting did not provide. Both doctrines rest on the principle that turning every contract breach into a treaty breach is an "extraordinary" result that requires clear and specific consent. The MFN jurisprudence and the restrictive umbrella clause reading share a common methodological foundation: interpretive restraint in the face of textual ambiguity. With that shared foundation established, the analysis now turns to operationalizing the framework.

# V. The Two-Step Test: An Operational Framework

## A. Overview and Sequential Operation

Building on the doctrinal analysis in Sections II through IV, this section synthesizes the findings into a proposed operational framework. The test is offered as a normatively desirable and doctrinally grounded approach to umbrella clause interpretation, not as a description of current tribunal consensus, which remains fragmented. The proposed test operates sequentially:

**Step 1 (Threshold):** Does the umbrella clause text clearly and unambiguously cover contractual obligations "with regard to" specific investments? Apply VCLT Article 31 analysis to the text, and test the result against Article 32 supplementary means where available. If the text is ambiguous, and Article 32 does not clearly resolve the ambiguity, apply a presumption against elevation. The inquiry ends, and the claim is not elevated.

**Step 2 (Filter):** If Step 1 is satisfied, does the impugned conduct involve an exercise of governmental authority (*jure imperii*)? Only breaches involving sovereign conduct---regulatory, legislative, or administrative interference---should be elevated. Purely commercial non-payment or performance disputes remain contract claims routed to the contractual forum.

## B. Step 1: Clause Clarity under VCLT Articles 31 and 32

The textual analysis compares wording along several dimensions:

-   **Scope of covered obligations**: "Commitments" (narrower) versus "any obligation" (broader).

-   **Nexus requirement**: "With respect to investments" (general) versus "with regard to specific investments" (explicit).

-   **Grammatical construction**: "Guarantee the observance" (secondary) versus "shall observe" (primary).

Where text is ambiguous, and Article 32 does not resolve the ambiguity on the basis of available supplementary means, tribunals should apply a rebuttable presumption against elevation, consistent with the principle that transforming every contract breach into a treaty breach is extraordinary and requires clear consent. This approach respects the dual object and purpose of investment treaties (protecting investors while preserving regulatory space) by declining to read one objective as eclipsing the other in the absence of clear textual warrant. As Section II.C argued, the default-rule function of Step 1 is what gives the framework its value in the typical BIT situation where travaux are thin and fine textual inferences may otherwise outrun their evidentiary support. The absence of Article 32 confirmation does not prove that a restrictive reading is historically correct; it means only that a tribunal lacks a sufficient basis to impose the broader consequence.

## C. Step 2: Sovereign Character (Jure Imperii) and the Anti-Abuse Safeguard

Even where umbrella clause text satisfies Step 1's clarity threshold, tribunals must assess whether the alleged breach involves sovereign conduct. This distinction between governmental acts (*jure imperii*) and commercial acts (*jure gestionis*) has deep roots in international law, particularly in sovereign immunity doctrine, and provides a principled basis for filtering umbrella clause claims.

Drawing on decisions such as *El Paso v. Argentina*, *Sempra v. Argentina*, and *CSOB v. Slovakia*,[^24] *jure imperii* encompasses conduct that only a state can perform: legislation, regulation, taxation, decrees, exercise of enforcement powers, administrative orders, or other governmental prerogatives. *Jure gestionis*, by contrast, refers to commercial acts that any private party could undertake: payment of invoices, delivery of goods, routine performance under contract, ordinary business decisions about timing or methodology of performance.

In terms of practical indicators of *jure imperii*, tribunals should ask whether the impugned conduct required the exercise of distinctively governmental powers. Relevant indicators include: legislative or regulatory action affecting the investment (e.g., changes to tariff structures, revocation or modification of licenses, emergency decrees); exercise of enforcement powers (e.g., seizure of assets, injunctions issued through public authority, deployment of police powers); administrative decisions issued under public law frameworks (e.g., permit denials, environmental compliance orders, tax assessments); sovereign acts directed at nullifying or substantially modifying contractual rights through governmental authority; and use of compulsory powers unavailable to private parties (e.g., eminent domain, regulatory taking, mandatory renegotiation under sector legislation).

By contrast, regarding *jure gestionis*, conduct falling on the commercial side includes: non-payment of invoices without any regulatory justification or governmental directive; ordinary disputes about contract interpretation, scope of services, or quality of performance; business decisions about timing, scheduling, or resource allocation; negotiating positions in commercial renegotiations where no sovereign compulsion is involved; and failure to perform due to commercial considerations (e.g., budget constraints, personnel changes, procurement delays) rather than governmental decisions.

The filter's rationale is that umbrella clauses exist to protect investors against sovereign abuse---the state wielding governmental power to undermine investment-related undertakings---not to insure against ordinary commercial risk. A state that legislatively nullifies a contract, expropriates contractual rights through regulatory action, or uses sovereign powers to prevent contract performance has engaged in conduct properly subject to treaty scrutiny. A state that simply fails to pay an invoice, without any exercise of governmental authority, has engaged in conduct properly subject to the contractual forum. The investor in the latter scenario has commercial remedies; the investor in the former scenario may need international protection because the state has used its unique powers to evade ordinary remedies.

An anti-abuse safeguard is essential. The *jure imperii* filter must not become a tool for states to escape treaty liability for genuine sovereign abuse by recharacterizing governmental interference as "mere contract" disputes. The filter works both ways: just as commercial disputes should not be elevated, sovereign abuse should not be disguised.

The *Vivendi I* Annulment Committee established the foundational principle that contract and treaty claims are analytically distinct, and that a contract forum clause cannot block an international tribunal from characterizing conduct as a treaty breach when the "essential basis" of the claim is the treaty itself.[^25] If a state terminates a contract through ordinary contractual mechanisms (e.g., exercising a termination for convenience clause), that is commercial conduct. But if a state uses regulatory power to declare a contract void, revokes necessary licenses to make performance impossible, or deploys administrative action to prevent the investor from exercising contractual rights, the governmental character of the conduct brings it within umbrella clause protection regardless of how the state labels its actions.

The *SGS v. Philippines* tribunal likewise recognized that treaty and contract claims can coexist.[^26] An investor might have a contract claim for breach of a payment obligation and a treaty claim for regulatory interference that prevented collection; these are distinct injuries flowing from distinct conduct, even if related to the same investment relationship.

The dividing line is functional rather than formal: regulatory or legislative interference that only a sovereign can effect goes to the treaty forum; pure commercial performance disputes go to the contract forum. Where conduct is genuinely hybrid, involving both commercial non-performance and sovereign interference, tribunals should assess whether the sovereign element is the "essential basis" of the claim. If the governmental interference is what caused the loss, and the commercial non-performance is merely derivative, the treaty claim is appropriate. If the commercial dispute would exist regardless of any governmental action, the contract forum is appropriate.

## D. Corollaries

Tribunals should accord significant deference to contractually designated dispute resolution mechanisms. Where a contract stipulates a specific forum for resolving disputes, international tribunals ought to utilize stays of proceedings or deferrals, allowing that forum to determine contested factual or legal issues strictly within its mandate. The procedural approach adopted in *SGS v. Philippines*, which stayed treaty proceedings pending the resolution of the underlying contractual dispute, exemplifies this necessary coordination between treaty and contract jurisdictions.

The requirement for textual clarity further precludes the importation of umbrella clause protections through MFN provisions. Consistent with the recent decisions in *Grupo Energía Bogotá v. Guatemala* and *Orazul v. Argentina*, the umbrella clause constitutes a specific substantive obligation that requires explicit state intent in the base treaty. Allowing its importation via MFN clauses would contravene the principle that extraordinary treaty effects cannot be inferred from silence or ambiguity.

Finally, the framework operates within the boundaries of standard privity and attribution rules under customary international law. The application of any umbrella clause is conditioned by the *ILC Articles on State Responsibility*: the state must be properly bound by the commitment in question, and the impugned conduct must be legally attributable to the state for the treaty provision to be engaged.

# VI. Normative Payoff: Why Interpretive Restraint Matters

Having established the operational framework, this section turns from doctrinal construction to normative justification. The Two-Step Test is not merely a tidier way to read contested clauses; its value lies in the substantive goods it serves: preservation of regulatory space, ISDS legitimacy, predictability, and alignment with contemporary state practice. Each is addressed in turn.

## A. Preserving the Right to Regulate

States must retain space to respond to market failures, environmental imperatives, fiscal constraints, and public-health emergencies. The right to regulate, sometimes framed as "policy space" or "regulatory autonomy," is not merely a defensive posture against investor claims but a prerequisite for effective governance. In sectors such as energy, infrastructure, telecommunications, and extractive industries, regulatory flexibility is crucial for states to achieve legitimate public objectives.

The tension becomes acute when states act in hybrid capacities: as regulators establishing the framework within which investments operate, and as commercial counterparties in specific investment relationships. A utility investor may hold a concession contract specifying tariff levels while also being subject to a regulatory regime that permits tariff adjustments under defined conditions. An energy investor may have a power purchase agreement while facing evolving environmental regulations. These overlapping relationships are ubiquitous in regulated sectors.

The Spanish renewable-energy cases under the Energy Charter Treaty (including *Charanne*, *Eiser*, *Novenergia*) demonstrate how changes to regulatory frameworks can generate high-stakes treaty litigation.[^27] Spain modified its renewable-energy support regime in response to fiscal pressures and electricity market developments, reducing subsidies that investors had anticipated would continue. Investors brought claims under the FET standard and, in some cases, under expropriation provisions. Although these cases involved FET and expropriation claims rather than umbrella clauses, they illustrate the systemic exposure that an expansive umbrella doctrine would magnify.

Consider the implications if umbrella clauses routinely elevated contract claims in this context. Every tariff-setting instrument, feed-in tariff commitment, capacity-payment arrangement, or power purchase agreement could become an international treaty obligation. States adjusting regulatory frameworks in response to changing circumstances---technological shifts, fiscal pressures, environmental imperatives, market developments---would face potential treaty claims not only under FET and expropriation standards (which include substantive defenses) but also under umbrella clauses that might treat any deviation from contractual terms as a treaty breach regardless of justification.

The Two-Step Test addresses this concern by filtering out commercial disputes lacking a sovereign element. Regulatory changes affecting the investment environment may give rise to FET claims where the changes are arbitrary or breach specific commitments, and to expropriation claims where they destroy investment value. But umbrella clauses should be reserved for cases where the state has used governmental authority specifically to breach the contractual undertaking, not merely cases where regulatory changes affect the economic context within which the contract operates.

## B. ISDS Legitimacy and Institutional Sustainability

Critics contend that investor-state arbitration has drifted from protecting against egregious sovereign abuse into a mechanism for contesting routine regulatory and commercial decisions. The legitimacy critique encompasses several distinct concerns: regulatory chill (states avoiding beneficial regulation due to fear of arbitration), democratic accountability (unelected arbitrators reviewing sovereign policy choices), and scope creep (expanding protections beyond original justifications).[^28]

An expansive umbrella doctrine compounds these concerns by extending treaty protection to every commercial expectation. If any contract breach becomes a treaty breach, the state's exposure multiplies dramatically. Every commercial relationship with foreign investors---every procurement contract, service agreement, joint venture arrangement, or licensing deal---becomes a potential treaty claim. The distinction between international investment protection and ordinary commercial law collapses.

Interpretive restraint in this doctrinal area helps address legitimacy concerns without requiring wholesale treaty overhaul. Tribunals cannot unilaterally reform the ISDS system, but they can apply existing treaty provisions in ways that respect their textual limits and underlying purposes. A regime perceived as over-broad may ultimately be more fragile---and less attractive to long-term investors who value stability---than one that calibrates protection to its original rationale: shielding investors from egregious sovereign abuse.

Moreover, maintaining the distinction between treaty claims and contract claims serves the interests of both states and investors. States benefit from predictable exposure and the ability to engage in ordinary commercial activity without automatically incurring international obligations. Investors benefit from knowing that genuine sovereign abuse will be addressed at the treaty level while commercial disputes are efficiently resolved through contractual mechanisms designed for commercial disputes.

## C. Enhancing Predictability Through Clear Dispute Allocation

A clearer allocation of disputes---sovereign abuses at the treaty level, commercial disputes in contract fora---can enhance predictability and thus serve all stakeholders. Predictability is not merely a procedural convenience; it enables better planning, more accurate risk assessment, and more efficient dispute resolution.

For investors, predictability means knowing ex ante which disputes will be resolved where. An investor structuring a deal can assess: for genuine regulatory interference, ISDS protection is available; for commercial performance disputes, the contractual forum governs. This clarity enables appropriate risk allocation. Investors can obtain political risk insurance for sovereign risks while managing commercial risks through ordinary commercial mechanisms.

For states, predictability means bounded exposure. A state entering into commercial arrangements with foreign investors can understand that umbrella clause exposure attaches to sovereign abuse of those arrangements, not to every commercial dispute. This bounded exposure may actually encourage states to engage more willingly in commercial relationships with foreign investors, knowing that ordinary commercial risks are allocated through ordinary commercial mechanisms.

The Two-Step Test thus represents not merely an interpretive constraint but an allocation principle: sovereign conduct goes to treaty fora; commercial conduct goes to contract fora. This allocation respects party autonomy (investors and states chose contractual dispute mechanisms for commercial disputes), promotes efficiency (contract fora are typically better equipped for commercial fact-finding), and maintains the coherence of the investment treaty system (treaty protection addresses distinctively international concerns).

## D. Contemporary Treaty Practice as Feedback Loop

Contemporary treaty practice confirms the alignment of the proposed framework with evolving state preferences---and, consistently with the audience distinction introduced in Section I, illustrates how interpretation and drafting operate as a feedback loop. UNCTAD's IIA Mapping Project shows that modern agreements and model texts increasingly use more calibrated treaty language, including omission of umbrella clauses or formulations that narrow their scope.[^29] This evidence should be used for the right purpose. It does not establish what the parties to the Switzerland--Pakistan or Switzerland--Philippines BITs subjectively intended in the 1990s. It does show how states now manage the risk revealed by the *SGS* split: by making textual clarity and sovereign-conduct limitations express rather than leaving them to adjudicative reconstruction.

The Netherlands Model BIT (2019), with its explicit "exercise of governmental authority" requirement, is the most explicit illustration of this evolution. Having observed the interpretive controversy generated by broad legacy language, Dutch treaty drafters have written the Step 2 filter directly into Article 9(5) of the Model BIT, thereby reducing the need for tribunals to infer intent through supplementary means and resolving the question at the drafting stage. This is the feedback loop at work: tribunal outputs became drafting inputs. The Two-Step Test therefore does double service---offering a principled interpretive default for the legacy-treaty stock that tribunals must continue to adjudicate, and articulating the rationale for the direction in which newer treaty practice appears to be moving.

# VII. Counterarguments and Rebuttals

Having made the positive case for the Two-Step Test, this section considers the principal objections it faces and explains why each is either overstated or already accommodated within the framework.

## A. Effectiveness and Treaty Fidelity (Effet Utile and Pacta Sunt Servanda)

**Objection.** Restrictive readings render umbrella clauses meaningless and violate the principle that treaties must be performed in good faith.

**Response.** Umbrella clauses retain meaningful effect under the Two-Step Test: they capture sovereign-type abuses of investment-related undertakings. A clause need not cover every commercial breach to have utility. Moreover, *pacta sunt servanda* cuts both ways. It requires respecting the treaty text actually agreed, including vague umbrella wording and explicit forum-selection clauses---not reading into provisions an unnegotiated guarantee of all commercial performance. Elevating all contract breaches where the text does not clearly authorize elevation can itself violate *pacta sunt servanda* vis-à-vis the host state.

## B. FDI Deterrence

**Objection.** Weaker umbrella clause protection may reduce investor appetite and deter foreign direct investment.

**Response.** Empirical evidence on the link between BIT protections and FDI flows is contested. Poulsen's research suggests that states' initial treaty programs often reflected bounded rationality rather than precise cost-benefit analysis.[^30] A regime perceived as over-broad and illegitimate may ultimately be more fragile than one that calibrates protection to its original rationale. Moreover, clearer dispute allocation---sovereign abuses going to treaty fora, commercial disputes to contract fora---can enhance predictability and thus investor confidence.

## C. Douglas's Functional Approach

**Objection.** Douglas's functional approach prioritizes the investment-related character of obligations over the formal treaty-contract distinction.[^31] Under this view, umbrella clauses should protect all obligations functionally connected to investments, regardless of their legal source. Douglas criticizes the "elevation theory" as conceptually confused, arguing that umbrella clauses do not transform contract claims into treaty claims but rather create a distinct treaty obligation to observe investment-related commitments, breach of which is independently actionable under the treaty.

**Response.** The Two-Step Test accepts Douglas's central functional insight: umbrella clauses exist to protect investment-related undertakings, and there is no metaphysical "transformation" of contract into treaty. However, Douglas's approach, taken to its logical conclusion, risks collapsing treaty and contract dispute resolution entirely, extending treaty protection to every commercial dispute tangentially connected to an investment. The Two-Step Test structures Douglas's functionalism through two anchors that he does not sufficiently emphasize. First, a textual anchor (Step 1): functional connection must be established through clear treaty language, because the contracting states' consent determines which obligations are protected. Second, a substantive anchor (Step 2): the *jure imperii* filter distinguishes sovereign abuse---the original concern motivating umbrella clauses in post-war investment protection---from ordinary commercial risk, which the clause was never designed to insure. These constraints preserve Douglas's functional core while addressing the practical concern that his approach, unstructured, would eliminate any principled stopping point. The Netherlands 2019 Model BIT's "governmental authority" formulation suggests that sophisticated treaty drafters share this concern.

# VIII. Conclusion and Drafting Implications

## A. Conclusion

This paper has advanced a structured framework for umbrella clause interpretation, arguing that tribunals should adopt a rebuttable presumption against elevating contract breaches into treaty violations unless specific cumulative conditions are met. Under this proposed test, the presumption against elevation is overcome only if the umbrella clause text clearly and unambiguously covers contractual obligations regarding specific investments---assessed under VCLT Article 31 and disciplined by Article 32's supplementary means where those are available---and the state's impugned conduct constitutes an exercise of governmental authority (*jure imperii*) rather than mere commercial non-performance.

When viewed through this two-step lens, the restrictive jurisdictional finding in *SGS v. Pakistan* emerges as the doctrinally superior application of the VCLT's interpretive framework: the treaty's ambiguous reference to "commitments" fails to meet the threshold for clarity, and the paucity of supplementary-means evidence for BITs of that vintage counsels restraint rather than aggressive textual inference. Conversely, while the clearer language in *SGS v. Philippines* justified an expansive Step 1 reading, that tribunal's decision to stay proceedings pending a resolution in the contractual forum properly acknowledged that not all textually covered breaches warrant immediate treaty adjudication. Both decisions, read through the Two-Step Test, point toward filters and defaults rather than binary holdings.

The interpretive logic of this framework is further reinforced by recent jurisprudence regarding Most-Favoured-Nation clauses, specifically in decisions like *Grupo Energía Bogotá v. Guatemala* and *Orazul v. Argentina*. These cases underscore the foundational principle that extraordinary treaty effects, such as the transformation of contract claims into international treaty breaches, require explicit state consent and cannot be inferred from silence or ambiguity.

Finally, contemporary drafting trends validate the normative alignment of this framework with current treaty practice. As documented by UNCTAD and exemplified by models like the Netherlands Model BIT and the 2012 U.S. Model BIT, states increasingly narrow or omit umbrella clauses, or make the governmental-authority limitation express. This evolution confirms that the proposed Two-Step Test not only offers a coherent doctrinal solution for the legacy-treaty stock but also reflects an emerging drafting judgment about how investor protection should be balanced against the state's right to regulate.

## B. Audience, Function, and Drafting Implications

Returning to the audience distinction introduced at the outset clarifies what the Two-Step Test is asking of whom. For tribunals interpreting the large legacy stock of BITs concluded before the jurisprudential split crystallized, the Test functions as a disciplined interpretive default: apply Article 31 to the clause text; test the result against whatever Article 32 material is available; and where ambiguity persists (as it will, for many BITs of that vintage), decline elevation at Step 1. Only where the text and available supplementary means justify finding sufficient clarity should the tribunal proceed to the *jure imperii* filter. This is not a call to rewrite treaties from the bench. It is a call to replace ad hoc intuitionism about treaty purpose with a stable rule that respects the limits of what fine textual distinctions can evidentially bear.

For states drafting or renegotiating investment treaties, the Test operates differently. Here it functions not as an interpretive default but as a design blueprint, identifying the two variables---textual breadth and sovereign-conduct limitation---that determine how elevation risk is allocated. States can operationalize those variables prospectively by making choices at the drafting stage that tribunals would otherwise have to make interpretively. Two principal models follow from this logic.

**Model 1: Omission of the umbrella clause.** The 2012 U.S. Model BIT deliberately omits an umbrella clause, relying instead on other substantive standards (FET, expropriation, non-impairment) and contractual fora for commercial disputes.[^32] This approach eliminates elevation risk entirely while preserving robust treaty protections. It is the strongest form of the Step 1 default, written directly into the treaty architecture.

**Model 2: Narrow public-law-only umbrella clause.** Article 9(5) of the Netherlands Model Investment Agreement (2019) provides: "When a Contracting Party has entered into a written commitment with investors of the other Contracting Party regarding a specific investment, that Contracting Party shall not, either itself or through an entity exercising governmental authority, breach the said commitment through the exercise of governmental authority in a way that causes loss or damage to the investor or its investment."[^33] This formulation directly operationalizes Step 2's *jure imperii* filter by confining umbrella protection to breaches occurring "through the exercise of governmental authority." It is the clearest existing example of the feedback loop described above: an interpretive rule distilled from litigation experience encoded back into treaty text, thereby reducing the need for future tribunals to reconstruct intent from thin supplementary materials.

Both models reflect the interpretive principles advanced in this paper and align with institutional reform pressures to recalibrate the balance between investor protection and regulatory autonomy. More fundamentally, they illustrate the core methodological claim of this paper: that the distinction between interpretation and drafting is productive rather than dichotomous. Clear drafting today answers interpretive questions tomorrow; principled interpretation today shapes drafting choices tomorrow. The Two-Step Test is designed to operate coherently across both sides of that loop.

## C. Limitations and Future Research

This paper focuses primarily on ICSID jurisprudence under bilateral investment treaties. Umbrella clause practice under the Energy Charter Treaty, NAFTA/USMCA, and non-ICSID fora warrants separate examination, particularly given the distinct interpretive mandates in those instruments. Beyond these scope limitations, the Two-Step Framework introduces specific conceptual and procedural challenges that merit future inquiry.

First, the application of the *jure imperii* filter to State-Owned Enterprises (SOEs) remains a complex frontier. This paper treats the state as a unitary actor, yet in practice, many umbrella clause claims arise from contracts with SOEs (e.g., *Noble Ventures*, *Salini v. Jordan*). A critical limitation of the proposed test is determining when an SOE's contractual non-performance constitutes a "sovereign act." Future research should examine whether the *jure imperii* presumption shifts when the counterparty is an entity legally separate from the state but functionally controlled by it---specifically, whether the attribution analysis under ILC Articles 5 and 8 (entities exercising governmental authority and conduct under state direction or control) coheres with the "functional" test for sovereign conduct proposed in this context.

Second, the framework's reliance on contractual fora (Step 2) assumes the functionality of domestic judicial systems. By categorizing commercial disputes as *jure gestionis* and routing them to local courts or contract arbitration, the framework risks leaving investors without recourse in jurisdictions with weak rule of law. Future scholarship could explore the interaction between this framework and the "Denial of Justice" standard: at what point does a local court's delay or procedural irregularity in a "commercial" dispute metastasize into a treaty breach that reactivates international jurisdiction?

Third, the "stay of proceedings" mechanism (exemplified by *SGS v. Philippines*) raises unresolved questions regarding parallel valuation and res judicata. If a tribunal stays a treaty claim pending a local contract decision, it is unclear how the international tribunal should treat the local court's factual findings on damages. Future research is needed to develop principles of comity and issue estoppel that prevent double recovery while ensuring that the distinct treaty interest---protection against sovereign abuse---is not extinguished by a narrow contract judgment.

Finally, while this paper addresses the interaction between umbrella clauses and MFN clauses, further work is required to map the interplay between the Two-Step Test and other substantive protections, such as FET. Research should investigate whether narrowing the umbrella clause inevitably leads to "claim shifting," where investors simply re-plead contract breaches as FET violations to bypass the *jure imperii* filter, and how tribunals might police such pleading strategies. Article 32-style discipline---asking what states actually negotiated rather than what plaintiffs' counsel can retrofit---may prove equally valuable there.

**Classification Legend**

| **Classification** | **Description and Implications**                                                                                                     |
|--------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **CLEAR**          | Text unambiguously covers contractual obligations "with regard to" specific investments. Satisfies Step 1; requires Step 2 analysis. |
| **AMBIGUOUS**      | Text uses vague terminology ("commitments," "guarantee") or lacks explicit investment nexus. Fails Step 1; no elevation.             |
| **MODERATE**       | Text contains narrowing language (e.g., "particular commitment," causation requirements). Passes Step 1 with limited scope.          |
| **RESTRICTED**     | Text explicitly limits coverage to governmental-authority exercise. Step 2 codified; narrows scope by design.                        |
| **OMITTED**        | Treaty deliberately excludes umbrella clause. Policy choice to rely on other standards (FET, expropriation) and contract fora.       |
