# Litigation Finance as Shadow Insurance: The Regulatory Arbitrage of Legal-Claim Risk

## Abstract

*Third-party litigation funding, after-the-event insurance, judgment-preservation insurance, and the contingency fee are usually studied as separate creatures of separate bodies of law. This Article argues that they are a single market in disguise: each transfers and prices one underlying commodity—legal-claim risk—yet each sits under a different regulatory regime, from the capital-and-solvency machinery of insurance to the professional-responsibility rules to a near-vacuum. That asymmetry is an engine of regulatory arbitrage, and the 2023–26 contraction of the judgment-preservation-insurance market—and the migration of reversal risk into uninsured funding—shows it operating in real time. Building on Tom Baker's demonstration that non-recourse funding functions like the earliest commercial insurance, the Article takes the regulatory step Baker brackets. It offers three contributions: a functional taxonomy that re-describes the field as one market; a diagnosis of the arbitrage and its three harms—uninsured counterparty risk, the capture of litigation control, and an unmonitorable systemic-and-foreign-capital channel—together with the claim that mandatory disclosure solves the wrong problem; and a prescription of functional, risk-based regulation calibrated to claimant vulnerability, anchored by a graded "functional insurance" trigger that attaches the insurance regime to indemnity and pooling wherever they appear.*

---

## Introduction

On April 30, 2024, the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit reversed a judgment of roughly $1.6 billion.[^1] The case, *BMC Software, Inc. v. International Business Machines Corp.*, was an ordinary-looking commercial dispute about a software-licensing contract and a customer's migration from one vendor to another.[^2] A bench trial in the Southern District of Texas had produced one of the largest contract awards in recent memory; a unanimous panel, in an opinion by Judge Edith Jones, concluded that the district court had erred on liability and wiped the award out entirely.[^3] To the parties, the reversal was a catastrophe and a windfall, respectively. To a market most lawyers had never heard of, it was a detonation.

For the reversed judgment had been insured. Sitting behind BMC's award was a "judgment-preservation insurance" (JPI) policy—contingent-legal-risk insurance that pays the holder of a large verdict if an appellate court later reduces or erases it, pricing and assuming the precise risk that an appeal will undo a trial victory. The BMC tower was, by trade-press accounts, large and syndicated, spread across roughly thirty to forty carriers in the manner of a reinsurance placement, with Liberty Mutual reportedly serving as lead insurer and total coverage reported in the neighborhood of $500 million to $750 million.[^4] Those tower figures come from legal and insurance journalism, not from any filing or opinion, and should be read as reported estimates rather than audited fact.[^5] What is not estimated is the structure: a fortuitous legal-claim risk, transferred for a premium to a pool of capitalized, solvency-regulated insurers, and triggered when the Fifth Circuit ruled.

Widen the lens one ring further and the event stops looking like an accident. Months later, on October 10, 2024, a separate ruling cut the law firm Quinn Emanuel's fee award from $185 million to $92.4 million; because a JPI policy had reportedly guaranteed roughly ninety percent of the original fee, underwriters faced an exposure of some $75 million.[^6] A second mega-verdict told the same story in the same grammar. Appian Corporation had won a trade-secret judgment against Pegasystems of approximately $2.036 billion and, in a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, disclosed that it had purchased a JPI policy with a $500 million aggregate limit, placed with an oversubscribed syndicate of insurers, for a premium of 9.8% of the limit—a one-time payment of $57.3 million.[^7] Those Appian terms are hard evidence: they come from the company's own 8-K. In July 2024, a Virginia appellate court reversed that verdict too.[^8] The wider ring is a market: insurers reportedly raised prices—premiums "as much as doubled," with quotes moving toward seventeen to twenty percent of the insured award—cut the percentage of a judgment they would cover, shifted from single-judgment to multi-case policies, and withdrew, with Liberty Mutual reportedly backing out of at least two litigation-insurance deals after the BMC loss.[^9] Paywalled trade commentary describing four to five large losses with roughly $2 billion of insured limits at risk is offered here only as the trade's own estimate of the squeeze.[^10]

Here is the pivot that becomes the whole Article. When the insurers retreated, the risk they had been pricing did not vanish. It migrated. Plaintiffs holding large but appealable verdicts turned to appellate and judgment-monetization funding—third-party capital advanced against the same downside-reversal risk, repriced and re-housed in a near-unregulated funding vehicle instead of a capitalized, solvency-regulated insurance one.[^11] The structural data point in the same direction: by one market survey, roughly nineteen percent of 2024 U.S. commercial funding commitments were already covered, in whole or part, by contingent-risk insurance—the cleaner 2024-specific signal of the insurance/funding adjacency—and claim monetization, on a longer-running upward trend, had reached twenty-six percent of commitments, up from eight percent three years earlier.[^12] One should not overstate the episode—the JPI market re-priced and "solidified" rather than disappeared, and "collapse" is shorthand for a sharp contraction.[^13] But the analytically load-bearing fact survives the qualification: the same legal-claim risk moved across a regulatory boundary, from a form the law capitalizes to a form the law barely sees.

### The Puzzle: One Risk, Three (or Four) Regimes

Name the commodity precisely. There is, beneath the field's proliferating labels, a single underlying asset: *legal-claim risk*—the fortuitous risk that a meritorious-looking claim or judgment yields less than its expected value, whether through loss at trial, reversal on appeal, adverse-cost exposure, or non-collection. Everything in the field is a way of transferring and pricing that risk, through at least four instruments that do the same economic work under four different legal labels. JPI and contingent-legal-risk insurance are written as indemnity and regulated *as insurance*—capital requirements, solvency supervision, guaranty funds, state commissioners.[^14] After-the-event (ATE) insurance, which covers a litigant's adverse-cost and disbursement exposure, is likewise insurance, though it remains vestigial in the United States because the American Rule rarely shifts fees.[^15] The contingency fee—the original and thoroughly normalized device for transferring a claim's downside from client to lawyer—is governed by the professional-responsibility rules, administered by courts and bars.[^16] And third-party litigation funding (TPLF) is regulated almost not at all at the federal level, reached only by a patchwork of nascent state statutes and ad hoc disclosure orders.[^17] Four instruments collapse to three regulatory regimes—JPI and ATE both sit under insurance, contingency fees under professional responsibility, funding in the near-vacuum—and the three regimes make wildly different demands of capital, disclosure, and conduct.

The puzzle sharpens to an embarrassing point in the ethics opinions. The New York City Bar has held that *lawyer-directed* funding—where the funder is repaid out of the lawyer's fee—violates Rule 5.4(a)'s ban on fee-sharing with nonlawyers, while *client-directed* funding—where the funder is repaid out of the client's recovery—does not, even when the lawyer signs the very same agreement.[^18] Two economically identical risk transfers, treated oppositely, on the sole basis of who signs the check.[^19] When functionally identical products face dramatically different regulatory costs, capital migrates to the cheapest-to-regulate form. That is regulatory arbitrage, stated as a falsifiable prediction rather than a complaint[^20]—and the JPI-to-funding migration in the hook is the prediction confirming itself in real time.

The doctrinal hinge on which the whole field turns is an unanswered question: *is it insurance?* Courts that decline to call funding a "loan"—because the obligation to repay is contingent on success—never reach for "insurance" either, because that same contingency defeats indemnity; the funder buys an asset and earns zero on loss rather than indemnifying a loss.[^21] So funding falls through every regulatory net, while JPI, written as indemnity, lands squarely inside the insurance regime. Same risk; opposite treatment. The answer to "is it insurance?" is, for now, *no*—not by reasoned decision but by default and inattention. No state insurance department or attorney general appears to have squarely opined that TPLF is or is not insurance,[^22] and that silence is itself a datum.

### The Thesis

The thesis can be stated in plain economic terms before any label is attached. TPLF, ATE insurance, JPI, and contingency fees are functional substitutes—each transfers and prices the same legal-claim risk for a risk-loaded return—yet they sit under radically divergent regulatory regimes, and the predictable, observable consequence is systematic regulatory arbitrage in which capital migrates to the least-regulated functionally equivalent form. The law regulates the *entity*—insurer, lawyer, funder—when it should regulate the *function*: the transfer of legal-claim risk, calibrated to the magnitude of the risk and the vulnerability of the claimant. And the mandatory-disclosure rules now being drafted, however sensible on their own terms, solve the wrong problem.

Only now attach the labels. This is "shadow insurance" for the courts, borrowing Daniel Schwarcz's term for the migration of insurance risk onto lightly regulated, affiliated balance sheets,[^23] and beyond it the shadow-banking template, in which an economically important sector operates outside the regulatory perimeter through gaps the law should close by attaching regulation to function.[^24] The frame is not eccentric: Tom Baker has already shown that non-recourse litigation funding functions like bottomry and respondentia, "the earliest forms of commercial insurance," and that an insurance market in litigation risk is emerging around it.[^25] This Article takes the regulatory step Baker brackets. Nor is the stakes claim hypothetical. The migration is documented and the market large—U.S. commercial funding stood at roughly $16.1 billion in assets under management across forty-two active funders, with global TPLF investment estimated by one reinsurer at about $17 billion and projected to reach roughly $31 billion by 2028—and it is contracting and stressed in ways that expose counterparty fragility: witness the wind-down of Therium, a pioneer funder, and secondary markets such as the Omni Bridgeway–Ares continuation fund that trade legal-claim assets like any securitized risk.[^26]

### Three Contributions, and the Gap They Fill

The Article makes three contributions, describe–diagnose–prescribe.

*First, a taxonomy.* The Article re-describes a field currently carved into four doctrinal silos—insurance law, civil procedure, legal ethics, and an emergent funding literature—as a single legal-claim-risk market, sorting every instrument (JPI, ATE, single-case and portfolio TPLF, claim monetization, consumer pre-settlement funding, contingency fees) by the risk it transfers, who bears it, how it is priced, and how asymmetrically it is regulated. This supplies the analytic structure Baker brackets (*supra* this Part), including the cluster of judgment-preservation, principal-protection, and litigation-expense products that may supplant funding.[^25] Prior work otherwise catalogs funding's economic functions in isolation[^27] or proposes category-based regulation of funding alone.[^28] The contribution is the structure: naming one commodity and showing four labels priced against it.

*Second, a diagnosis.* Applying a rigorous account of regulatory arbitrage,[^29] the Article identifies three concrete harms: under-capitalization and counterparty risk borne by claimants when uninsured funders absorb risk that capitalized insurers shed; agency-cost externalization, as opaque capital captures settlement control and pursues mass-tort "claim alchemy";[^30] and a systemic and foreign-capital channel, in which sanctioned or sovereign money can enter courtrooms through an unmonitored vehicle, as a Bloomberg investigation into Russian-linked funding and a 2024 Government Accountability Office report on patent-suit financing each document.[^31] To this comes the Article's most counter-conventional claim: mandatory *disclosure*—the entire thrust of the current reform agenda—solves the wrong problem. Not that disclosure is unimportant, but that it is a mismatch of remedy to harm: it answers "who is funding this case?" without capitalizing a fragile funder, constraining captured control, or measuring aggregate exposure—the prudential and vulnerability harms that actually bite the claimant.[^32] The tell is in the reform text itself: the leading disclosure proposal expressly analogizes funding disclosure to the 1970 insurance-disclosure rule, then carves out insurance and contingency fees—reproducing the very arbitrage inside the cure.[^33]

*Third, a prescription.* The Article proposes functional, risk-based regulation calibrated to risk and claimant vulnerability—a tiered regime that touches sophisticated commercial risk transfer lightly and protects vulnerable, consumer, and aggregate claimants more heavily—with worked-out institutional design and a federalism account. It builds on the functional-regulation tradition[^34] and on Sahani's harmonization proposal but adds the two ingredients they lack: insurance-equivalence and vulnerability calibration. Each contribution feeds the next—the taxonomy makes the arbitrage visible, the diagnosis identifies which arbitrage harms, the prescription regulates only what the diagnosis flags—by function and vulnerability, not by label.

The scholarship this Article joins is distinguished and largely unanimous in its level of analysis. Maya Steinitz pioneered the frame of the claim as a tradable asset and a tailored disclosure proposal;[^35] Jonathan Molot supplied the economics of litigation finance as a settlement-market correction;[^36] Anthony Sebok dismantled the champerty "authenticity" intuition;[^37] Elizabeth Chamblee Burch theorized funders as governance monitors in aggregate litigation;[^38] John Coffee placed financed litigation in comparative and historical perspective;[^39] Victoria Sahani proposed category-based and harmonized regulation;[^40] and Bedi and Marra named the regulatory "shadows" in which funding operates, but did not argue functional equivalence to insurance, deploy a regulatory-arbitrage analytic, or treat ATE, JPI, and contingency fees as substitutes for one priced commodity.[^41] The gap is not a topic no one covered but a level of analysis no one adopted. Where prior work asks *how should we regulate litigation funding?*, this Article asks *why do we regulate four substitutes for one risk so differently, and what follows?* Reframing the question is the contribution.

### Roadmap, Method, and Scope

The argument proceeds in five Parts. Part I builds the functional account and the taxonomy, defining legal-claim risk before naming any instrument. Part II maps the regulatory fault lines—the insurance regime and the federal "business of insurance" articulation, the professional-responsibility rules, and the funding near-vacuum—and isolates the "is it insurance?" hinge. Part III applies arbitrage theory and uses the JPI contraction as a natural experiment to establish the three harms and the disclosure critique. Part IV constructs the functional, vulnerability-calibrated prescription and its institutional design. Part V answers objections and draws comparative lessons from Australia, the post-*PACCAR* United Kingdom, the Singapore and Hong Kong arbitration carve-outs, and the European Union's representative-actions reforms.

A word on method. The argument is anchored in a genuine natural experiment: the 2023–26 JPI contraction is a discrete, exogenous shock—an appellate merits reversal unrelated to funding regulation—followed by a predicted behavioral response, the migration of reversal risk from insurance into funding. The Article claims no identified causation; the migration is offered as a documented market episode and a testable hypothesis, the measurement limitation is conceded, and the empirical methods of the shadow-insurance literature are noted as the template for future quantification.[^42] The claim is triangulated across primary market data, SEC filings, case law, and the regulatory record. The scope is United States–focused, with targeted comparative engagement; it centers on commercial and aggregate litigation while attending to consumer and pre-settlement funding, where claimant vulnerability is highest and aggregate data most conspicuously absent.[^43] Where figures derive from interested or press sources, the Article flags them and rests its load-bearing claims on verified primary data. This is not a brief for or against litigation funding's existence. It is a brief for regulating the risk by its function rather than its label—wherever it hides.

## I. The Legal-Claim Risk Market: A Functional Account

A lawsuit is, among other things, a balance-sheet item. Once a cause of action exists, its holder owns something with an uncertain but estimable future cash flow — a right that will, with some probability, convert into money on some future date, net of the costs of getting there. That this asset is intangible, contested, and embarrassing to price openly does not make it any less an asset; it makes it a *risky* asset. And wherever there is a risky asset, there is a market in its risk. This Part reconstructs that market. It argues that third-party litigation funding (TPLF), after-the-event (ATE) insurance, judgment-preservation insurance (JPI), the contingency fee, and the hybrid wrappers that stack them are not four or five distinct industries but a single market trading a single underlying commodity, which I will call *legal-claim risk*. Each instrument performs the identical economic operation — it transfers and prices the risk attached to a chose in action — and the field's apparent diversity is an artifact of the labels under which the law files each transaction, not of any difference in what is being bought and sold.

The argument proceeds in four moves. Section I.A defines the underlying asset and decomposes legal-claim risk into five priceable components, grounding the "one market" claim in the asset rather than in any instrument. Section I.B maps the instruments empirically as one capital stack wearing many labels, supplied increasingly by the same regime-agnostic institutions. Section I.C — the analytic core — establishes functional equivalence in two registers: an economic register that models the instruments as positions on one underlying, and a doctrinal register that walks the elements of insurance and shows that funding satisfies the risk-transfer core while failing the formal elements. That gap — economic substitution despite doctrinal non-identity — is the phenomenon the rest of the Article names and pursues. Section I.D situates the account within the function-over-entity tradition in financial regulation and within the recognized family of "shadow" sectors, renders the signature taxonomy, and hands off to Part II. The ambition here is descriptive and analytic, not yet normative: Part I's task is to make the single-market claim undeniable, not to diagnose its harms.

I should say at the outset what this Article does *not* claim, because the disclaimer is load-bearing. It does not claim that anyone has overlooked that litigation funding resembles insurance. Tom Baker has already shown that non-recourse funding is "a contemporary form of the respondentia and bottomry that insurance historians regard as the earliest forms of commercial insurance," instruments that fused financing with risk-bearing, and he has named the emerging cluster of litigation-risk insurance products that grows up alongside funding.[^44] The functional resemblance is Baker's foundation, and this Article builds on it explicitly. What Baker brackets — and what this Article supplies — is the regulatory step: treating that resemblance not as an observation about market evolution but as a problem of regulatory classification, working out why the law sorts functionally identical risk transfers into radically different regimes, and tracing the consequences. The contribution begins where Baker's descriptive project ends.

### I.A. Defining Legal-Claim Risk: The Underlying Asset

Begin with the asset, before naming a single instrument. A legal claim, once it has accrued, is a contingent financial entitlement: a probability distribution over future court-determined outcomes, discounted to present value. Its expected value is the probability-weighted average of those outcomes less the costs of prosecution; its *risk* — what this Article means by "legal-claim risk" — is the variance of that distribution, the dispersion of possible cash flows around the expected value. A near-certain $1 million settlement and a coin-flip on either $0 or $2 million share the same expected value but are utterly different assets, because the second carries risk the first does not. Every instrument in this market exists to do something about that variance: to move it from a party who holds it to a party better placed, by capital, diversification, or risk appetite, to bear it, in exchange for a price. This is the same operation that defines the markets in insurance, derivatives, and credit, and it situates legal-claim risk within that analytic family rather than within any sui generis category of "litigation."[^45]

Maya Steinitz captured the asset side of this when she described modern funding as institutional investors taking "an ownership stake in a legal claim and a contingency in the recovery."[^46] The claim, in other words, is alienable property whose risk can be carved off and sold. But the asset is not monolithic, and the analytic payoff of this Part depends on disaggregating it. Legal-claim risk decomposes into five separately priceable components, each of which the market's own participants underwrite as a distinct variable:

*First, merits (or liability) risk* — the probability that the claimant prevails on the law and facts at all. *Second, quantum risk* — conditional on liability, the dispersion of the damages award, from a token recovery to a record verdict. *Third, timing and duration risk* — the time value and uncertainty of *when* any recovery arrives, across trial, post-trial motions, appeal, and remand, during which capital is locked up and the discount rate bites. *Fourth, collectability (or enforcement) risk* — the probability that a favorable judgment, once final, is actually paid by a solvent and reachable defendant, a risk that can dominate in cross-border or thinly capitalized cases. *Fifth, adverse-cost and downside-exposure risk* — the claimant's liability for the opponent's costs (in fee-shifting regimes) and for its own irrecoverable disbursements if the claim fails.

This five-component decomposition is not a definitional convenience engineered to make everything count as the same thing. It tracks how the participants themselves price the asset. Funders screen and underwrite merits and quantum before committing capital, declining the weakest cases, which is precisely why the existence of funding operates as a quality signal, as Abramowicz and Alper have shown.[^47] Shepherd and Stone catalog the functions funders perform — risk-bearing, capital provision, and the devices used to secure repayment without controlling the litigation — in terms that map directly onto these variables.[^48] Molot's account of why a market in litigation risk improves outcomes turns on exactly the risk-preference asymmetries that the decomposition isolates.[^49] And the insurance side prices the same components under different names: an ATE insurer underwrites principally the adverse-cost and disbursement tail (component five), while a JPI underwriter, pricing a post-judgment policy, underwrites a narrow post-adjudication slice of merits-and-quantum reversal risk (components one and two), with timing folded into the premium. The asset is defined by how the market prices it, not by this Article's needs.

The decomposition does real analytic work because *different instruments target different components*, and because the components are separately transferable. Early-stage commercial funding prices merits, quantum, and duration; ATE targets adverse-cost exposure; JPI and appellate monetization price a discrete reversal risk on an already-adjudicated claim; post-settlement consumer funding prices mostly collectability and timing on a near-certain receivable. Because the components can be sliced and sold separately, a single claim can carry several instruments at once — funding to prosecute it, ATE to cap the downside, JPI to lock in a verdict on appeal — which is why the hybrid wrappers examined below are not anomalies but the predictable consequence of an asset whose risk is divisible. This pre-loads the temporal axis of the taxonomy: as a claim matures, the risk narrows from the full five-component bundle toward the single, estimable, low-frequency/high-severity event of appellate reversal, and it is that narrowing that makes the late-stage product look insurable.

The common law spent six centuries refusing to recognize any of this. The doctrines of maintenance and champerty — the prohibition on a stranger's support of, or trafficking in, another's suit — were, at bottom, a refusal to treat the claim as an alienable asset whose risk could be transferred to an outsider for profit.[^50] What is striking, and what the functional account recovers, is that the common law itself once saw the unity this Article reconstructs. As Anthony Sebok has shown, the law of maintenance grouped together as species of a single genus what we now treat as distinct: "profit maintenance" (champerty, the funder's bet on a stranger's suit) and "lawyer maintenance" (the contingency fee).[^51] The funder and the contingency-fee lawyer were doing, in the common law's own taxonomy, variations on one prohibited thing: supporting litigation in exchange for a share of its proceeds. The modern erosion of champerty, then, is not the disappearance of an irrational taboo so much as the market's gradual recognition that the asset is real and its risk is tradeable. State after state has abolished or narrowed the doctrine: Massachusetts discarded champerty in *Saladini v. Righellis*, Minnesota in *Maslowski*, and even jurisdictions that retain it, like New York, confine it to narrow circumstances.[^52] The doctrine that survives is best understood not as a coherent prohibition but as a vestigial and gameable gate, a point Part II develops. For present purposes the lesson is simpler: the historical objection to funding was an objection to alienability, and once alienability is conceded, the only remaining questions are functional ones about how the risk is priced, to whom it is transferred, and under what regulatory terms.

### I.B. Mapping the Instruments: One Capital Stack, Many Labels

That the market is one market is, in the first instance, an empirical claim about who supplies capital to whom — not a metaphor. The single most telling datum comes from the market's own annual accounting. According to Westfleet Advisors, whose 2024 report is the most rigorous public census of U.S. commercial litigation finance, roughly 19% of new 2024 commitments were fully or partially covered by contingent-risk (litigation) insurance — the first time the figure has been measured, and a direct window onto the convergence this Article describes.[^53] Nearly one in five commercial funding deals now arrives pre-blended with insurance. The market is not merely adjacent to insurance; it is wrapping itself in insurance on the same transactions. That single fact does most of the work of this Section: if funders and insurers are co-underwriting the same claims, the line the law draws between "funding" and "insurance" is being erased by the participants before the regulator has even located it.

The surrounding numbers establish that this is a large, concentrated, and institutionally mature market. Westfleet reports approximately $16.1 billion in assets under management across forty-two active U.S. commercial funders, with $2.3 billion in new capital committed across 287 deals in 2024 — a figure down roughly 16% from 2023 and about 30% below the 2022 peak, the contraction that frames the natural experiment of Part III.[^54] Portfolio deals (cross-collateralized books of claims) account for about 67% of commitments, single-matter deals the remaining 33%; claim monetization — the sale of an interest in a pending or entered judgment — has grown to roughly 26% of commitments, up from 8% just three years earlier.[^55] These are the contours of an industry that prices and pools legal-claim risk at institutional scale.

Three features of that industry establish the "one market" claim concretely. The first is *layering*: commercial single-case and portfolio funding, consumer pre-settlement and post-settlement funding, ATE, JPI, the contingency fee, recourse law-firm financing, claim and appellate monetization, secondary-market securitization, and hybrid wrappers are not separate industries but layers of capital sitting on the same underlying claims, frequently supplied by overlapping providers. The second is *provider regime-agnosticism*. The same institutional capital flows wherever the risk-adjusted return is best, indifferent to the regulatory label that attaches at the point of contact. Burford Capital, the largest publicly traded funder, reported a group-wide portfolio of roughly $7.48 billion as of the third quarter of 2024, deployed across plaintiff funding, law-firm portfolio funding, and claim monetization alike.[^56] Omni Bridgeway, a global platform, has gone a step further, establishing a continuation fund through which Ares Management acquired a roughly 70% interest in a diversified portfolio of more than 150 legal assets, valued at approximately A$320 million — a textbook secondary-market sale of pooled claim risk to institutional capital, executed entirely outside any insurance regime.[^57] The provider is regime-agnostic; the law is shedder-specific.

The third feature is that the insurance industry *itself* already treats legal-claim risk as adjacent insurance risk and books it on both sides of the regulatory line. Swiss Re, a major reinsurer, has studied TPLF as a driver of "social inflation," estimating global funding investment at roughly USD 17 billion in 2020 — a reinsurer characterizing funding as a risk-transfer phenomenon within its own analytic frame.[^58] On the other side of the line, JPI is sold by insurers through syndicated "towers" of reportedly thirty to forty carriers, a structure indistinguishable in form from the reinsurance pooling that defines the insurance industry's core.[^59] When the insurance industry studies funding as a competitor and underwrites verdicts as policies, it is confirming, from inside the perimeter, that funding and insurance trade the same commodity.

The most vivid proof that one claim can carry stacked, differently-regulated instruments is the *Appian/Pegasystems* matter. After winning a roughly $2.036 billion trade-secret verdict against Pegasystems, Appian disclosed in an SEC filing a judgment-preservation insurance policy with a $500 million aggregate limit, placed with "a large syndicate of highly rated insurers" and oversubscribed, at an aggregate premium of 9.8% of the limit — a one-time payment of $57.3 million — paying Appian the shortfall if its final enforceable judgment fell below $500 million.[^60] The same verdict was simultaneously the subject of a monetization strategy, the conversion of contingent litigation upside into near-certain present value.[^61] One asset; two regimes, stacked. If a single judgment can be insured against reversal and monetized for cash on the same balance sheet, the insurance and the funding must be pricing the same underlying risk, a point that carries directly into the functional-equivalence analysis of Section I.C.

A word on scope, because tier-zero credibility depends on resisting the temptation to manufacture a grand aggregate. The figures above measure *different universes* and must not be stacked. Westfleet's $16.1 billion covers only U.S. commercial, business-to-business litigation finance and expressly excludes consumer and pre-settlement funding, mass-tort and personal-injury law-firm finance, and receivables factoring.[^62] Swiss Re's $17 billion is a global, all-segment estimate. The GAO's foundational study draws the commercial/consumer line but, by design, publishes no single aggregate dollar figure at all.[^63] To add these together would be to double-count and to conflate incommensurable measures. More telling than any number is a number that does not exist: there is no reliable public aggregate for the U.S. consumer pre-settlement funding segment, the very segment serving the most vulnerable claimants. The GAO describes consumer funding as typically involving advances under $10,000 to individual plaintiffs for living expenses, but reports that public data on the industry's rates of return and total volume are seriously deficient.[^64] That measurement gap is not a footnote to the regulatory story; it is part of it. The opacity the Article later critiques begins with the simple fact that no regulator can say how large the most consumer-facing corner of this market is.

One methodological commitment follows. Several of the sources mapping this market are authored by interested participants — Burford and GLS Capital are funders; Swiss Re is a reinsurer with a stake in how funding is regulated. This Article treats such sources as *industry characterization*, valuable as evidence of how the market understands itself, and pairs each with neutral authority — the GAO, Westfleet's audited methodology, SEC filings, and the case law — wherever a factual proposition does load-bearing work. The convergence claim does not rest on any funder's say-so; it rests on the SEC-filed Appian policy, the Westfleet census, and the reinsurer's own published study, read together.

### I.C. Functional Equivalence: Same Risk, Different Regimes

We can now state the equivalence precisely, in two registers. The economic register shows that the instruments are positions on one underlying that lay off the same risk in opposite directions; the doctrinal register shows that funding satisfies the risk-transfer core of insurance while failing both its risk-spreading prong and its formal elements. The two registers converge on a single holding: these instruments are economic substitutes and legal non-equivalents, and the divergence between those two facts is the phenomenon this Article names.

#### 1. The Economic Register: Laying Off the Same Risk in Opposite Directions

Take the claim's value as the underlying — a random variable, the probability distribution over outcomes defined in Section I.A. Every instrument in the market is a position on that underlying, and the instruments differ not in *what* they price but in the *sign and trigger* of their payoff.

The instruments map naturally onto option positions, and deploying that standard apparatus to show their substitutability is the analytic point here.[^65] An ATE policy and a JPI policy are downside protection — *puts* the claim-holder buys: in exchange for a premium paid up front, the holder acquires the right to be made whole on the *downside*, paid if the claim is lost and adverse costs fall due (ATE), or paid the shortfall if a verdict is reversed or reduced below a floor (JPI). The premium is the option price; the insured event is the strike. A non-recourse funder, by contrast, takes the *upside* position: it advances cash now and recovers a capped or multiple-of-investment share of the proceeds *only if the claim succeeds*, and owes nothing if it fails. The funder profits when the underlying realizes high and earns zero when it realizes low — the payoff profile of a long call written on a portion of the claim's value. The contingency fee is the same upside position, written in services rather than cash: the lawyer advances effort and costs, takes a percentage of recovery on success, and bears the loss of her investment on failure.

So framed, the instruments are not rivals occupying different markets; they are positions that lay off the *same* risk in opposite directions. The put pays when the underlying falls; the upside position pays when it rises; both are priced off the same distribution over the same court outcomes. They are not, however, identical bets — a downside-only indemnity and an upside-sharing investment have different payoff profiles, and that difference is precisely why they are *substitutes* rather than the same instrument. Substitutability holds at the level that matters for this Article — *laying off the same reversal risk* — not at the level of mirror-image option payoffs. And substitutability is not a metaphor but a testable proposition about how a sophisticated party chooses among the available ways to shed a risk. Consider a corporation holding a large verdict on appeal, exposed to a single risk: reversal. It can *buy a put* — a JPI policy — paying a premium to be indemnified if the verdict is overturned, retaining the upside if it is affirmed. Or it can *write* a claim on its position — monetize the judgment to a funder, taking cash now in exchange for a share of the eventual recovery, thereby transferring its net exposure, downside and upside together, to the funder. The two transactions are not the same trade: the put hedges only the downside and keeps the upside, while monetization sells the whole position. But both lay off the *same* reversal risk on the *same* adjudicated claim, and the choice between them is a question of price and structure — whether the holder prefers to pay a premium and keep the upside or take cash now and surrender the upside. That a sophisticated holder treats them as live alternatives for shedding one risk is what makes them economic substitutes, notwithstanding their different payoff profiles. And the substitution is not hypothetical. When JPI repriced sharply upward after 2024 — premiums reportedly doubling, insurers cutting covered percentages and exiting deals — practitioners documented a "reemergence of appellate monetization funding" as holders sought alternative ways to protect and cash out verdicts, exactly the migration one expects when the relative price of two substitutes shifts.[^66] Capital does not flow between products that price different things. That it flowed from the insurance form to the funding form, along a fixed point on the maturity curve, is the market revealing the substitution that the doctrine denies.

#### 2. The Doctrinal Register: The Risk-Transfer Core Satisfied, the Spreading and Formal Elements Escaped

The economic substitution does not make funding insurance as a matter of law, and the productive result is the gap between those two facts. Tested against insurance's own definition, funding satisfies the *risk-transfer* core but not the *risk-spreading* prong, and fails the formal elements — indemnity of a loss, an insurable interest, an adverse effect on the insured — as well. Part II.D walks that definition element by element, against New York Insurance Law § 1101, the "business of insurance" cases (*Royal Drug*, *Pireno*), *VALIC*, *Jordan*, and the "neither loan nor insurance" holdings of *Ruth* and *Maslowski*. *See infra* Part II.D. For Part I the salient points are two, and both follow from the functional framing above. First, the failed elements are artifacts of contract design and counterparty structure, not differences in the risk the claimant lays off: a funder pays *nothing* when the adverse event occurs and earns its return on the claim's *success*, holding the upside position rather than writing a put — a contingent purchase, not an indemnity. Second, the federal cases on which Part II.D leans are construing "business of insurance" under the McCarran-Ferguson Act, not the state product-definition test: *Royal Drug* and *Pireno* are antitrust-exemption doctrine under § 1013(b), and *National Securities* is a § 1012(b) reverse-preemption case; none is the test for whether a given product *is* insurance subject to state regulation, which is governed by § 1101.[^67] They are borrowed only as the best-developed federal gloss on insurance's *function*; conflating that gloss with the state product-definition test would be an error this Article does not commit.

One feature of the doctrine deserves emphasis here because it sharpens the functional account. *Pooling does occur in this market — but on the funder's side of the transaction, and outside the insurance perimeter.* Portfolio funders cross-collateralize books of claims; secondary-market continuation funds pool legal assets and sell them to institutional investors; JPI is syndicated across towers of carriers. This is real pooling, but it is *funder-side diversification of an investment book*, not the mutualization of *policyholders'* risk that the spreading prong requires — a portfolio funder managing its own variance, as a venture fund diversifies across portfolio companies, not distributing one claimant's risk across a community of other claimants. The distinction defeats any facile assertion that funding "spreads risk like insurance" in the doctrinal sense, and it sharpens the arbitrage: the pooling the law treats as the definitional hallmark of insurance is performed, in this market, most conspicuously by the *least*-regulated actors — the portfolio funder, the Omni Bridgeway–Ares secondary, the JPI tower — but on the *investor* side, where the perimeter does not reach. The function the doctrine makes definitional is being carried out next door to the regime built to govern it, by parties the regime does not capture.

The position is therefore firm and, I think, unavoidable: *funding is not insurance as a matter of law*. It transfers risk like insurance, but it does not spread risk across a pool of insureds, does not indemnify a loss, does not rest on an insurable interest, and does not leave the funder "adversely affected" by the insured event. That is not a concession that weakens the thesis; it is the thesis. The claim is functional equivalence *despite* doctrinal non-identity, and the gap between the two is the arbitrage. The posture honors Steinitz's influential framing of funding as investment — an ownership stake in a claim, closer to venture capital than to indemnity.[^68] Steinitz is right about the funder's economics and the doctrinal category, and the Article agrees that funding is investment, not insurance de jure. But the regulatory question is orthogonal to the business-analogy question: whether the funder's economics resemble venture capital tells us nothing about whether the *risk transfer the claimant buys* is a functional substitute for the risk transfer an insurer would sell — and it plainly is, as the put/upside analysis and the documented migration show. The doctrinal non-identity does not refute the equivalence; it explains how the equivalence escapes regulation.

A fair-minded objector — and the strongest version is Matthew Turk's — will respond that formal definitions are not arbitrary at all: bright entity-based lines reduce the administrative cost of classification and the volume of litigation over which regime applies, and economic convergence among products does not by itself justify harmonizing their regulation.[^69] The point has force, and Part IV engages it directly. For now it is enough to mark the steel-man and to note what it concedes: Turk's defense of formalism is a defense of its *administrative* virtues, and those virtues accrue to whoever bears the classification cost. When the formal line lets functionally substitutable risk transfer escape oversight, the benefit of the bright line is captured by the arbitrageur, not the public, and the costs are borne by the claimants and courts that Part III identifies. That the line is *administrable* is not the same as showing it is *justified*; a line can be easy to draw and still fall in the wrong place.

### I.D. The Theoretical Frame: Function Over Entity, and the "Shadow" Family

The functional-equivalence claim is an instance of a well-worn move in financial regulation: regulate the *function*, not the *institution*. Merton and Bodie's foundational insight is that financial functions are more stable over time than the institutions that perform them, so that function, not entity, should anchor regulatory analysis; institutional structure follows function, not the reverse.[^70] Morgan Ricks has pressed the same logic in the monetary domain, arguing that financial instability traces to the mismatched regulatory treatment of functionally identical instruments — deposit substitutes that are regulated lightly because they are not called "deposits" — and that sound regulation should track the function (here, the issuance of money-like short-term debt) rather than the charter of the issuer.[^71] The lesson generalizes: where the law keys regulation to entity labels rather than economic function, functionally equivalent instruments performing the same operation will be regulated differently, and capital will notice.

What capital does when it notices is regulatory arbitrage. Victor Fleischer's canonical account defines it as a legal planning technique that exploits "a gap between the economics of a deal and its regulatory treatment, restructuring the deal to reduce or avoid regulatory costs without unduly altering the underlying economics."[^72] The definition is exacting in a way that matters for this Article's honesty: arbitrage is not innovation. Innovation creates genuinely new economic substance; arbitrage rearranges the legal form of existing substance to escape a regulatory cost. The migration of legal-claim risk from the insurance form to the funding form — same reversal risk, same claim, different label and different oversight — is restructuring, not new substance. It is, in Fleischer's precise sense, arbitrage. And arbitrage carries Fleischer's named harms: it distorts the competition among regulatory regimes, shifts the incidence of regulatory costs onto parties who did not choose to bear them, and fosters the opacity that erodes accountability — harms Part III develops, but whose source is located here.

This places litigation finance within a recognized family of arbitrage-driven "shadow" sectors, and the family resemblance is rigorous rather than rhetorical. The template, drawn from the shadow-banking and shadow-insurance literatures, has five elements: (a) a large and economically significant sector, (b) performing a function that is regulated when performed in its canonical form, (c) operating outside the regulatory perimeter, (d) because of a definitional or legal gap, (e) thereby creating fragility and externalized risk. Element (a) requires immediate calibration, because it carries a stronger sense in the source literatures than this Article can or needs to claim. In the shadow-banking and shadow-insurance work, "large and economically significant" did *systemic* work — those sectors were big enough to threaten the financial system. This Article uses the element only in the weaker, non-macroprudential sense: legal-claim risk transfer is large relative to the claimants and courts it affects, and large enough to make the arbitrage incentive operative for sophisticated capital — *not* systemically significant in the way shadow banking's trillions were, a magnitude disclaimer Part III states in full. With element (a) so understood, Gorton and Metrick documented the pattern in shadow banking — money-market funds, securitization, and repo, each grown through a regulatory or legal gap into a systemically important substitute for regulated deposit banking.[^73] Daniel Schwarcz named and analyzed its insurance cousin: "shadow insurance," the migration of life-insurance risk to affiliated, lightly regulated captive reinsurers, "a form of regulatory arbitrage that moves traditional insurance risks" to entities "subject to more limited regulatory restrictions and scrutiny."[^74] The term and the analytic are Schwarcz's and the New York Department of Financial Services', not this Article's; the title of this Article is a deliberate, credited analogy to their work.[^75] The novelty claimed here is the *domain* and the *cross-regime synthesis* — Schwarcz's arbitrage is *intra*-industry, risk moving from regulated insurers to their own captives, whereas this Article's is *inter*-industry, risk moving across the insurance, professional-responsibility, and funding regimes among unaffiliated instrument types — not the coinage.

All five template elements map onto litigation finance using Part I's own evidence. The sector is large in the operative, non-systemic sense and is concentrated (Section I.B). The function — transfer and pricing of legal-claim risk — is regulated heavily when performed as insurance and barely when performed as funding (Section I.C). The perimeter-escape is doctrinally located in the *Royal Drug*/§ 1101 analysis: funding satisfies the risk-transfer core and slips both the spreading prong and the formal shell. The definitional gap is the "neither loan nor insurance" limbo that *Ruth* and *Maslowski* expose. And the fragility — the uncapitalized counterparty, the externalized solvency and control risk — is what Part III documents. The analogy is disciplined by the template, which answers the natural objection that "shadow-X" reasoning proves too much. The claim is not that funding is suspect because one can append the word "shadow" to it; it is that funding satisfies the *specific* five-element condition under which Fleischer's arbitrage incentive arises. Where an instrument creates genuinely new economic substance, the template does not fit and the charge does not lie.

A disciplined analogy also requires a disciplined trigger, lest the functional claim sweep in every contingent contract. A stock option, a product warranty, an equity stake — all "price risk" in some sense, yet none belongs in this market. The boundary is a conjunctive, three-prong trigger that I state here and develop in Part IV: the instrument must (i) effect a *non-recourse transfer of a specific litigant's claim risk*, (ii) to a counterparty *on whose performance the claimant relies* for the value of the transfer, (iii) under conditions of *information asymmetry or claimant vulnerability*. A stock option fails the trigger — there is no specific litigant's claim risk, no claimant relying on a counterparty's performance, no vulnerable party — and so does ordinary equity. This is why the taxonomy below includes, as a deliberate *negative control*, the recourse law-firm loan: it shares the *lending form* with funding but lacks the *contingent-on-outcome risk transfer*, and so falls outside the category. A definition that excludes recourse lending and stock options is not gerrymandered to include everything; it is doing exactly the discriminating work a functional account must do.

Two further theoretical resources complete the frame. First, the boundary the law is policing is not natural or self-evident, because "insurance" is itself a contested category. Kenneth Abraham has shown that the concept sustains at least four distinct conceptions — insurance as contract, as a public-utility/regulated industry, as a product, and as a form of governance — each carrying its own regulatory agenda.[^76] If insurance has no single essence, then the insurance/non-insurance boundary across which legal-claim risk migrates is a regulatory *choice*, not a discovered fact, which makes the question "where should the line fall?" a live one that Part IV can answer functionally rather than a fixed datum to be respected. Second, the empirical study of shadow sectors offers a method, not merely an analogy. Koijen and Yogo measured shadow-insurance migration with hard data, and the DFS report quantified it — ceded liabilities reportedly growing from $11 billion to $364 billion — because the reporting infrastructure existed to measure it.[^77] One caution must travel with that figure every time it appears, including here: the $364 billion captive-reinsurance channel dwarfs the entire $16 billion U.S. commercial funding market, and the borrowing is purely *methodological* — the point is that a reporting perimeter *made migration measurable*, not that the magnitudes are remotely comparable. With that disclaimer fixed, the same method could in principle measure legal-claim-risk migration; that it cannot yet be applied is a function of the data gap this Part has already flagged, and a limitation Part III confronts candidly. I therefore frame the migration claim, throughout, as a documented market *episode* paired with a *testable hypothesis*, with Koijen and Yogo's design as the template for the empirical work the field still owes itself — not as identified causation.

#### The Signature Taxonomy: The Legal-Claim Risk Transfer Matrix

The functional account can be rendered as a single matrix that sorts the instruments along five shared axes and then, in a visually segregated final column, records the regulatory regime each occupies. The matrix is built so that two facts become legible at once: the instruments cluster by *function*, and they scatter by *regime*. Following Merton and Bodie, the analysis anchors on function and lets entity and label fall out as a residual.

The five axes are these. *Axis 1, the identity of the risk-shedder* — claimant, defendant or judgment-debtor, law firm, or post-verdict judgment-holder — matters because it determines which body of protective law (consumer, professional-responsibility, corporate, or none) presumptively attaches; the same provider, Burford, appears at multiple points on this axis, proving the provider regime-agnostic while the law remains shedder-specific. *Axis 2, the payoff form*, is load-bearing, because it is what the "is-it-insurance/is-it-a-loan" doctrine actually keys on: a non-recourse contingent advance or purchase (pays on the upside), an indemnity against a defined loss (pays on the downside, premium up front), or a fee-share (the lawyer's upside position written in services). *Axis 3, the temporal trigger* — pre-filing, interlocutory, or post-judgment — tracks the narrowing of risk as a claim matures. *Axis 4, risk granularity* — single-matter, portfolio, or securitized — locates the funder-side pooling that the doctrine treats as definitional of insurance but that funding performs, on the investor side, outside the perimeter. *Axis 5, counterparty vulnerability* — individual consumer, SME, large corporate, or law firm — is deliberately orthogonal to current regulatory intensity, the mismatch that becomes Part IV's normative engine. Five axes, no more: a taxonomy earns authority by parsimony.

Row 8, the recourse law-firm loan, is the negative control: it shares the lending *form* with non-recourse funding but lacks the contingent-on-outcome risk transfer, and so is excluded, demonstrating that the category is not drawn to swallow everything. Row 11, the hybrid wrapper, is the boundary on the other side: it is the arbitrage made literal, a single transaction split across regimes, exemplified by *Appian*'s simultaneous JPI policy and monetization strategy and by the roughly 19% of 2024 commitments now insurance-wrapped.[^78]

The same data can be read along two analytic projections that extract the payoff. Hold the instruments on a *function space* whose coordinates are Axis 2 (payoff form, running advance → fee-share → indemnity) and Axis 3 (timing, running ex ante → ex post). On these coordinates the eleven instruments cluster by economic substitutability, and the decisive cluster is the near-coincidence of JPI (row 6) and appellate monetization (row 9): both sit at the ex post extreme, both lay off the same post-adjudication reversal risk, and they differ only in the sign and trigger of their payoff — one a downside indemnity the holder buys, the other a non-recourse upside position the holder sells to shed the same exposure. They occupy effectively the same location because they are opposite-facing ways to lay off one underlying risk. Now hold that function space fixed and re-sort the very same points by regulatory regime — insurance, professional responsibility, consumer statute, or near-unregulated. The regime sort does not respect the functional clusters. The two instruments that sit on top of each other in function space fall on opposite sides of the regulatory line: JPI is regulated as insurance, its monetization twin is near-unregulated, though they occupy the same functional coordinates. *The regulatory partition is orthogonal to the functional partition.* That orthogonality is the definition of an arbitrage opportunity, and it operationalizes Fleischer's "gap between the economics of a deal and its regulatory treatment" as a literal gap between two ways of sorting one set of instruments — the same functional point in risk-space, two regulatory worlds apart.

That is the descriptive and analytic predicate of the Article. One market, one underlying commodity — legal-claim risk — transferred and priced by a stack of instruments that are economic substitutes and legal non-equivalents, fractured across three regulatory regimes along lines that track form rather than function. Part I has not yet argued that this fracture is harmful; it has argued only that it exists and that it is no accident, but the predictable product of keying regulation to entity labels in a market whose function is stable across them. The next Part maps the three regimes whose seam this Section has located — insurance's prudential and solvency architecture, professional responsibility's entity-based bans, and funding's near-vacuum — and shows that the single classification act on which the whole partition turns, the question whether a given risk transfer "is insurance," is doctrinally unstable at exactly the point where the functional account predicts it will be.

## II. The Regulatory Fault Lines

### II.0. Three Regimes, One Risk

Part I established that third-party litigation funding (TPLF), after-the-event (ATE) insurance, judgment-preservation insurance (JPI), and the contingency fee are economic substitutes—four instruments that transfer and price a single commodity, legal-claim risk.[^79] If the law tracked economic substance, one would expect these substitutes to be governed by one regime, or by regimes that converge as the instruments converge. They are not: the same risk transfer is sorted into one of three radically different regulatory worlds depending not on the risk transferred but on a prior act of classification—a decision about what to *call* the transaction.

The first world is insurance regulation: a dense, prudential architecture of licensing, reserves, risk-based capital, corrective action, and a guaranty-fund backstop, all keyed to a statutory term of art—the "business of insurance"—and committed by federal statute to the states. The second is professional responsibility: a regime of entity-based prohibitions on fee-sharing and nonlawyer ownership, administered by bar authorities and justified not by solvency but by the "professional independence" of the lawyer. The third is funding's near-vacuum: an unstable residue of champerty doctrine, a thin consumer-credit overlay, and an emergent patchwork of disclosure and registration rules—none of it prudential, none of it protecting the claimant against the failure of the counterparty to whom she has transferred her risk.

These three regimes differ along three axes this Part tracks throughout: in what *triggers* regulation (a transaction, an entity's proximity to the lawyer, or almost nothing at all), in *intensity* (prudential solvency regulation, conduct-and-ethics regulation, and near-silence), and in *whom they protect* (policyholders through reserves and guaranty associations; clients through the lawyer's undivided loyalty; and, on the funding side, essentially no one ex ante). The mapping is meant to be read side by side, because the asymmetry is the argument: it is hard to look at three regimes governing one risk and not ask why the law treats functional twins as strangers.

Everything funnels to a single doctrinal hinge. Which regime attaches turns on one classification question—is the instrument "insurance"?—and that question, worked through the Supreme Court's "business of insurance" trilogy and the operative state-law definition, is doctrinally unstable at precisely the seam that matters. The same contingency feature that pulls funding out of the "loan" category also pulls it out of the "insurance" category, dropping it into a regime of its own. This is the gap between "the economics of a deal and its regulatory treatment" that Victor Fleischer identified as the structural precondition of regulatory arbitrage.[^80] Part II's task is to locate that gap and show it is arbitrary at the seams; the diagnosis of arbitrage as a live, costly phenomenon is the work of Part III.

### II.A. The Insurance Regime: Heavy Prudential Regulation Keyed to a Statutory Term

#### II.A.1. McCarran-Ferguson and the Stakes of the Line

Begin with what is at stake in the classification. Under the McCarran-Ferguson Act of 1945, Congress committed the regulation and taxation of "the business of insurance" to the several states and declared that "silence on the part of the Congress shall not be construed to impose any barrier" to such state regulation.[^81] The Act's operative provision is a reverse-preemption rule: § 1012(b) provides that "[n]o Act of Congress shall be construed to invalidate, impair, or supersede any law enacted by any State for the purpose of regulating the business of insurance . . . unless such Act specifically relates to the business of insurance."[^82] The ordinary preemption hierarchy is thus inverted. A generally applicable federal statute yields to state insurance law unless Congress speaks to insurance by name—a clear-statement rule that protects the state regulatory apparatus from incidental federal displacement.

The phrase "business of insurance" thus carries enormous regulatory freight. To classify a risk-transfer product as insurance is to summon the entire state prudential apparatus—licensing, reserves, capital adequacy, examination, guaranty-fund coverage—and, through § 1012(b), to clothe it in protection against federal interference. To classify the same risk transfer as something else—an investment, an assignment, a fee arrangement—is to leave it outside that apparatus altogether. The line between "insurance" and "not insurance" is therefore the switch that selects between heavy regulation and none—exactly the gradient along which capital can be expected to flow, the gradient this Article calls arbitrage.

#### II.A.2. The "Business of Insurance" Trilogy as a Narrowing Sequence

Because McCarran-Ferguson does not define "business of insurance," the content of the term has been supplied by a trilogy of Supreme Court decisions. A precision note is owed at the outset, because the three decisions do not all arise in the same federal context. *National Securities* is a § 1012(b) reverse-preemption case—it construes "business of insurance" to decide whether a state statute displaces a federal one (there, Rule 10b-5)—while *Royal Drug* and *Pireno* construe the same phrase for the narrower § 1013(b) antitrust exemption. None of the three asks the question this Part ultimately cares about: whether a particular product is "doing an insurance business" for licensing purposes. That operative product-definition test is a matter of state law, addressed below.[^83] The trilogy is borrowed here not as the statutory definition of an insurance *product* but as the most authoritative federal articulation of what insurance functionally *is*—and, read in sequence, it progressively narrows the term to its risk-bearing core. *National Securities*' reverse-preemption posture sits closest to the classification question this Article pursues: like this Article, it asked which regulatory regime a transaction belongs to, not whether conduct escapes the antitrust laws.

The sequence begins with *SEC v. National Securities, Inc.*, which located the term in a *relationship*. Holding that an Arizona statute used to block an insurance-company merger regulated the stockholder-company relationship, not the business of insurance, and so did not reverse-preempt SEC Rule 10b-5,[^84] the Court explained that what Congress was concerned with was "the relationship between the insurance company and the policyholder"; "[s]tatutes aimed at protecting or regulating this relationship, directly or indirectly, are laws regulating the 'business of insurance.'"[^85] "The relationship between insurer and insured, the type of policy which could be issued, its reliability, interpretation, and enforcement—these were the core of the 'business of insurance.'"[^86]

*Group Life & Health Insurance Co. v. Royal Drug Co.* narrowed the term further, to *risk*. Holding that pharmacy-provider agreements between Blue Shield and pharmacies were not the business of insurance—because they were cost-saving purchasing arrangements rather than risk transfers, "merely arrangements for the purchase of goods and services" that "d[id] not involve any underwriting or spreading of risk"—the Court announced the proposition that anchors this Article's functional account: "The primary elements of an insurance contract are the spreading and underwriting of a policyholder's risk."[^87] *Royal Drug* thus did two things at once. It made risk transfer the doctrinal heart of insurance, and it distinguished "the business of insurance" from the broader "business of insurers,"[^88] signaling that not everything an insurer does falls within the term. That "spreading and underwriting" language is double-edged for the present inquiry: it identifies the very risk-transfer function that TPLF, ATE, and JPI all perform, while pointing to the pooling-and-underwriting structure that, as Part II.D shows, single-claim funding lacks.

*Union Labor Life Insurance Co. v. Pireno* completed the narrowing by reducing the inquiry to a canonical three-factor test. Holding that an insurer's use of a chiropractic peer-review committee to evaluate claims was not the business of insurance, the Court asked:

> [F]irst, whether the practice has the effect of transferring or spreading a policyholder's risk; second, whether the practice is an integral part of the policy relationship between the insurer and the insured; and third, whether the practice is limited to entities within the insurance industry.[^89]

Critically—and this caveat does much of the work in Part II.D—the Court added that "[n]one of these criteria is necessarily determinative in itself."[^90] *Pireno*'s first factor restates *Royal Drug*'s risk-transfer core; its second restates *National Securities*'s relationship focus; its third adds an entity criterion. The resulting test is the operative instrument this Part later applies to the four instruments—and its three factors are not of a kind: the first is *functional* (does the practice transfer or spread risk?), while the second and third are *formal* (is the relationship styled as insurer-insured, and is the actor inside the insurance industry?). Whether the formal factors can carry the entire classification when the functional factor is satisfied is the question the trilogy poses but does not answer.

#### II.A.3. The Prudential Payload

What does classification as insurance actually summon? A three-stage prudential architecture with no analogue in funding. The National Association of Insurance Commissioners describes solvency regulation as a three-stage process: first, *guardrails* that mitigate risk ex ante (licensing, investment limits, reserves, risk-based capital); second, ongoing *financial oversight and corrective action* (financial reporting, own-risk-and-solvency assessment, examinations) designed to catch and cure deterioration before failure; and third, a *backstop*—receivership and guaranty associations—when rehabilitation or liquidation becomes necessary.[^91]

The first stage can be quantified, which is what makes "heavy regulation" tangible rather than rhetorical. Under the NAIC's Risk-Based Capital for Insurers Model Act, an insurer must hold statutory minimum capital scaled to its size and risk, with four graduated regulatory action levels pegged to its Authorized Control Level (ACL) capital: a Company Action Level at 200% of ACL (the insurer must file a corrective plan), a Regulatory Action Level at 150% (examination and corrective orders), the Authorized Control Level itself at 100% (the commissioner may seize the company), and a Mandatory Control Level at 70% (the commissioner *must* take over).[^92] These thresholds were a response to a wave of insurer insolvencies—a 1992 study counted 176 life and health insurer failures between 1975 and 1990, roughly eighty percent of them after 1982—and they embody a judgment that a risk-bearing counterparty must be capitalized against the contingency it has assumed and supervised on a sliding scale as that capital erodes.[^93]

The third stage is the starkest. Every state, the District of Columbia, Puerto Rico, and (for property and casualty) the U.S. Virgin Islands maintain guaranty mechanisms that pay covered claims when an insurer fails; the National Organization of Life and Health Insurance Guaranty Associations coordinates multistate insolvencies, and in a life or health liquidation the guaranty mechanism continues eligible contracts that would otherwise terminate.[^94] The home-state commissioner, acting as receiver, winds down the insolvent insurer.[^95] The policyholder is thus protected at every stage: capitalized against ex ante, supervised on the way down, and made whole, within limits, after a failure. Part III's first harm is simply this architecture's absence on the funding side—the claimant who transfers the same risk to a funder is protected at none of these stages.[^96]

#### II.A.4. *Fabe* and the Elasticity of the Term

One more feature of the insurance regime matters: the term "business of insurance" is not monolithic, and the Court has read it differently for different statutory purposes. In *Department of Treasury v. Fabe*, the Court held that an Ohio insurer-liquidation priority statute was a law "enacted for the purpose of regulating the business of insurance" under § 1012(b)—and therefore reverse-preempted the federal priority statute—*to the extent* it protected policyholders, but not as to other creditor categories.[^97] In so holding, the Court expressly distinguished the broader § 1012(b) "purpose of regulating" standard from the narrower *Pireno* antitrust test, reading the term *more* broadly when policyholder protection was the object.[^98]

*Fabe* thus establishes that the same words expand or contract with the regulatory purpose in view—and expand when the protective function of insurance regulation is at stake. The doctrine itself treats "insurance" not as a fixed natural kind but as a purposive category whose boundaries follow the policyholder-protective aim. That seed is harvested later: it foreshadows both the incoherence argument that closes this Part and the protective, functional reading on which Part IV's prescription depends. For now it is enough that the heaviest regime in the field rests on a term the Supreme Court has itself declared elastic.

### II.B. The Professional-Responsibility Regime: Entity-Based Bans Policing the Same Money

When legal-claim-risk capital flows through or near the lawyer rather than directly to the client, a different regime attaches—and the contribution of this section is to insist that it, too, is a regime governing risk-transfer money, not a freestanding code of "ethics" sealed off from the field. It simply selects its trigger differently: it attaches by the capital's *proximity to the lawyer*, and regulates by entity-based prohibition rather than by prudential or risk-based control.

#### II.B.1. The Entity Wall

The wall is Model Rule 5.4. Rule 5.4(a) provides that "[a] lawyer or law firm shall not share legal fees with a nonlawyer," subject to narrow enumerated exceptions; Rule 5.4(d) bars nonlawyer ownership or control of a law-practice entity.[^99] The justification, stated in the comment, is to "protect the lawyer's professional independence of judgment."[^100] These are categorical entity bans: they ask *who* holds the interest (a nonlawyer) rather than *what* the arrangement does to the lawyer's judgment in a given case. The function—an outside supplier of capital taking a contingent interest in litigation proceeds—is permitted or forbidden depending on the formal identity of the party holding the interest.

#### II.B.2. The Profession Regulating Itself: Rule 1.8(e) Versus the 1.8(f) Counter-Template

The profession's internal treatment of claim-risk financing reveals the same entity logic—and contains, side by side, both the entity model and its functional alternative. Rule 1.8(e) historically forbade a lawyer from providing "financial assistance to a client in connection with pending or contemplated litigation," with a single exception permitting the lawyer to advance court costs and litigation expenses, "the repayment of which may be contingent on the outcome."[^101] A lawyer could finance the *litigation* on a contingent, non-recourse basis—but could not advance a client's *living expenses*, on the rationale that doing so would "encourage clients to pursue lawsuits that might not otherwise be brought" and give lawyers "too great a financial stake" in the litigation.[^102] That living-expense function is precisely what consumer TPLF now performs for the client; the same risk transfer is permitted or forbidden by reference to *who* performs it, not *what it does*. And the rule has proved malleable: in 2020 the ABA amended Rule 1.8(e) to allow a lawyer representing an indigent client pro bono to provide "modest gifts" for "food, rent, transportation, medicine and other basic living expenses"—an admission that the categorical bar bends when the equities press.[^103]

Against this entity logic the same body of rules supplies a cleaner, function-based alternative. Rule 1.8(f)—the third-party-payor rule—provides that a lawyer may accept compensation for representing a client "from one other than the client" so long as three conditions are met: the client gives informed consent, "there is no interference with the lawyer's independence of professional judgment or with the client-lawyer relationship," and client information is protected under Rule 1.6.[^104] Rule 5.4(c) reinforces the second condition by barring any "person who recommends, employs, or pays the lawyer" from "direct[ing] or regulat[ing] the lawyer's professional judgment."[^105] This is the structural opposite of Rule 5.4(a)'s flat ban. Rule 1.8(f) permits the function—a non-client paying for representation—*regardless of the payor's identity*, and protects the value at stake (independent judgment) through enforceable conditions rather than a categorical prohibition. It is the cleanest existing function-with-conditions template in the field, and Part IV returns to it as the model the prescription scales up.[^106] The profession's own rulebook thus contains both regulatory grammars—the entity ban and the function-with-conditions permission—governing the same flow of outside money.

#### II.B.3. The Formalist Seam

Nowhere is the entity logic more nakedly on display than in the New York City Bar's treatment of litigation funding. Formal Opinion 2018-5 holds that a lawyer "may not enter into a financing agreement with a litigation funder . . . under which the lawyer's future payments to the funder are contingent on the lawyer's receipt of legal fees"—because such *lawyer-directed* funding violates Rule 5.4(a)'s fee-sharing ban.[^107] But the Opinion expressly holds that *client-directed* funding—where the funder is repaid "by the client out of the client's recovery"—does "not implicate Rule 5.4 . . . because the lawyer is not a party to the arrangement."[^108] Two economically identical transfers of legal-claim risk, priced off the same litigation, are treated oppositely based solely on *who signs the check*; only the formal path of the payment differs. Formal Opinion 2024-2 reaffirms the line and catalogs the dozen-plus rules a lawyer must navigate by hand, case by case, when advising on a funding agreement—Rules 1.0, 1.1, 1.2, 1.4, 1.6, 1.7, 1.8, 1.9, 1.15, 1.16, 5.4, and 5.7—a dense, manual, situational overlay that stands in vivid contrast to the structured, ex ante solvency-and-conduct regulation of insurers.[^109]

The gap on this side of the field is as telling as the seam. The ABA's most pertinent ethics opinion, Formal Opinion 484, addressed a lawyer's obligations when a client uses a company to finance the *lawyer's fee* on a recourse basis and expressly declined to opine on non-recourse litigation funding at all.[^110] The profession's flagship guidance, ABA Resolution 111A, is voluntary "best practices" recommending that funding arrangements be in writing and that "the client remain[] in control of the case," but imposing no licensing, no capital requirement, and no mandatory disclosure—and conceding that the profession's own positions on fee-splitting are "far from unanimous."[^111] The professional-responsibility regime's answer to non-recourse funding is guidance, not regulation—a gap that parallels, on the lawyer-proximate side, the funding vacuum mapped in Part II.C.

#### II.B.4. Dissolving the Wall

That the entity wall is contingent rather than natural is shown by the two jurisdictions that have removed it. In 2020 Arizona became the first state to abrogate Rule 5.4 entirely, licensing "Alternative Business Structures" that permit nonlawyers to hold economic interests and decision-making authority in entities providing legal services, subject to a supervising licensed lawyer.[^112] Utah, the same year, launched a regulatory "sandbox" overseeing nonlawyer-owned providers under data-driven, risk-based supervision rather than a categorical ban.[^113] Where the wall comes down, capital reorganizes around its absence: Arizona's ABS structure has proved especially attractive, by one account, for "private equity investments in mass tort/class action firms" that "litigate on contingency terms"—the very legal-claim-risk financing this Article unifies, now routed through equity ownership of the firm rather than a funding contract with the client.[^114] The lesson is arbitrage in miniature: a rule that selects its trigger by entity does not eliminate the function but relocates it to whatever structure or jurisdiction the entity line does not reach—to client-side funding where Rule 5.4 does not bite, and to Arizona and Utah where the rule has been repealed.

### II.C. The Funding Near-Vacuum: Champerty Residue, the Consumer-Credit Debate, and Emergent Disclosure

The third regime is best understood as a structured absence. Commercial TPLF operates in a near-regulatory vacuum, but the vacuum has internal strata: an unstable common-law residue (champerty), a thin consumer-credit overlay, and an emergent disclosure patchwork. None is prudential; none capitalizes a funder or protects a claimant against counterparty failure; and all quietly draw a funding/insurance boundary they never pause to defend.

#### II.C.1. Champerty as an Unstable, Gameable Gate

The historical anti-funding doctrines—champerty, maintenance, barratry—have eroded unevenly into a patchwork that runs from outright abolition to active revival. At one end, the Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts held in *Saladini v. Righellis* that champerty, barratry, and maintenance "no longer shall be recognized," reasoning that existing fee regulations, sanctions rules, and the doctrines of "unconscionability, duress, and good faith" were "more than sufficient" to address the evils the old doctrines targeted.[^115] Minnesota followed in *Maslowski v. Prospect Funding Partners LLC*, abolishing champerty on the ground that litigation-financing agreements "allow plaintiffs who would otherwise be priced out of the justice system to assert their rights."[^116] The reasoning of these decisions is sound: abolition reflects a defensible access-to-justice judgment, and *Saladini*'s confidence that modern doctrines could absorb the function is the friend of this Article's argument, not its target. But abolition substituted a *replacement* assumption for a *replacement regime*. *Saladini* assumed that fee rules, sanctions, and unconscionability would suffice; it did not erect any apparatus calibrated to a commercial funding market that would, in the decades after 1997, grow into a multibillion-dollar industry. Abolition without a successor regime is one face of the vacuum.

At the other end, New York retains a broad statutory champerty regime. Judiciary Law § 489(1) bars any "person or co-partnership, corporation or association" from "buy[ing] or tak[ing] an assignment of" a claim "with the intent and for the purpose of bringing an action or proceeding thereon," subject to a safe harbor in § 489(2) for transactions with an "aggregate purchase price" of at least $500,000.[^117] In *Justinian Capital SPC v. WestLB AG*, the New York Court of Appeals showed the doctrine is no dead letter: it voided a claim purchase as champertous and held the $500,000 safe harbor inapplicable because the nominal purchase price was contingent on litigation recovery rather than a "binding and bona fide obligation to pay," the legislature having intended buyers to have "skin in the game."[^118] Yet even in New York the doctrine is narrowing at the edges: in *IKB International S.A. v. Morgan Stanley*, the First Department confined champerty to acquisitions by a "stranger" to the dispute, inapplicable where the assignee had an independent beneficial interest in the claims.[^119]

The instability is most vivid where the doctrine has been reduced to a structural checklist that funders simply draft around. In *Charge Injection Technologies, Inc. v. E.I. du Pont de Nemours & Co.*, the Delaware Superior Court rejected a champerty-and-maintenance challenge to a Burford-funded claim and supplied a structural safe-harbor test: financing is not champertous where the plaintiff does not assign its claims, the funder has no right to control the litigation, and the plaintiff "retain[s] the unfettered right to settle . . . for any amount."[^120] This is doctrine reduced to a drafting specification: a funder need only satisfy the three conditions—no assignment, no control, retained settlement authority—and the champerty gate swings open. The Delaware test is the clearest exhibit in the field of a doctrine being engineered around rather than enforced, and it underscores why a function-based regime, indifferent to the formal structure of the paper, is the alternative the rest of this Article develops.

#### II.C.2. The Consumer-Credit Characterization—and Why It Is Also the Insurance Exit

When courts have tried to slot non-recourse funding into a *substantive* regulatory category, they have reached for the loan, and rejected it—a move with decisive significance for the doctrinal hinge in Part II.D. In *Ruth v. Cherokee Funding, LLC*, the Supreme Court of Georgia held that non-recourse, contingent consumer litigation funding is not a "loan" under the state's Industrial Loan Act or Payday Lending Act, because repayment was "contingent" on a successful recovery and "limited to the extent of the damages . . . recover[ed]."[^121] The Minnesota Supreme Court, returning to *Maslowski* on the usury question, likewise wrestled with whether a repayment "contingent" on recovery—where "if [the claimant] was not successful in her suit, she would owe . . . nothing"—is an "absolute obligation of repayment," and declined to resolve whether such agreements are categorically non-loans.[^122]

Here is the pivotal observation, stated as doctrine and not yet as critique. The very feature that exits the "loan" category—the absence of an unconditional obligation to repay—is the same feature that exits the "insurance" category. A loan requires an absolute obligation to repay; an insurance contract requires an obligation to indemnify a loss. Non-recourse funding has neither: the claimant owes nothing if she loses, and the funder promises to pay her nothing if she loses. The contingency that defeats "loan" simultaneously defeats "indemnity," which is why courts that reject loan classification never reach for insurance classification either; the transaction falls between the two regulated categories, classified—when classified at all—as an investment or an assignment.[^123] This double exit is the doctrinal mechanism of the arbitrage, and Part II.D works it out in full.

Where legislatures *have* imposed substantive controls, it has been almost entirely on the *consumer* side, and the controls they chose are unmistakably credit-and-insurance-style price-and-term regulation. Ohio's pioneering statute, enacted in direct response to a state-court champerty ruling, governs "non-recourse civil litigation advance" contracts through attorney attestations and a mandatory boldface disclosure that the funder has no right to make decisions about the litigation or its settlement.[^124] Tennessee caps the fee at "an annual percentage rate of 25 percent" and limits the term to three years.[^125] West Virginia caps the annual fee at eighteen percent, bars fees for any period exceeding forty-two months, restricts assignment and securitization, and requires registration.[^126] These are the tools of financial-product regulation—rate caps, term limits, anti-securitization rules, registration—deployed because legislatures already understand consumer legal-claim-risk transfer as a regulated financial product. The contrast is the point: the *commercial* funder—the functional twin of the JPI insurer, transferring the same litigation risk at far greater scale—remains, in most of the country, untouched by any analogous control.

#### II.C.3. The 2023–2026 State Wave: Registration and Foreign-Funder Rules, Not Prudential Regulation

A wave of state legislation since 2023 might be thought to refute the "vacuum" premise. It does not, and characterizing it accurately is essential to the diagnosis. The wave is converging on *entity registration*, *foreign-funder restriction*, and *disclosure*—not on prudential solvency regulation. Montana's amended Litigation Financing Act requires registration, caps a funder's recovery at twenty-five percent of any judgment or settlement, bars assignment and securitization, prohibits funder control of legal decisions, and adds a "Foreign Investment in Litigation Financing" chapter.[^127] Georgia's 2025 statute requires registration with the state banking regulator (via the Nationwide Multistate Licensing System), bars funding affiliated with a foreign adversary, imposes joint-and-several liability for frivolous-litigation sanctions, makes larger agreements discoverable, and forbids funder control of settlement or strategy.[^128] Indiana reaches *commercial* funding, barring funding financed by a "foreign entity of concern," prohibiting funder control, and making agreements discoverable—without any rate cap.[^129] Louisiana, Oklahoma, and Kansas pair foreign-funder disclosure with in-camera review of the agreements.[^130]

This is a real regulatory response, and a largely function-based one in its targeting—but it is fragmented, heavily national-security-driven, and conspicuously *not* prudential. Tabulated by dominant regulatory tool, the wave makes its own pattern legible: registration here, a recovery cap there, a foreign-ownership ban almost everywhere—and, in the rightmost column, nothing at all.

The proliferation of registration-and-disclosure statutes, far from filling the vacuum, confirms its character—legislatures reflexively reach for transparency and foreign-ownership screens precisely because the function remains unclassified as the risk-transfer product it is.

#### II.C.4. Emergent Federal Disclosure as a Patchwork

The dominant federal response is procedural disclosure, and it is at once incomplete, judge-by-judge, and—most tellingly—built around an insurance carve-out. The one federal disclosure rule actually adopted, the 2022 amendment to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 7.1, reaches corporate parentage and the citizenship of parties in diversity cases but does not reach funders at all.[^132] Funder disclosure exists only through single-judge standing orders and district local rules. The most prominent, Chief Judge Connolly's standing order in the District of Delaware, requires a party whose litigation is financed by a non-party "on a non-recourse basis" to disclose the funder's identity and any control rights—but expressly excludes "insurance," along with personal and bank loans.[^133] The District of New Jersey's Local Civil Rule 7.1.1 imposes a district-wide version, again carved to exclude "insurance,"[^134] and the Northern District of California's standing order reaches funding only in "proposed class, collective, or representative action[s]."[^135] The leading reform proposal makes the boundary explicit: Lawyers for Civil Justice and the Chamber's Institute for Legal Reform have suggested amending Rule 26(a)(1)(A) to require automatic disclosure of non-recourse funders—expressly invoking the 1970 adoption of the Rule 26 *insurance*-disclosure requirement as precedent—while carving out "contingency-fee arrangements, insurance, or ordinary personal and bank loans."[^136] The proposal thus analogizes funding to insurance disclosure in the same breath that it exempts insurance and contingency fees: it recognizes the functional equivalence and then re-enacts the very discrimination this Article diagnoses, drawing the funding/insurance boundary inside the cure.[^137]

The federal rulemaking apparatus, meanwhile, remains in study. The Advisory Committee on Civil Rules created a Third-Party Litigation Funding Subcommittee only in October 2024, and reported itself still "getting its arms around the topic" months later.[^138] Congress's levers are fragmented and function-blind: the Litigation Transparency Act would mandate disclosure of any party with a contingent right to payment,[^139] while a separate bill would impose a tax—reportedly around 40.8%—on funders' "qualified litigation proceeds," treating funding as taxable investment income rather than regulating it as risk transfer.[^140] The net picture is unmistakable: an unstable common-law residue, a consumer-only statutory layer, and an emergent disclosure patchwork—none prudential, none protecting the claimant ex ante against funder under-capitalization or counterparty failure, all drawing a funding/insurance line it never defends.

### II.D. The Doctrinal Hinge: Applying the Insurance Test to TPLF, ATE, and JPI

The entire three-regime sort reduces to the hinge question—is the instrument insurance? This section runs the three risk-transfer instruments through one analytic test, side by side, and asks whether the resulting line is coherent. The answer, established here and handed to Part III, is that it is not: funding satisfies the *functional* core of the insurance definition and escapes only on *formal* features that map poorly onto the risk transferred.

#### II.D.1. The Operative Test(s)

Three sources supply the operative criteria. First, the *Pireno* three-factor test, set out above, asks whether the practice transfers or spreads a policyholder's risk, whether it is integral to the insurer-insured relationship, and whether it is limited to entities within the insurance industry.[^141] *Pireno* is the best federal articulation of insurance's functional core, but—again—it is § 1013(b) antitrust-exemption doctrine, not the test for whether a product is "doing an insurance business" for licensing purposes. That operative product-definition test is state law—and it furnishes the second source. New York Insurance Law § 1101(a)(1) is representative and unusually precise: an "insurance contract" is an agreement whereby the insurer "is obligated to confer benefit of pecuniary value upon another party . . . dependent upon the happening of a fortuitous event in which the [other party] has . . . a material interest which will be adversely affected by the happening of such event."[^142] Decomposed, the statute supplies four elements: (i) an obligation to confer a pecuniary benefit, (ii) on a fortuitous event, (iii) in which the counterparty holds a material interest, (iv) which the event will adversely affect.[^143] Third, for hybrid arrangements, the canonical *Jordan v. Group Health Ass'n* "principal object and purpose" test asks whether risk transfer and distribution is the dominant object of the contract; as Judge Rutledge put it, "[i]f there is no risk, or there being one it is not shifted to another or others, there can be neither insurance nor indemnity," and insurance "by the better view" involves "distribution of the risk."[^144] And *SEC v. Variable Annuity Life Insurance Co.* supplies a complementary issuer-risk requirement: a product is not insurance where the issuer "assume[s] no true risk in the insurance sense," because "the concept of 'insurance' involves some investment risk-taking on the part of the company."[^145] The operative test for regulatory classification is the state product definition—§ 1101's four statutory elements—with *Griffin* and the synthesis that follows offered as judicial and functional gloss, not competing black-letter tests. Distilled across statute, Supreme Court, and the leading circuit test, insurance requires (a) the transfer of a fortuitous risk of loss, (b) to a counterparty that indemnifies that loss, (c) pooled or underwritten across a body of insureds, and (d) in which the insured holds a material interest adversely affected by the event. Elements (a), (b), and (d) track the § 1101 statutory text; element (c)—pooling and distribution—does not appear in § 1101's definition of an insurance *contract* but is supplied by *Jordan* and *Royal Drug* as a functional hallmark, flagged here as a contestable, judicially derived add-on rather than a statutory element because the arbitrage turns on it.[^146]

#### II.D.2. JPI and ATE Inside the Line

Run the first two instruments through the test, and they sit squarely inside it. JPI indemnifies a fortuitous loss—the reversal of a favorable judgment on appeal—and the insurer, not the policyholder, bears and pays out that risk. *BMC Software, Inc. v. International Business Machines Corp.*, where a judgment-preservation tower reportedly led by Liberty Mutual paid on the Fifth Circuit's reversal of BMC's ~$1.6 billion judgment as an insured loss, is the proof.[^147] JPI satisfies *Pireno* factor one (it transfers the policyholder's reversal risk), factor two (it is integral to an insurer-insured relationship), and factor three (it is sold by insurers)—and the § 1101 elements: the insurer confers a pecuniary benefit on the fortuitous event of reversal, in which the policyholder holds a material interest that reversal adversely affects. ATE is the same analysis one stage earlier: it indemnifies the insured against a defined fortuitous litigation loss—adverse-costs liability and own-side disbursements if the claim fails—is written and sold by insurers, and is regulated as insurance in its United Kingdom home; it would satisfy the U.S. product-definition test but remains nearly absent from the U.S. market because the American Rule rarely shifts fees.[^148] Both instruments are insurance and are regulated as such. They are the control group.

#### II.D.3. TPLF Outside the Line: Four Escape Features, Stated in the Funder's Strongest Form

TPLF escapes. It does so on four formal features, and intellectual honesty requires stating them first in their strongest form—the form a sophisticated funder and a formalist insurance scholar would press—because that steel-man is the premise the rest of the argument must earn the right to qualify.

*First, non-recourse, no indemnity.* The funder does not promise to make the claimant whole upon an adverse event; if the claim fails, the claimant simply owes nothing and the funder loses its advance. There is no indemnification of a "loss" because the funder undertakes no obligation to restore the claimant to a pre-loss position. *Second, no fortuitous-loss trigger on the claimant's side.* The payout runs the *other* way: the funder is paid *on success*, not on the claimant's loss—an inversion of the insurance logic, under which the insurer pays when the insured suffers the insured-against harm. *Third, assignment or investment rather than pooling.* The funder buys a contingent asset—a stake in the proceeds of a particular claim—and does not underwrite or spread a pool of insureds' risks; *Royal Drug*'s "spreading and underwriting of a policyholder's risk" is, on this view, simply absent.[^149] *Fourth, no insurable or material interest adversely affected.* Unlike an insured, whose house burns or whose judgment is reversed, the funder is not made *worse off* by the claimant's loss in the § 1101 sense; it merely earns zero on its investment. The four-element functional test associated with *Griffin Systems, Inc. v. Washburn*—as synthesized in the GAO's catalog of insurance definitions, requiring among other things an "insurable interest possessed by the insured" and "the assumption of risk by the insurer who agrees to indemnify the insured"—fails at both prongs.[^150] Measured against the distilled definition, then, TPLF satisfies element (a)—transfer of a fortuitous risk—but cleanly fails (b) indemnity, (c) pooling, and (d) insurable interest. On the formal test, the funder is right: TPLF is not insurance.

#### II.D.4. Why the Loan Exit Is Also the Insurance Exit

The doctrinal mechanism that drops funding into the gap, the bridge promised in Part II.C, is now visible in full. *Ruth* and *Maslowski* reject the "loan" characterization because repayment is contingent and capped—there is no "absolute obligation of repayment."[^151] But that same contingency defeats "indemnity": an instrument with no unconditional duty to repay and no duty to indemnify a loss is neither a loan nor an insurance contract. It therefore falls between the two regulated categories, classified—where classified at all—as an "investment" or "assignment," as the leading scholarship and comparative literature have settled.[^152] This is why courts that labor over whether funding is a usurious loan never pivot to ask whether it is unlicensed insurance: the feature that answers the first question "no" answers the second "no" as well. The product lands in unregulated space not by oversight but by the structure of the categories themselves.

#### II.D.5. Is the Boundary Coherent?

Now turn the steel-man. The four escape features are real, but they exclude funding on *form*, not *function*—and the test's own functional core, satisfied by funding, is the element the doctrine itself calls primary.

Return to *Pireno* factor one, stated in the disjunctive: does the practice "transfer[] or spread[] a policyholder's risk"? TPLF unmistakably *transfers* the claimant's legal-claim risk: the claimant offloads the downside of losing—the sunk cost of litigating a claim that may fail—onto a counterparty that absorbs it in exchange for a contingent share of the upside. That is precisely the risk transfer *Royal Drug* identifies as "[t]he primary element[]" of an insurance contract.[^153] What funding does *not* do is *spread* that risk across a pool: the single-claim funder underwrites one claim, not a book of insureds, and so fails the spreading-and-pooling element that *Jordan* and *Royal Drug* supply. Funding thus satisfies the *transfer* half of factor one and fails the *spreading* half—and fails outright on factors two and three: the relationship is not styled "insurer-insured," and the funder is not an entity "within the insurance industry." But the element the doctrine calls primary, funding shares with insurance fully; factors two and three are formal artifacts—the *name of the relationship* and the *identity of the counterparty*—not differences in the risk transferred, and the pooling requirement merely distinguishes a single-claim investor from a book-writing insurer. That is the precise sense in which the boundary is incoherent: it sorts functional twins by formal and structural factors while the core functional factor—risk transfer, satisfied on both sides—does no sorting.

Three observations sharpen the incoherence without yet prescribing a cure. *First*, *Pireno* expressly cautions that "[n]one of these criteria is necessarily determinative in itself"[^154]—yet in operation the two formal factors do all the work of excluding funding, and the functional factor, which funding satisfies, counts for nothing. A test that disclaims any single determinative factor has been applied as though factors two and three were dispositive. *Second*, *Fabe* establishes that the term expands when policyholder protection is the regulatory purpose.[^155] A protective reading—the reading *Fabe* licenses where the object is to protect the party who has transferred risk—*could* reach the funded claimant, who stands in materially the same protective posture as the ATE or JPI policyholder. The doctrine does not compel the formal exclusion; it permits a functional inclusion it has simply not been asked to make. *Third*, and most fundamentally, Tom Baker has shown that non-recourse litigation funding is "a contemporary form of the respondentia and bottomry that insurance historians regard as the earliest forms of commercial insurance."[^156] The lineage matters: respondentia and bottomry were non-recourse advances repaid only on the safe arrival of the venture, blending finance and risk-bearing exactly as modern funding does—so the function this Article calls insurance-like is not a novel re-description but the oldest form the function has taken. This Article does not claim to have first seen that funding resembles insurance; Baker saw it. What it adds is the regulatory step Baker brackets: treating that resemblance, and the formal/functional gap the doctrine opens around it, as a regulatory problem rather than a historical curiosity.[^157]

A skeptic will object that the escape features track a real economic difference, not an arbitrary label—that an insurer indemnifies a loss the insured already faces, whereas a funder buys an upside it helps to create, so pooling, indemnity, and insurable interest are not "mere labels" but markers of genuinely different risk structures.[^158] The objection has force—it is the strongest single attack on the whole enterprise, and the full answer awaits Parts III and V—but three points belong here. The difference the skeptic identifies is one of *degree and direction*, not of *kind*: both the funder and the ATE insurer price and assume the *same* underlying variable—whether this claim will succeed and at what value—which is what *Pireno* factor one and *Royal Drug* make the primary element of insurance. Some escape features are themselves contestable: non-recourse funding *does* relieve the claimant of the "loss" of being unable to vindicate a meritorious claim for want of capital, and ATE—which indemnifies essentially the same downside—*is* insurance, making the funding/ATE line especially thin. Insurable interest, however, is not contestable in the same way and should not be waved off as a drafting artifact. It is a substantive anti-wagering doctrine with centuries of pedigree—the principle that historically distinguished legitimate insurance from a bet on a stranger's misfortune.[^159] The funder genuinely lacks it: it stands to *gain* from the claim's success rather than to be made whole for a loss it would otherwise suffer, and so is a profit-seeker, not a loss-bearer. This Article concedes that difference and locates the regulatory equivalence elsewhere. What makes funding insurance-like for *regulatory* purposes is not that the funder holds an insurable interest but that the claimant, having transferred her legal-claim risk, now depends on that counterparty's solvency exactly as a policyholder depends on an insurer's—the dependency the prudential regime exists to protect. Insurable interest answers the anti-wagering question; risk transfer plus counterparty dependence answers the claimant-protection question. Why the former should not control regulatory classification, when the protective stakes are identical on both sides, is the argument Parts III and IV develop.

One final fact completes the map, and it is a silence. Despite a multibillion-dollar commercial funding industry operating for two decades, no state insurance department bulletin and no attorney general opinion has squarely held that TPLF is—or is not—"doing an insurance business" under a statute like § 1101.[^160] The natural inference is not that the question is easy but that no regulator has been moved to ask it: the product has never been pressed against the insurance definition by the apparatus that would have to enforce the answer. That silence is not the absence of evidence; it is affirmative evidence of the arbitrage—an instrument that performs the insurance function, sits one contractual clause away from the insurance line, and has never been called to account before any insurance regulator in the country. The boundary that sorts the field has never been authoritatively drawn by anyone responsible for the consequences of drawing it.

The boundary is incoherent. This Part has mapped the three regimes, located the seam, and shown that the classification on which everything turns rests on form rather than function and has gone unexamined by the regulators who would enforce it. The normative payoff—regulate the function, not the label—is the work of Part IV. What remains is to show that this incoherence is not merely an aesthetic defect but a mechanism that moves capital and shifts risk onto those least able to bear it. To that mechanism, and the natural experiment that makes it visible, the Article now turns.

## III. Regulatory Arbitrage and Its Harms

Parts I and II established two facts that, standing alone, are merely curious. Part I showed that third-party litigation funding (TPLF), after-the-event (ATE) insurance, judgment-preservation insurance (JPI), and the contingency fee are functional substitutes: four instruments that transfer and price a single commodity, the contingent downside of a legal claim.[^161] Part II showed that the law nonetheless sorts these substitutes into radically divergent regulatory regimes — heavy prudential supervision for the instruments labeled "insurance," professional-responsibility rules for the contingency fee, and a near-vacuum for funding — and that the sorting turns on a classification act ("is it insurance?") that the doctrine itself cannot defend at its seam.[^162] This Part supplies the connecting tissue. It argues that the asymmetry is not an inert inconsistency but a live mechanism with predictable, observable, and costly consequences. Where economically equivalent products face unequal regulatory cost, capital migrates toward the cheapest regulatory form; and where the cost evaded was a form of protection — solvency supervision, control oversight, gatekeeping — the migration leaves the protected party exposed. The remainder of this Part names the mechanism (III.A), tests it against the natural experiment of the 2023–26 JPI-market contraction (III.B), develops the three harms the mechanism inflicts (III.C–III.E), and shows why the reform agenda's near-exclusive fixation on mandatory disclosure addresses none of them (III.F).

### III.A. The Arbitrage Mechanism: From Functional Equivalence to Capital Migration

The argument of this Part is a syllogism. The major premise is Victor Fleischer's theory of regulatory arbitrage, developed at length in Part I.D: arbitrage exploits "a gap between the economics of a deal and its regulatory treatment," restructuring a transaction's legal form to shed regulatory cost "without unduly altering the underlying economics."[^163] The two minor premises are the findings of Parts I and II — that the four instruments are functional substitutes, and that the law nonetheless sorts them into divergent regimes. The conclusion is migration. Legal-claim risk is a near-perfect arbitrage candidate on both of Fleischer's dimensions: the economics are identical across the four instruments while the regulatory treatment ranges from full prudential supervision to nothing, and the migration this Part documents is restructuring rather than innovation — when JPI retrenched, the same post-judgment reversal risk did not find a new economic home but a new legal label, reappearing as appellate-monetization funding (see infra Part III.B).[^164] The shadow-insurance precedent that supplies this Article's title — the regulator-documented migration of insurance risk to lighter-regulated captives, framed by Schwarcz and the New York DFS as "a form of regulatory arbitrage" — is developed in Part I.D and need not be restated here, except to mark the difference that makes the present case the more brazen: there the risk moved within the insurance perimeter, among affiliated entities; here it moves across regulatory perimeters, among unaffiliated instrument types, in the open.[^165]

What is genuinely new to this Part is the boundary-relevance narrowing that answers the mechanism's most serious limit — the objection, pressed by Matthew Turk, that functional convergence does not compel regulatory convergence, because formalistic, entity-based definitions retain advantages and functional regulation is not costless.[^166] Turk is right, and the concession is not grudging: a "function" can be gerrymandered to manufacture any equivalence, and the bare fact that two instruments both "price risk" cannot indict the line between them. The claim of this Part is therefore deliberately narrow. Arbitrage is harmful — and a regulatory line indefensible — only where the line fails to track a difference relevant to the regulation's purpose. The purpose of insurance solvency regulation is to protect the party who has transferred a risk to a counterparty and now depends on that counterparty's continued ability to perform.[^167] Measured against that purpose, the claimant who transfers litigation risk to a funder occupies materially the same protective posture as the claimant who buys ATE or JPI: each has parted with the downside of a claim in exchange for a counterparty's promise, and each is exposed if the counterparty fails. The two instruments do differ on an orthogonal dimension — the non-recourse funding claimant has shed her downside-on-loss exposure, which the JPI or ATE policyholder retains — but on the specific dimension solvency regulation exists to target, the counterparty-performance risk the claimant bears when she relies on the risk-bearer's promise, the funder's claimant and the insurer's claimant are similarly situated. That is the boundary claim this Part substantiates.

A clarification internal to the theory sharpens it. A formalist will insist that a funder is not an "insurer" because it does not indemnify a "loss" and pools no book of insureds — that TPLF fails the risk-transfer-plus-relationship test of the *Royal Drug*–*Pireno* line that state law codifies in provisions like New York Insurance Law § 1101 (see supra Part II.D).[^168] That is correct, and this Article does not contest it; the doctrinal non-identity is the engine of the arbitrage, not a refutation of it. The classification gap Part II located — the same contingency that pulls funding out of "loan" regulation simultaneously pulls it out of "insurance" regulation — is precisely Fleischer's "gap between the economics of a deal and its regulatory treatment."[^169] To say "but it is not insurance" restates the arbitrage in the formalist's preferred vocabulary; it does not dissolve it. The label is not the economics — the recurring error this Part attributes, in turn, to definitional formalists (here) and to disclosure proponents (in Part III.F).

The stakes of this particular arbitrage are distinctive. In the classic cases — tax shelters, regulatory-capital structuring — the cost shifted by arbitrage is borne by a sophisticated counterparty or the public fisc; here it is shifted onto an often-unsophisticated claimant and onto the courts, which inherit a destabilized or captured case.[^170] And the mechanism yields a falsifiable prediction: if these instruments are substitutes facing unequal regulation, then a shock raising the cost of transferring legal-claim risk through insurance should push the same risk into the cheaper form, holding the underlying risk constant. The 2023–26 JPI contraction is exactly such a shock, and the next Section treats it as the experiment that tests the mechanism. Fleischer's three named harms — the distortion of regulatory competition, the shifting of cost incidence, and the erosion of transparency and accountability that "undermines the rule of law" (see supra Part I.D) — are not topics this Article has assembled but the predictable signature of the mechanism, and they map onto the three harms developed below: the shifting of cost incidence onto the claimant who inherits uninsured insolvency risk (III.C); the distortion of competition as the funding form escapes the control-supervision burdens that bind insurers and lawyers (III.D); and the erosion of transparency as legal-claim risk is pooled and channeled through a form that escapes the gatekeeping and aggregate visibility the insurance and securities perimeters impose (III.E).[^171]

### III.B. The JPI Collapse as Natural Experiment: The Balloon Squeeze

The mechanism predicts that a shock to the insurance form of legal-claim-risk transfer, holding the underlying risk constant, should relocate that risk to the cheaper form. The 2023–26 contraction of the judgment-preservation-insurance market provides an unusually clean opportunity to observe whether it does. The argument proceeds in four beats: the two products price the same risk; an exogenous shock hit only the insurance form; the regulated supply contracted; and the risk relocated. The image that captures the dynamic is a balloon: squeeze the insurance-regulated end and the air — the legal-claim risk — does not escape but bulges into the unregulated end.

*The same risk, priced two ways.* JPI insures the holder of a litigation award against the risk that the award is reversed or reduced on appeal; the insured pays a premium quoted as a percentage of the insured limit and recovers a guaranteed floor if the judgment falls.[^172] The cleanest primary exhibit is Appian Corporation's 2023 Form 8-K, detailed in the Introduction: a $500 million-limit policy on Appian's roughly $2.036 billion Virginia judgment against Pegasystems, placed at a 9.8% premium with an oversubscribed syndicate, paying the shortfall if the final enforceable judgment falls below the limit.[^173] Strip away the insurance label and the structure is indistinguishable from a funder's contingent position: a counterparty accepts a fixed payment today in exchange for assuming the downside of a specified, already-adjudicated litigation outcome. The Appian policy is indemnity against a priced contingency; a funder's monetization of the same verdict would be the purchase of a contingent asset at a discount reflecting the same reversal probability. The risk transferred is identical; only the legal form — and therefore the regulatory regime — differs.

*An exogenous shock to the insurance form only.* On April 30, 2024, the Fifth Circuit in *BMC Software, Inc. v. International Business Machines Corp.* reversed a roughly $1.6 billion judgment in BMC's favor to zero, holding that Section 5.4 of the parties' contract permitted IBM's conduct and that "the district court's determination concerning liability was in error."[^174] The reversed judgment had been JPI-insured; legal-press reporting describes a Liberty Mutual–led tower of some thirty to forty carriers, with coverage reported at roughly $500 million to $750 million and Liberty Mutual's share at approximately $100 million to $150 million.[^175] Those tower figures are press and industry estimates — insurers do not publicly disclose line-by-line JPI losses — and are presented here as such; the verified core is the reversal and its amount.[^176] A second nine-figure loss followed when, on July 30, 2024, the Court of Appeals of Virginia reversed the $2.036 billion Appian verdict and remanded, putting the $500 million Appian policy in the money.[^177] A third came in October 2024, when a ruling cut Quinn Emanuel's fee award from $185 million to $92.4 million; because the relevant JPI policy guaranteed roughly 90% of the original fee, underwriters were reportedly left exposed to approximately $75 million.[^178] The methodological significance of this cluster is that the shock was exogenous to the litigation-finance market. Each reversal turned on case-specific merits — a contract-interpretation question in *BMC*, a damages-causation and burden-of-proof error in *Pegasystems* — and none was a response to conditions in the funding market.[^179] That exogeneity is what makes the episode a usable natural experiment: the migration that follows cannot be explained as both markets reacting to a common demand shock, because the trigger originated in appellate merits rulings independent of either market's economics.

*The regulated supply contracts.* The carriers responded as a prudentially exposed industry would. Bloomberg Law reported that big litigation losses "slammed" the award-insurance market: premiums had "as much as doubled" (with policies quoted around 17–20% of the insured award, up from the high single digits the Appian filing reflected), insurers cut the insured percentage of a judgment, and carriers shifted from single-judgment to multi-case policies; Liberty Mutual "backed out of at least two litigation-insurance deals" after the *BMC* reversal.[^180] Industry commentary describes the regulated product as, for many cases, "no longer appear[ing] available at all."[^181] The supply of the regulated form, in short, sharply contracted and repriced upward.

*The risk relocates — with a confound that must be confronted head-on.* The reversal risk did not disappear with the insurance that had covered it; verdict-holders still needed to de-risk or monetize their judgments. Industry trend-trackers describe the result as a "reemergence of appellate monetization funding" — claimants and judgment-holders turning to near-unregulated TPLF and claim-monetization to protect or cash out verdicts as JPI retrenched.[^182] That this is a funder-authored characterization counsels caution. The neutral data series counsels still more caution, and in a direction that must be stated plainly rather than smoothed over. Westfleet Advisors, whose annual report covers the U.S. commercial market, found that claim monetization rose to 26% of new commitments in 2024, up from 21% in 2023, 14% in 2022, and 8% in 2021.[^183] That series does not show a shock-driven jump in 2024. It shows a steep secular climb that long predates the April 2024 *BMC* reversal: the year-over-year increments are +6 points (2021 to 2022) and +7 points (2022 to 2023), the steepest single-year acceleration arriving a full year before the shock, with the 2024 increment (+5 points) the smallest of the three. Monetization was already migrating into the funding form, briskly, for reasons unrelated to any JPI loss — and that pre-existing trend is precisely the confound that forbids attributing the 2024 datum to the squeeze. If anything, the simple extrapolation cuts the other way: a continuation of the prior gradient would have predicted roughly 27% to 28% in 2024, so the year of the shock coincided with a mild *deceleration*, plausibly because total new commitments fell about 16% that year.[^184] The honest characterization of the 2024 monetization figure is therefore not "caused by" the JPI squeeze but "consistent with, and a continuation of," a migration already underway — corroborating the direction of the balloon-squeeze hypothesis without supplying its proof.

The cleaner 2024-specific signal lies elsewhere in the same report, in a datum with no multi-year trend behind it to confound. For the first time, Westfleet measured the funding-insurance convergence directly: roughly 19% of 2024 commercial commitments were fully or partially covered by contingent-risk (litigation) insurance — a figure with no prior-year series, newly salient in the year the JPI market repriced.[^185] That nearly one in five commercial funding deals now carries an insurance wrapper is the sharper evidence that the two forms are interpenetrating as the regulated form retrenches. The air, squeezed out of the insurance end of the balloon, bulges toward the funding end.

Two qualifications discipline the inference. First, the contraction must not be overstated. A Law360 report in February 2025 found that the judgment-insurance market "continues to solidify" after the 2024 losses, with practitioners flagging open questions about mandatory disclosure of JPI policies and whether large carriers would enter.[^186] The thesis does not require that JPI vanished. It requires that legal-claim risk migrated along the regulatory-cost gradient — and a regulated product that doubles in price and cuts coverage percentages while an unregulated substitute absorbs the residual demand is the arbitrage operating in the predicted direction. Repricing upward in the regulated form is the push; the availability of monetization in the unregulated form is the pull. The structural claim survives whether JPI collapsed or merely re-priced.[^187] Second, and as the monetization series already establishes, the natural experiment is a documented market episode paired with a testable hypothesis, not a causal estimate identified from observational data; the corroborating commentary is in part funder-sourced and interested.[^188] The rigorous next step would be to measure the cross-instrument migration directly — netting out the secular trend to isolate any shock-specific increment — as Koijen and Yogo measured the captive-reinsurance channel in the shadow-insurance market.[^189] That work is possible there because reporting requirements generate the data, and impossible here because they do not; the asymmetry of measurability is not a defect in the argument but, as Section III.E shows, one of the harms.

### III.C. First Harm: Uninsured Counterparty and Solvency Risk Shifted to Claimants and Courts

When legal-claim risk migrates from the insurance form to the funding form, it sheds the entire apparatus of solvency regulation that exists to protect the party relying on the risk-bearer's promise. This is Fleischer's "shifting the incidence of regulatory costs," made concrete: the cost of solvency supervision — the price the insurance form pays for being trustworthy — is avoided by the funding form, and the avoided protection is shifted onto the claimant and, derivatively, onto the court managing the case. The cleanest way to see the harm is to walk the architecture the funding form escapes.

Insurance solvency regulation is a three-stage structure. The first stage is preventive: licensing, investment limits, mandatory reserves, and risk-based capital scaled to the insurer's size and risk profile.[^190] The second stage is corrective: continuous financial reporting, own-risk-and-solvency assessment, regulatory examinations, and the risk-based-capital ladder, whose action levels escalate as capital deteriorates — a company-action level at 200% of authorized control level, regulatory action at 150%, authorized control at 100%, and mandatory regulatory takeover at 70%.[^191] The third stage is a backstop: when rehabilitation fails, the home-state commissioner acts as receiver, and every U.S. jurisdiction maintains a guaranty association that continues covered claims when an insurer becomes insolvent.[^192] A claimant who buys ATE or JPI sits inside all three stages. The carrier was licensed and capitalized before it wrote the policy; its solvency is monitored and corrected as it writes; and if it fails, a guaranty association stands behind the covered obligation.

A litigation funder is subject to none of this. There is no minimum capital scaled to its book of contingent claims, no reserve requirement, no regulator-run solvency examination, and — most starkly — no guaranty-fund backstop.[^193] The point is not that an insolvent funder faces no insolvency process at all: general bankruptcy law supplies a wind-down and a creditor-priority scheme like that available against any failed firm.[^194] The point is that the funding form lacks the claimant-protective overlay that defines insurance insolvency — the prudential floor that makes failure rare, the special receivership that prioritizes the policyholder, and the guaranty fund that pays covered claims when failure comes. If a funder becomes insolvent or simply exits mid-case, the claimant holds an unsecured contract claim and the litigation may be stranded for want of the committed capital. The contrast is near-total: the insurance-form claimant is protected at every stage; the funding-form claimant at none calibrated to the risk transfer. The guaranty fund is the asymmetry's sharpest expression — every state, the District of Columbia, and the territories maintain a mechanism to pay covered claims on insurer insolvency, coordinated across multistate failures by NOLHGA, and no claimant-protective analogue exists for funder insolvency.[^195] A claimant cannot privately reconstruct that backstop, and even one who negotiates collateral cannot manufacture the regulator-run receivership that would prioritize its claim in a funder's wind-down.

The fragility is not hypothetical. Therium Capital Management — a 2009 pioneer of the industry, which funded the United Kingdom Post Office/Horizon litigation — exited active funding into run-off, laid off staff across its global offices, registered a successor advisory affiliate in 2025, and in June 2025 transferred day-to-day oversight of a large part of its litigation-finance portfolio to Fortress Investment Group.[^196] Press attributes the stress to large losses, reportedly a roughly £24 million near-breakeven investment in the PACCAR truck-cartel litigation and an expected total loss of about £20 million in the Sabah arbitration; those figures are journalistic estimates from a funder whose audited accounts were reportedly filed late, and are flagged accordingly.[^197] But the wind-down, the run-off, and the Fortress transfer are well documented, and they convert the abstract proposition that "funders could fail" into the demonstrated fact that they do. The recursive point compounds the harm: the migration documented in Section III.B pushes more legal-claim risk onto exactly this uncapitalized, visibly fragile model.

The asymmetry is sharpened by the doctrinal hinge Part II.D located: the contingency that defeats "loan" classification — the non-recourse, repayment-contingent structure that *Ruth* and *Maslowski* held lacks any "absolute obligation of repayment" — simultaneously defeats "indemnity," because it negates the loss-indemnification and pooling that define insurance, and no court has reached for insurance classification instead (see supra Part II.D).[^198][^199][^200] The consequence is a double exclusion. The claimant who transfers litigation risk to a funder receives neither the borrower protections that attach to a loan — usury caps, disclosure — nor the policyholder protections that attach to insurance — solvency supervision, guaranty-fund backstop. The classification gap Part II diagnosed is, at the level of claimant protection, a protection vacuum — and not one a single reclassification could fill, because the instrument falls between two regimes rather than outside one. It is structural.

Two objections, each conceding more than it contests, frame the calibrated response Part IV will build. The first is that commercial funders' counterparties are sophisticated repeat players who can price counterparty risk and demand collateral, so that solvency regulation is paternalism they neither need nor want. That is an argument for tiering, not for the vacuum, and this Article accepts its premise where it holds — Part IV calibrates protective intensity to claimant vulnerability — but the premise does not hold across the market: Westfleet's methodology excludes the consumer and pre-settlement segments, the larger and more vulnerable slices, and the migrating JPI-substitute demand includes individual judgment-holders, not only corporations.[^201] And even a sophisticated claimant cannot privately reconstruct the public backstop. The second objection is that non-recourse funding reduces the claimant's risk — she owes nothing if she loses — so where is the harm? The relevant exposure is not downside-on-loss but counterparty risk on the funder's performance: insolvency mid-case, failure to advance committed tranches, or the collapse of a monetization counterparty can strand a meritorious case. Non-recourse protects the claimant against losing; it does not protect her against the funder failing. That is the risk the insurance form's solvency apparatus is built to absorb, and the risk the funding form leaves wholly unaddressed — the architecture the insurer-insolvency wave of the 1980s produced precisely because uncapitalized risk-bearing harms those who rely on it.[^202]

### III.D. Second Harm: Agency-Cost Externalization and the Capture of Litigation Control

The second harm is that the funding form externalizes an agency cost that both the insurance regime and the legal-ethics regime are designed to constrain — the conflict that arises when a non-party with money at stake seeks to control the litigation it is financing. The economic substance is constant across forms: a risk-bearer with capital exposed to a litigation outcome wants a say in the settlement decision that determines its return. What varies, and varies wildly, is the regulatory treatment of that control. Insurer control is supervised; lawyer-mediated control is ethically constrained; funder control is unconstrained. This is Fleischer's "distortion of regulatory competition": the funding form competes by escaping the supervision its functional substitutes bear.

Begin with the two regimes that constrain control. When a liability insurer controls a defense, it does so under a duty to settle and the threat of bad-faith liability, backstopped by insurance-department oversight; the insurer's control comes bundled with enforceable obligations running to the insured.[^203] When a lawyer is paid by a third party, the professional-responsibility rules police the lawyer's independence: Model Rule 5.4(c) bars a person who "recommends, employs, or pays the lawyer" from directing the lawyer's professional judgment, and Rule 1.8(f) permits third-party payment only on conditions of client consent, non-interference with the lawyer's independent judgment, and confidentiality.[^204] The ABA's Formal Opinion 484 polices the funder-lawyer line for fee-financing, and its Best Practices resolution insists that "the client remains in control of the case."[^205] Both regimes thus already regulate the precise agency conflict that funder control presents — but they regulate it only when control is exercised by an insurer or mediated through a lawyer. A funder that contractually captures settlement control answers to neither. Its control is not bundled with a duty to settle, is not exposed to bad-faith liability, and — because the funder is neither the lawyer nor someone "who . . . pays the lawyer" in the client-directed structures Part II described — slips the ethics rules as well.[^206]

The *Sysco*/*Burford*/*Carina* saga is the centerpiece exhibit, and its load-bearing facts rest on the record rather than on characterization. Burford Capital invested more than $140 million for a share of the proceeds of Sysco's antitrust claims across five matters (Broiler Chicken, Pork, Beef, Turkey, and Keurig).[^207] When Sysco moved to settle, the constraint appeared in a finding read directly from the record: a magistrate judge found that "[t]he litigation finance contract between Sysco and Burford was amended to give Burford the authority to approve or disapprove of settlements."[^208] When Sysco nonetheless sought to settle its Pilgrim's Pride claims for roughly $50 million, Burford-affiliated entities obtained a preliminary injunction from a London Court of International Arbitration tribunal that, per secondary reporting of the confidential award, enjoined Sysco from executing the settlement on the theory that the amended funding agreement gave Burford veto rights over settlements.[^209] Sysco then assigned its claims outright to a Burford affiliate, Carina Ventures LLC.[^210] The funder did not passively provide capital; it captured the settlement decision. The reported routing of that decision into a confidential private forum is the rule-of-law dimension of the harm in its sharpest form: a claimant reportedly enjoined by a secret arbitration from settling its own claims in public court.

The doctrinal response to the capture has been incoherent, and the incoherence is itself diagnostic. Presented with materially identical Sysco-to-Carina assignments in parallel antitrust MDLs, two federal courts reached opposite results on Rule 25(c) substitution. In the Pork antitrust litigation, Chief Judge Tunheim denied substitution, reasoning that the assignment would "allow a financer with no interest in the litigation beyond maximizing profit on its investment to override decisions made by the party that actually brought suit."[^211] In the Broiler Chicken litigation, Judge Durkin granted it, characterizing the arrangement as unremarkable because "[l]itigation funding agreements are a fact of contemporary complex litigation."[^212] Part of the divergence is the case-specific discretion Rule 25(c) confers and the different records before each court; but that two able judges, applying the same rule to structurally parallel facts, split on whether a funder may step into the plaintiff's shoes is the signature of a field with no functional regulatory framework to channel the discretion. The Seventh Circuit supplied a vivid coda. Reversing the enforcement of the disputed settlement on the mundane ground that no settlement had ever been formed, Judge Maldonado, concurring in the judgment, characterized what the underlying record revealed: the case "exemplifies the foremost danger of litigation finance: funders aggressively interfering with, and exerting control over, ongoing litigation, while rejecting the funded party's preferences with the hope of maximizing returns," and "Burford wrested total control over the settlement of Sysco's claims."[^213] That is one judge's observation in a separate writing, not a holding; the structural weight of the harm rests on the court-found facts — the record finding that the contract was amended to give Burford settlement-veto authority, and the Tunheim/Durkin split — with the concurrence serving as the bench's own naming of what those facts permit.

If Sysco shows the harm at the sophisticated end of the spectrum — a large, counseled corporation that still lost control of its own claims — the Delaware patent cases show it at the claimant-vulnerable extreme. In the *Nimitz*/Mavexar matter, Chief Judge Connolly, after evidentiary hearings, found that the nominal patent owners did not control the litigations filed in their names; an undisclosed non-party, Mavexar (tied to IP Edge), controlled them, while the nominal owners had obtained the asserted patents for no money and would receive only a small fraction of any recovery — per the court's findings as reported, roughly 10% for the nominal owner in *Nimitz* and roughly 5% for a food-truck-owner patentee in a companion case.[^214] Opaque funding, in that posture, hollows out the claimant's role entirely: the nominal plaintiff is a shell, the real party in interest undisclosed and unsupervised.[^215] Sysco and Mavexar bracket the vulnerability axis Part IV's calibration will track, but they share one structural feature: the control conflict is neither absent nor new; it is simply unregulated when it travels through a funding contract.

Two objections refine rather than rebut. Anthony Sebok has argued that insurance law has always permitted the alienation of litigation control — through subrogation and liability-insurer defense control — so that funder control is nothing novel, and that the law's stricter scrutiny of funding reflects an anachronistic "double standard."[^216] The point is correct and embraced, not resisted: it proves the functional equivalence at the heart of this Article, and the relevant question is therefore not whether control may be transferred but whether its transfer is supervised — bundled, for the insurer, with a duty to settle, bad-faith exposure, and regulatory oversight, and for the funder, with none.[^217] The remedy sought is parity of supervision, not prohibition. Elizabeth Chamblee Burch has argued, complementarily, that funders can serve a beneficial monitoring function in aggregate and MDL litigation that lacks Rule 23's safeguards.[^218] That too is real, and it is an argument for calibration, not deregulation: a monitoring funder and a controlling funder present different risk profiles, which is why Part IV calibrates by control and vulnerability rather than banning funding outright. A final objection holds that Sysco and Mavexar are outliers and that most funding is genuinely passive — but that claim is unfalsifiable from outside the contract, because funding agreements are confidential, their control terms unobservable, and courts have held funding a non-discoverable "side issue" absent a showing that "something untoward occurred."[^219] The harm is not that all funding captures control; it is that nothing regulates the funding that does, and the opacity that makes the passive-majority claim unprovable is itself a product of the vacuum.

### III.E. Third Harm: Systemic Aggregation and the Foreign-Capital National-Security Channel as Arbitrage Symptoms

The third harm is the one most easily mistaken for two separate topics — a systemic-risk worry and a national-security worry, each with its own constituency and its own overstatements. The analytic move of this Section is to deny the separation. Both are symptoms of the same arbitrage: both are what Fleischer's third harm, the "lack of transparency and accountability that undermines the rule of law," looks like when legal-claim risk is pooled, traded, and channeled through a form that escapes the gatekeeping and aggregate visibility the insurance and securities perimeters would impose.[^220] One vacuum produces both symptoms.

*The aggregation strand.* Legal-claim risk is now pooled and, in substance, securitized outside any prudential perimeter, and the institutional plumbing of a shadow market is already in place. A functioning secondary market exists: in April 2025, Omni Bridgeway and funds managed by Ares Management established a continuation fund in which Ares acquired a 70% interest in a diversified global portfolio of more than 150 legal assets for approximately A$320 million.[^221] Portfolio concentration is real: Burford's group-wide portfolio stood at roughly $7.48 billion as of September 2024, a single funder's book that rivals a meaningful share of the entire U.S. commercial market, which Westfleet sized at $16.1 billion in assets under management across forty-two active funders and which Swiss Re estimated at roughly $17 billion globally as of 2020.[^222] The shadow-banking literature must be borrowed with care, because two of its danger mechanisms do not carry over and one does. Gorton and Metrick describe shadow banking as a large sector that grew outside the perimeter through legal gaps and proved fragile; Morgan Ricks locates the precise danger in maturity transformation — continuously rolled-over *short-term* debt funding long-term assets, the source of run-and-rollover risk.[^223] That mechanism is largely absent here, and the analogy should not smuggle it in: litigation-finance capital is equity-like, multi-year, and locked up (Ares acquired a continuation-fund interest, not a demandable claim, and limited-partner commitments cannot "run"), so there is no maturity transformation and no classic run channel — only the milder residual concern of secondary-market refinancing.[^224] Correlation, too, must be specified rather than assumed: litigation outcomes across unrelated antitrust, patent, and contract matters are plausibly *less* correlated than the mortgage tranches whose comovement made shadow banking dangerous, and the genuine correlation channels are narrower — funder concentration (a single book can fail as a unit) and common exogenous appellate-doctrine shocks of the kind the 2024 JPI cluster illustrates. What the market does unambiguously share with shadow banking is the perimeter-escape itself: institutional capital (Ares, Fortress) holds and trades baskets of litigation risk with no capital floor, no resolution regime, and — the load-bearing point — no aggregate data by which anyone could measure the exposure at all.

Honesty about the systemic claim is essential, and the claim is deliberately structural rather than magnitude-based. At roughly $16 to $17 billion, the market is trivial beside shadow banking's trillions, and — having neither that scale nor the maturity-transformation run-risk just discussed — this Article does not predict an imminent 2008-style crisis; to suggest otherwise would be alarmist and false.[^225] The claim is narrower and harder to dismiss: the market is being built without a capital floor and with concentrated, perimeter-escaping exposures whose aggregate magnitude no one can even measure. The Government Accountability Office, in successive reports, found that there is no federal law specifically regulating the industry, that major gaps exist in public market data, and that "public data sources do not fully capture TPLF."[^226] The data gap is the indictment. A sector whose aggregate exposure cannot be measured cannot be monitored, and the inability to measure is not an accident of immaturity but the direct product of the form's escape from the reporting that the insurance and securities perimeters impose.

*The foreign-capital strand.* The same perimeter-escape opens a channel for foreign and sanctioned capital into U.S. courts that the insurance and securities regimes would gatekeep. The flagship documented exhibit is Bloomberg Law's investigation of A1, a subsidiary of Russia's Alfa Group, which backed bankruptcy litigation in New York and London — roughly $20 million invested across cases — before and after its billionaire founders were sanctioned following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine.[^227] Six days after the founders were sanctioned in the United Kingdom, A1 was sold for about $900 to a non-sanctioned director, an arrangement a UK court characterized as "to some degree a scheme of concealed sanctions evasion"; and in March 2022, attorneys attempted to wire $6.1 million to a sanctioned account at Russia's Central Bank, blocked only after an escrow agent flagged it.[^228] The investigation's structural observation is the one that matters here: funders can pour millions into U.S. litigation "without ever appearing on a court docket" because litigation finance has "no reporting requirements and few regulations."[^229] The perimeter cannot see the capital, and what it cannot see it cannot gatekeep.

That regulators are improvising across other regimes to reach foreign funding confirms, rather than cures, the absence of a perimeter of its own. The Department of Justice's FARA Unit issued an advisory opinion in 2024 concluding that a U.S. law firm pursuing impact litigation funded by a private foreign charity must register as a foreign agent — reaching foreign-funded litigation through a foreign-agent statute because no funding-specific regime exists.[^230] The GAO's December 2024 patent report found that half the technology companies interviewed considered foreign funding of U.S. patent litigation significant, that DOJ was examining whether foreign entities use funding to acquire proprietary information through discovery, and again that public data do not fully capture the practice.[^231] And the Protecting Our Courts from Foreign Manipulation Act — reported out of the House Judiciary Committee on a 15–11 vote in November 2025 — would bar a foreign state or sovereign wealth fund from funding a civil suit in which it is not a named party, reaching for insurance-style gatekeeping, but only within a narrow foreign-adversary frame and only through mandatory disclosure.[^232] The pattern is the tell: regulators reach across FARA, the tax code, and ad hoc disclosure to patch a hole that exists because funding has no perimeter, and they reach only when a particular frame — here, a foreign-adversary frame — makes the patch politically salient. The selective, frame-dependent patching is the arbitrage symptom.

One caution must be foregrounded rather than buried. The foreign-capital concern has been advanced most vigorously by the U.S. Chamber's Institute for Legal Reform — an interested tort-reform advocate whose institutional project is to constrain plaintiff-side litigation — and its national-security report was authored by defense-side firm partners and released at an ILR summit; its framing should be treated as advocacy, not neutral analysis, and more measured non-industry observers, including the Center for Strategic and International Studies, have questioned whether the risk is as acute as the advocacy suggests.[^233] This Article does not adopt that framing, and its claim does not depend on it: not that foreign funding is presumptively malign — most of it is ordinary institutional capital — but that the perimeter cannot see or gatekeep it, which is true regardless of how much is benign. The documented neutral spine just laid out — the A1 case, the GAO data gap and DOJ examination, the FARA opinion — carries the weight. The same opacity that defeats systemic measurement defeats foreign-capital detection: one vacuum, two symptoms, and neither is touched by the remedy on which the entire reform conversation has converged.

### III.F. Why Mandatory Disclosure Solves the Wrong Problem

The reform conversation about litigation funding has, almost without exception, been a conversation about disclosure. The Advisory Committee's TPLF Subcommittee is studying a disclosure rule; the leading practitioner proposal, from Lawyers for Civil Justice and the Institute for Legal Reform, would amend Rule 26(a)(1)(A) to require disclosure of non-recourse funders and their agreements; the Litigation Transparency Act of 2025 would require disclosure of any party with a contingent right to payment; a wave of state statutes and judicial standing orders requires the existence and identity of funders to be disclosed.[^234] The shared premise of all of it is that the funding problem is, at bottom, an information problem — that the trouble with funding is that courts and adversaries cannot see it, and that the cure is to make them able to see it. The argument of this Section is that the premise is mismatched to the harms. The three harms of Sections III.C through III.E are not information deficits; they are unsupervised-risk-transfer deficits. Disclosure tells the court and the adversary that a funder exists. It does nothing about the funder's lack of capital, its captured control rights, or the systemic and foreign exposure that no one can measure.

The category error is precise. Disclosure is the right tool when the harm is hidden information that a court or party could act on if it were revealed — an undisclosed conflict, a concealed real party in interest, discovery being used as intelligence. It is the wrong tool when the harm is substantive: an uncapitalized counterparty, an unconstrained control right, an unmonitored aggregation. Run each harm through the question "does disclosure fix this?" and the answer comes back no. Against the first harm (III.C): revealing the funding agreement does not capitalize the funder, does not impose a reserve requirement, and does not create a guaranty fund; a disclosed but insolvent funder strands the case exactly as a secret one would. Against the second harm (III.D): disclosure of a control term does not constrain it; knowing that Burford holds a settlement veto does not dissolve the veto, impose a duty to settle, or supply the bad-faith liability that would discipline an insurer exercising the same control.[^235] Against the third harm (III.E): single-case disclosure does not let anyone measure aggregate exposure or gatekeep foreign capital across the market; the GAO's data gap is a gap in aggregate, system-level data that case-by-case disclosure does not close.[^236] On every harm, disclosure reveals the transfer without supervising it.

The most striking demonstration that the field already half-understands this is that the leading disclosure proposal reproduces the arbitrage inside the cure. The LCJ/ILR Rule 26 suggestion expressly invokes the 1970 adoption of the insurance-disclosure provision, Rule 26(a)(1)(A)(iv), as its precedent and analogy — grounding funding disclosure in the recognition that a party's risk-transfer arrangements are properly disclosable.[^237] Having drawn the analogy to insurance, the proposal then carves insurance out: by its terms the rule "does not apply to funding in the nature of contingency-fee arrangements, insurance, or ordinary personal and bank loans."[^238] The proposal thus treats functional substitutes differently at the very threshold of the reform, re-enacting the discrimination this Article diagnoses — this time inside the remedy. It recognizes the functional equivalence (that is why it reaches for the insurance precedent) and then deliberately ignores it (that is the carve-out). The same single move — invoke the insurance-disclosure precedent, then exclude insurance and contingency — recurs across the reform landscape, and it is the most economical proof available that the field perceives the substitution and declines to act on it.[^239]

The rulemakers' own deliberations register the mismatch as an unanswered question. The TPLF Subcommittee has posed nine questions to stakeholders, and among them is the one that reveals the difficulty: "What is the court to do with the information if the [funding is] disclosed?"[^240] That question has no good answer, because disclosure was never matched to a harm the court is positioned to remedy with the disclosed information. A court told that an undercapitalized funder backs the case cannot capitalize it; a court told that a funder holds a settlement veto has, under current law, no calibrated tool to constrain it; a court told the identity of a foreign funder cannot measure aggregate foreign exposure. The Subcommittee's question is not a drafting wrinkle to be resolved; it is the internal admission that disclosure is a tool in search of a harm.

None of this is a brief against disclosure. The critique is not that disclosure is naive or that its proponents have misjudged their own tool; it is that the tool, correctly aimed at the harms it fits, has been asked to remedy harms of a different kind. Maya Steinitz's "Follow the Money?" and Victoria Sahani's work are serious scholarship advancing disclosure for exactly the harms disclosure fits: detecting conflicts, identifying the real party in interest, enabling judicial case management, and supporting access-to-justice transparency.[^241] On that information-shaped slice of the problem, disclosure does genuine and indispensable work — and this Article's own arguments depend on it. It was disclosure, compelled through Chief Judge Connolly's standing orders, that exposed the Mavexar shell-plaintiff structure; without the power to compel that information, the hollowing-out of the nominal claimant's role would have remained invisible.[^242] It is disclosure, or its absence, that determines whether foreign capital "ever appear[s] on a court docket."[^243] Sahani's category-based, harmonizing proposal is in important respects congenial to this Article's own functional approach, and is a near ally of the critique rather than a target.[^244] The claim is that disclosure is necessary but radically insufficient against the substantive harms — and dangerous when mistaken for a complete regulatory response. Disclosure that feeds a substantive regime is welcome and, indeed, presupposed by Part IV's design; disclosure that substitutes for one entrenches the arbitrage by giving the appearance of regulation where there is none. The quarrel is with disclosure-as-endpoint, the implicit framing of the entire current debate.

A final empirical observation marks the transition to the prescription. The most rigorous study of consumer-funding regulation to date — the "Whac-A-Mole" analysis by Avraham, Sebok, and Shepherd, drawing on more than 100,000 consumer funding agreements — finds that entity-based bans and caps tend to fail because funding behavior migrates to evade them.[^245] The study is a forthcoming working paper, cited as such.[^246] Its lesson is structural and bears directly here: if even bans and caps — interventions far more muscular than disclosure — are defeated when behavior migrates around an entity-based line, then disclosure, the thinnest intervention of all, is the least likely to alter the substance; it shifts the locus of opacity rather than closing it. And the lesson cuts toward function, not away from regulation: the whac-a-mole dynamic indicts entity-based rules that attach to "funders" who reorganize to escape them, not function-based rules that attach to the risk transfer itself regardless of the entity's label.[^247] That is the design Part IV develops.

The field has spent its regulatory energy on a single question — who is funding this case? — and has built its entire reform apparatus to answer it. The question the apparatus leaves unanswered is the one the three harms pose: who bears the risk if the funder fails, captures the case, or channels foreign capital — and is anyone supervising that transfer? Disclosure answers the first question and is silent on the second. The next Part takes the second question seriously.

## IV. Functional, Risk-Based Regulation

Parts I through III leave the field with a diagnosis but no remedy. Part I showed that third-party litigation funding, after-the-event (ATE) insurance, judgment-preservation insurance (JPI), and the contingency fee are functional substitutes—each pricing one underlying commodity, legal-claim risk—yet legal non-equivalents the doctrine sorts into separate boxes.[^248] Part II located the seam: the "is it insurance?" classification act, on which a product written as indemnity is pulled into the solvency-and-guaranty machinery of insurance regulation while a contingent advance escapes it—the contingency that defeats "loan" defeating "indemnity" as well.[^249] Part III converted that incoherence into a mechanism—capital migrates to the least-regulated equivalent form—using the 2023–26 JPI contraction after the *BMC* reversal as the natural experiment in which appellate-reversal risk visibly moved out of the regulated insurance form and into near-unregulated appellate-monetization funding.[^250] Its climax was a counter-conventional claim: mandatory disclosure—the thrust of the current reform agenda—solves the wrong problem, addressing what the court does not know rather than the capital-adequacy and counterparty harms that actually bite the claimant.[^251]

This Part supplies the structural answer. If the four instruments are one risk market fractured by labels, the corrective must attach to the shared function; if the harms are tiered by claimant vulnerability, the regulation must be too; and if the arbitrage runs through the indemnity/pooling line, the framework must police that line directly rather than leaving it to how a transaction is captioned. The output is a tiered functional framework—a common perimeter, two vulnerability tiers, and a cross-cutting "functional insurance" trigger—routed across four existing institutional competencies and made compatible with McCarran-Ferguson by design. It is not utopian; it is assembled almost entirely from levers that regulators, courts, and legislatures already hold, recombined around function.

### IV.A. From the "Is It Insurance?" Binary to a Functional Risk-Transfer Perimeter

The threshold reform move is to stop asking the binary question. "Is it insurance, a loan, or a fee?" assigns a transaction, in full, to one regulatory universe and exempts it from the others—and it is precisely the sorting question the arbitrage exploits, because two economically identical risk transfers receive opposite treatment on the strength of how the obligation is drafted.[^252] In its place the framework asks a graded functional question: how much legal-claim risk does this instrument transfer and price, to whom, and how vulnerable is the party shedding it? Regulatory *intensity* then becomes a function of risk severity times claimant vulnerability, and regulatory *modality* a function of where the instrument sits on a single doctrinal axis—the degree to which it indemnifies a loss or pools risk across a book rather than merely purchasing a contingent upside.

That a regulatory perimeter should track function rather than entity is the settled lesson of financial regulation. Merton and Bodie's functional perspective begins from the premise that financial *functions* are more stable than the *institutions* that perform them—institutional structure follows function, and competition drives an innovation spiral in which the same operation migrates across organizational forms.[^253] Ricks builds a program of monetary reform on the corollary that one should regulate the function (the issuance of money-claims) rather than the chartered entity, so that functionally identical deposit-substitutes are not governed by accidentally divergent regimes.[^254] Gorton and Metrick's account of shadow banking supplies the design template most directly: a large sector grew up outside the perimeter through definitional gaps, and the response was to bring its functional substitutes inside through chartering and collateral rules rather than to chase each new instrument with a bespoke prohibition.[^255] The litigation-finance debate has proceeded almost entirely without this apparatus.

The deepest support comes from inside insurance law itself. Abraham has shown that "insurance" is not a fixed natural kind but a contested functional category—insurance as a contract, as a regulated public-utility industry, as a product, and as a mode of governance—each carrying its own regulatory agenda.[^256] If insurance law cannot itself agree which of these insurance is, then "is it insurance?" cannot bear the weight of a clean on/off switch; the classification is already a functional and normative judgment made covertly. The framework makes that judgment openly and by degree. This is the destabilizing lemma on which the prescription turns: entity-formalism is not a stable baseline from which functional regulation departs, but an unstable equilibrium that conceals the very judgment the framework proposes to make transparently.

Two prior functional proposals are the closest precursors, and the delta from each must be stated precisely. Sahani's reshaping-and-harmonizing project advances function-based regulation of third-party funding, sorting funder interactions into transactional, procedural, and ethical categories tailored to protect the dispute-resolution system.[^257] Steinitz's *Follow the Money* proposes a disclosure framework for funding agreements.[^258] The difference here is structural. Sahani's tripartite scheme keys on the *type of interaction* a funder has with a dispute and operates entirely within the funding silo, taking the funding instrument as its fixed object. This framework keys instead on the *risk-transfer function across instruments*, placing funding on the same plane as ATE, JPI, and the contingency fee, so that the cross-instrument arbitrage—invisible from inside any single-instrument frame—becomes the central object of regulation. The functional-insurance trigger has no analogue in Sahani's categories because she never engages the insurance-classification question; the trigger is orthogonal to her axis, reclassifying any instrument—funding included—that crosses into indemnity and pooling. Nor does she calibrate by claimant vulnerability. Steinitz's disclosure regime is, on this Article's diagnosis, mismatched to the prudential harms.[^259] The novelty is therefore neither "regulate the function" nor "disclose," but the synthesis: drawing *one* perimeter around funding, insurance, and the contingency fee together; calibrating intensity by vulnerability; and closing the relabeling gap with a graded functional-insurance trigger keyed to the indemnity-and-pooling line the business-of-insurance doctrine itself draws.

A word of steelmanned caution is owed before the design. Turk has argued that economically convergent products do not automatically warrant functionally harmonized regulation, that formalistic entity-based definitions retain subtle advantages, and that functional regulation is not costless—it spawns its own boundary problems.[^260] The framework answers this structurally. It does not abolish definitions but *grades* them—the functional-insurance trigger preserves a hard definitional core at the indemnity/pooling line—and the relevant comparison is not function-based regulation against a costless formalism but against the status quo of arbitrage, under-capitalization, and improvised cross-regime enforcement that Part III documents.[^261] Turk's full argument is engaged in Part V; here it suffices that the framework is built to take the boundary-clarity objection seriously rather than wish it away.

### IV.B. Tier 0: The Common Perimeter

Every legal-claim-risk transfer the framework reaches shares a minimal floor. The Tier 0 trigger is functional and deliberately broad: any non-party that, for profit, provides capital or an indemnity whose return is contingent on the outcome of identified litigation or a portfolio of claims.[^262] That captures single-case and portfolio TPLF, ATE, JPI, and claim and appellate monetization; it also reaches the contingency fee, not to burden a centuries-old institution but to establish it as the calibration baseline against which the others are measured—the original, normalized risk-transfer arrangement the field has always tolerated.

Three baseline obligations attach, uniform and minimal. First, *registration* with a designated regulator: identity, beneficial ownership, and place of formation. This floor makes the market observable, curing the data opacity the Government Accountability Office repeatedly flags—no aggregate figure for funders' returns or capital deployed, and public data that do not capture funding at all—and the "no court docket" invisibility that let sanctioned-adversary capital move millions unobserved.[^263] Second, an *independence/control rule*: the capital provider may not direct litigation or settlement. This rule is function-based and payor-blind, built on the *structure* of Model Rule 1.8(f)'s three conditions for third-party-funded representation—informed client consent, no interference with the lawyer's independent professional judgment, and protection of confidences—rather than on Rule 5.4(a)'s flat entity ban on fee-sharing with nonlawyers.[^264] A caveat keeps the borrowing honest: Rule 1.8(f) triggers only when a *lawyer* accepts third-party compensation, so it does not, by its terms, reach the common case in which a funder pays the *client*. The proposal does not claim 1.8(f) already governs client-directed funding; it generalizes its consent-non-interference-confidentiality template into a payor-blind, funder-facing rule policing whether a non-client payor distorts the representation, regardless of whose pocket the repayment leaves. Rule 5.4(a), by contrast, generates the field's most embarrassing line—funding repaid from the lawyer's fee violates the fee-sharing ban while economically identical funding repaid from the client's recovery does not—an entity-of-payment distinction tracking who signs the check rather than any difference in risk or control.[^265] Generalizing 1.8(f) and displacing 5.4(a) converts that formalist line into a substantive one, tracking the control limits already in the Montana, Georgia, Indiana, and Louisiana statutes and the ABA's best-practices guidance.[^266]

Third, *relevance-gated disclosure*—disclosure done right, against Part III's critique. The existence and identity of a funder are disclosed to the court; the terms are discoverable only on a relevance or need showing (conflicts, control, real-party-in-interest, standing) under a Rule 26(c) protective order.[^267] Part III's "wrong problem" claim was never that disclosure is unimportant—only that it is mismatched to the prudential harms and, in its leading current form, perversely asymmetric across functional substitutes.[^268] Relevance-gating answers both objections: it preserves the genuine information and conflict-detection functions that this Article's arguments about funder control and foreign-capital detection depend upon, while declining to treat disclosure as a substitute for the substantive regulation the other tiers supply.

### IV.C. Tier 1: Light-Touch Prudential and Integrity Rules for Sophisticated Transfer

The vulnerability axis selects the tier. Tier 1 governs the sophisticated, counseled, repeat-player end of the market: commercial TPLF to corporations and law firms, portfolio funding, claim monetization, and Big Law–directed deals. The empirical profile confirms the sophistication: Westfleet's 2024 commercial data are dominated by portfolio structures (67%), lawyer-directed deals (58%), and the largest 200 U.S. firms (37% of commitments).[^269] These are not vulnerable counterparties. They need the system's integrity protected and their counterparties' solvency assured; they do not need paternalistic price or suitability controls, which would suppress the efficiency gains the instrument exists to capture. The economic literature is decisive: Molot shows that litigation finance corrects the risk-preference asymmetries that distort settlement; Shepherd and Stone catalog its risk-bearing and capital-efficiency functions; and Burch theorizes funders as monitors who reduce agency costs in aggregate litigation.[^270] Over-regulating at this tier destroys value.

There is, however, one serious harm at Tier 1—the harm Part III isolated: under-capitalization and counterparty risk. A funder's undrawn commitments and balance-sheet fragility can leave a funded litigant stranded mid-case, and the recent record supplies the evidence. Therium—a pioneer that backed the UK Post Office litigation—exited active funding into run-off, with press reports (journalistic estimates, not audited figures) attributing the stress to large losses.[^271] Burford's group-wide portfolio of roughly $7.48 billion is a NYSE- and LSE-listed risk-pricing balance sheet operating wholly outside insurance solvency regulation, and the Omni Bridgeway–Ares secondary—Ares acquiring a 70% interest in a continuation fund holding more than 150 legal assets—is direct evidence that claim risk is pooled and traded to institutional capital with no solvency overlay.[^272] What the regulated insurance market would treat as risk distribution is being performed in the least-regulated corner of the market.

The correct remedy is prudential discipline an order of magnitude lighter than full insurer solvency regulation—analogy with deliberate downscaling. The NAIC's Risk-Based Capital framework runs four action levels keyed to the Authorized Control Level (Company Action at 200%, Regulatory Action at 150%, Authorized Control at 100%, Mandatory Control at 70%) within a three-stage solvency architecture of preventive guardrails, corrective action, and a guaranty-fund backstop.[^273] Tier 1 takes only the first stage, in diluted form: a capital-adequacy or bonding requirement scaled to undrawn commitments and counterparty exposure, satisfiable by a surety bond, proof of committed capital, or a light RBC-style ratio. It does *not* take the guaranty-fund backstop—that policyholder safety net is unnecessary for sophisticated counterparties and is reserved for the functional-insurance category—and imposes no reserves-for-indemnity and no rate review.[^274] That is what keeps Tier 1 from collapsing into insurance regulation by another name: it is prudential-integrity-only, which is precisely why the functional-insurance trigger of Part IV.E is a separate switch.

The feasibility of light prudential gatekeeping is not speculative. Singapore conditions "qualifying third-party funder" status on a paid-up capital (or managed-assets) threshold of S$5 million—a prudential filter, far lighter than insurance regulation, that screens out the undercapitalized without dictating returns.[^275] And the Civil Justice Council's 2025 *Final Report*, the most considered recent regulatory study of funding anywhere, recommends light-touch statutory regulation including capital adequacy and a control bar while expressly declining to impose any statutory cap on funder returns—the precise Tier 1 combination.[^276] The remaining Tier 1 obligations are the Tier 0 floor, enforced: the payor-blind independence rule and relevance-gated disclosure. And there is no return cap, because sophisticated parties price legal-claim risk themselves—a choice that matches Indiana's commercial regime, which imposes control limits and foreign-funding rules without any rate cap.[^277]

### IV.D. Tier 2: Insurance-Like and Consumer-Credit-Like Protection for Vulnerable Claimants

At the other end of the vulnerability axis sits the individual, distressed, non-repeat-player claimant. The consumer pre-settlement advance—typically under $10,000 to an injured plaintiff for living expenses, per the GAO's commercial/consumer line—is transacted under information asymmetry, liquidity pressure, and an inability to price the risk being shed; mass-tort claimants subject to opaque financier control occupy the same vulnerable position.[^278] The function of these transfers is consumer-protective, not efficiency-enhancing, and the harm Parikh documents—financiers seizing the plaintiff's right to decide when to settle and engaging in "claim alchemy" that enhances low-value claims—is the agency-cost harm of Part III concentrated where the claimant can least absorb it.[^279] Tier 2's modality is correspondingly stronger: insurance-like and consumer-credit-like protection.

The framework's strongest claim at this tier is that the law already does most of this; Tier 2 systematizes a normative consensus rather than inventing one. A wave of state statutes already treats consumer legal-claim-risk transfer as a regulated credit-or-insurance-like product. West Virginia caps the annual fee on a consumer advance at 18% and bars charges beyond 42 months; Tennessee limits the total fee to a 25% APR-equivalent over three years; Montana caps recovery at 25% of the judgment or settlement and—critically—prohibits the funder from assigning *or securitizing* the contract; and Ohio, responding to a champerty ruling, requires attorney attestations and a twelve-point boldface statement that the funder has no right to direct the litigation or its settlement.[^280] The bar's own rule points the same way: Model Rule 1.8(e) bars *lawyers* from funding a client's living expenses for the identical paternalistic reason, and its 2020 amendment permitting modest living-expense gifts to indigent pro bono clients shows the rule calibrating rather than categorically prohibiting when access-to-justice concerns press.[^281] That convergence of legislatures and the bar is the proof of concept; the framework's contribution is an organizing principle and a uniform floor.

The model Tier 2 rule composes these instruments into four protections. First, *cost and APR transparency* modeled on the Truth in Lending Act and Regulation Z—standardized, comparable cost disclosure. This is the "is it credit?" analogue to the "is it insurance?" question, and the doctrinal mirror is live: regulators are working out, for adjacent fintech products such as earned-wage access, whether a contingent advance is "credit" subject to Reg Z at all—the same form-versus-function problem in a different corner of the market.[^282] The loan-label evasion is no obstacle. That *Ruth* and *Maslowski* hold these advances are not "loans"—repayment being contingent on recovery—does not defeat functional disclosure; West Virginia, Tennessee, and Ohio already impose cost transparency on non-recourse advances without calling them loans, and a legislature may require cost disclosure regardless of usury classification.[^283] The contingency that defeats "loan" defeats "indemnity" as well (see supra Part II.D), which is exactly why a bespoke functional rule is needed rather than borrowed usury or insurance law.[^284]

Second, *rate or return discipline*: either a cap modeled on the existing state statutes or, more flexibly, a minimum-net-to-claimant rule. The Voss Report (the European Parliament's 2022 resolution, discussed in Part V) recommends that claimants retain at least 60% of gross settlement or damages, and the CJC recommends court approval of returns for non-commercial claimants—templates that floor what the claimant keeps rather than cap what the funder charges, preserving access while capturing the consumer-protective core.[^285] Third, *suitability* protections: a plain-language contract, a cooling-off period, and attorney attestation on the Ohio model.[^286] Fourth, the Tier 0 independence rule plus *anti-assignment and anti-securitization* restrictions on the Montana model, so that a vulnerable claimant's chose in action cannot be repackaged and sold out from under the protections the tier supplies.[^287]

### IV.E. The "Functional Insurance" Trigger

The vulnerability tiers key on the claimant. The framework's signature analytic device keys on the *instrument*, and it operates independently of the tiers. Any instrument that crosses from buying a contingent upside into *indemnifying a defined loss* or *pooling risk across insureds* is reclassified as functional insurance and pulled into the full state insurance regime—licensing, reserves, risk-based capital, and guaranty-fund participation. This is the doctrinal payoff of Parts I through III: it draws the regulatory line exactly where risk-transfer becomes indemnity, and it does so by graded application of doctrine the courts already possess.

A precision essential to this audience must be stated at the outset. The trigger borrows the Supreme Court's "business of insurance" trilogy—*National Securities*, *Royal Drug*, and *Pireno*—as the best-developed federal articulation of what insurance functionally *is*, not as the operative product-definition test. The trilogy construes "the business of insurance" in the McCarran-Ferguson context—*National Securities* under the § 1012(b) reverse-preemption clause, *Royal Drug* and *Pireno* under the narrow § 1013(b) antitrust exemption—not as a freestanding product definition; the controlling test for "doing an insurance business" is state law, paradigmatically New York Insurance Law § 1101, which requires an obligation to confer a pecuniary benefit on a fortuitous event in which the insured has a "material interest which will be adversely affected."[^288] The trigger runs the trilogy's *factors* as a graded functional inquiry while leaving the controlling classification to state law—never conflating the two.

The trigger has three factors. Factor one, the *Royal Drug* and *Pireno* core: does the instrument transfer and spread the holder's risk by indemnifying a defined loss—paying upon an adverse outcome—rather than buying a contingent share of an upside? *Royal Drug* fixes "the spreading and underwriting of a policyholder's risk" as "[t]he primary elements of an insurance contract."[^289] Factor two: is the practice integral to an insurer-insured-type relationship—a premium paid for an indemnity—within *National Securities*' focus on "the relationship between the insurance company and the policyholder"?[^290] Factor three is the one a hostile reader will press, and the framework does *not* dilute it: is the risk distributed—pooled or spread across a book—rather than borne in an isolated bilateral exchange? Distribution of risk is not an optional gloss on the definition; *Jordan* makes it constitutive, holding that insurance "by the better view" involves "distribution of the risk," and *Royal Drug* anchors the "spreading" of risk at the core of the contract.[^291] Keeping that element intact prevents the trigger from sweeping in every ordinary bilateral indemnity—a stand-alone indemnification clause, a surety-like guarantee, a single-risk hedge—and is why the framework declines the tempting shortcut of treating any single-claim indemnity as insurance. *Fabe* is invoked only for the proposition it actually supports: not that pooling may be dropped from the *product* definition, but that the Court reads "the business of insurance" *protectively and by purpose* for reverse-preemption under § 1012(b), so a policyholder-protective construction of which arrangements warrant the insurance regime is the Court's own posture rather than a reformer's invention.[^292] *Pireno*'s caution that "[n]one of these criteria is necessarily determinative in itself" governs how the three factors combine, not whether distribution survives.[^293]

Applied, the trigger sorts the field correctly and non-arbitrarily.

| Instrument | Indemnity of a defined loss? | Distribution / pooling of risk? | Functional-insurance result |
|---|---|---|---|
| ATE insurance | Yes—indemnifies adverse costs and own disbursements on an adverse outcome | Yes—underwritten across a book of insureds | Inside (already insurance) |
| JPI / contingent-legal-risk | Yes—pays the shortfall on reversal or reduction below a floor | Yes—reversal risk syndicated across a tower of carriers | Inside (already insurance) |
| Pure single-case or portfolio TPLF | No—funder earns zero on loss; no material interest adversely affected | No | Outside |
| Contingency fee | No—lawyer takes a share of recovery, nothing on loss | No | Outside |

ATE and JPI fall inside on all three factors, and the distribution factor answers the worry that single-judgment JPI is merely a one-off bilateral indemnity. It is not: the JPI on *BMC* was written through a syndicated insurance tower in which the reversal risk was shared across roughly thirty to forty carriers via managing general agents and underwriters—genuine pooling and underwriting, not an isolated two-party hedge—and the tower paid the reversal as an insured loss exactly as an indemnity contract pays.[^294] ATE and JPI thus satisfy indemnity *and* distribution; they are already insurance, correctly regulated as such, and the trigger merely confirms the status quo. Pure TPLF falls outside, and on grounds the formalist should welcome: the funder holds no "material interest which will be adversely affected" by an adverse outcome within § 1101—it merely earns zero—failing the statutory element; it offends the *VALIC* line, under which the issuer must bear "some investment risk-taking" in the insurance sense for a product to be insurance rather than a pure investment placing the risk on the customer; and under *Jordan*'s "principal object and purpose" test the principal object of a funding agreement is financing and investment, not indemnity.[^295] The contingency fee falls outside for the same reason. This is the function-versus-doctrine discipline made operational: the Article does not claim TPLF *is* insurance de jure—it agrees with Steinitz and with *Ruth* and *Maslowski* that it is not—and that doctrinal non-identity is the engine of the arbitrage, not a refutation of the thesis.[^296]

The load-bearing point is that the trigger is *instrument-specific*, and that is what makes it an anti-arbitrage valve rather than a static restatement of current classifications. Because it reaches the *indemnity-and-distribution feature* rather than the entity, it crosses wherever that feature appears—even inside an instrument the market calls "funding." Westfleet's first-ever datum that roughly 19% of 2024 commercial commitments were fully or partially covered by contingent-risk insurance describes funding deals with an insurance wrapper; because that wrapper is genuine indemnity laid off and distributed through the insurance market, the wrapped portion crosses the trigger.[^297] So does any TPLF agreement engineered with a loss-floor or principal-protection feature whose downside is underwritten or syndicated across a book—the indemnity is then both present and distributed, and a guaranteed payment on an adverse outcome *is* insurance whatever the caption. The distribution element keeps this from condemning every bilateral loss-floor: a true two-party, un-laid-off indemnity is an ordinary indemnity contract, not the business of insurance, and the trigger leaves it alone. The valve is symmetric: it prevents escaping insurance regulation by relabeling distributed indemnity as "investment," and—paired with Tier 2—escaping consumer protection by relabeling a consumer loan as a "non-recourse purchase." A bright-line proxy answers the administrability worry: a guaranteed payment triggered by an adverse litigation outcome, or a contractual loss-floor, flags the indemnity feature and presumptively crosses the line once that risk is distributed across a pool rather than retained bilaterally.

That courts already toggle these instruments across regulatory boxes is the comparative proof that a single reclassification has system-wide bite—and the cautionary lesson that the toggling should be principled and prospective rather than accidental and retroactive. In the United Kingdom, *PACCAR* re-characterized percentage-based litigation funding agreements as damages-based agreements under the Courts and Legal Services Act, rendering the vast majority of them unenforceable overnight.[^298] In Australia, the courts ran the reverse: *Brookfield Multiplex* held funded schemes to be managed investment schemes requiring registration, and *LCM Funding v. Stanwell* overruled it as "plainly wrong"—Lee J.'s image was of "placing a square peg into a round hole"—on the ground that group members contributed no "money or money's worth" and there was no "pooling."[^299] These episodes prove the boxes are live switches; the framework's contribution is to make the switch turn on a principled functional criterion (indemnity or pooling) applied prospectively, rather than on the contingency of how a captioning question reaches a court.

### IV.F. Institutional Design: A Deliberately Plural Allocation

The framework's modalities map onto four existing institutional competencies, and the right design is plural-by-function rather than a single new agency. Institutional design is a routing problem: each modality has a best-fit institution, and forcing all of it into one "federal funding agency" would be both politically unattainable and functionally wrong, over-insurance-ifying funding or under-protecting consumers.

*State insurance commissioners* are the natural home for anything crossing the functional-insurance trigger, and choosing them requires almost no new machinery. They already license ATE and JPI carriers, set risk-based capital, and run guaranty funds; the NAIC apparatus—Model Act #312, the three-stage solvency framework, the guaranty associations—already governs exactly the indemnity-and-pooling products the trigger reaches.[^300] The reform here is mostly one of resolve: commissioners must resist letting indemnity be relabeled out of their regime. The precedent is exact—Schwarcz and the DFS's "shadow insurance," in which a regulator named and acted on intra-insurance captive-reinsurance arbitrage (see supra Part I.D).[^301] A commissioner who can name captive-reinsurance arbitrage can name a loss-floor "investment" that is functionally indemnity.

A *narrow functional federal overlay* is warranted only where state-by-state regulation structurally fails: systemic and secondary-market interconnection, the foreign- and sovereign-capital channel, and a uniform Tier 1 prudential floor. The justification is the shadow-banking logic—bring functional substitutes inside the perimeter where fragmentation leaves gaps.[^302] The foreign-capital channel is the clearest case: the GAO's patent-funding report finds foreign funding significant but unmeasurable on public data, with the Department of Justice examining discovery-as-intelligence concerns; the Bloomberg investigation documents a sanctioned Russian-linked funder moving roughly $20 million "without ever appearing on a court docket"; and the DOJ's FARA advisory opinion shows a regulator reaching foreign-funded litigation through an improvised application of the foreign-agent statute.[^303] The pattern is telling: Congress reaches for FARA, a punitive tax on funder proceeds, and bespoke foreign-funding bans precisely *because* there is no functional perimeter. The Protecting Our Courts from Foreign Manipulation Act, advancing in both chambers in 2025, would ban foreign-state and sovereign-wealth-fund funding and require annual reporting.[^304] A federal overlay would absorb and rationalize these partial proxies, handling the foreign-capital concern as one application of a general registration-and-perimeter rule rather than through scattered national-security workarounds. (The advocacy provenance of some of this material counsels resting the structural claim on the neutral spine—the Bloomberg case, the GAO data gap, and the DOJ opinion—which hold regardless of how much foreign capital is benign.)[^305]

*The rulemaking courts* are the correct instrument for relevance-gated disclosure and litigation-control rules, and the wrong forum for prudential or price regulation. The civil-rules process is already seized of the disclosure question: the Advisory Committee's TPLF Subcommittee, created in October 2024, framed the operative question as "[w]hat is the court to do with the information if the [funding is] disclosed?"—and the framework answers it: disclosure exists for relevance-gated litigation purposes (conflicts, control, standing), not as a substitute for substantive regulation.[^306] That answer also cures the incoherence in the leading reform text. The Lawyers for Civil Justice and Institute for Legal Reform proposal to amend Rule 26 invokes the 1970 adoption of automatic insurance disclosure as precedent, yet carves out "contingency-fee arrangements, insurance, [and] ordinary personal and bank loans"—analogizing funding to insurance to justify disclosure while exempting insurance and the contingency fee from the rule.[^307] That is functional equivalence recognized and then ignored: the carve-out reproduces the arbitrage inside the cure. The existing local-rule patchwork—the Delaware standing order, the District of New Jersey's Rule 7.1.1, and the Northern District of California's order limiting disclosure to class, collective, and representative actions—shows courts groping toward both relevance-gating and, in the Northern District of California, vulnerability calibration.[^308]

*A consumer-credit regulator* should house Tier 2, and here the design must be candid about institutional fragility. The natural federal home is the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, whose earned-wage-access "is it credit?" work is directly on point—but the Bureau's acute funding and political instability in 2025–26 counsels against making it the linchpin.[^309] The design therefore makes state consumer-credit regulators primary, with the CFPB an optional overlay: Oklahoma already licenses consumer litigation funders through its Department of Consumer Credit, and Georgia routes funder registration through its banking regulator using the Nationwide Multistate Licensing System.[^310] That a supervisory, data-driven regulator can govern nonlawyer capital in legal-claim risk without a categorical ban is established by Arizona, which abrogated Rule 5.4 and licenses alternative business structures with nonlawyer ownership, and Utah's data-driven sandbox authorizing nonlawyer ownership and fee-sharing under ongoing oversight—the institutional-form precedent for calibrated supervision rather than prohibition.[^311]

A plural design invites the charge of turf wars and gaps, and the charge of capture. The first is answered by the coordinating spine—the common Tier 0 perimeter and the functional-insurance trigger run across all four institutions—and by pluralism's tracking the functional map (insurance versus credit versus procedure versus systemic) using existing competencies rather than a miscalibrated super-agency. The second is answered, in part, by pluralism itself: dispersing authority across insurance commissioners, a federal overlay, the rulemaking courts, and consumer regulators denies any single industry a chokepoint to capture. The fuller treatment belongs to Part V.

### IV.G. Federalism, McCarran-Ferguson, and the Live Fights

McCarran-Ferguson appears, at first, to be the framework's great obstacle: if the functional-insurance trigger reclassifies products as insurance, does it not hand everything to fifty state regimes and trigger reverse-preemption against any federal role? The answer is that McCarran-Ferguson is a *routing rule*, not a barrier, and the framework is built to work with it.

The statute commits "[t]he business of insurance" to the states and provides that no Act of Congress shall impair a state law "enacted for the purpose of regulating the business of insurance . . . unless such Act specifically relates to the business of insurance."[^312] The functional-insurance trigger is designed around the *same* risk-transfer-and-indemnity core that *Royal Drug* and *Pireno* place at the heart of "the business of insurance." Everything the trigger reclassifies as insurance—genuine indemnity, genuine pooling—is therefore routed to the states, honoring § 1012's reverse-preemption rather than fighting it. The framework asks the states to regulate as insurance exactly what the trilogy says insurance is.

The funding side dissolves the objection from the opposite direction. Because pure TPLF *fails* the trilogy—no indemnity, no pooling, no material interest adversely affected, none of *National Securities*' insurer-insured relationship—a federal overlay regulating funding does not "relate to the business of insurance" at all, so § 1012(b)'s clear-statement reverse-preemption never engages.[^313] The same doctrinal feature that makes TPLF escape *state* insurance regulation frees a *federal* funding overlay from McCarran-Ferguson. And *Fabe* confirms the interpretive posture is the Court's own: reading "business of insurance" functionally and protectively, tethered to policyholder protection.[^314] A state could not expand "business of insurance" to capture funding instruments lacking indemnity and pooling merely to block a federal overlay, because the trilogy forecloses that expansion for § 1012(b) purposes.

With the federalism objection reframed, the three live regulatory fights reveal themselves as partial, mislabeled proxies for the missing functional regime—each capturing one fragment of the right design. *First, FRCP 26.* The framework answers the Subcommittee's open question directly: disclosure should be relevance-gated, deployed for conflicts, control, and standing, and—decisively—*symmetric across functional substitutes*. That symmetry is the fix for the LCJ/ILR carve-out, which exempts insurance and the contingency fee while targeting funding; treating substitutes alike is the procedural expression of the whole thesis.[^315] *Second, the state-statute wave.* The Montana, West Virginia, and Tennessee consumer caps are Tier 2 done piecemeal; Georgia's registration-through-the-banking-regulator with control limits is Tier 1 done piecemeal; Kansas's in-camera disclosure compromise is relevance-gating done piecemeal.[^316] The wave is uncoordinated Tier 1 and Tier 2 fragments, and the framework supplies the missing organizing principle and a uniform floor that defines the consumer/commercial boundary by vulnerability rather than by the funder's self-chosen label. *Third, the foreign-funding bills.* The POCFMA family and the foreign provisions of the Louisiana, Oklahoma, and Indiana statutes are a national-security-flavored slice of the federal overlay's perimeter—one rule of a general registration regime, not a freestanding category.[^317]

The comparative record delivers the governing lesson: prospective, principled reclassification beats retroactive judicial shock. *PACCAR* imposed a regime on funders retroactively and threw the market into multi-year limbo—a lapsed enforceability bill, then the CJC's light-touch functional recommendations, and finally the Government's December 2025 decision to reverse *PACCAR* *prospectively only*, departing from the CJC's recommended retrospective fix to avoid disturbing legacy contracts.[^318] Australia's in-out-in oscillation—into the managed-investment-scheme box in 2009, the AFSL regime in 2020, and back out in 2022—is the same lesson written larger.[^319] Each shows the cost of leaving classification to ad hoc adjudication. The framework's graded, prospective trigger is the antidote: any reclassification should follow the Sackman model, prospective, with legacy JPI and funding contracts honored under the regime in which they were written.

### IV.H. Implementation Objections and Sequencing

Two implementation objections are real but bounded. The first is *line-drawing*: "how vulnerable?" and "how much indemnity?" are graded questions, answered by bright-line proxies. Vulnerability is proxied by claimant type—a natural person versus a represented commercial entity—and by deal size, using thresholds the existing law already supplies (the GAO's sub-$10,000 consumer line; Georgia's $25,000 discoverability threshold).[^320] The indemnity-and-distribution feature is proxied by a guaranteed payment on an adverse outcome or a contractual loss-floor whose downside is underwritten or syndicated across a pool, distinguishing it from a retained bilateral indemnity. The status quo already spawns satellite litigation over disclosure and champerty with *no* organizing principle; proxies that cabin discretion improve on a regime that has none.

The second is *incrementalism*, and here the framework degrades gracefully because each piece can be adopted by the institution that already holds the lever. The Tier 0 perimeter and relevance-gated disclosure can be adopted now through the civil-rules process and state registration, without waiting for a federal overlay—and even registration plus relevance-gated disclosure alone improves on the status quo by making the market observable. The Tier 2 consumer floor builds on the existing state statutes. The federal overlay and the functional-insurance trigger are the politically heaviest lifts and come last—though the trigger is applicable by courts under existing "business of insurance" doctrine even before legislation, since the trilogy's factors—indemnity, the insurer-insured relationship, and distribution—operate here only as functional gloss on the state-law "doing an insurance business" inquiry (§ 1101), which a distributed loss-floor independently satisfies, and not as a freestanding product test in their own right. The sequencing is therefore: perimeter and disclosure first, the consumer floor second, the overlay and trigger last.

Here the apparent objection from empirics becomes support. The forthcoming Whac-A-Mole study (an SSRN working paper, cited as such) analyzes more than 100,000 consumer funding agreements and reports the dynamic its title names: entity-based regulation merely shifts behavior, the product re-emerging in a relabeled form.[^321] On a disclosure-or-entity-ban model that is a counsel of despair. On the functional model it is the affirmative case: rules that attach to the *function*—indemnity, pooling, vulnerability—rather than the *entity* or *label* are the only kind the product cannot evade by relabeling, which is the entire point of the trigger and the vulnerability proxies.

The framework's four heaviest objections—access-to-justice chilling, innovation-chilling, the "it's not insurance" formalism, and capture—are previewed here and met in full in Part V. The access objection is answered by calibration: the heaviest burdens fall where access is least implicated (sophisticated commercial parties), and the consumer tier's minimum-net-to-claimant rule is itself pro-access. The innovation objection mistakes arbitrage for progress. The formalism objection—that TPLF lacks indemnity, pooling, and an insurable interest—is conceded and reframed: the framework *agrees*, which is why the trigger leaves pure funding out, and the concession is a confession of the arbitrage, not a refutation. And the worry that "transfers and prices risk" proves too much—sweeping in stock options and warranties—is met by the trigger's conjunctive structure, which reaches an instrument only where three conditions hold together: a non-recourse transfer of a *specific litigant's claim risk*, to a counterparty on whose performance the claimant *relies*, under *information asymmetry or claimant vulnerability*. A stock option lacks the vulnerable, performance-dependent claimant, and so falls outside by construction.[^322]

The migration on which the whole diagnosis rests should be claimed for no more than it is worth. It is a documented market *episode*—the JPI contraction following *BMC* and the concurrent rise of claim monetization to 26% of commitments—paired with a *testable hypothesis*, not an identified causal estimate; the measurement limitation is real, and the method for future empirical work is the Koijen and Yogo template that quantified the analogous captive-reinsurance migration.[^323] But the prescription does not depend on a precise migration coefficient. It depends only on the structural claim that functionally equivalent risk transfers sit under divergent regimes, that capital responds to the gradient, and that a regime attaching to function rather than label closes it. That claim is the spine of Part V's defense, to which the argument now turns.

## V. Objections and Comparative Lessons

### V.A. Roadmap and the Stakes of Part V

A reform proposal earns enactment only after it survives the objections of those who would live under it and confronts the experience of those who have already tried something like it. Part V does both. It steelmans the five strongest objections to functional, risk-based regulation of legal-claim risk and answers each; it then mines the comparative record—Australia, the United Kingdom, Singapore and Hong Kong, and the European Union—as evidence, not tourism. The objections fall into two families. The first is the generic anti-regulation case—that any regime will chill access to justice (V.B) and freeze beneficial innovation (V.C)—answered by calibration, not abandonment. The second attacks the functional *method* itself: that funding simply is not insurance (V.D); that any regulator will be captured or overreach (V.E); and that the functional trigger cannot be measured or administered (V.F). Only this family is distinctive, and its hinge—indeed the Article's—is the formalist objection in V.D, which must be put at full doctrinal strength before it can be answered. The comparative payoff is a single generalization: every jurisdiction that has governed legal-claim risk has confronted the same fault line, and the regimes that regulated by *entity classification* destabilized on a single judicial reclassification while those that regulated by *purpose-built function* endured. The thesis is thus corroborated on foreign data, not merely domestic theory.

### V.B. Objection One: Access-to-Justice Chilling

The first objection is the funders' rhetorical high ground, and it deserves its full force. Third-party litigation funding (TPLF), after-the-event (ATE) insurance, and contingency fees are the principal mechanisms by which under-capitalized claimants and small firms vindicate meritorious claims against repeat-player defendants.[^324] To layer insurance-style capital, solvency, and disclosure burdens onto funding is to raise its cost, shrink its supply, and—on this view—price out exactly the medium-sized claims that only funding makes viable. Regulation, the objector says, is regressive: it falls hardest on the vulnerable claimant it purports to protect. Jonathan Molot's efficiency case supplies the strongest version of the premise—a market in litigation risk corrects the settlement distortions that liquidity constraints impose on under-capitalized parties.[^325]

The answer is calibration, not prohibition, conceding Molot's point and then locating it. The prescription is tiered by risk and claimant vulnerability, so the heaviest obligations fall where access to justice is *least* implicated—large commercial and portfolio funding among sophisticated repeat players—and the lightest fall on the individual consumer. That is the inverse of a flat ban. Crucially, in the consumer tier the access-to-justice value cuts *for* protective regulation: when an under-reserved funder fails, the counterparty and under-capitalization risk lands on the very claimant the objection invokes. Molot's efficiency case, properly read, is about the commercial tier, where the Article's heaviest obligations do the least access damage.

Second, the closest live comparator has already threaded this needle. The United Kingdom's Civil Justice Council (CJC), in its June 2025 Final Report, recommended light-touch, function-focused statutory regulation including capital adequacy, paired with *enhanced* protections for consumer and non-commercial claimants—independent King's Counsel review, ATE cover in some cases—expressly on access-to-justice grounds.[^326] An independent judicial body, asked to choose between deregulation and a flat insurance overlay, chose calibration. The two values are reconcilable, not opposed.

Third, the unregulated baseline is not neutral. Opacity and the externalization of agency costs already chill access for the wrong claimants: Samir Parikh documents opaque capital seizing settlement control and pursuing "claim alchemy" in mass torts, and Elizabeth Chamblee Burch shows the monitoring gap that lets financiers capture settlement value where Rule 23's safeguards do not reach.[^327] Doing nothing is not a refusal to redistribute; it is a redistribution toward the best-capitalized, most opaque players. Functional regulation redistributes toward claimants.

Finally, the magnitude of any chilling effect is an empirical question, not an axiom—and a natural experiment is available. The 2012 LASPO reforms stripped the inter-partes recoverability of ATE premiums and conditional-fee success fees, reshaping the substitution economics among ATE, funding, and conditional-fee agreements; the market adapted rather than collapsed.[^328] The rejoinder that calibration is illusory because capital is fungible mistakes the unit of tiering, which runs by transaction and claimant type, not by firm: Singapore's paid-up-capital-plus-principal-business gate, discussed below, shows that a prudential filter can govern the wholesale market without touching retail claimants.[^329] And the worry that even modest disclosure deters funders is empirically weak—every comparative regime surveyed here mandates disclosure, and none has collapsed.

### V.C. Objection Two: Innovation and Efficiency Costs

The innovation objection finds its most sophisticated expression in Matthew Turk's work, the named steelman.[^330] Legal-claim-risk markets are young and fast-evolving; funders screen claims, monitor agency costs, and correct risk-preference distortions, and the emerging secondary-market plumbing—continuation funds, portfolio securitization—is genuine financial innovation.[^331] Turk's deeper point is that economically convergent products do not necessarily warrant functionally harmonized regulation, that formalistic entity definitions retain subtle administrative and predictability advantages, and that a premature perimeter freezes product design, entrenches incumbents who can bear compliance, and forecloses beneficial instruments. The Merton-Bodie "innovation spiral" cuts the same way.[^332]

The first answer is that function-regulation is innovation-neutral by design. The point of regulating the function rather than the entity is that a new product form inherits obligations according to its risk profile, so a novel structure is never penalized merely for being novel—only for re-creating the same unpriced risk under a fresh label. It is *entity* regulation that distorts innovation, because it rewards relabeling; the arbitrage is the distortion. The second answer reframes the contested word: much of what is paraded as "innovation" here is arbitrage innovation. The 2023–26 migration of appellate-reversal risk out of judgment-preservation insurance (JPI) into uninsured funding and appellate monetization is not welfare-improving product design; it is the same risk moving to the lighter regime after the insured form took losses.[^333] Daniel Schwarcz's shadow-insurance and Gorton and Metrick's shadow-banking analyses teach that this pattern—risk migrating to the under-supervised functional substitute—is the fingerprint of arbitrage, not the signature of progress.[^334] Genuine product innovation (better screening, secondary-market liquidity) is protected under a function-regime; arbitrage innovation (relabeling insured risk as "financing") is what the Article targets.

The third answer takes Turk's net-benefit comparison head-on rather than co-opting him. Turk's load-bearing claim is not merely that arbitrage potential is troubling—on that we agree, and Turk himself traces both boundary problems to arbitrage potential that can spawn an under-supervised shadow-finance sector[^335]—but that the *cure* may cost more than the disease: a functional perimeter generates its own boundary litigation, and a manageable formal line may be worth more than the externality it leaves uncorrected. The concession is real: functional lines do get litigated, and *PACCAR* and *Stanwell* (discussed below) are precisely functional-versus-formal classification fights. So the question is genuinely comparative and must be won on the facts of *this* market. Two features tip it decisively toward function, even granting that the systemic externality is modest. First, the relevant externality is not the systemic one but the counterparty-and-vulnerability one: an under-reserved funder's failure lands on a specific, information-poor claimant whose access to justice depends on the funder's solvency—a concentrated welfare loss the boundary-clarity benefit (an administrative convenience enjoyed mostly by sophisticated repeat players) cannot offset. Second, the formal line here is not a neutral convenience but the very instrument of the arbitrage, drawn and redrawn to keep identical risk on the lighter side (the JPI-to-funding migration is the demonstration); a boundary whose location is gamed forfeits the predictability premium Turk would credit it. Against that, the functional perimeter is kept nearly as administrable as the formal one by the conjunctive trigger specified in V.D, supplemented by bright-line proxies such as a paid-up-capital floor and a contingent-recovery loss-floor that do most of the sorting without case-by-case adjudication. The residual boundary cost is thus small and far outweighed by the counterparty harm the formal line leaves unaddressed. Finally, proportionality supplies the innovation safeguard: a regulatory sandbox or tiered entry—of the kind Utah's Office of Legal Services Innovation and Arizona's alternative-business-structure framework have pioneered for nonlawyer capital—lets novel structures be observed under monitoring before they incur full obligation.[^336] The rejoinder that a sandbox merely delays regulation has the comparison backwards: the alternative—no perimeter at all—produced the JPI contraction's disorderly migration. Bounded uncertainty under supervision beats unbounded uncertainty under none.

### V.D. Objection Three: "It Is Just Non-Recourse Financing, Not Insurance"

This is the hinge. The doctrinally strongest single attack on the Article is that TPLF is categorically not insurance and that the functional-equivalence claim is a category error. It must be put at full strength, because a tier-0 insurance reader will nod along, and the answer depends on the reader having nodded.

As Part II.D established at length, non-recourse funding fails every positive-law element of insurance: it confers no benefit triggered by an adverse event and so flunks New York Insurance Law § 1101(a)(1)'s indemnity and material-interest requirements; it underwrites a single claim rather than pooling and distributing a book of insureds' risks, and so fails the risk-spreading core of *Royal Drug*, *VALIC*, and *Jordan*; and the contingency that *Ruth* and *Maslowski* held defeats the unconditional repayment obligation of a loan defeats the indemnity obligation of insurance as well.[^337] It is claim *investment*, not litigation *insurance*. The formalist's case is, on its own terms, airtight.

All true—and beside the point. The Article does not claim that funding satisfies the positive-law definition of insurance. It claims that funding is a *functional substitute* that transfers and prices the *same underlying legal-claim risk*. The objection, pressed to its conclusion, *is* the Article's thesis. The doctrinal definition tracks form—indemnity versus assignment, pooling versus single-asset—rather than economic function, and that gap between a deal's economics and its regulatory treatment is the textbook definition of the arbitrage Victor Fleischer identified.[^338] The objection does not refute the equivalence; it confesses the mechanism.

Three reinforcing moves convert the confession into a demonstration. First, the boundary is contested even within insurance theory. Kenneth Abraham shows that "insurance" is not one essence but four conceptions—contract, regulated industry, product, and governance—each carrying a distinct regulatory agenda.[^339] Once one sees that the *regulatory* agenda attaches to insurance-as-governance—to risk-bearing that creates counterparty and systemic exposure—the indemnity-versus-assignment formalism loses its grip: ATE (indemnity, regulated) and TPLF (assignment, unregulated) discharge the same governance-relevant function. Second, the mirror image proves the equivalence on the facts. JPI is the identical appellate and legal-claim risk written as indemnity and regulated as insurance—the Appian 8-K disclosure described in the Introduction is the exemplar.[^340] When the *BMC Software v. IBM* reversal put such towers into the money and the insured form retrenched, the identical risk reappeared in the assignment form—funding and monetization.[^341] That a single contractual switch flips the regulatory regime on economically identical risk is the definition of arbitrage, not a principled distinction. Third, formalism is not a safe harbor but a tripwire, unstable on its own terms. The United Kingdom Supreme Court in *PACCAR* re-characterized percentage-of-damages funding agreements as "damages-based agreements" because the funder provided "claims management services" paid by reference to the benefit obtained—rendering most of the British market unenforceable overnight.[^342] When labels control, the result is arbitrary and destabilizing; the functional approach is the only one that does not blow up on reclassification.

That leaves the hardest rejoinder: that functional equivalence proves too much. If "bears risk" is the test, every contingent contract—stock options, warranties, equity itself—would be swept in, and the thesis would regulate the capital markets. The answer is that the trigger is not "bears risk" but a *conjunction* of three elements: (i) a non-recourse transfer of a *specific litigant's claim risk*, (ii) to a counterparty *on whose performance the claimant relies*, (iii) *under information asymmetry and claimant vulnerability*. That conjunction is what ATE, JPI, TPLF, and contingency fees share and what a stock option lacks: an option has no vulnerable, counterparty-dependent claimant whose access to justice turns on the counterparty's solvency. The further rejoinder—that contingency fees are lawyer services governed by fiduciary law and so categorically different—concedes the regime difference while confirming the function identity. The contingent-fee lawyer is the original legal-claim-risk bearer, which is why Model Rules 1.8(e) and 5.4 are best understood as function-regulation the profession already accepts.[^343]

The last word belongs to a leading insurance scholar. Tom Baker characterizes non-recourse litigation funding as "a contemporary form of the respondentia and bottomry that insurance historians regard as the earliest forms of commercial insurance"—instruments that fused financing with risk-bearing.[^344] The functional-equivalence claim is thus mainstream insurance scholarship, not a litigator's gambit. Every premise of the formalist objection is correct—funding is not, in positive law, insurance—yet each names a difference in *form* while leaving the *function* untouched. The objection is not a refutation but a confession that specifies the formal seam along which identical legal-claim risk slips from the regulated regime into the unregulated one. This Article takes the regulatory step Baker brackets.

### V.E. Objection Four: Regulatory Capture and Overreach

The capture-and-overreach objection is two-sided, and both sides deserve steelmanning. On capture: to hand legal-claim-risk regulation to state insurance commissioners is to invite capture by insurance incumbents who would benefit from kneecapping funding competitors—and, conversely, whichever body regulates will be worked by the funding lobby or the defense bar, producing rules that entrench rather than protect. On overreach: the federal national-security framing—foreign capital, sensitive-sector discovery—risks weaponizing "regulation" as a pretext to suppress disfavored litigation, a genuine civil-liberties and access concern.

The first answer is that capture is an argument about institutional design, not about whether to regulate. The prescription is deliberately polycentric—insurance commissioners for prudential and solvency questions, court rules for procedural disclosure, consumer-protection authority for the consumer tier—so that no single capturable chokepoint exists. Divided authority is itself an anti-capture device. The second answer is comparative: capture-resistant process is demonstrably feasible. The CJC's review—an independent judicial body, public consultation, fifty-eight recommendations—and the European Parliament's Voss-Report route, adopted 504 to 57, are legitimacy-conferring, non-captured processes.[^345] Contrast the United States state-by-state scramble, where industry lobbies visibly drove content: Georgia's SB 69, Louisiana's Act 765, and Oklahoma's foreign-funding statute bear the fingerprints of the interests that wrote them.[^346] The third answer is that overreach is a reason to *prefer* function-based, content-neutral triggers. The national-security harm is real—the GAO documents foreign funding of patent litigation that public data cannot capture, and the DOJ's FARA advisory opinion reached a foreign-charity-funded impact suit—but the cure is risk-based prudential and disclosure rules that apply regardless of viewpoint, not viewpoint-sensitive bans.[^347] A functional trigger does not ask what the suit is about, only what risk is transferred and to whom; it is the overreach-minimizing option, letting the Article take the national-security concern seriously while distinguishing itself from the crude foreign-funding prohibitions proliferating in the states. The fourth answer reframes the burden: as Part V.B noted, "no regulation" is not neutrality but a settlement favoring the best-capitalized, most opaque players—the capture outcome funders prefer—so naming the status quo as the captured equilibrium reallocates the burden onto the objection's proponent.

The federalism rejoinder—that polycentric authority means gaps and turf wars, and that McCarran-Ferguson makes any federal-state overlay incoherent—is a drafting constraint, not a defeater. McCarran-Ferguson reverse-preemption attaches to the narrowly defined "business of insurance"; the functional regime can occupy the conduct-and-disclosure space that is *not* that business, leaving solvency to the states.[^348] And where consumer-tier conduct authority is politically fragile federally, it is pleaded in the alternative through state UDAP regimes—an authority states already exercise, as West Virginia's consumer-funding fee caps and Oklahoma's consumer-funder licensure demonstrate.[^349]

### V.F. Objection Five: Measurement and Administrability

The final objection is that the functional trigger—"transfers and prices legal-claim risk"—is too indeterminate to administer. Regulators cannot measure the risk being transferred, because the market is opaque and there is no standardized data; they cannot draw the perimeter without sweeping in benign financing; and they will generate classification litigation that reproduces the very chaos the Article decries. A standard one cannot measure, the objector concludes, is a standard one cannot enforce.

The first answer inverts the premise. The data gap is an argument *for* the regime, not against it. The absence of standardized data—the GAO's repeated findings that public sources cannot capture funding, and Westfleet's own report, which expressly excludes consumer, mass-tort, and personal-injury finance from its scope—is itself a symptom of regulatory arbitrage.[^350] Opacity is the product of non-regulation, and insurance regulation generates exactly the solvency and reserve data that funding hides. This is not speculation: as Part I.D explained, the shadow-insurance literature could quantify the captive-reinsurance migration *because* insurance reporting existed.[^351] You can measure what you require to be disclosed; you cannot measure what you leave dark.

The second answer dissolves the perimeter problem with the conjunctive test stated in V.D rather than an open "prices risk" standard. The comparative regimes supply working perimeter language that is administrable, litigated, and stable: Singapore's "qualifying Third-Party Funder," defined by a principal-business test plus a S$5 million paid-up-capital floor, and the EU Representative Actions Directive's "financial overview" disclosed to the court.[^352] These are not aspirations; they are operative law. The third answer turns the objection against the rival solution. Mandatory disclosure—the Federal Rule path, H.R. 1109, the state disclosure statutes—is touted as the administrable middle ground precisely because it is easy to administer. But it is easy to administer because it measures nothing of regulatory consequence: it reveals *who* funds without capitalizing or measuring the risk, leaving counterparty and solvency exposure untouched.[^353] Administrability that measures the wrong thing is false economy. The fourth answer re-attributes the chaos the objector fears: the classification turmoil of *PACCAR* and Australia's *Brookfield* came from forcing funding into a borrowed *entity* box—a damages-based agreement, a managed investment scheme—and a purpose-built functional definition avoids the over- and under-inclusion that doomed those borrowed categories.[^354] The administrability objection thus boomerangs onto the objector's preferred entity approach. The residual worry—that funders will engineer around a prong by adding nominal recourse or fragmenting the claim—is the generic anti-avoidance problem of all functional regulation, met by substance-over-form drafting and by the flexibility entity tests lack: the function regulator follows the risk, whereas the entity regulator is stuck with its box.[^355]

### V.G. Comparative Lesson One: Australia's Managed-Investment-Scheme Round-Trip

Australia is the centerpiece because it is the only jurisdiction that ran this Article's experiment—it regulated litigation funding as a financial product—and then unwound it, a rare before-and-after natural experiment in functional regulation done the *wrong* way. The narrative is a round-trip. In 2009, the Full Federal Court in *Brookfield Multiplex* held that a funded class action constitutes a "managed investment scheme" (MIS) under the Corporations Act, triggering registration; the government issued exemptions in response.[^356] In 2020, the government reversed field and *required* class-action funders to operate as registered MISs and to hold an Australian Financial Services Licence under ASIC oversight—the high-water mark of treating funding like a regulated financial product.[^357] In 2022, the Full Federal Court in *LCM Funding v Stanwell* overruled *Brookfield* as "plainly wrong," Lee J memorably describing the MIS characterization as placing "a square peg into a round hole," because group members contributed no "money or money's worth" and there was no "pooling."[^358] And later in 2022, the government repealed the regime, expressly exempting funding from the MIS, AFSL, product-disclosure, and anti-hawking rules.[^359] The whole 2009-in / 2020-in / 2022-out oscillation collapsed onto a single contested classification.

The cautionary core must be held precisely. Australia regulated the *function* by borrowing an ill-fitting *entity* box. Because the obligation hung on a statutory classification a court could revisit, the regime was hostage to one appellate re-characterization—and *Stanwell* vaporized it. This is the Article's thesis *in vivo*: entity-classification regulation of functionally identical risk-transfer is doctrinally and politically unstable. The lesson is emphatically *not* that functional regulation failed; it is that entity-classification regulation of a function failed. *Stanwell*'s reasoning is entirely about MIS-fit—no pooling, no money's worth—not a judgment that funders should bear no prudential or conflict obligations. The policy debate that preceded the regime in fact engaged the right targets: the Australian Law Reform Commission's 2018 inquiry was reportedly oriented toward function-relevant variables—conflict management and the fairness of the claimant's share—rather than the entity question the courts later seized on.[^360] The 2020 regime's accidental gesture at tiering—it applied only to class-action funders, exempting insolvency and single-plaintiff funders—is what the Article's vulnerability-calibration does on purpose. The design instruction follows: build a purpose-built functional category, never a borrowed financial-product or insurance box, so that no single reclassification can collapse it. The rejoinder that Australia's repeal proves functional regulation unworkable misreads it: the regime died by classification incoherence, not by a verdict against prudential obligation, and the parallel British and European moves toward bespoke funder rules show the trend running *toward* function-regulation, away from entity-borrowing.

### V.H. Comparative Lesson Two: The United Kingdom After *PACCAR*

The United Kingdom supplies the cleanest demonstration that entity and form classification is a tripwire, and a live, unfinished response the Article can track in real time. *PACCAR*, the triggering event introduced in V.D, rendered most percentage-of-damages funding agreements unenforceable as non-compliant damages-based agreements and crippled opt-out collective proceedings in the Competition Appeal Tribunal, where such agreements are barred.[^361] The chaos came with a lag that itself illustrates how arbitrage thrives in legal uncertainty: the first legislative fix, the Litigation Funding Agreements (Enforceability) Bill, lapsed at the 2024 dissolution, and a multi-year limbo followed while the CJC reviewed.[^362]

The response, however, is a near-direct analogue to this Article's prescription. The CJC Final Report recommends treating funding agreements as a *distinct* form of funding—not damages-based agreements—and pairs bespoke, function-focused statutory regulation with capital adequacy, a prohibition on funder control, court approval of returns in collective proceedings on a "fair, just and reasonable" standard rather than a flat cap, and protections calibrated to consumer and non-commercial claimants.[^363] That is functional, vulnerability-calibrated regulation from an independent judicial body. But the response is also a warning: the government's December 2025 written statement chose to reverse *PACCAR* prospectively only, departing from the CJC's recommended retrospective reversal and leaving legacy contracts in limbo.[^364] A functional regime must therefore specify transition and legacy treatment for risk-transfer contracts written under a regime a court later upends—directly relevant to how the United States should treat JPI policies and funding agreements written before reform. To the three instructions that follow (purpose-built category, vulnerability calibration, legacy rules), the LASPO strand adds a fourth, often-overlooked lever: cost-shifting rules powerfully shape which instrument claimants choose, so a functional regime that ignores fee-shifting interaction will merely push risk into the cheapest-to-regulate channel.[^365] The objection that the United Kingdom's loser-pays, ATE-mature system does not travel concedes the cost-rule divergence—which is why LASPO matters—but the *PACCAR* lesson is system-independent: it is the fragility of definitional classification, identical to the United States "is it a loan? is it insurance?" fights in *Ruth* and *Maslowski*.

### V.I. Comparative Lesson Three: The Singapore and Hong Kong Arbitration Carve-Outs

Singapore and Hong Kong show function- and forum-calibrated liberalization: champerty abolished only for a defined channel, with conduct and disclosure rules attached. Singapore's Civil Law (Amendment) Act 2017 abolished maintenance and champerty and validated funding for prescribed proceedings, and the accompanying regulations impose a "qualifying Third-Party Funder" test—a principal-business requirement plus paid-up capital or managed assets of at least S$5 million—backed by unenforceability for non-compliance; the 2021 expansion to domestic arbitration and Singapore International Commercial Court proceedings shows incremental, evidence-based perimeter-widening.[^366] Hong Kong's 2017 Ordinance abolished champerty for arbitration and mediation, mandated disclosure of the funding agreement's existence and the funder's identity, and backed both with a Code of Practice.[^367]

The design lesson is twofold. A capital threshold plus a principal-business test is an administrable, vulnerability-relevant prudential filter far lighter than insurance solvency regulation but not zero—a template for the prescription's middle tier and a concrete rebuttal to the measurement objection, because the S$5 million test is litigated and stable. A mandatory-disclosure-plus-code regime is a workable conduct floor. Both regimes confirm that disclosure is the common tool across jurisdictions—and that neither serious regime *stops* at disclosure; each pairs it with gatekeeping or capital, exactly the Article's disclosure-plus position. One adaptation: forum-gating—arbitration only—is a feature of jurisdictions easing *into* liberalization from a champerty baseline. The United States, having largely abandoned champerty, should import the prudential and disclosure *tools* but swap the arbitration-only *trigger* for a claimant-vulnerability trigger. That the Asian carve-outs were motivated by pro-arbitration competitiveness rather than claimant protection does not impair the borrowing: the tools are motive-neutral. The Article borrows mechanisms, not motives.

### V.J. Comparative Lesson Four: The EU Representative Actions Directive and the Voss Report

The European Union brackets the design space with two contrasting data points: the enacted minimalism of the Representative Actions Directive (RAD) and the aspirational maximalism of the Voss Report. The RAD engaged funding only obliquely, through a consumer-protection instrument rather than financial-product regulation; its Article 10 addresses funding solely through conflict-of-interest safeguards—the funder's economic interest must not divert the action, and a funded action may not be brought against the funder's competitor—plus a "financial overview" disclosed to the court.[^368] This disclosure-plus-conflicts *minimum* solves the wrong problem in the same way the United States disclosure proposals do: it reveals funding and ignores solvency and counterparty risk. The Voss Report is the ceiling. The European Parliament's 2022 resolution, adopted 504 to 57, recommends a dedicated authorization-and-supervision regime for commercial funders, minimum capital adequacy, conflict rules, a minimum-claimant-share rule under which claimants should as a rule receive at least 60% of gross recovery, and a bar on funders limiting adverse-costs liability.[^369] That is the most directly on-point comparator for the prescription: functional, prudential, claimant-protective.

The gap between RAD (enacted, minimal) and Voss (recommended, robust) maps the Article's argument exactly—disclosure-only is the politically easy floor, genuine risk-based regulation the harder, correct ceiling—and the European Union is mid-journey between them, mirroring the choice the United States now faces. The design menu harvests Voss's four instruments—authorization, capital adequacy, minimum claimant share, and adverse-costs liability—treating the 60% share and the adverse-costs rule as *tier-specific*: apt for the vulnerable, consumer, and collective tiers, not necessarily for sophisticated commercial portfolios. The objection that a hard 60% share is paternalistic price control is the access objection again, answered the same way: route the share rule to the vulnerable tiers and prefer court approval of returns over a flat cap—the calibrated path the CJC chose.[^370] That the Commission never took up the resolution does not diminish its value as a worked-out functional blueprint; its non-enactment is itself instructive about the politics of moving from the disclosure floor to the functional ceiling.

### V.K. Synthesis: What the Comparative Record Teaches

Two findings emerge from the four comparators, and together they prove the thesis on foreign data. The first is a convergence finding: disclosure is the universal tool, but every regime that took the problem seriously paired it with something more—a capital floor and principal-business test in Singapore, a code of practice in Hong Kong, authorization and a claimant-share rule in Voss, capital adequacy and court approval of returns in the CJC. Disclosure-only is the global *floor* that no thoughtful regime treats as sufficient—direct comparative support for the Article's claim that disclosure solves the wrong problem. The second is the central anti-entity lesson: the two regimes that regulated by entity and form classification—Australia's managed-investment-scheme regime and the United Kingdom's damages-based-agreement regime via *PACCAR*—both suffered destabilizing judicial reclassification in *Stanwell* and *PACCAR*, while the regimes that regulated by purpose-built, function-targeted rules—Singapore, Hong Kong, and the CJC and Voss blueprints—are stable. The comparative record is therefore a controlled demonstration of the core thesis: regulate the function, not the entity.

That demonstration yields a design menu the prescription inherits directly: (i) a purpose-built statutory category, never a borrowed box; (ii) a capital and prudential threshold scaled to claimant vulnerability—the Singapore floor, the Voss capital-adequacy standard, insurance-grade solvency only at the top; (iii) disclosure-plus-conflicts as the universal base, from the RAD and Hong Kong; (iv) claimant-share and return-fairness through court approval rather than a flat cap, the CJC over the hard Voss number; (v) explicit legacy and transition rules for contracts written before reform, the British prospective-versus-retrospective lesson applied to United States JPI policies and funding agreements; and (vi) content-neutral triggers that avoid the overreach of viewpoint-based foreign-funding bans. By foregrounding the comparator that cuts hardest against it—Australia's repeal—and showing that, read correctly, it corroborates rather than refutes the thesis, the Article pre-empts the cherry-picking charge by leading with its hardest case. The prescription thus survives its strongest objections and is corroborated by every jurisdiction that has engaged the same fault line. That sets up the choice the Conclusion presses: the United States is deciding, right now, between the RAD-style disclosure floor and the Voss-and-CJC-style functional ceiling—with the JPI contraction the warning of precisely what the floor leaves unaddressed.

## Conclusion

We began with a date and a reversal. On April 30, 2024, the Fifth Circuit set aside a roughly $1.6 billion judgment in *BMC Software, Inc. v. International Business Machines Corp.*,[^371] and the loss did not fall where a casual observer would have looked for it. It fell on a tower of judgment-preservation insurers, a Liberty Mutual-led syndicate that, according to legal-press reporting, had reportedly written coverage on the reversal risk somewhere in the range of $500 to $750 million.[^372] When a second large award-insurance loss followed within months, the reported reduction of a Quinn Emanuel fee award from $185 million to $92.4 million left underwriters exposed on a policy that had guaranteed roughly ninety percent of the original figure;[^373] carriers did what regulated risk-bearers do when their loss experience deteriorates. They raised prices, cut the insured share of a judgment, and exited deals.[^374] Premiums, by one account, as much as doubled.[^375]

The lesson of that contraction is the lesson of this Article. The reversal risk that judgment-preservation insurers had priced did not evaporate when the insurance form grew expensive. It is still there, embedded in every large judgment on appeal, and it remains a thing that holders will pay to shed. What changed was only the wrapper. As the regulated form repriced upward, the same downside — the risk that a verdict will not survive review — became available to monetize through non-recourse appellate funding and claim sales that sit under no prudential regime at all.[^376] The air pressed out of one end of the balloon bulged into the other. It did so not because funding is a better product than insurance, but because the law made the move cheap: identical economic risk, transferred and priced by functionally substitutable instruments, meets sharply unequal regulation depending on which label the parties select. That asymmetry is not an incidental feature of the field. It is the field's organizing logic, and it is the engine of the arbitrage.

Stated at altitude, the claim is this. The law of legal-claim risk is built instrument by instrument — solvency and guaranty regulation for what is called insurance, professional-responsibility rules for what is called a fee, near-silence for what is called funding — and that very formalism is what makes the risk mobile. Capital flows to the form the law taxes least. This is the dynamic that Victor Fleischer named as the signature of regulatory arbitrage: the exploitation of a gap between the economics of a transaction and its regulatory treatment, restructuring the deal to shed regulatory cost without altering the underlying economics.[^377] And it is the dynamic that Daniel Schwarcz and the New York Department of Financial Services documented, under the borrowed name this Article adopts, when life insurers moved blocks of risk to affiliated captives subject to lighter oversight, ceded liabilities that grew, on the Department's figures, from $11 billion to $364 billion.[^378] The contribution here is neither the resemblance nor the metaphor. Tom Baker has already shown that non-recourse funding functions like the earliest forms of commercial insurance and that an insurance market is forming around it;[^379] this Article takes the regulatory step Baker brackets. It is worth restating, too, what the Article does *not* claim: that funding *is* insurance as a doctrinal matter. It is not. Non-recourse funding fails the indemnity, pooling, and insurable-interest elements that define the business of insurance,[^380] and that doctrinal non-identity is precisely why the arbitrage exists. The equivalence is functional, a matter of risk transfer and pricing, and the divergence in classification is the phenomenon.

The three contributions follow from that single reframing. The functional taxonomy makes the arbitrage *visible*: when the instruments are plotted by what they do rather than by what they are called, they cluster by economic substitutability while the regulatory colors scatter across those clusters — adjacent points, identical risk, different regimes. The diagnosis identifies *which* arbitrage harms are real, sorting the genuine injuries — uninsured counterparty risk shifted onto claimants and courts, captured litigation control, and a sector the public data cannot see — from the rhetorical ones, and showing why mandatory disclosure, whatever else it accomplishes, solves the wrong problem. It addresses information and conflicts, not the capital-adequacy and counterparty-default exposures the migration actually creates. Disclosure is necessary; it is not sufficient; and the field's reflexive reach for it, even as the federal rulemakers concede they do not yet know what a court should do with the information once disclosed,[^381] is itself a symptom of a function left unclassified. The prescription then regulates *only* what the diagnosis flags, by function and by claimant vulnerability rather than by entity or label, through a graded functional-insurance trigger that attaches to true indemnity and pooling wherever they appear, and a tiered floor calibrated to the sophistication of the party bearing the risk.

The United States is making this choice now. One path is the disclosure floor: the procedural, information-forcing model toward which the pending federal proposals and the 2023–26 state wave overwhelmingly tend, and which the European Union's Representative Actions Directive exemplifies, with conflicts safeguards and a financial overview but no prudential supervision of the risk-bearer.[^382] The other path is the functional ceiling: authorization and supervision, capital adequacy, a minimum share preserved for the claimant, and court approval of returns — the architecture of the Voss Report and, in near-direct analogue, the Civil Justice Council's recommendation of bespoke, function-focused regulation calibrated to vulnerable claimants.[^383] The collapse of the judgment-preservation market is the warning the floor leaves unanswered. Disclosure would have named the carriers on the *BMC* tower; it would not have capitalized the funder to whom the reversal risk migrated next.

Honesty about method requires one concession. The migration this Article describes is a documented market episode and a testable hypothesis, not yet a measured causal fact. The qualitative record is strong — a regulated form that repriced and contracted, a substitutable unregulated form that re-emerged to absorb the same risk — but it is qualitative, drawn in part from interested industry sources, and it lacks the hard cross-instrument flow data that gives shadow insurance its empirical force. That gap is itself an indictment: opacity is the product of non-regulation, and the figures exist in the insurance setting only because reporting was required. The method for closing it already exists. Koijen and Yogo measured the captive-reinsurance channel because the data were there to measure;[^384] a registration-and-reporting perimeter would make the legal-claim-risk market measurable in the same way, and future empirics could then test the hypothesis this Article frames rather than assume it.

The risk in *BMC* did not disappear. It moved, because the law made it cheap to move, and it will keep moving — out of whichever form the next shock makes expensive, into whichever form the regulatory line leaves cheapest — until the law stops chasing labels and attaches to the thing itself. Regulate the risk, wherever it hides.
